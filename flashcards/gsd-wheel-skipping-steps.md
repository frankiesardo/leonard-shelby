<summary>Kim Scott's first act as a new Google manager was to restructure her teams because it was "obviously" the right move. Three of five direct reports resigned. What step(s) of the GSD wheel did she skip, and what principle does this illustrate?</summary>

She skipped almost every step: Listen, Clarify, Debate, and Persuade. She jumped straight from her own private Decide to Execute. Even though the restructuring may have been objectively correct, the process was catastrophically wrong. The GSD wheel (Listen, Clarify, Debate, Decide, Persuade, Execute, Learn) exists because telling people what to do doesn't work, even when you're right. People need to feel heard, need to understand the reasoning, and need to buy in. Skipping steps doesn't save time -- it creates resistance, resentment, and attrition that cost far more than the time the steps would have taken.

See: [The Get Stuff Done Wheel](../ideas/the-get-stuff-done-wheel.md)
