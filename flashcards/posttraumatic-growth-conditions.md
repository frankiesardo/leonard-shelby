<summary>"What doesn't kill you makes you stronger" is a dangerous oversimplification. Under what four conditions does adversity actually produce growth?</summary>

1. **Right time** -- late teens to mid-twenties, when identity is actively being formed and the life story is being written. Glen Elder found that people whose lives had "crystallized" after age thirty were less resilient.

2. **Right degree** -- severe enough to shake assumptions and force reappraisal, but not so severe as to cause PTSD. There is a sweet spot between challenge and devastation.

3. **Right social support** -- people embedded in strong social networks fare much better. Social ties provide both practical help and the raw material for meaning-making. Adversity faced alone is far more likely to damage than to strengthen.

4. **Right personal resources** -- a sense of agency, prior experience with difficulty, and psychological flexibility to reinterpret events.

The practical implication: shelter children when young, but by late adolescence begin allowing real challenges. Build strong social networks *before* you need them. And when adversity comes, look for the meaning it might generate rather than only the damage it has caused. Wisdom -- defined as tacit knowledge for balancing competing needs -- typically grows through this process and cannot be taught directly.
