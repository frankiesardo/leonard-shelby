<summary>What is the most commonly violated step in the Five Focusing Steps, and why is it the most dangerous?</summary>

Step 5: "If in the previous steps a constraint has been broken, go back to step 1 -- but do not allow INERTIA to cause a system's constraint." When you successfully elevate a constraint, it shifts somewhere else. But the policies, procedures, and habits you created to manage the old constraint remain in place. These become the new constraint if you don't question them. In Rogo's plant, the red/green tag priority system was essential when the bottleneck was the NCX-10 and oven. But after the constraint shifted to the market, those same tags were delaying non-bottleneck parts at assembly and creating unnecessary holes in the buffer. Stacey discovered that eliminating the tags -- the very system that had saved them -- would free up significant capacity. Inertia from past solutions is the silent killer of improvement.
