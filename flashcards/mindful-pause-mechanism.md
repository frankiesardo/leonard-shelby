<summary>What is the "mindful pause," and why does Kornfield say it transforms our days even though it takes only a few breaths?</summary>

The mindful pause is the practice of taking a breath (or a few breaths) before responding when triggered. It creates a gap between stimulus and response -- the same gap Frankl identified as the seat of human freedom.

Without the pause, one triggering event (a slight in a meeting, being cut off in traffic) colors the entire day. You snap at people, judge yourself, see things as more dire than they are. The unprocessed emotion becomes the lens for everything that follows.

Kornfield used this as a therapist: five minutes of shared silence before every session. Clients who arrived caught up in road rage or workplace drama couldn't listen to themselves or anyone else. The silence created enough space for the real conversation to begin.

The mechanism: reactivity hijacks the primitive brain. The pause gives the prefrontal cortex time to catch up, shifting from reaction to response. The same words -- "What did you mean?" -- can be an accusation or a genuine inquiry. The difference is the half-second of intention that the pause allows.
