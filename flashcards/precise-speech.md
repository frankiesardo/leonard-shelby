<summary>Why does Peterson claim that an unspecified problem is a dragon, but a specified problem is usually manageable?</summary>

When you refuse to articulate what is wrong, the threat remains shapeless and unlimited -- it can be anything, and therefore it is everything. This is the dragon: a vague dread that infects your whole life. When you name the problem precisely -- say exactly what is wrong, what you want, what needs to change -- you collapse the infinite unknown into a bounded, specific challenge. Most specific problems, once articulated, turn out to be solvable or at least manageable. The act of precise speech is world-creating: it converts chaos into order by making the unknown known.
