<summary>Jobs's father taught him a lesson about carpentry that became Apple's defining philosophy. What was the lesson, and how did it manifest in Apple products?</summary>

Paul Jobs told his son: "When you're a carpenter making a beautiful chest of drawers, you're not going to use a piece of plywood on the back, even though it faces the wall and nobody will ever see it. You'll know it's there, so you're going to use a beautiful piece of wood on the back." At Apple, this meant the original Macintosh team signed their names inside the case where no user would see them. The iPod's circuit board was beautiful though sealed inside the device. The principle: taste means caring about the parts no one sees, because quality is not a surface treatment -- it is an orientation toward the work itself.
