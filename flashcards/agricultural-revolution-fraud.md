<summary>In what sense did wheat "domesticate" humans rather than the other way around?</summary>

Wheat went from a wild grass covering a small area of the Middle East to one of the most successful plants on Earth, covering 2.25 million square kilometers. It achieved this by getting *Homo sapiens* to devote enormous effort to clearing fields, carrying water, collecting dung, weeding, and guarding it -- all for the privilege of eating a nutritionally inferior diet while working harder than foragers. The "domestication" of wheat was a spectacular success for wheat's DNA. For individual humans, it meant harder work, worse nutrition, more disease, and less varied lives. The currency of evolution is copies of DNA, not individual happiness.
