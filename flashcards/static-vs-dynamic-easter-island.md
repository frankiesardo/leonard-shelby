<summary>Why does Deutsch argue that Easter Island is the *wrong* analogy for modern civilization, not a warning?</summary>

Easter Island was a **static society** -- one that actively suppressed the creation of new knowledge through taboo, conformity, and the destruction of creativity. When problems arose (deforestation, soil erosion), the islanders could not create new knowledge to solve them. They were chronically unable to innovate. Their collapse was not caused by poor resource management; it was caused by the inability to solve problems, which is the defining feature of *all* static societies.

Modern Western civilization is fundamentally different. It is a **dynamic society** -- one that creates knowledge rapidly through traditions of criticism, tolerance of dissent, and institutional error correction. The knowledge that would have saved the Easter Islanders (navigation, agriculture, a scientific outlook) has been in our possession for centuries.

Comparing a dynamic society to a static one is exactly backwards. If we fail, it will not be for the same reasons or in the same way. "Sustainability" in the static sense -- maintaining an unchanging way of life -- is itself the most unsustainable strategy, because it guarantees that the first unsolvable-with-current-knowledge problem will be fatal.
