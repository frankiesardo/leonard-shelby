<summary>Why do lottery winners and paraplegics converge toward similar happiness levels within about a year, and what does this reveal about the pursuit of wealth?</summary>

The adaptation principle: the mind is exquisitely sensitive to *changes* in conditions but largely blind to absolute levels. The lottery winner's new comforts become the new baseline within months -- the contrast with the old life fades, and there is no way to rise further. The paraplegic hits bottom but then sets more modest goals; each step of physical therapy provides a sense of progress.

This is the hedonic treadmill: you can increase the speed all you want, but you stay in the same place. Accumulating wealth and status resets the baseline each time, so rising wealth does not produce rising happiness. In the last fifty years, GDP in wealthy nations has doubled or tripled, but reported happiness and life satisfaction have not changed -- and depression has actually increased.

The crucial caveat: adaptation does not apply equally to everything. Relationships, control over stressors, freedom from chronic noise and long commutes all resist adaptation. The practical question is not "does anything matter?" but "what doesn't adapt away?"
