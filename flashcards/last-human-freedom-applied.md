<summary>Frankl says the last human freedom is "to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances." A critic objects: "That's easy to say from a comfortable office. It doesn't apply to real extremity." How does Frankl's own biography answer this?</summary>

The critic has it exactly backwards. Frankl did not discover this principle in a comfortable office -- he validated it in Auschwitz, where he was prisoner Number 119,104 digging railway tracks in winter. He lost his manuscript, his wife, his parents, his brother. Ninety percent of his transport was gassed on arrival.

And yet:
- He watched men give away their last bread to comfort others
- He observed a dying woman express gratitude for her suffering because it made her take spiritual life seriously
- He himself chose to stay with his patients rather than escape, and felt an "inward peace" he had never experienced before
- He mentally reconstructed his lost manuscript on scraps of paper during a typhus delirium

The proof is not that choosing one's attitude is easy. It is that even in the most extreme conditions conceivable, some people still did it -- "but even one such example is sufficient proof that man's inner strength may raise him above his outward fate."

Covey later built his entire first habit (Be Proactive) on Frankl's insight: between stimulus and response, there is a space.
