<summary>Kornfield tells the story of two former prisoners of war meeting 25 years later. One asks about forgiveness. What's the punchline, and why does Kornfield say forgiveness is ultimately for yourself?</summary>

"Have you forgiven our captors yet?" asks the first. "No. Never," says the second. "Well then, they still have you in prison, don't they?"

Forgiveness is not for the other person -- they may never change. It doesn't condone what happened, doesn't mean forgetting, and doesn't mean rolling over. You still see the wrong clearly, feel the suffering, and resolve to stop it from continuing. But you refuse to carry the hatred in your own heart.

The question is existential: how do you want to live? With a closed heart full of bitterness, or with the dignity of someone who says "it stops here"? Nelson Mandela walked out of 27 years in prison with such magnanimity that he changed not just South Africa but the world's imagination of what's possible. That was a choice, not an accident.
