<summary>In the Boy Scout hike analogy, why does the line of hikers keep spreading out even though everyone walks at roughly the same average speed?</summary>

Because dependent events and statistical fluctuations don't average out -- they accumulate. Each boy's speed fluctuates around an average, but slower-than-average fluctuations propagate backward through the chain while faster-than-average fluctuations can't propagate forward (you can't walk faster than the kid in front of you). The result: gaps grow relentlessly, with each boy having to make up not just his own slow moments but the accumulated slowness of everyone ahead of him. The line length (inventory) increases, and the speed of the last person (throughput) decreases. The fix is to put the slowest hiker (the bottleneck) at the front and lighten his load -- which is what the Theory of Constraints does for any system.
