<summary>A teenager reports constant boredom, plays video games compulsively, and says "nothing matters." A therapist diagnoses depression. Frankl would likely see a different root cause. What is it, and how does the treatment differ?</summary>

Frankl would identify the **existential vacuum** -- a pervasive feeling of emptiness and meaninglessness that is not itself a disease but is potentially pathogenic. The boredom is not a symptom of depression but of frustrated meaning. The compulsive gaming is a substitute: when the will to meaning is blocked, the will to pleasure rushes in to fill the void.

The treatment differs fundamentally:
- Standard therapy might explore childhood trauma or prescribe antidepressants
- Logotherapy would help the teenager discover a concrete meaning -- something that asks something of *them specifically*. Frankl showed that unemployed workers recovered from depression when they volunteered for meaningful unpaid activities, even though their economic situation was unchanged

The key diagnostic question is not "What happened to you?" but "What is missing?" -- and the answer is almost always a purpose worth getting out of bed for.
