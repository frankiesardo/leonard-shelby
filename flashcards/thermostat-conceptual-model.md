<summary>Norman uses the thermostat as his canonical example of a wrong conceptual model. What do most people think a thermostat does, what does it actually do, and what design principle does the mismatch illustrate?</summary>

Most people think a thermostat is a valve: turn it higher and more heat flows out, so cranking it to maximum should heat the room faster. In reality, most thermostats are simple on/off switches -- the heater runs at full power until the set temperature is reached, then shuts off completely. Setting it to maximum doesn't speed heating; it just raises the shutoff point, causing the room to overshoot and waste energy. The mismatch illustrates that the "system image" (what the product communicates through its appearance and behavior) failed to project the correct conceptual model. A well-designed thermostat (Norman cites the Nest) shows current temperature, target temperature, whether it's heating or cooling, and estimated time to reach the target -- giving the user enough information to build an accurate model.
