<summary>Why does Storr say the five-act plot has been popular for over 2,000 years? What engineering principle makes the second half of a story so dramatic?</summary>

The five-act structure is the most efficient machine for breaking and rebuilding a character's theory of control. In the first half, the old theory is tested and found wanting. At the midpoint, the protagonist transforms and adopts a new strategy. The genius of the second half: when the protagonist abandons their old theory of control, they lose the *protection* it had offered against their deepest fears. Their nightmares are unleashed. Every fear that had been keeping them from changing now actually happens. This massive ratcheting of drama at precisely the point when the audience risks getting bored is the engineering insight that has made the structure matchlessly popular.
