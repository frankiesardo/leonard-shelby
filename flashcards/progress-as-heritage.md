<summary>The Durants define progress differently from most optimists. What is their definition, and why does it survive the objection that human nature hasn't changed?</summary>

Progress is **the increasing control of the environment by life** — not increasing happiness (our capacity for fretting is endless), not technological change per se (just new means for old ends), and not moral improvement (human nature hasn't changed in recorded history).

What has progressed is the **heritage we transmit**. Today's heritage is richer than that of Pericles (it includes all the Greek flowering that followed him), richer than Leonardo's (it includes the full Renaissance), richer than Voltaire's (it includes the entire Enlightenment).

"If education is the transmission of civilization, we are unquestionably progressing." But civilization is not inherited — it must be learned and earned by each generation anew. If the transmission were interrupted for one century, we would be savages again.

This definition survives the objection because it doesn't require any improvement in human nature — only the accumulation and transmission of what previous generations created.
