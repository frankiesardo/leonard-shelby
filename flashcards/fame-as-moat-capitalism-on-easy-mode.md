<summary>What does Rory Sutherland mean by "having a great brand means you get to play the game of capitalism on easy mode," and when should founders start building brand fame?</summary>

A famous brand doesn't need to win every comparison — it gets considered automatically. It can charge a premium not because it's objectively better but because it reduces uncertainty for the buyer. The famous brand is the default when choosing feels risky. Fame is a structural competitive advantage that compounds.

For founders: start building brand signals from day one, not after product-market fit. Fame is slow to build — there's always a delay between investment and return. Companies that defer brand thinking until "later" often find the window has closed; they've optimized into commodity.

The counterintuitive implication: sometimes the irrational-seeming brand investment (Durex at the Olympics, Red Bull in space) is exactly right — a one-time memorable event that buys years of recognition.
