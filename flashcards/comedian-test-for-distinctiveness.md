<summary>What is Rory Sutherland's "comedian test" for product distinctiveness, and what does it reveal about the relationship between seriousness and marketing?</summary>

"If you could imagine a stand-up comedian doing a routine about your product, you're onto something. The urge to appear serious is in many ways a disaster in marketing."

A product that's memorable enough to be joked about is a product with a distinctive mental footprint. Generic, serious, category-expected products offer no comedic surface — because there's nothing surprising about them. The comedian test is a proxy for memorability: if it's laughable, it's also shareable, discussable, and notable.

The deeper point: institutional seriousness and marketing effectiveness are in tension. Committees optimize for defensibility and dignity. Great brands optimize for distinctiveness and psychological resonance, which often requires accepting some level of weirdness or self-deprecation.
