<summary>What are the three "between" relationships that Haidt says produce happiness, and why does he reject both the Buddhist and the Western striving approaches as incomplete?</summary>

The three relationships:
1. **Between yourself and others** -- love, attachment, belonging. A good marriage is one of the strongest happiness predictors; the need to belong never fully adapts.
2. **Between yourself and your work** -- flow, vital engagement, using your strengths. Work matched to challenge and skill produces ongoing satisfaction.
3. **Between yourself and something larger** -- religion, nature, a moral tradition, a community. Self-transcendence provides meaning and purpose.

The Buddhist approach (happiness comes from within through detachment) is incomplete because it risks emotional flatness and withdrawal from the very connections that sustain well-being. The Western striving approach (happiness comes from achieving external goals) is incomplete because the hedonic treadmill neutralizes most achievements. "From between" resolves the dichotomy: engage fully, attach deeply, strive with purpose -- but direct your energy toward relationships, meaningful work, and self-transcendence rather than toward wealth, fame, and prestige.

Happiness is not something you find or achieve directly. You set up the conditions and then wait.
