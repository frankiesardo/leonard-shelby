<summary>A friend says "I just want to be happy." According to Frankl, why is this goal self-defeating, and what should they pursue instead?</summary>

Happiness cannot be pursued directly -- it must *ensue* as the unintended side-effect of dedication to a cause greater than oneself or surrender to a person other than oneself. The more you aim at happiness and make it a target, the more you miss it. This is the same logic that applies to success, pleasure, and even sleep.

Frankl calls the direct pursuit "hyper-intention" -- it produces the opposite of what is intended. The friend should instead ask: *What is asking something of me right now?* Meaning is found in three ways: (1) creating a work or doing a deed, (2) experiencing something or loving someone, (3) the attitude taken toward unavoidable suffering.

The shift is from "What can I get from life?" to "What is life asking of me?"
