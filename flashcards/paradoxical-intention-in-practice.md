<summary>You have a colleague who is terrified of public speaking -- specifically, of their voice trembling. The fear is so bad that it causes the very trembling they dread. How would Frankl's paradoxical intention technique apply here?</summary>

Instead of trying to suppress the trembling (which is hyper-intention -- and makes it worse), the colleague should deliberately *intend* to tremble. Before the next presentation, they should resolve: "I'm going to show this audience the most spectacular trembling they've ever witnessed. I'll shake so hard the podium rattles."

This works because:
1. **Fear produces what one fears** (anticipatory anxiety) -- fearing the tremble causes the tremble
2. **You cannot fear what you are actively wishing for** -- the paradoxical wish breaks the vicious circle
3. **Humor enables self-detachment** -- the absurdity of trying to "out-tremble" yourself creates distance between the person and their symptom

Frankl's sweating patient resolved to "pour ten quarts" and was cured in one session after four years of the phobia. A stutterer who tried to stutter on purpose found he could not. The technique leverages the uniquely human capacity to laugh at oneself.
