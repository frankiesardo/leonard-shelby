<summary>What makes an explanation "good" according to David Deutsch, and why does this criterion matter more than evidence?</summary>

A good explanation is one that is **hard to vary** while still accounting for the phenomena it purports to explain. Every detail in the explanation is functional -- change any part and the explanation breaks.

The Greek myth of Persephone "explains" the seasons, but you could swap in any gods with any personalities and the story would work equally well. It is easy to vary, therefore bad. The real explanation -- Earth's axial tilt -- cannot be varied without destroying the explanation.

This matters more than evidence because observations alone cannot generate explanations. Countless bad explanations are compatible with any set of observations. The hard-to-vary criterion filters for explanations that capture real structure in the world, which is why good explanations tend to have reach far beyond their original domain.
