<summary>A retired executive feels life has become meaningless. Her career is over, her spouse has died, and she has a chronic illness. Using Frankl's framework, which of the three paths to meaning are closed and which remain open?</summary>

Frankl identifies three paths to meaning:

1. **Creative work or deeds** -- partially closed (no career), but volunteering, mentoring, or any form of contribution reopens it
2. **Experiencing something or encountering someone** -- still open. Beauty, nature, art, music, grandchildren, new relationships, even a single conversation
3. **Attitude toward unavoidable suffering** -- fully open. This is the path most relevant to her. She can choose how she bears her illness and grief. Suffering ceases to be suffering the moment it finds a meaning

The mistake is assuming that only path 1 (career achievement) counts. Frankl's elderly doctor found meaning in his grief by reframing it as a sacrifice that spared his dead wife from the same pain. The path through suffering is always available -- and for Frankl, it is the most distinctly human.

See: [Meaning Through Suffering](../ideas/meaning-through-suffering.md), [Will to Meaning](../ideas/will-to-meaning.md)
