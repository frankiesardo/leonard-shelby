<summary>Catmull argues that the desire for zero defects is a "false goal." What is the hidden cost of preventing errors, and how does it compare to the cost of fixing them?</summary>

The cost of preventing errors is almost always greater than the cost of fixing them. When organizations make "zero defects" their goal, people learn to avoid situations where errors are possible -- which means they avoid the novel, uncertain, high-value work the organization most needs. The real cost isn't the errors that get through -- it's the innovations that never get attempted. An organization that penalizes failure doesn't get fewer failures; it gets failures that are hidden, discovered late, and far more expensive to fix. Pixar's approach is to make it safe to fail early: "Better to have train wrecks with miniature trains than with real ones."
