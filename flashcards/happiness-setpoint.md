<summary>If a medieval peasant and a modern banker both achieve a major life goal, why might their happiness levels be roughly equal?</summary>

Because happiness is determined mainly by internal biochemistry (serotonin, dopamine, oxytocin), not by external conditions. Each person has a genetically determined setpoint, and while events temporarily push happiness above or below baseline, the system always returns to equilibrium -- like an air-conditioning unit set to a fixed temperature. The peasant finishing his mud hut and the banker paying off his penthouse both experience a serotonin surge to roughly the same level X. The brain does not care that the penthouse is more comfortable. It only measures the delta from baseline. If true, millennia of "progress" have rearranged external conditions without changing how it feels to be alive.
