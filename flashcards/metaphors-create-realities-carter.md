<summary>How does Lakoff use Carter's energy-crisis framing to show that metaphors create realities rather than merely describing them?</summary>

When Carter declared the energy crisis "the moral equivalent of war," the WAR metaphor generated a network of entailments: an external enemy (OPEC, pictured in Arab headdress by cartoonists), a threat to national security, targets, strategy, marshaling forces, imposing sanctions, demanding sacrifices. This was not merely a description -- it was a license for policy change. It hid alternative framings, like Amory Lovins's SOFT vs. HARD PATHS metaphor, which highlighted the technical, economic, and sociopolitical structure of the energy system and pointed toward decentralized, renewable solutions. "People in power get to impose their metaphors" -- and the accepted metaphor defines the reality that everyone else inhabits, making certain actions thinkable and others invisible.
