<summary>How does reframing saving as "buying independence today" change your relationship to the practice of saving?</summary>

The standard frame — saving as delayed gratification — makes saving feel like sacrifice: you deny yourself now to have it later. Housel's reframe: every dollar saved is a claim check on the future *you* control. He gets the value today, right now, when he wakes up and feels the cushion there. The product he's buying is the *feeling of optionality and safety*, not a future expenditure. This reframe makes saving immediately rewarding rather than a deprivation. The complementary image: your financial "channel of endurance" — the range of bad outcomes you can survive. Every dollar of savings widens that channel. Survival is the only word that matters financially.
