<summary>What is the difference between self-affirmation and self-acceptance in Adlerian psychology, and why does the philosopher call self-affirmation a "life-lie"?</summary>

**Self-affirmation** is telling yourself "I can do it" or "the real me is 100%" when you're actually at 60%. It's positive-thinking delusion that inflates your self-image to avoid confronting reality. It is a life-lie because it requires ongoing maintenance and crumbles when tested.

**Self-acceptance** is acknowledging "I'm at 60%, and that's my starting point. How do I move toward 100%?" No pretending, no inflation -- just honest acknowledgment and the courage to grow from where you actually are.

The philosopher calls this "affirmative resignation" -- seeing clearly with fortitude. It echoes the Serenity Prayer: accept what you cannot change, change what you can, and have the wisdom to know the difference.

Self-acceptance is the foundation of community feeling. You cannot trust others or contribute genuinely while running on a false self-image.
