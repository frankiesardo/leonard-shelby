<summary>How does Deutsch argue that the "problem of induction" is solved, and what role does explanation play in justifying predictions?</summary>

Past observations cannot logically justify predictions about the future -- Hume was right about that. But Deutsch argues the problem is dissolved, not solved by finding some hidden principle of induction. The justification for relying on a theory is that it provides the best available *explanation* and its rivals have been refuted. Any rival theory that makes different predictions but offers no new explanation (e.g., "gravity works as usual, except David floats") is a bad explanation because it introduces an unexplained anomaly. Bad explanations spoil good ones without solving any problem, so they are to be summarily rejected. Only argument ever justifies anything -- tentatively. We need no principle that "the future resembles the past"; we need only the principle that unexplained qualifications make explanations worse.
