<summary>What is the double-diamond model of design, and what is Norman's "never solve the problem I am asked to solve" rule?</summary>

The double diamond, introduced by the British Design Council in 2005, describes two phases: finding the right problem, then finding the right solution. Each phase has a divergence stage (exploring broadly) and a convergence stage (narrowing down). In the first diamond, designers diverge to understand the fundamental issues behind the stated problem, then converge on the real problem. In the second diamond, they diverge across many possible solutions, then converge on the best one. Norman's consulting rule -- "never solve the problem I am asked to solve" -- captures the first diamond's purpose: the stated problem is almost always a symptom, not the root cause. The quarter-inch drill example: people don't want a drill, they don't even want a hole, they want a shelf on the wall. Keep asking "why" until you reach the real need, then design for that.
