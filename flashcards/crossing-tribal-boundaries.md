<summary>Why does Scott Alexander call crossing tribal signaling boundaries "by far the most important persuasive technique" — and what two things does it require?</summary>

Before anyone evaluates your argument, they evaluate *you*: friend or foe, in-group or out-group. If you trip the wrong tribal signals (vocabulary, framing, tone, assumed values), the argument never gets a hearing — defenses activate and everything is filtered through hostility. Crossing the boundary means using the right signals to sound like you belong. It requires two things: (1) actually understanding the other side's signals — not a caricature — and (2) at least an ounce of honest sympathy. Faking tribal membership is transparent and backfires.
