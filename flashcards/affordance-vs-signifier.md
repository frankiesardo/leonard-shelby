<summary>Norman distinguishes between affordances and signifiers. What is the difference, and why does it matter that the first edition of DOET conflated them?</summary>

An affordance is a relationship between an object and a person -- it defines what actions are possible (a button affords pushing). A signifier is any perceivable indicator that communicates what action is possible (the raised surface of the button, its color, its label). Affordances exist whether or not they are perceived; signifiers make affordances perceivable. A hidden button still affords pushing, but it's useless without a signifier. The first edition conflated them, leading designers to talk about "perceived affordances" when they really meant signifiers. The practical consequence: designers can't always control affordances (the physics), but they can always control signifiers (the communication). If a door needs a "push" sign, the signifier has failed -- the hardware itself should communicate the action.
