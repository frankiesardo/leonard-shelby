<summary>Why does Storr say the brain is a "hallucination generator," and what does this mean for storytelling?</summary>

The brain does not perceive reality directly. It is locked in a dark, silent skull and constructs a "controlled hallucination" from predictions, updating only when sensory fact-checkers detect something unexpected. Color, sound, and pain are all effects manufactured inside the brain, not properties of the external world. For storytelling, this means reading works by model-building: the brain converts words into electrical pulses and hallucinates the story. This is why specific detail creates vivid scenes, why show-don't-tell works (abstract adjectives give the model-maker nothing to build with), and why two characters standing side by side literally experience different worlds.
