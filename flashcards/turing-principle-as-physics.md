<summary>What is the Turing principle in its strongest form, and why does Deutsch call it a law of physics rather than a theorem of mathematics?</summary>

In its strongest form: "It is possible to build a virtual-reality generator whose repertoire includes every physically possible environment." Deutsch calls it a law of physics because what can and cannot be computed depends entirely on the laws of physics, not on abstract mathematical models. Turing's original conjecture was about abstract machines; Deutsch points out that the computation that matters is computation performed by real physical objects subject to real physical laws. Quantum computers, for instance, can perform computations that no classical Turing machine can, because they exploit the parallelism of the multiverse. The principle's deepest consequence: the laws of physics, by conforming to it, mandate their own comprehensibility -- they make it physically possible for those same laws to become known.
