# Tilt

Tilt is a poker term for the state of emotionally degraded decision-making triggered by a recent bad outcome. A player loses a big hand on a bad beat, and the amygdala hijacks the prefrontal cortex. What follows isn't rational play with a negative emotional tint — it's a qualitatively different mode of cognition. The player knows the correct strategy but can't execute it because the emotional system has taken over.

The word is useful precisely because it names a specific, recognizable state that exists far beyond poker. The investor who doubles down after a loss. The manager who fires someone in anger. The driver who tailgates after being cut off. In each case, the person is not making decisions — they are reacting to the last outcome while the next situation unfolds around them.

## Why it matters

Tilt is distinct from ordinary bad judgment. It's a temporary state with recognizable symptoms: racing heart, flushed face, verbal cues ("Seriously? Again?"), and a characteristic feeling of wanting to get even or get back to where you were. The good news is that it passes. The bad news is that the decisions made during tilt don't un-make themselves.

The practical response is not willpower but architecture. Recognize you're tilted. Accept that you're temporarily not "decision fit." Defer any consequential decisions until the state passes. Poker players use loss limits and walk-away rules. The civilian version is sleeping on it, calling a friend, or simply saying "I'm not going to decide this right now."
