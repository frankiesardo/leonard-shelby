# Idealized Cognitive Models

An idealized cognitive model (ICM) is a structured, idealized representation of some domain of experience that we use to organize knowledge and make sense of the world. ICMs come in four types: propositional (frame-like structures with roles and relations), image-schematic (spatial patterns like CONTAINER, PATH, LINK), metaphoric (systematic mappings from one domain to another), and metonymic (part-for-whole or subcategory-for-category substitutions). Categories are defined relative to ICMs, and what we call "prototype effects" — the fact that some members seem like better examples than others — are by-products of how well things fit the relevant models.

The concept comes from George Lakoff's *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things* (1987), where it serves as the central theoretical apparatus unifying prototype theory, metaphor theory, and embodied cognition.

## Why it matters

The ICM framework resolves a puzzle that bedeviled cognitive science: why do categories have "better" and "worse" members if membership is all-or-nothing? The answer is that categories are defined by models, not by lists of properties. A robin is a "better" bird than a penguin not because it's more of a bird, but because it fits the bird ICM (flies, sings, perches in trees) more closely. The ICM is idealized — it doesn't perfectly describe any particular bird — but it's what we reason with.

The "mother" example is the clearest illustration. We have at least five ICMs for mother: the birth model (the person who gave birth), the genetic model (female who contributed genetic material), the nurturance model (the person who raises the child), the marital model (the wife of the father), and the genealogical model (the closest female ancestor). When all five converge on one person, there's no question about who "the mother" is. When they diverge — a surrogate, an adoptive parent, a stepmother — we get conceptual conflict, and people disagree about who the "real" mother is. This is not vagueness; it's the structure of the concept.

## Connections

- ICMs generalize the [Conceptual Metaphor](conceptual-metaphor.md) framework from *Metaphors We Live By* — metaphoric models are one of four ICM types
- [Prototype Effects](prototype-effects.md) are by-products of ICM structure, not reflections of graded membership
- [Radial Categories](radial-categories.md) are organized around ICMs, with noncentral members linked to the center by other ICMs
- Norman's conceptual models in *The Design of Everyday Things* are practical applications of ICMs — the gulf of execution is what happens when the designer's ICM doesn't match the user's
- Harari's imagined orders in [Sapiens](../sources/books/sapiens/summary.md) are shared ICMs at civilizational scale
