# Radial Categories

A radial category has a central case and conventionalized extensions that are *motivated* but not *predictable* by general rules. The extensions make sense given the center — you can see why they're in the category — but you couldn't have generated them in advance. Each extension must be learned individually, yet none is arbitrary.

The concept comes from George Lakoff's analysis of cross-linguistic categorization. The title of *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things* is itself a radial category: in the Australian Aboriginal language Dyirbal, the classifier *balan* groups women, fire, dangerous things, and certain birds. The chain runs women → sun (a female deity in Dyirbal myth) → fire (associated with the sun) → dangerous things (associated with fire). Each link is motivated; the endpoints share no common properties.

## Why it matters

Radial categories are the strongest evidence against the classical theory that categories are defined by shared properties. They show that human categorization is fundamentally a matter of chaining, association, and motivated extension — not logical containment. This applies far beyond exotic languages:

- The English word *over* has 100+ senses (spatial, covering, completion, excess, repetition) organized radially around a central ABOVE-ACROSS image schema
- English *there*-constructions (deictic, existential, presentational) form a radial category around the central spatial deictic
- Even grammatical categories like "subject" have a prototypical center (agent of a transitive verb) with motivated but non-predictable extensions

The key methodological distinction is **motivation vs. prediction**. In a classical system, noncentral members are either predicted by rules or arbitrary. Radial categories introduce a third possibility: motivated but conventional. This resolves the false dichotomy between "it follows from a rule" and "it's just a list."

## Connections

- Radial categories are organized around [Idealized Cognitive Models](idealized-cognitive-models.md), with links between center and periphery characterized by other ICMs
- They produce [Prototype Effects](prototype-effects.md) — central members are judged as "better" examples
- [Conceptual Metaphor](conceptual-metaphor.md) is one of the linking mechanisms between central and noncentral members
- Polysemy (multiple related word meanings) is radial categorization applied to the lexicon
