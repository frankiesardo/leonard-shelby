# The Four Laws of Behavior Change

Every habit follows the same four-step pattern: cue (you notice something), craving (you want something), response (you do something), reward (you get something). Clear turns this loop into a practical framework by assigning one "law" to each step:

1. **Make it obvious** (cue) -- Use implementation intentions ("I will [behavior] at [time] in [place]"), habit stacking ("After [current habit], I will [new habit]"), and environment design. Make the cues of good habits visible.
2. **Make it attractive** (craving) -- Use temptation bundling (pair something you need to do with something you want to do). Join a culture where your desired behavior is the norm. Reframe hard habits by highlighting their benefits.
3. **Make it easy** (response) -- Reduce friction. Use the Two-Minute Rule. Prime your environment. Master the decisive moment.
4. **Make it satisfying** (reward) -- Use habit tracking. Never miss twice. Get an accountability partner. Make the ending feel good.

To break a bad habit, invert each law: make it invisible, unattractive, difficult, unsatisfying.

The framework works as a diagnostic tool. When a habit isn't sticking, you can check which law is failing and intervene at that specific point. Not remembering to do it? The cue isn't obvious enough. Dreading it? It's not attractive enough. Too much effort? It's not easy enough. Not sticking over time? It's not satisfying enough.

The beauty is in the simplicity. Four laws, four questions, four interventions. The framework scales from tiny personal habits (flossing) to organizational change (British Cycling's aggregation of marginal gains).
