# Teleology vs. Etiology

Etiology asks "what caused this?" Teleology asks "what is this for?" Freud is etiological: your childhood trauma caused your present neurosis. Adler is teleological: your present neurosis serves a present purpose, and you maintain it because it's useful to you.

The recluse who can't leave his room isn't trapped by past abuse. He has the *goal* of not going out -- staying home gets him parental attention, avoids the risk of social judgment, and preserves a known identity. The anxiety he feels is real, but it is manufactured to serve this goal, not produced by a cause he can't control. The mother who screams at her daughter, then answers the phone in a polite voice, then goes right back to screaming -- she isn't controlled by anger. She uses anger as a tool and puts it away when the tool is no longer appropriate.

This is not a denial that bad things happen. Adler explicitly acknowledges that childhood experiences have strong influence. But influence is not determination. Two people can experience the same abuse and construct opposite lives. The decisive factor is not what happened to you, but the meaning you assigned to it -- and that meaning serves your present goals.

## Why it matters

The etiological lens is attractive because it removes responsibility. "I'm this way because of what happened to me" is a complete explanation that requires no action. The teleological lens is uncomfortable because it restores agency: if your current state serves a purpose, you can choose a different purpose. You are not a victim of your biography. You are the author of your interpretation.

This is the same move Frankl makes with [the last human freedom](the-last-human-freedom.md) -- the freedom to choose your attitude -- but from a different angle. Frankl says you *can* choose. Adler says you already *are* choosing, whether you acknowledge it or not.
