# Environment Design

People with the best self-control are typically the ones who need to use it least. They structure their lives so that temptation rarely arises. Self-control is a short-term strategy, not a long-term one. In the long run, we become a product of the environment we live in.

The most dramatic evidence comes from the Vietnam heroin study. Over 15% of U.S. soldiers in Vietnam were heroin addicts. When they returned home, nine out of ten quit nearly overnight -- not because of willpower or rehab, but because they were in a radically different environment, devoid of every cue that had triggered the habit. Compare this to the typical rehab patient who returns to the same neighborhood, the same friends, the same apartment: 90% relapse.

The practical implication is that you should not try to be a more disciplined person. Instead, be the architect of a more disciplined environment. Clear breaks this into two strategies:

**For good habits, make the cues obvious.** Put the guitar in the middle of the living room. Leave the book on the pillow. Set out workout clothes the night before. Put the fruit bowl on the counter instead of in the fridge. Associate each habit with a specific context -- "this is the reading chair," "this is where I meditate."

**For bad habits, make the cues invisible.** Put the phone in another room. Delete social media apps. Hide the beer in the back of the fridge. Unplug the TV after each use. Each bit of friction you add between yourself and a bad habit makes it less likely to occur.

Environment priming extends the idea further: you are not just removing obstacles, you are actively preparing the space for your next action. Nuckols's "resetting the room" -- cleaning up after each activity so the room is ready for the next one -- turns environment design from a one-time intervention into a continuous practice.
