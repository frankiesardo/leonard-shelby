# The Reach of Explanations

Some explanations apply far beyond the domain for which they were originally created. Newton devised his laws to explain planetary orbits, but they turned out to explain tides, bridges, ballistics, and spacecraft trajectories. This reach is not a coincidence or a lucky bonus. It is a property of deep truth. An explanation that captures something real about the structure of reality will keep working wherever that structure is relevant -- including in domains its creator never imagined.

Deutsch contrasts explanations with reach against "rules of thumb." A rule of thumb works in its original parochial context and breaks silently elsewhere. "Red sky at night, sailor's delight" predicts weather in certain latitudes using a correlation that has no explanatory depth. It cannot be extended, improved, or combined with other knowledge. A good explanation of atmospheric optics, by contrast, would explain when and why the rule works, when it fails, and what would happen on a planet with a different atmosphere.

The reach of an explanation is not something you can design in advance. You discover it after the fact, when the explanation turns out to illuminate problems you did not know existed. Every problem that is interesting is interesting because some good explanation has reach into it.

## Why it matters

The concept of reach explains why fundamental science is valuable even when its practical applications are unforeseeable. The knowledge that "nothing is forbidden unless it violates the laws of physics" means that any physical transformation is achievable if we know how. The "how" comes from explanations with reach.

It also explains why shallow thinking -- collecting data points, finding correlations, building prediction models without understanding *why* -- is fundamentally limited. Predictions without explanation have no reach. They will fail the moment conditions change in ways the model did not foresee. Only understanding *why* gives you the power to adapt to the unforeseen.
