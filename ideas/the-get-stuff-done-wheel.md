# The Get Stuff Done Wheel

Kim Scott's framework for how teams achieve results collaboratively. It's a seven-step cycle: **Listen** --> **Clarify** --> **Debate** --> **Decide** --> **Persuade** --> **Execute** --> **Learn** (then back to Listen). The key insight: a boss's job is not to make all the decisions, but to ensure this wheel turns smoothly.

Each step matters, and most failures come from skipping one:

- **Listen** -- Create a culture of listening. Quiet people have the best ideas, but won't share them in a room full of talkers.
- **Clarify** -- Push ideas through more rigor before debating. An idea should be sharp enough that others can engage with it meaningfully.
- **Debate** -- Keep it about ideas, not egos. Create "obligation to dissent" norms. Rock the boat.
- **Decide** -- The decider should have the best information, not the highest rank. Push decisions down.
- **Persuade** -- The decision-maker must bring others along. Credibility of the decider, not authority, is what persuades.
- **Execute** -- Minimize the tax that collaboration puts on individual execution time. Block time for doing the actual work.
- **Learn** -- Results inform the next cycle. What happened? Why? What will we do differently?

Scott contrasts Google and Apple as two radically different implementations of the same wheel. Google's culture emphasized bottom-up Listen/Clarify/Debate; Apple's emphasized top-down Decide/Persuade. But both companies used all seven steps -- they just started from different points.

## Why it matters

Scott's first act as a new manager at Google was to skip the wheel entirely. She had the "obvious" right answer about how to restructure her team, so she just did it. Three of five direct reports promptly handed in their resignations. The restructuring might have been right; the process was catastrophically wrong.

The wheel is not bureaucracy. Each step is fast when the culture supports it. The tax shows up when steps are skipped: if you don't debate, you get false consensus that unravels during execution. If you don't persuade, you get compliance without commitment. If you don't listen, you miss the crucial detail that would have changed the decision.

The boss's temptation is always to collapse the wheel into two steps: Decide and Execute. This works in emergencies. It fails in every other situation because it produces decisions that people don't understand, don't buy into, and therefore execute poorly.
