# Autonomy over the Four T's

Autonomy is the most important of the three elements of intrinsic motivation (autonomy, mastery, purpose). Pink breaks it into four dimensions -- the four T's:

- **Task** -- what you do. Google's 20 percent time, Atlassian's FedEx Days, 3M's bootlegging policy. Post-it notes and Gmail were both born from employees choosing their own tasks.
- **Time** -- when you do it. ROWE (results-only work environment) at Best Buy increased productivity 35% and slashed voluntary turnover. Nobody had schedules. They just had to get their work done.
- **Technique** -- how you do it. Zappos stopped monitoring call times and requiring scripts. Customer service reps handle calls their own way. The result: minimal turnover and world-class service ratings.
- **Team** -- whom you do it with. At Whole Foods, department employees vote on whether to hire new teammates after a 30-day trial. At W. L. Gore, leaders must assemble willing followers.

Autonomy is not independence. You can be autonomous and happily interdependent. It's not about going it alone -- it's about acting with choice. The distinction matters because autonomy is a universal human need, not a Western one. Researchers have found the link between autonomy and well-being in Russia, Turkey, South Korea, and even Bangladesh.

## Why it matters

The word "empowerment" assumes the organization holds the power and graciously ladles some into the waiting bowls of employees. That's not autonomy -- it's a slightly more civilized form of control. "Flex time" is a con game: it widens the fences but doesn't remove them.

The alternative is to start from the assumption that people want to be accountable and that giving them control over task, time, technique, and team is the pathway to that accountability. Cornell studied 320 small businesses: those offering autonomy grew at four times the rate of control-oriented firms and had one-third the turnover.

The challenge is transition. People plucked from controlling environments and dropped into undiluted autonomy will struggle. Organizations need scaffolding. And different individuals prize different T's. But the direction is clear: management isn't the solution, it's the problem. What we need is not better management but a renaissance of self-direction.
