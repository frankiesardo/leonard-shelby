# Delete Before Optimizing

The most common mistake in process improvement is running the steps in the wrong order. Engineers by training are optimization machines — handed a problem, they make it better. But making something better that shouldn't exist is pure waste, and it's invisible waste because it *looks* like progress.

Musk's algorithm makes the ordering explicit:
1. Make requirements less dumb
2. Delete the part or process step
3. Simplify or optimize
4. Accelerate cycle time
5. Automate

Steps 3-5 are where most people start. Step 2 is where they should. The rule of thumb: if you're not adding back 10% of what you deleted, you're not deleting aggressively enough. Deletion is never the safe choice in organizations — the incentive always points toward adding ("just in case"). That bias must be actively fought.

## Why it matters

Automation locked in a bad process is Musk's clearest example: a $2 million robot was built to install glass mats that turned out to be unnecessary. The optimization was real, the process was imaginary. The cost wasn't just the robot — it was all the engineering time spent perfecting the installation of something that should have been deleted at step 2.

The sequence matters because each step amplifies the previous one. Automating something that was simplified is efficient. Automating something that was never questioned is a monument to the wrong thing.

## Connections

- The ordering principle here is structurally similar to [The Five Focusing Steps](the-five-focusing-steps.md) — in both, identifying and exploiting the constraint (or deleting the non-essential) must come before optimization
- [Theory of Constraints](theory-of-constraints.md) makes the same point from a throughput angle: improving a non-bottleneck improves nothing. Optimizing a deletable step is even worse than improving a non-bottleneck — at least the non-bottleneck exists for a reason

- [Clutter](clutter.md) is the same principle in writing: every word that doesn't earn its place is actively working against you. Zinsser's 50% cut rule is step 2 applied to prose
