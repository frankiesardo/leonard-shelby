# Precise Speech

When things fall apart, the temptation is to leave everything vague. Vagueness feels safer because it avoids confrontation -- with the problem, with the person, with the full scope of the damage. But vagueness is not safety. It is the replacement of one specific, manageable problem with a thousand unspecified, unmanageable terrors. The unknown, unnamed threat is a dragon. The specified, articulated problem is usually something you can handle.

Peterson draws on the Logos tradition -- the idea, running from ancient Egypt through the Gospel of John, that conscious speech is world-creating. "In the beginning was the Word." Consciousness and articulate language are how human beings transform the chaos of raw experience into habitable order. When you name what is wrong -- with your relationship, your job, your health, your life -- you shrink the monster down to size. When you refuse to name it, it grows without limit.

The practical application: say exactly what is wrong. Say exactly what you want instead. Don't hint. Don't "wait for them to figure it out." Don't hope it resolves itself. Vague problems have no solutions. Precise problems sometimes do. "If you can't even take a small step toward the alleviation of the suffering that is right in front of you, why would you presume that you know better what the world needs?"

## Why it matters

Precise speech is the antidote to both the [life-lie](the-life-lie.md) and to chaos. When a marriage is failing, the worst thing you can do is "not talk about it." What you refuse to specify remains a shapeless dread that infects everything. What you articulate becomes a defined battlefield where you at least have a chance. This is also why [clear writing is clear thinking](clear-thinking-clear-writing.md): the act of articulation forces you to know what you actually think, not what you vaguely feel.
