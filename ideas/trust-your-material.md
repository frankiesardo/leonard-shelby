# Trust Your Material

When the facts are strong enough to make the point on their own, get out of the way. Don't add a sentence explaining that something is remarkable -- if it's remarkable, the reader can marvel without your help. Don't use words like "surprisingly" or "predictably" that put a value on a fact before the reader encounters it. The reader plays a major role in the act of communication and must be given room to play it.

Zinsser illustrates this with a baseball scout who mentions that the average double play takes 4.3 seconds, so any runner reaching first base in less than that is worth watching. The fact is self-sufficient. There's no need to add that 4.3 seconds is remarkably little time -- readers can figure that out, and they'll enjoy figuring it out more than being told.

## Why it matters

Over-explaining is a failure of nerve disguised as helpfulness. It signals that you don't trust your audience to draw the obvious conclusion. In any domain -- teaching, presenting, designing, writing -- the impulse to annotate your own work with significance ("This is important because...") usually weakens it. The audience feels patronized, and the material loses its punch.

The same principle applies to storytelling, data presentations, product demos, and arguments. Strong evidence doesn't need a lawyer's closing statement after every exhibit. If you've chosen your details well -- and choosing well is the real work -- those details will carry the point.

The practical test: after writing a sentence that states a powerful fact, check whether the next sentence merely tells the reader how to feel about it. If so, cut it.
