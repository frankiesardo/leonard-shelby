# Systems over Goals

You do not rise to the level of your goals -- you fall to the level of your systems. Every Olympic athlete wants gold. Every job candidate wants the offer. The goal is the same for everyone, so it can't be what differentiates the winners from the losers. What separates them is the system of continuous small improvements that puts them in a position to succeed.

Goals are useful for setting direction, but systems are what produce results. A goal is a one-time achievement. A system is a repeatable process. If you're a coach, your goal is to win the championship. Your system is how you recruit, train, and play. If you're a writer, your goal is to publish a book. Your system is how you write, edit, and get feedback.

Clear identifies four problems with goal-first thinking: (1) winners and losers have the same goals, so goals can't explain the difference; (2) achieving a goal is a momentary change -- clean the room and it gets messy again without a system; (3) goals restrict happiness to the future ("I'll be happy when..."); (4) goals are at odds with long-term progress because once you hit the target you stop trying.

The alternative: fall in love with the process, not the product. Fix the inputs and let the outputs follow. You don't need to predict the future -- you need to build a system that works regardless of what the future brings.
