# Companies as Technology

A company is not a fixed institution. It is a social technology -- a path-dependent solution to social and legal problems that is only about 500 years old and still deeply immature. Tobi Lütke: "If it wouldn't exist for some reason, and someone would propose the whole idea now, it would sound insane. From first principles, none of this makes sense, really."

The implication: if companies are technologies, they can be engineered. Not in the MBA sense of "managing" them, but in the literal sense of treating the organization as a system you design from axioms, test against reality, and iterate on. You choose your first principles (team sizes, reporting ratios, compensation philosophy, decision-making structure), derive the consequences, hold them up against what you actually have, and reconcile the delta.

Most founders don't do this. They cosplay -- they imitate the templates they've seen, the "serious public company CEO" archetype, the org chart of the last company they worked at, the management books written by people who never built anything. The result is path-dependent mediocrity: 8,000 people with 5,500 different titles, random projects nobody approved, boondoggles nobody can find.

## Why it matters

The technology framing gives you permission to rederive. When COVID invalidated Tobi's assumptions, he didn't patch the existing system -- he went back to axioms and rebuilt. That's what engineers do when a core assumption breaks: you prune back the decision tree to the broken node and recompute forward. Most organizations can't do this because they treat their structure as a given, not as a design choice.

It also means companies should improve over time. "One of the saddest days of my life was when I opened old code and was really impressed with how good it was" -- the implication being that if you look at your company's systems from a year ago and aren't embarrassed, you've stopped learning.
