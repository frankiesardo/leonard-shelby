# Experiential Grounding

Abstract thought is not free-floating. It is built, layer by layer, on the foundation of bodily experience. We understand HAPPY IS UP because drooping posture goes with sadness and erect posture with positive emotion. MORE IS UP because adding substance to a pile raises its level. KNOWING IS GRASPING because we understand physical objects by picking them up and examining them. CONTROL IS UP because the victor in a physical fight typically ends up on top.

These are not arbitrary cultural conventions. They arise from the fact that we have bodies of a particular kind that function in particular ways in a physical environment. An extraterrestrial being with a radically different body would develop radically different foundational metaphors -- and therefore a radically different conceptual system.

The grounding works in layers. Orientational metaphors (up/down, in/out, front/back) are the most directly grounded in bodily experience. Ontological metaphors build on those by letting us treat abstract experiences as bounded entities and substances -- inflation as a thing, the mind as a container. Structural metaphors build on both, mapping the full richness of one experiential domain onto another: TIME IS MONEY draws on our experience of money as a limited resource, which itself draws on our experience of physical substances that can be measured and used up.

Lakoff and Johnson call this "experientialism" -- the view that meaning and truth are always grounded in human experience, never floating free of it. This is neither objectivism (meaning is in the world independent of us) nor subjectivism (meaning is whatever I want it to be). It is the claim that meaning arises from the interaction between embodied beings and the physical and cultural environments they inhabit.

## Why it matters

If abstract thought is grounded in concrete experience, then the path to understanding anything abstract is through metaphors rooted in direct experience. This is why good teachers use analogies, why physical manipulatives help children learn math, and why the best explanations make you *feel* something, not just know it.

It also means that the conceptual system of any culture is constrained by the physical realities its members inhabit. Desert cultures, island cultures, and mountain cultures will develop different metaphorical systems because their bodily interactions with the world differ. But they will all have bodies, and those bodies will provide universal foundations -- up/down, container/surface, force/resistance -- on which culture-specific metaphors are built.
