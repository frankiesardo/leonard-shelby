The job is not the atomic unit of work. The task is. A job is a bundle of tasks, and when technology changes, tasks reshuffle long before jobs disappear.

The canonical example: the executive secretary. When email arrived, the secretary went from taking dictation and mailing letters to managing inboxes, then to travel planning and event orchestration. The job title persists; the task bundle is completely different. Meanwhile, executives absorbed tasks they never used to do — typing their own memos — which would have been unthinkable in 1970.

This is the correct lens for thinking about AI displacement. "AI will take your job" reasons at the wrong level of abstraction. The actual mechanism is: AI will change which tasks compose your job. Some tasks vanish, new tasks appear, and the job reshuffles. The people who can swap tasks fluidly — who treat the task bundle as mutable — thrive. The people who identify with a fixed set of tasks get stuck.

Alex Rampell extends this with a concrete SaaS taxonomy: some companies are impervious (Workday, NetSuite — systems of record with hostages), some are fatally exposed (Zendesk — per-seat licensing for tasks AI does better), and some are in the middle (Adobe — fewer designers needed, but the tool still matters). The Zendesk case is pure task loss: the task of answering support tickets vanishes, but the *job* of customer care might evolve — Tony Hsieh's Zappos model of turning support from a cost center into a revenue center by investing in human relationships that AI can't replicate.

This is the same reframing move as [Theory of Constraints](theory-of-constraints.md): stop reasoning about the plant and start reasoning about the bottleneck. Stop reasoning about the job and start reasoning about the task. Find the actual atomic unit and work there.
