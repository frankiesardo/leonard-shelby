# Self-Authorization

No one gives you the authority to do your work. You give it to yourself, and you keep giving it to yourself — every day, perpetually. Being a writer, a designer, an engineer, a leader, whatever you are: it's an act of continuous self-authorization.

This matters because most education teaches the opposite. You learn that your own perceptions are unimportant and unauthorized. You learn to defer to pre-existing authorities, to write about pre-authorized subjects in pre-authorized language, to take shelter under someone else's perceptions rather than trusting your own. The result is a chronic self-deprecation that feels like humility but is actually learned helplessness.

Klinkenborg puts it bluntly: "Being a writer is an act of perpetual self-authorization. No matter who you are. Only you can authorize yourself. No one else can authorize you. No one."

## Why it matters

Self-authorization is the prerequisite for original work in any domain. If you wait for permission — from a credential, a title, an institution, a mentor — you'll either never begin or you'll produce work shaped entirely by what you believe is already permitted. You'll write about pre-authorized subjects, build pre-authorized products, think pre-authorized thoughts.

The word "perpetual" is critical. This isn't a one-time event. You don't authorize yourself once and carry the card forever. Every time you sit down to do serious work, you face the same quiet question: *Who asked you to do this?* And the answer has to come from you, again.

The first person who needs to be persuaded of your authority is you. Don't make it impossible to persuade yourself.
