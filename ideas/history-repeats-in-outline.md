# History Repeats in Outline

History repeats itself, but only in outline and in the large. The broad patterns recur because human nature changes with geological leisureliness and man responds in stereotyped ways to frequently occurring situations: hunger, danger, sex, the concentration of wealth, the decay of moral codes. But in complex civilizations, individuals are more differentiated, situations contain novel elements, and the results are less predictable. "There is no certainty that the future will repeat the past. Every year is an adventure."

The Durants expect that in the future, as in the past: new states will rise and old ones subside; civilizations will begin with agriculture, expand into commerce, and luxuriate with finance; thought will pass from supernatural to naturalistic explanations; experiments in morals will loosen tradition; and the excitement of innovation will be forgotten in the unconcern of time.

But they distrust grand schemas. Spengler's civilizational life-cycles are too deterministic — "a group is no organism physically added to its constituent individuals; it has no brain or stomach of its own." Civilizations don't die from mystical exhaustion but from the failure of their leaders to meet the challenges of change. The pattern is real but not fate.

## Why it matters

This is the right mental model for reading history: loose patterns, not iron laws. The wealth concentration cycle *tends* to repeat, but Solon proves it can be interrupted. The moral interregnum *tends* to follow economic upheaval, but the new code doesn't have to look like the old one. Knowing the pattern is useful because it reduces surprise — but treating it as destiny is the error Spengler made.

"History smiles at all attempts to force its flow into theoretical patterns or logical grooves; it plays havoc with our generalizations, breaks all our rules; history is baroque."
