# Throughput Accounting

A measurement framework that replaces traditional cost accounting with three operational measures: throughput, inventory, and operating expense. The hierarchy matters: throughput comes first.

- **Throughput**: the rate at which the system generates money through sales. Not production — sales. Producing parts that sit in a warehouse is not throughput.
- **Inventory**: all the money the system has invested in things it intends to sell. Raw materials, work-in-process, finished goods. Notably, this definition excludes "value added" — a half-finished product is worth only its material cost, not the labor invested in it.
- **Operating expense**: all the money the system spends turning inventory into throughput. Labor, overhead, depreciation — everything.

The goal expressed through these measures: increase throughput while simultaneously reducing inventory and operating expense. Not one at the expense of the others — all three, moving in the right direction, at the same time.

## Why it matters

Cost accounting, invented in the early twentieth century, treats every dollar of cost reduction as equally valuable. This makes intuitive sense: save a dollar here, save a dollar there, and profits go up. But in a system with constraints, this logic breaks down catastrophically.

A plant manager using cost accounting sees robots producing parts at high speed and calls it efficient. Throughput accounting asks: did we sell any more product? Did inventory go down? Did operating expense decrease? In Alex Rogo's plant, the robots scored brilliantly on cost-per-part metrics while the plant hemorrhaged money. The robots were producing parts nobody needed, building mountains of inventory, and the carrying costs were eating the supposed savings.

The deeper problem: cost accounting penalizes the right behavior. When Rogo's plant reduced inventory by 50%, the balance sheet showed a loss — because the "value added" by labor was written off when inventory shrank. The plant was healthier than ever, shipping on time, with cash flow surging, and the books said it was losing money. Lou, the controller, discovered this distortion and realized the actual net profit was over 20%, not the 12.8% the reports showed.

Throughput accounting is the measurement system that makes the [Theory of Constraints](theory-of-constraints.md) actionable. Without it, you can't tell productive from unproductive action.
