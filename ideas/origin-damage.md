# Origin Damage

Origin damage is the childhood incident that created a character's (or person's) flawed belief about the world. It occurs when the brain's models are still being built -- during the heightened plasticity of the first two decades of life. Because the flaw gets incorporated into the neural model before it hardens, it becomes invisible to its owner: part of their perception, not an idea they can examine and discard.

The mechanism: something intense happens to a young person. It teaches them that if they do not believe or behave in a certain way, something terrible will happen. This lesson has two components: (1) it tells them who they must be to get what they want, and (2) it tells them how to avoid what they fear. Both components persist into adulthood. The character builds an entire life -- career, relationships, environment, values -- around this origin lesson without ever recognizing its influence.

Shakespeare's radical innovation was to *remove* neat explanations of origin damage from his source material. In the pre-Shakespeare version of *King Lear*, we know exactly why Lear does the love test. Shakespeare excised the explanation, and the play became "immeasurably deeper." Great stories hint at origin damage, tease it, use it to build empathy, or orient entire plots around a hunt for it (as in *Citizen Kane*), but rarely spell it out.

## Why it matters

For writers, knowing the origin damage scene precisely -- even if it is never shown on the page -- is what makes a character come alive in the imagination. You should be able to "see" how they will behave in any situation because you know the moment that made them and the belief that crystallized from it.

For life, the concept is humbling. Our personalities are shaped by events we may not remember or may have misremembered. We see and feel the world partly with our damage. What we attend to, what we find threatening, what we instinctively trust or distrust -- all are colored by formative experiences whose influence is invisible to us.
