# Painkillers Not Vitamins

Vitamin pills are good for you, but they're not essential. You can skip your morning vitamin for a day, a month, a lifetime and never notice the difference. But you'll notice real quick if you forget a painkiller. The best product ideas are painkillers -- they eliminate something that's constantly bothering a lot of people.

The strongest version of this is when the pain comes from your own life. Fadell spent a decade being driven crazy by thermostats -- freezing in his Tahoe cabin, spending vacations elbow-deep in wiring, watching every hotel thermostat in every country fail in the same ways. The thermostat chased him. The best ideas do that: you try to put them aside, but they won't leave. After months or years of research and prototyping, you realize you can't stop thinking about it. That's the signal.

Fadell calls the research period "delayed intuition," borrowing from Daniel Kahneman. The more amazing an idea seems, the longer you should wait before committing. Sit with it. Prototype. Gather information. If the idea is still chasing you after months of reality-testing, it's probably worth building. If it fades, it was a distraction. The thermostat took ten years to catch Fadell. The second-generation thermostat took a week to decide on.

## Why it matters

Most failed startups are vitamins. They solve problems that customers can live without solving. Cool technology isn't enough. A great team isn't enough. If your product doesn't address a pain that people feel daily, you can't start a revolution. General Magic built incredible technology that solved problems nobody recognized for more than a decade. Google Glass was a technology in search of a problem. Palm beat both of them by solving a simple, real pain: putting your phone numbers in your pocket.

The corollary is important: if you're passionate about an idea that seems too early -- the infrastructure isn't there, the customers don't exist -- don't give up. Stick with it. Build expertise. Make connections. When the world catches up and your idea's moment arrives, you'll already be there. Fadell followed personal electronics through five companies over a decade before the iPod finally worked. Each failure taught him something essential.
