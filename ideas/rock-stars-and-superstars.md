# Rock Stars and Superstars

Kim Scott divides exceptional performers into two archetypes based on their current growth trajectory:

- **Rock stars** are on a gradual growth trajectory. They do exceptional work, love their craft, and don't want the next job. They want to go deeper, not higher. They are the force of stability on a team.
- **Superstars** are on a steep growth trajectory. They need constant new challenges, are ready for new roles, and will leave if they don't get them. They are the force of change on a team.

Both are essential. A team of all superstars has no institutional memory and constant churn. A team of all rock stars risks stagnation. The manager's job is to maintain the right ratio and to recognize that what each type needs is radically different: rock stars need recognition for their expertise and freedom to go deep; superstars need new challenges and a clear path to the next role.

The labels are borrowed from the music industry metaphor but inverted: in most corporate cultures, "rock star" means "high potential fast mover." Scott reclaims the term for the person who, like a rock, provides a stable foundation.

## Why it matters

Most management systems systematically undervalue rock stars. Performance reviews reward promotions. Career conversations default to "What's next?" Team structures assume that the best people want to move up. This pushes rock stars into roles they don't want, promotes them past their zone of excellence, or -- worst -- makes them feel like second-class citizens for not wanting to climb the ladder.

Scott's stories make this concrete. A product manager at Google named "Gloria" was the team's best performer -- in her current role. When pushed to manage people, she was miserable, her team was miserable, and the work suffered. Another example: the engineer who would have been perfectly happy optimizing the same system for ten years, but left because no one ever acknowledged that his stability was valuable.

The key insight for managers: when you look at your team, you should be able to name the rock stars and the superstars. If you can't, you don't know your team well enough. And if you're treating both groups the same way, you're failing at least one of them.
