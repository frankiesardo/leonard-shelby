# MAYA

Raymond Loewy's principle: **M**aximally **A**dvanced **Y**et **A**cceptable. There is a pace of change consumers will absorb. Push past it and they reject the product — not because it's bad, but because it's too unfamiliar.

Loewy designed a fan that was insanely quiet. People didn't believe it worked. A startup made a razor nearly silent using machine learning. Sutherland warned them: the buzz and the crackle are what convince us the razor is doing its job. You can over-optimize for the engineering ideal and end up with something consumers distrust. The iPhone succeeded not as a bolt from the blue but as the next step after the iPod — consumers migrated their behavior rather than reinventing it.

The Scandinavian crime drama formula captures it perfectly: "Scandinavians are just the right amount of weird." Familiar enough that you understand what's happening, different enough to be interesting. Too alien and people tune out. Too familiar and there's nothing to notice.

## Why it matters

MAYA is the antidote to two equal and opposite engineering failures. The first: building something so conservative it's invisible. The second: building something so radical it's terrifying. Both fail, but for product-obsessed teams the second failure is more common and more painful — you built something genuinely better and the market rejected it anyway. MAYA explains why. The product wasn't too good. The *distance from familiar* was too great.

The practical implication: when launching something genuinely new, find the bridge. The iPod bridged to the iPhone. The hybrid car bridged to the EV. The Kindle bridged physical books to digital reading. The bridge is not a compromise — it's the pathway that lets consumers migrate rather than leap.
