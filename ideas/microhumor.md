# Microhumor

Not jokes. Not comedy. Just the tiniest ghost of a smile on the reader's face while they're skimming through. Microhumor is background-level wit — a slightly unexpected word choice, a dry aside, a self-aware parenthetical. The reader may not even consciously register it, but their experience of the writing is fundamentally different because of it.

Scott Alexander calls this "maybe the number one thing that separates really enjoyable writers from people who are technically proficient but still a chore to read." The behaviorist model is simple: reading provides a few sources of reward — important insights, tribal validation, and humor. If you're not serving one of those, the reader's reward signal flatlines and they wander off. Microhumor keeps the reward signal ticking between the big insights.

It also works as a disarming mechanism. It's hard to hate someone who's making you laugh. When you challenge people's cherished beliefs while deploying microhumor, you've bought yourself a tiny bit of extra good will — just enough to keep them reading instead of closing the tab.

## Why it matters

Most writing advice focuses on structure, clarity, and argument. Microhumor is the unspoken ingredient that explains why two equally clear, equally well-structured essays can feel completely different to read. One is a pleasure; the other is homework. The difference is often not in the architecture but in these tiny moments of wit distributed throughout.

The skill is hard to teach because it depends on voice. You can't bolt microhumor onto prose that doesn't have it — it has to grow from the writer's natural way of seeing things. This is why it's so tightly connected to style being organic: writers who are genuinely amused by their subject produce microhumor naturally. Writers who are performing amusement produce cringe.
