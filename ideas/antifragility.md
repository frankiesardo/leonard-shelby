# Antifragility

Some things break under stress (fragile). Some survive (resilient). Some get *stronger* (antifragile). Taleb's key insight: we had no word for this third category, which made it invisible — even though it's everywhere.

## The concept

Antifragility is not robustness. A robust system absorbs a shock and returns to its previous state. An antifragile system absorbs a shock and comes back *better*. Your muscles are antifragile: stress them, they grow. Your bones are antifragile: load them, they densify.

The opposite of fragile is not robust — it's antifragile. This asymmetry matters. Most risk management tries to prevent problems (fragile strategy). Antifragile strategy assumes problems will happen and builds systems that benefit from them.

## Where I see it

- **Teams.** A team that deploys frequently and handles incidents well is antifragile. A team that avoids deploying to avoid risk is fragile — it just hasn't been tested yet.
- **Career.** The barbell strategy: keep a stable foundation (skills, savings, reputation) and make small asymmetric bets (side projects, new domains, bold moves). You can't lose more than the bet, but you can gain disproportionately.
- **Philosophy.** The stoics practiced [voluntary discomfort](stoicism.md) to become antifragile in character. Marcus Aurelius: "[The obstacle is the way](the-obstacle-is-the-way.md)."
- **Martial arts.** In tai chi, you don't brace against force — you yield and redirect. The yielding posture is the antifragile one. You use the opponent's energy to improve your position.
- **Software.** Chaos engineering (Netflix's Chaos Monkey) intentionally introduces failure so the system learns to handle it. The Cognitect "New Normal" series makes this connection explicit.

## The paradox of stability

The more stable a system appears, the more fragile it may actually be. A system that hasn't failed in years has no one who knows how to fix it. This is the paradox of automated operations: stability breeds fragility. Frequent small failures build antifragility.

This connects to why [seasonal content](../STRUCTURE.md) matters in the garden: you don't want only perennial ideas. You want fresh inputs that stress-test your thinking.
