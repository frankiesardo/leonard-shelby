# The Hidden and the Unseen

Leaders systematically overestimate how much they understand about what is happening in their organizations. Information gets filtered as it flows upward. People minimize problems to avoid blame. Mental models cause leaders to see confirmation of their existing beliefs and miss disconfirming evidence. The result is that the bulk of what matters in any organization is invisible to the people nominally in charge of it.

Catmull distinguishes between the hidden (things that could be known if you looked) and the unseen (things that are structurally invisible, that you don't even know to look for). Both are dangerous, but the unseen is worse because you can't search for what you don't know exists. The maxim "you can't manage what you can't measure" is, in Catmull's words, "ridiculous -- something said by people who are unaware of how much is hidden."

Data is one lens, not the answer. Catmull advocates measuring what you can, evaluating what you measure, and then appreciating that you cannot measure the vast majority of what you do. The danger isn't lack of data -- it's the illusion that data paints a full picture.

## Why it matters

This is the meta-problem of management: the very position of being in charge makes it harder to see reality clearly. The more authority you have, the more people tell you what you want to hear. The more success you've had, the more your mental models feel validated -- even when they're wrong. The more structure you build, the more it channels information in predictable (and therefore blind) ways.

The practical response isn't paranoia or micromanagement. It's humility plus mechanisms. Postmortems, dailies, the Braintrust, research trips, Notes Day -- every structural mechanism Pixar uses is, at bottom, a way of surfacing what's hidden before it becomes a crisis. The discipline is not just to look harder, but to accept that looking harder will never be enough.
