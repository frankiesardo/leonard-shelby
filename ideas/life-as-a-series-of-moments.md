# Life as a Series of Moments

Most people treat life as a line -- a narrative from birth to death with a trajectory, a destination, and a "point." You are en route to something: a career, a legacy, a summit. The present is a "preparatory period," and the real life begins later, when you arrive.

Adler rejects this. Life is not a line but a series of dots -- moments of "now." If you look at a chalk line through a magnifying glass, you see individual dots. What appears to be a continuous story is actually a series of discrete moments. There is no destination. There is only what you are doing right now.

The philosopher uses Aristotle's distinction between *kinesis* (movement toward a goal -- traveling, building, healing) and *energeia* (activity complete in itself -- seeing, thinking, dancing). A kinesis has a terminal point: when you arrive at the destination, the journey is over. An energeia has no terminal point: the dancing itself is the point, and it is complete at every moment. Adler advocates an energeial life: "live like you're dancing."

The consequence: "the greatest life-lie is to not live here and now." Focusing on the past (etiology, regret, nostalgia) or the future (planning, anxiety, ambition) is a way of avoiding the present. The person who says "I'll be happy when I get the promotion" is living a tentative life. The person who lives earnestly *now* -- doing what they can, contributing to others, dancing the dance -- has a complete life at every moment.

## Why it matters

If life were a line, dying before reaching your goal would make your life incomplete. If life is a series of moments, every moment lived earnestly is already complete. A life that ends at twenty and a life that ends at ninety are both complete lives, if both were lived in earnest.

This reframes the fear of wasted time. There is no wasted time if every moment was genuinely lived. There is only wasted time if you spent the present waiting for a future that might never arrive.
