# Stay in Character

People can't see what you feel. Even when your internal world is screaming that something went wrong, the audience just sees a normal, competent speaker. The moment you break character — "sorry, I'm not making sense" — you force a lens of doubt onto your audience that wasn't there before.

The pilot analogy: sharing the blinking red light in the cockpit doesn't calm anyone; it just makes every bit of turbulence feel fatal. The cue is simple: "stay in it." The worse it gets, the more you stay in it.

The feedback loop is self-reinforcing: you stay in character, the audience responds as if you're confident, and that makes you feel more confident. The inverse is also true — break character once and the audience's doubt reinforces your own. Peak-end rule means the ending matters disproportionately, so even if the middle was rough, ending strong can redeem the whole thing.
