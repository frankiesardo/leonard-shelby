# Speaking with Adjacency

When you need a story to explain a product, idea, or lesson, don't match content to content — match theme, meaning, or message. A scientist selling specialized lab tubes doesn't tell a story about tubes. He tells a story about buying different apples for every member of his picky family at the grocery store. Then he snaps it into place: "That's what my company does — we believe you deserve to get exactly what you need."

That snap — when the audience realizes the story about apples was really about tubes — is extraordinarily powerful. It works because you're not asking people to care about your product; you're making them feel something universal and then connecting that feeling to your product.

The technique works everywhere: Dicks uses it with misbehaving students (tells them a story about his dog when he was 12, and they sit confused about why he's not yelling, until the story snaps into place and the lesson lands harder than any lecture would). The less obvious the connection between the story and the point, the more powerful the snap.
