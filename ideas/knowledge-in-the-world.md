# Knowledge in the World

People carry two kinds of knowledge: knowledge in the head (memory, learned rules, practiced skills) and knowledge in the world (visible labels, affordances, constraints, mappings -- anything in the environment that tells you what to do). Norman's central argument is that good design minimizes the need for knowledge in the head by putting it into the world.

The classic example is the light switch panel. Six identical switches on a wall, each controlling a different light in the room. After years of living in the house, Norman still couldn't remember which switch controlled which light. The knowledge was entirely in his head, and it wasn't sticking. His solution: mount the switches on a floor plan of the room, each switch positioned where its corresponding light was located. Now the mapping is visible -- the knowledge is in the world. No memorization required.

Constraints are another form of knowledge in the world. A Lego motorcycle with fifteen pieces can be assembled without instructions because physical constraints (this piece only fits here), cultural constraints (red light goes on the back), semantic constraints (the rider faces forward), and logical constraints (only one piece left, only one place for it) eliminate wrong choices. The knowledge of how to assemble the motorcycle is embedded in the pieces themselves.

## Why it matters

Every error that arises from someone forgetting a step, misremembering a procedure, or confusing two similar actions is a failure to put knowledge in the world. Checklists are knowledge in the world. Signage is knowledge in the world. Color-coded indicators, shape-coded controls, natural mappings -- all reduce the load on fallible human memory.

The tradeoff: knowledge in the head allows for faster, more fluid expert performance. A skilled typist doesn't need to look at the keyboard. But knowledge in the world makes the system accessible to novices and reliable under stress, fatigue, or interruption -- which is to say, under normal human conditions. The ideal design supports both: experts can ignore the world-knowledge and operate from memory, while novices (or stressed experts) always have the environment to fall back on.
