# Imagined Orders

An imagined order is any system of human cooperation that exists only in the shared imagination of its participants. Money, nations, corporations, human rights, laws, religions -- none of these have any existence outside the stories humans tell each other. They are not lies (everyone sincerely believes in them) nor objective truths (they have no physical reality). They are *inter-subjective* realities: they exist as long as the collective continues to believe.

Harari argues that imagined orders are the operating system of civilization. Every large-scale human cooperation -- from Sumerian city-states to modern multinational corporations -- is rooted in shared myths. The Peugeot corporation does not exist in any physical sense: destroy every car, factory, and office and "Peugeot" still exists as a legal fiction in which millions participate. The American Declaration of Independence declares truths to be "self-evident" that are, from a biological standpoint, purely imaginary.

Three mechanisms keep imagined orders stable: (1) they are embedded in the material world (architecture, consumer goods, city plans all reflect and reinforce the prevailing order); (2) they shape our desires (we want a "dream holiday" because romantic consumerism tells us to, not because of any biological need); (3) they are inter-subjective, meaning you cannot change them alone -- dismantling one requires convincing millions, and typically just replaces one imagined order with another.

## Why it matters

This is the central insight of *Sapiens* and possibly the single most useful lens for understanding human civilization. Once you see imagined orders, you see them everywhere: in corporate culture, in national identity, in legal systems, in the money in your wallet. The power of the concept is not that it "debunks" these things -- they are genuinely functional and powerful -- but that it reveals them as *choices* rather than inevitabilities. Understanding that the current order is imagined opens the door to imagining a different one.

The trap, however, is real: there is no escape from imagined orders. Breaking out of one just lands you in another. The revolutionary who overthrows the monarchy believes in the nation-state. The libertarian who rejects government believes in property rights. You cannot run a large-scale society on objective reality alone.
