# Horizontal Relationships

Vertical relationships rank people: superior/inferior, capable/incapable, evaluator/evaluated. They produce praise ("Good job!" -- a judgment from above), rebuke (the same judgment with a negative sign), intervention (intruding on others' tasks because you know better), and manipulation (steering others toward your goals under the guise of "helping"). Horizontal relationships treat people as equal in worth, though different in role and ability. "Equal but not the same."

Adler's strongest claim: if you have even *one* vertical relationship, you will unconsciously make all your relationships vertical. You cannot be horizontal with friends but vertical with subordinates. The orientation is a character trait, not a situational choice.

The practical consequence: neither praise nor rebuke. Both are vertical acts. A mother praising her child for helping with dinner ("You're such a good helper!") would never say the same to her husband for doing the same thing. The praise reveals the hierarchy. The alternative is encouragement -- not evaluating the person's ability, but expressing gratitude, acknowledging effort, and helping them develop the courage to face their own tasks. "Thank you, this is helpful" rather than "Good job!"

## Why it matters

Most management and parenting frameworks operate on the praise/rebuke axis and therefore reinforce vertical dynamics. Praise creates dependence on external validation. Rebuke creates fear or resentment. Both teach the other person to look *up* for direction rather than *inward* for motivation. Encouragement, by contrast, strengthens the other person's autonomy.

This maps directly onto the [Socratic method](socratic-method-in-management.md): Jonah doesn't praise or correct Alex Rogo. He asks questions that help Alex develop his own understanding. The philosopher in this book does the same thing with the youth. Both refuse the vertical position of the authority who dispenses judgments.
