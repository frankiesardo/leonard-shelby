# Habits Plus Deliberate Practice

Habits are necessary but not sufficient for mastery. The formula is: **Habits + Deliberate Practice = Mastery.**

The upside of habits is that we can do things without thinking. The downside is that we stop paying attention to little errors. Once a skill becomes automatic, you become less sensitive to feedback. You fall into mindless repetition. Research shows that once a skill is mastered, there is typically a slight *decline* in performance over time -- not because you get worse, but because you stop actively improving.

This is the dark side of automaticity. The basketball player who can dribble without thinking is free to focus on court vision. But the player who never reflects on his dribbling will never refine it. The surgeon who has done a thousand incisions can do them with her eyes closed -- but without deliberate review, bad micro-habits creep in unnoticed.

Pat Riley's "Career Best Effort" program with the Lakers is the antidote. Riley tracked each player's performance nightly using a composite score (points, rebounds, assists, steals, minus turnovers, divided by minutes played). He asked each player to improve by 1% over the course of a season. The Lakers won back-to-back championships. The system worked because it combined habitual excellence with continuous reflection.

Clear practices two modes of reflection: an Annual Review (What went well? What didn't? What did I learn?) and a mid-year Integrity Report (What are my core values? Am I living them? How can I raise the standard?). These periodic check-ins prevent the gradual slide that comes from running on autopilot.

The process of mastery is an endless cycle: automate a skill through repetition, then return to the effortful part and begin building the next layer. Each habit unlocks the next level of performance.
