# MAYA — Most Advanced Yet Acceptable

Raymond Loewy's principle for product design and innovation adoption: the sweet spot between novelty and familiarity. Loewy, the industrial designer who shaped everything from the Coca-Cola bottle to the Shell logo to NASA interiors, formulated it as "Most Advanced Yet Acceptable." The ideal product or design is as different as people can tolerate — no more, no less.

The failure modes on each side:
- **Too novel**: people reject it. The threat response kicks in. Google Glass, the Segway, early electric cars with unusual silhouettes. Objectively impressive technology that failed because the psychological signal was wrong — too alien, too weird, too much of an identity commitment.
- **Too familiar**: nobody cares. Me-too products with zero psychological pull. You may win on price, but you're playing a margin game with no differentiation.

The Prius is Loewy's principle in action. Toyota designed a car that looked distinctly different — a signal that you could broadcast your values without saying a word. The weird silhouette was a feature, not a bug. It was *just* familiar enough (it's still a car) and *just* novel enough (you can spot it from half a block away). This is why the Prius outsold technically superior hybrid alternatives that looked like ordinary sedans.

## Why it matters

MAYA reframes the debate between "build what people want" and "build what's ahead of its time." It says: both framings miss the point. The question is never "how much novelty?" in the abstract — it's "how much novelty is this specific market, in this specific cultural moment, psychologically equipped to absorb?" That's an empirical question, and the answer shifts over time.

For founders: MAYA is why timing matters so much. The same product can fail in 2012 and succeed in 2017 not because the technology changed, but because the cultural context did — people become familiar with the concept, the social proof accumulates, the psychological distance closes. "Being too early" is often a MAYA problem, not a technology problem.

MAYA also explains why great marketing often works by pre-familiarizing an audience with something before the product launches — seeding the concept, making the novel familiar in small doses, until the product itself lands in pre-softened ground.
