# Protect the New

Our job as managers in creative environments is to protect new ideas from those who don't understand that in order for greatness to emerge, there must be phases of not-so-greatness. Protect the future, not the past.

Organizations have immune systems. New ideas -- by definition unfamiliar, unproven, and initially crude -- trigger the immune response. Budget-holders see risk. Schedule-keepers see disruption. Experienced hands see something that looks worse than what they already know how to do. The institutional bias is always toward the proven, the familiar, the safe. Left unchecked, this bias kills innovation not through malice but through reasonable-seeming caution.

The manager's role is not to originate the new ideas, nor to evaluate them prematurely, but to create the conditions in which they can survive long enough to show what they might become. This means actively shielding nascent work from the organizational forces that would smother it -- not indefinitely, but long enough for it to develop past its initial ugliness.

## Why it matters

Every Pixar film started out bad. Toy Story was nearly killed by Disney executives who wanted to make it edgier. The reboot that saved it only happened because the team was given the time and space to find the real story. If the institutional response to "this isn't working yet" is to pull the plug, replace the leadership, or impose external solutions, then the organization can only ever produce variations on what already exists.

The distinction between protecting and enabling matters. Protection isn't uncritical enthusiasm. It's creating the structured space -- through mechanisms like the Braintrust, through research trips, through the willingness to let things be ugly for a while -- where honest feedback can improve the idea without destroying it. You protect the new by giving it both shelter and sunlight.
