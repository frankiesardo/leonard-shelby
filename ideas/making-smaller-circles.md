# Making Smaller Circles

Touch the essence of a technique, then incrementally condense its external form while keeping the essence intact. Over time, expansiveness decreases while potency increases. The motion becomes invisible but the power remains — or grows.

Start with a straight right punch. Practice the full motion slowly: power generating from the ground, through the legs, torso, shoulder, to the knuckles. Thousands of repetitions until the body mechanics are in your skin. You don't think about the path from foot to fist — you just feel the ground connecting to your fingertips. Now begin condensing. Coil a little less. Start the punch from closer to the target. Each refinement is monitored by the *feeling* you gained from the large motion. Slowly, the hand can barely move and still deliver a devastating blow. Mike Tyson's knockouts looked completely unrealistic — you had to watch in slow motion to see any punch at all. He had condensed large circles into very small ones.

The idea is inspired by a scene in Pirsig's *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*: a student can't write about her town — it feels too small and dull. Her teacher tells her to write about the upper-left brick of the opera house facade. A torrent of creativity unleashes. The theme is depth over breadth — plunge into the micro to understand what makes the macro tick.

## Why it matters

We live in an attention-deficit culture, always hungering for something new. When this translates to learning, the effect is devastating. Martial arts students collect forms but master none. Chess players memorize openings but don't understand endgames. Developers learn frameworks but not fundamentals.

The alternative: take something small and go deep. Waitzkin spent hours moving his hand six inches through the air, refining the body mechanics of a single Tai Chi movement. When he improved the feeling in that small motion, he could translate it onto the entire form. Everything flowed at a higher level. The principles making one simple technique tick were the same fundamentals fueling the whole expansive system.

This is how Waitzkin won his first Push Hands National Championship after only two years of study. His opponents knew more techniques. But he was very good at what he did know. Depth beats breadth because it opens a channel for the unconscious, creative components of hidden potential.

The chess parallel: the British Grandmaster Michael Adams appears to completely disregard the fundamental principle of central control. His pieces are often on the flanks. Yet he wins — because he has internalized central domination so deeply that he controls the center without appearing to. He has made the circles so small that even Grandmasters cannot see them.
