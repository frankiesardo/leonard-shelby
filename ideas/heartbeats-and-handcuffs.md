# Heartbeats and Handcuffs

The best constraint in the world is time. When you're handcuffed to a hard deadline, you can't keep trying this and that, changing your mind, putting the finishing touches on something that will never be finished. The external heartbeat -- the constraint -- drives the creativity, which fuels the innovation.

There are three levels of heartbeat:

1. **Team heartbeats:** Each team makes its own rhythm and deadlines for delivering their piece. Design has a different tempo than engineering, which differs from marketing.

2. **Project heartbeats:** Regular milestones where all teams sync to make sure the product still makes sense and the pieces are moving at the right pace. These slow you down in the short term but speed up the whole project.

3. **External heartbeats:** Immovable dates set by outside forces -- Christmas, a major conference, a competitor's launch. The best external heartbeats are not set by the company but by the world. Apple's annual rhythm (WWDC, September launch, etc.) is a heartbeat so steady that customers, press, and employees all feel it.

General Magic had no heartbeat. The launch got delayed by six months, then another six, then another, for four years. They only shipped when competition forced their hand. The first iPod, by contrast, went from concept to launch in ten months because Fadell handcuffed the team to Christmas.

## Why it matters

Without heartbeats, teams optimize endlessly. Perfectionism expands to fill the available time. Features creep in. Decisions get deferred. The product never ships.

But heartbeats can be too fast or too slow. If a team constantly updates their product, customers start tuning out -- they don't have time to learn one version before it changes again. Google's erratic heartbeat means they can never communicate cohesively about their entire organization. Apple, by contrast, has two to four major announcements per year -- enough to stay relevant, few enough that each one matters.

For V1, the heartbeat is entirely internal and a bit wobbly (too many unknowns). For V2 and beyond, external heartbeats drive internal ones. The ideal for any new product: 9 to 18 months from concept to launch, 24 months at the limit. After launch, two to four external announcements per year.

Also: keep the team small as long as you can. Don't allocate too much money at the start. People do stupid things when they have a giant budget -- they overdesign, overthink, and slow the heartbeat to a crawl. The iPhone started with a handful of people and grew to eight hundred. If all eight hundred had been there from day one, the project would have collapsed.
