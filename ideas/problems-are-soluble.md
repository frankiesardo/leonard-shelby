# Problems Are Soluble

Every evil is caused by a lack of knowledge. There is no law of nature, no supernatural decree, no fundamental barrier that prevents us from solving any given problem. The only thing that can prevent a solution is the failure to seek one -- or the active suppression of the search.

This is Deutsch's definition of optimism, and it is far harder-edged than the colloquial sense. It is not the expectation that things will go well. It is the *explanatory* claim that failures are always due to insufficient knowledge, never to the inherent impossibility of progress. If a bridge collapses, the cause was insufficient knowledge of engineering. If a disease kills, the cause was insufficient medical knowledge. If a society stagnates, the cause was the suppression of knowledge creation.

But there is a crucial companion principle: problems are also *inevitable*. Every solution creates new problems. There is no final state, no equilibrium, no "sustainability" in the static sense. Progress does not move toward a destination; it moves from problems to *better problems*. The only sustainable mode of existence is perpetual problem-solving.

## Why it matters

The principle of optimism is the philosophical refutation of all prophets of doom. When Malthus predicted mass starvation, when Ehrlich predicted the collapse of civilization by 1991, when the europium shortage was supposed to end color television -- they were all making the same mistake. They prophesied the content of future knowledge: they assumed that the problems they could see would not be solved. But the content of future knowledge is, by definition, unknowable. You cannot predict what you have not yet thought of.

This does not mean every problem *will* be solved in time. It means that if a problem goes unsolved, the explanation is always that the relevant knowledge was not created -- not that the problem was inherently insoluble. The practical implication: invest in the capacity to create knowledge (science, education, wealth, freedom of thought) rather than in specific predictions about which problems will arise.
