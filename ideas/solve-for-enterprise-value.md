# Solve for Enterprise Value

EV > TV > MV. Enterprise Value is greater than Team Value is greater than My Value. Always optimize for the whole company first, then your team, then yourself. HubSpot eventually added CV (Customer Value) in front: CV > EV > TV > MV — solve for the customer first, then the company, then the team, then yourself.

The failure mode appears at scale. When a company is small and everyone sits in one room, alignment is natural — everyone's solving for EV because there's no distinction between the team and the company. As departments form, managers start solving for their own metrics. The head of sales maximizes bookings even if it creates downstream problems for service. Marketing optimizes for leads even if they're low quality. Each department's local optimization produces a global suboptimization.

## Why it matters

This is the Theory of Constraints applied to organizational incentives rather than manufacturing. Just as a non-bottleneck optimizing for local efficiency can actually harm throughput, a department optimizing for its own KPIs can harm the enterprise. The mechanism is identical: resources get consumed in ways that don't help the constraint.

The hardest version of this problem is when TV-solvers are popular with their teams. A VP who fights for resources, shields the team from accountability, and never pushes back on their people will have great employee NPS — right up until the quarter it collapses. HubSpot discovered that when a department's employee NPS dropped significantly, it almost never recovered. Once you lose your team's trust, you can't rebuild it.

HubSpot fought TV-optimization with three mechanisms: (1) explicit scoring in performance reviews, (2) public feedback — Halligan would criticize TV-solving in large management meetings, and (3) quarterly employee NPS by department, which made the problem visible before it became terminal.
