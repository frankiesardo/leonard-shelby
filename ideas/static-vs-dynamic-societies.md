# Static vs. Dynamic Societies

Every society before the Western Enlightenment was static: changing too slowly for its members to notice. Static societies are not merely traditional or slow-moving. They are societies that actively suppress the creation of new knowledge. Their memes replicate by disabling their holders' creativity and critical faculties -- through taboo, enforced conformity, and the systematic destruction of the human capacity for innovation.

This is not an accident. It is a logical necessity. If people are free to think and to improve upon their ideas, change happens fast -- exponentially fast. A single person has a good idea; others adopt it; the society changes; that change creates new conditions for new ideas. Within a few generations, the exponential growth of knowledge would transform every aspect of life. Static societies prevent this by ensuring that most new ideas are never thought of in the first place, and those that are thought of are never enacted.

Dynamic societies -- of which the post-Enlightenment West is the only sustained example -- do the opposite. They enable knowledge creation through traditions of criticism, tolerance of dissent, and institutions that protect the new. Their characteristic state is rapid, open-ended change. Problems are solved, new problems emerge, and those are solved in turn.

## Why it matters

The static/dynamic distinction dissolves many common confusions. "Sustainability" is a static-society concept -- it means preventing things from changing. But static societies are inherently fragile. They survive until the first problem they cannot solve with existing knowledge, and then they collapse. Easter Island did not fall because of bad forestry. It fell because it was a static society that could not create new knowledge when a problem arose. Every static society eventually meets such a problem. Sustainability in the static sense is the most *unsustainable* strategy possible.

The only genuinely sustainable mode of existence is dynamic: perpetual problem-solving, perpetual creation of new knowledge, perpetual change. This is unsustainable in the narrow sense that no specific practice can continue forever -- but it is sustainable in the deep sense that the *capacity to solve problems* can continue forever.
