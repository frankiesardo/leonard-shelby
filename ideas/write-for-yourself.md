# Write for Yourself

"Who am I writing for?" is a question that every writer must answer. Zinsser's answer is blunt: you are writing for yourself. Never say anything in writing that you wouldn't comfortably say in conversation. If you wouldn't use a certain word in everyday speech, don't use it in your writing.

The point is not selfishness but authenticity. If you lose the dullest readers by being too interesting for them, you're writing better, not worse. If you try to write for a demographic — "the reader" — you'll produce something bland and calculated. You'll be afraid to use humor or a specialized word or a personal reference, because somebody somewhere won't like it.

This sounds paradoxical: how can you serve the reader by ignoring the reader? Because craft and intention are separate. Your *craft* must serve the reader — be clear, be logical, be organized, don't waste their time. But your *intention* must serve yourself: write about what interests you, in a voice that sounds like you. If you're finding it interesting, there's a good chance others will too. If you're bored, they'll know.

Zinsser watched countless students and writers tie themselves in knots trying to "reach" an imagined audience. The ones who wrote well were the ones who stopped guessing what strangers wanted and started writing what they genuinely cared about, as well as they could.

## Why it matters

Any creative or intellectual act can be paralyzed by trying to please an imagined crowd. The designer who focus-groups every decision. The speaker who tries to say what the audience wants to hear. The coder who writes for "other developers" instead of for clarity. In every case, the work suffers from a divided loyalty.

The rule "write for yourself" doesn't mean "ignore quality" — it means make yourself the first judge. If you wouldn't want to read it, no one else will either.
