# Psychological Moonshots

Weather apps report two temperatures: the actual temperature and the "feels like" temperature — a composite of ambient temperature, humidity, breeze, and sun exposure. When you're deciding what to wear, the "feels like" number is more useful. Sutherland's argument: we should apply this same logic to every business problem.

Most organizations define their objectives in "psychology-free terminology" — delivery speed, cost per unit, click-through rate. They then pursue engineering or logistical solutions to improve these numbers. But the customer's experienced reality is a "feels like" composite of functional performance, emotional context, trust, and narrative. You might be able to solve the problem very cheaply and efficiently by changing the psychology rather than changing the technology.

The classic example: the Eurostar train from London to Paris. Engineers spent billions shaving 40 minutes off the journey time. For a fraction of that cost, you could have hired supermodels to serve free champagne on board, and passengers would have asked for the train to go *slower*. The engineering solution was expensive and invisible; the psychological solution would have been cheap and transformative.

## Why it matters

Psychological moonshots are contrarian because they're low-cost, high-impact, and deeply uncomfortable for engineers, finance people, and management consultants. They can't be justified with a spreadsheet. They don't look like "real" solutions. They require understanding humans rather than systems. And the people with the most organizational power — the quantifiers — are the least equipped to see them.

This doesn't mean engineering solutions are wrong. It means you should always ask both questions: "What's the technological fix?" and "What's the psychological fix?" The best organizations do both. The worst do only the first and congratulate themselves on their rigor.

Sutherland's meta-point: rationality is the bronze standard of problem-solving, not the gold standard. A rational solution is where you *start*. Then you ask: is there a way to achieve the same effect through framing, context, trust, emotion, or narrative? If yes, that solution is usually cheaper, faster, and more durable — because it works *with* human nature instead of against it.
