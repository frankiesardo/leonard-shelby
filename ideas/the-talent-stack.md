Scott Adams could draw pretty well. He also understood business pretty well. Neither skill alone was world-class. But the combination made Dilbert — one of the most successful cartoons in history — because no one else occupied that exact intersection. The additive effect of being good at two things is more than double. Being good at three is more than triple. You become a "super relevant specialist in the combination of the domains."

Larry Summers' version is punchier: *don't be fungible*. If you're "just" a designer or "just" a coder, you can be swapped in or out. If you're the person who can design, code, *and* make product decisions, you're competing with almost no one. The combination is the moat.

AI makes this strategy radically more accessible. Before, acquiring a second deep skill took years. Now you can use AI to bootstrap competence in adjacent domains at a speed that was never possible. You don't need to become a world-class designer — you need to become a coder who can use AI to produce genuinely good design. That's a qualitatively different person than a coder who can't.

This connects directly to [The Sampling Period](the-sampling-period.md) from Range: early breadth creates the foundation for later combinatorial advantage. It's also the career-strategy implication of [The Mexican Standoff](the-mexican-standoff.md) — the roles are converging, so the people who stack skills across them fastest win.

The shape of the ideal career is shifting from T (one deep vertical, broad surface awareness) to something more like M: two or three real verticals, with AI bridging the gaps. Find the intersection that almost nobody else occupies, and you become unfungible.
