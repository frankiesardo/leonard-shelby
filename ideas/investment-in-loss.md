# Investment in Loss

Deliberately put yourself in positions where you will lose, get thrown around, look foolish — because that's where the learning lives. Ego must yield before growth can begin.

Waitzkin's Tai Chi teacher William Chen coined the phrase. In Push Hands practice, a big strong guy walks in and someone pushes him. His instinct is to push back — to prove he's strong. The problem is he isn't learning anything. To grow, he needs to give up his current mindset. He needs to let little guys toss him around while he figures out how to use something other than brawn. That's investment in loss: giving yourself to the learning process even when it means losing.

Waitzkin lived this during months of getting smashed into walls by a training partner named Evan — a 200-pound second-degree karate black belt who didn't believe in going easy. At first it was brutal and humiliating. But as Waitzkin got used to taking hits, something shifted. He stopped fearing the impact. His body learned to absorb blows. Then Evan seemed to slow down in his mind. He started sensing attacks before they began, reading intention, finding space. Eventually the tables turned completely — and Evan, who had never been willing to invest in loss himself, quit rather than face the reversal.

## Why it matters

Most people instinctively protect their current level of competence. They avoid situations where they might look bad. They train against opponents they can beat. They stick with approaches that feel comfortable. This is the opposite of investment in loss — it's investment in ego. And it has a ceiling.

Michael Jordan missed more last-minute shots than any player in NBA history. He also made more. What made him the greatest wasn't perfection — it was the willingness to put himself on the line as a way of life. The nights he sent twenty thousand fans home heartbroken were the price of basketball immortality.

The principle scales down to everyday decisions. Taking on the project you're not ready for. Asking the question that reveals what you don't know. Sparring with someone who will expose your weaknesses. The discomfort is the signal that learning is happening.

If a student of any discipline could avoid repeating the same mistake twice — both technical and psychological — they would skyrocket to the top. That's impossible in practice, but the aspiration requires a willingness to be thrown around while you figure things out.
