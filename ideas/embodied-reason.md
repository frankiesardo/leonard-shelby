# Embodied Reason

Reason is not an abstract, disembodied capacity that happens to be implemented in a brain. It grows *from* the body — from perception, motor activity, spatial orientation, physical manipulation, and bodily interaction with environments. The structures that make abstract thought possible are extensions (via metaphor) of structures that first emerged from bodily experience.

This is the deepest claim of Lakoff's *Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things*: that the classical Western view of reason as transcendental — independent of any particular organism, potentially implementable in any medium — is wrong. Reason is *ours*, and it has the shape it has because we have the bodies we have.

## Why it matters

The evidence is strongest at the level of basic-level categories. The most cognitively natural categories (chair, dog, car) are the level at which we have distinctive motor programs (how you interact with a chair), gestalt perception (you recognize a chair as a whole shape), and rich mental imagery. This is not a coincidence — it's the mechanism. Our categories are shaped by how our bodies work in environments.

Abstract thought doesn't escape embodiment — it extends it. We understand HAPPY IS UP because erect posture goes with positive states. We understand TIME IS MONEY because industrialized labor made both into measurable resources. We understand ARGUMENT IS WAR because verbal conflict shares the structure of physical confrontation. Every chain of abstraction bottoms out in bodily experience.

The implications run deep. If reason is embodied, then:
- Machines that manipulate symbols without embodiment don't "understand" in the way we do
- Different bodies (differently abled, differently cultured) may reason differently in specific ways
- Cultivating bodily awareness (martial arts, dance, meditation) is not separate from cultivating reason — it's part of it
- Education should attend to the embodied foundations of understanding, not just the abstract structures
