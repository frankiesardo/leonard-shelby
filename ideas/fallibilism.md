# Fallibilism

There are no authoritative sources of knowledge. No foundations, no certainties, no final answers. All knowledge is conjectural -- created by guessing and then subjecting those guesses to criticism. What survives criticism is our best current understanding, but it remains provisional and improvable.

Deutsch follows Popper in taking this further than most people are comfortable with. It is not merely that we *might* be wrong. It is that we are *certainly* wrong about virtually everything we currently believe, in the sense that future explanations will supersede our current ones just as Einstein superseded Newton and Newton superseded Kepler. Each step was an improvement -- less wrong than the last -- but none was the final truth.

Deutsch suggests we should call all theories "misconceptions" from the start. Einstein's Misconception of Gravity improved on Newton's Misconception, which improved on Kepler's. The neo-Darwinian Misconception of Evolution improved on Darwin's. If people thought of it this way, no one would need reminding that science claims neither infallibility nor finality.

## Why it matters

Fallibilism is the philosophical foundation of all open-ended progress. If knowledge could be completed, there would be a ceiling. If any source -- scripture, tradition, sensory experience, mathematical proof -- were immune to error, it would become an obstacle to improvement. The infinite potential of knowledge requires infinite ignorance as its precondition.

This is not cause for despair. It is cause for the deepest form of optimism: we can always do better. The progress from problems to *better problems* never ends. Our current problems in physics embody more knowledge than Einstein's did, and his embodied more than Newton's. Each generation inherits not just answers but richer, more interesting questions.

The practical upside: any time you encounter someone who claims to have found the final answer -- in science, politics, management, personal development -- you know they are wrong. Not because you know the right answer, but because there are no final answers. The question is always: what is the best available explanation, and how can we improve it?
