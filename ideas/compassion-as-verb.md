# Compassion as Verb

Empathy and compassion are not the same thing. Kornfield's image: a kid is being bullied on the schoolyard. Empathy is when you feel for that kid — when your nervous system resonates with their pain, like a violin on a table vibrating in sympathy with a bowed violin nearby. That's the mirror neuron response. It's involuntary and it's real.

Compassion is what happens next. Compassion moves you — to walk over, to talk to the teacher, to say "hey, there's suffering here, let's do something." Compassion is empathy with the heart's response to alleviate suffering. Empathy feels. Compassion acts.

The distinction matters practically because empathy alone can lead to empathic distress — you absorb others' pain without channeling it toward anything useful, and you burn out. Compassion transforms the feeling into energy for action, which is sustaining rather than depleting.

## Why it matters

Most people conflate empathy and compassion, which creates two failure modes. First: "I feel your pain" becomes the end point rather than the starting point, and nothing changes. Second: people who act without empathy call it compassion, but it's really just fixing — they skip the feeling and go straight to the solution, which often misses the actual need.

The complete circuit is: feel with (empathy) → be moved to act (compassion) → act wisely (discernment). Kornfield trains all three as distinct, practicable skills.
