# Match Quality

The degree to which a person's work matches their abilities and interests. Economists call it "match quality," and it turns out to be more predictive of success and fulfillment than raw grit or early specialization. The problem is you can't figure it out by introspection alone -- you have to discover it through action.

Economist Robert Miller modeled career choice as a "multi-armed bandit" problem: you have limited pulls on multiple slot machines and need to figure out which one pays best. The optimal strategy is not to pick one early and pull forever -- it's to sample broadly, gather information, and gradually shift toward the best-paying machine. Short-term exploration pays off in long-term exploitation.

Van Gogh was a walking multi-armed bandit. He tried art dealing, teaching, bookselling, ministry, and multiple artistic styles with maniacal intensity, discarding each when it didn't fit. His Grit Scale score would have been low -- he scored in the 40th percentile according to his biographer's assessment. But his relentless testing of options is what led him to the unique style that changed art forever.

Frances Hesselbein never applied for any of her four CEO positions and tried to turn down three of them. She took a series of "temporary" roles, each one teaching her something new. At sixty, she became CEO of the Girl Scouts and was later called the best CEO in America by Peter Drucker. "I was unaware that I was being prepared," she said.

The Dark Horse Project at Harvard found that virtually every fulfilled, successful person they studied had followed a winding path. They all practiced short-term planning, not long-term planning. "Here's who I am at the moment, here are my motivations, here's what I'd like to learn, and here are the opportunities. Which of these is the best match right now?"

## Why it matters

Herminia Ibarra's research on career changers found that we learn who we are only by living, not before. "First act and then think" -- the opposite of conventional wisdom. The entire strengths-finder, personality-quiz industry sells the comforting fiction that you can identify your ideal path through introspection. You can't. Match quality is discovered through experimentation.

Paul Graham encapsulated it: "Instead of working back from a goal, work forward from promising situations." Phil Knight, Nike's founder, had no long-term vision -- he just kept making short-term pivots and applying what he learned. Michelangelo constantly changed his sculptural plans as he worked, leaving three-fifths of his sculptures unfinished to move on to something more promising.

The end of history illusion (Dan Gilbert) explains why this matters: at every age, we believe our current preferences, values, and personality are basically final. But they keep changing. Specializing early is betting on a person who doesn't exist yet.
