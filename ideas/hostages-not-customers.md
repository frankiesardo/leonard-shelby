# Hostages Not Customers

Some software vendors don't compete on product quality — they compete on the catastrophic cost of leaving. SAP wins an enterprise customer, then does "the bare minimum" because switching to Oracle requires a multi-year migration that will probably fail and definitely get someone fired. The customer isn't staying because the product is good; they're staying because escape is too dangerous.

Alex Rampel's framing: "Some companies have hostages, not customers." The distinction matters enormously. A hostage-based business model is defensible as long as switching costs stay high — but it creates no real value, invites no product discipline, and collapses the moment exit becomes cheap.

## Why it matters

Coding agents are the first credible threat to hostage-based enterprise moats. When an AI can dramatically reduce the complexity and risk of migrating from SAP to Oracle — handling the systems integration work that used to require armies of consultants — the hostage becomes a customer again. They can now choose. This is a long-term positive for the enterprise software ecosystem (more competition, better products) and a serious structural threat for incumbents who confused captivity with loyalty.

The inverse lesson for builders: build loyalty, not lock-in. Lock-in is fragile to any technological shift that reduces switching costs. Loyalty compounds. The difference is whether customers would stay if they *could* leave easily.
