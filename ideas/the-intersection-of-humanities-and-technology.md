# The Intersection of Humanities and Technology

Steve Jobs's core belief was that the best products emerge where liberal arts meet engineering -- and that this intersection is where Apple lived. He cited Edwin Land of Polaroid as his model: someone who saw no boundary between science and aesthetics. Jobs took a calligraphy class at Reed College after dropping out; a decade later, it became the reason the Macintosh had beautiful typography. He spent time at Pixar watching storytellers and animators work alongside computer scientists; the result was movies that were both technically groundbreaking and emotionally profound.

The principle is not that engineers should learn to paint, or that artists should learn to code. It is that the people making final product decisions must understand and value both domains. Jobs's deepest critique of Microsoft and other technology companies was that they were run by engineers and salespeople who didn't have humanities in their DNA. "They totally didn't get it," he said of Microsoft's attempts to copy the Mac. Not because Microsoft lacked engineering talent -- it had plenty -- but because its culture could not appreciate why fonts mattered, why the scrollbar needed to feel a certain way, why the weight of the device communicated something.

## Why it matters

Most organizations treat design as a step that happens after engineering. The product is built, then the designers make it look nice. Jobs inverted this: design and engineering were simultaneous, interwoven, and co-equal. When Jony Ive described how a new product should feel, that description had the same authority as an engineer's specification of processor speed. The emotional experience of using the product was not a nice-to-have -- it was the product.

This has implications far beyond technology. Any field where human beings are the end users -- medicine, education, law, government -- can benefit from placing humanistic sensibility alongside technical expertise in the decision-making seat. The pattern Jobs identified -- that companies decline when they let salespeople and financiers push product people out of leadership -- is visible in hospitals run by administrators, schools run by bureaucrats, and media companies run by MBAs.
