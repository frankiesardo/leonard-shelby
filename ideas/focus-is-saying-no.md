# Focus Is Saying No

When Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company was making dozens of products -- multiple versions of the Macintosh, printers, scanners, Newton PDAs, servers, and more. It was ninety days from bankruptcy. Jobs drew a simple two-by-two grid on a whiteboard. One axis: consumer vs. professional. The other: desktop vs. portable. "We should make four great products," he said. "One for each quadrant." Everything else was killed. Thousands of people were laid off. The remaining teams focused with an intensity that had been impossible when resources were spread across dozens of mediocre products.

"People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on," Jobs explained at a 1997 Apple developer conference. "But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are. You have to pick carefully. I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done. Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things."

The principle applied at every level: not just product lines, but features within products, buttons on devices, options in interfaces. The iPod Shuffle had no screen. The iPhone had one button. Each release of iOS said no to features that competitors considered essential. The courage was in the removal.

## Why it matters

Focus is easy to preach and agonizing to practice. Saying no means disappointing people, killing projects that teams have poured their hearts into, and accepting that some customers will go elsewhere. Most leaders hedge -- they keep the extra product line "just in case," add the extra feature because one important customer asked for it, maintain the underperforming division because shutting it down would mean layoffs. The result is a company that does many things adequately and nothing superbly.

Jobs attributed his ability to focus to his Zen training, which honed his appreciation for simplicity and his ability to filter out distractions. But it was also, he admitted, a natural inclination: he could simply ignore things he didn't want to deal with -- a legal problem, a health issue, an uncomfortable family situation. The same trait that made him a brilliant product strategist made him a difficult human being. Focus is a blade that cuts in all directions.
