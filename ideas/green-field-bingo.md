# Green Field Bingo

Don't try to free the incumbent's hostages. Sell to companies that haven't been captured yet.

Alex Rampell's name for the strategy that made Stripe, Mercury, and Toast work. When enterprise incumbents have hostages (not customers), the naive startup play is to build something better and try to win the migration. But migration is a multi-year, career-threatening project — even when your product is clearly superior. The smarter play: build the best product and sell it to new companies that haven't made a choice yet. As long as the rate of new company creation is high enough, you can build a dominant position without ever confronting the incumbent's lock-in.

Patrick Collison's "genius answer" to "where will your customers come from?" was: "My customers don't exist yet." Stripe didn't steal a single payment-processing customer from Chase Paymentech. Mercury never stole a customer from SVB (until SVB literally failed). They sold to the next generation of companies, who were free to pick the best product because they weren't hostages.

## Why it matters

The strategy tells you both where to play and where not to play. It works when the rate of new entity creation in the target market is high enough to sustain growth: startups (Stripe), SMBs (Mercury, Toast), new legal cases (Eve). It fails when new entity creation is too slow: building a better EHR doesn't work via green field because new hospitals aren't created fast enough.

The AI acceleration makes this more powerful. Software that does the job of labor opens entirely new markets — plaintiff attorneys who previously spent $0 on software, categories where "the dominant software product is Microsoft Office." These are green field markets not because new companies are being created, but because the *category of software buyer* didn't exist before.
