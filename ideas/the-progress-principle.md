# The Progress Principle

Pleasure comes more from making progress toward goals than from achieving them. The elephant gets its dopamine in real time, with each step in the right direction. The final moment of success -- the promotion, the acceptance letter, the book launch -- is often no more than the relief of taking off a heavy backpack at the end of a long hike. If you went on the hike only to feel that relief, you are a fool. Shakespeare captured it: "Things won are done; joy's soul lies in the doing."

Richard Davidson distinguishes two types of positive affect: "pre-goal attainment positive affect" (the pleasure of making progress) and "post-goal attainment positive affect" (a short-lived feeling of release when the left prefrontal cortex reduces its activity after a goal has been achieved). The first is ongoing, energizing, and deeply satisfying. The second is brief, fading, and often anticlimactic. Most of the pleasure in any endeavor is had along the way.

This principle is the positive mirror of the [hedonic treadmill](the-hedonic-treadmill.md). The treadmill explains why achievement disappoints. The progress principle explains why effort satisfies. Together, they point the same direction: invest in the process, not the outcome.

## Why it matters

The progress principle explains why people who achieve great success often feel empty ("Is that all there is?"). It explains why retirement -- the supposed reward for decades of work -- so often brings depression rather than joy. It explains why Csikszentmihalyi's "flow" state is so satisfying: flow is pure progress, challenge matched to skill, with immediate feedback at every step. And it explains why the best voluntary activities in the [happiness formula](the-happiness-formula.md) are ongoing processes (gratitude practice, using your strengths, acts of kindness), not one-time achievements.

The practical implication: structure your life around activities that offer a continuous sense of progress, not around distant goals whose achievement will disappoint. Break large goals into steps. Celebrate the steps. And choose goals that are intrinsically rewarding to pursue, not just instrumentally valuable when completed.
