# The Last Human Freedom

Everything can be taken from a person except one thing: the freedom to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances. Viktor Frankl discovered this in Auschwitz, where his captors controlled his body, his environment, his schedule, his food, and his proximity to death -- but could not control his inner response. He watched men walk into gas chambers with prayers on their lips. He saw prisoners give away their last bread to comfort others. These were not acts of instinct or conditioning. They were choices.

This is not optimism or positive thinking. It is a harder, colder claim: that between stimulus and response, there is a space, and in that space lives the capacity to decide what kind of person you will be. The guards could make Frankl dig ditches. They could not make him stop being a psychiatrist in his own mind, or stop loving his wife, or stop reconstructing his lost manuscript in shorthand on scraps of paper.

## Why it matters

The last human freedom is the foundation of responsibility. If you are not free to choose your response, you cannot be held responsible for it. If you are free, then responsibility is inescapable. Frankl insisted on both: man is free, therefore man is responsible. He recommended that the Statue of Liberty be supplemented by a Statue of Responsibility on the West Coast.

This principle is not limited to extreme situations. It applies every time someone cuts you off in traffic, every time a project fails, every time a relationship disappoints. The space between stimulus and response is always available. Most people never notice it. The ones who do change everything.
