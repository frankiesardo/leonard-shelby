# The Turing Principle

It is physically possible to build a virtual-reality generator whose repertoire includes every physically possible environment. This means a single, buildable physical object can mimic, with unlimited accuracy, the behaviour of any other physical object or process in the universe. Deutsch calls this the Turing principle, elevating Turing's Church-Turing conjecture from a claim about abstract mathematics into a fundamental law of physics.

The implications are staggering. If the laws of physics are to be comprehensible, they must be capable of being embodied in a physical object -- the knower. The Turing principle says they are. The laws of physics, by conforming to the Turing principle, mandate their own comprehensibility. Just as the existence of steam engines is a direct expression of the laws of thermodynamics, the existence of minds capable of understanding physics is a direct expression of the Turing principle.

Turing himself proved that one particular abstract machine -- the universal Turing machine -- could simulate any other Turing machine. Deutsch extends this: quantum computers have a wider repertoire than classical Turing machines, and the principle must be stated in terms of physically possible computers, not abstract ones. The strongest form says: every physically possible environment is renderable.

## Why it matters

This principle is the bridge between physics and knowledge. It explains why the universe is comprehensible without requiring any mystical connection between mind and nature. It also constrains cosmology: any cosmological model in which the Turing principle would be violated -- say, a universe that collapses too quickly for unlimited computation -- is ruled out. This leads Deutsch (following Tipler) to infer that the universe must support unlimited knowledge creation, all the way to its end.

The Turing principle also unifies computation, science, and virtual reality as the same activity, viewed from different angles. Science *is* virtual-reality rendering: the scientist builds a model of reality in their mind and tests it against the real thing.
