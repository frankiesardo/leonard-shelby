# Think Small

Most projects fail not because they're too small but because they're too big. The instinct is to be comprehensive -- to cover everything, to say the last word. But there is no last word. What you think is definitive today will be undefinitive by tonight. The writer who doggedly pursues every last fact will pursue the rainbow and never settle down to write.

The solution is drastic reduction. Decide what corner of your subject you're going to bite off, and be content to cover it well and stop. Nobody can write a book "about" something. Tolstoy couldn't write a book about war and peace -- he made certain reductive decisions about time and place and individual characters. One man pursuing one whale.

Every piece of work should leave the audience with one thing they didn't have before. Not two, not five -- just one. Decide what that one thing is before you start. It will determine your route, your destination, and your tone.

## Why it matters

This is a principle of scope as a creative force. It applies to any project where ambition threatens to outrun execution. A startup that tries to solve ten problems ships nothing. A presentation with five main points makes none of them. A memoir that covers an entire life captures no moment vividly.

The fear behind thinking big is that the small thing won't be enough -- that it won't seem important. But the opposite is true. A precisely drawn corner of a subject illuminates the whole. Zinsser's chapter on memoir advises: don't rummage around in your past looking for episodes that seem "important" enough. Look for small, self-contained incidents that are still vivid. If you still remember them, it's because they contain a universal truth your audience will recognize from their own lives.

The practical method: go to your desk and write about one thing. Don't worry about how it fits into the larger project. Put it in a folder. Tomorrow, write about another thing. After months, spread the pieces on the floor and see what patterns emerge. They'll tell you what your project is about -- and what it's not about.
