# Psycho-Logic

Rory Sutherland's core framework: behavior that appears irrational from an economic perspective is often perfectly rational at the psychological level. Economic logic says: maximize utility, minimize cost. Psychological logic says: context, framing, signaling, and perceived meaning often determine behavior more than objective attributes do. "All value is perceived value. There is no other kind."

The practical implication: marketing is not a coating applied on top of a good product. It is the act of understanding the psychological register in which decisions are actually made — and operating there. A 20% price cut may move fewer units than a well-chosen endorser. A redesigned label may outsell a reformulated product. Perceived quality often drives actual quality perceptions: an expensive wine tastes better to the drinker even in blind tests. This isn't irrationality — it's the brain correctly using price as a quality signal in an uncertain world.

## Why it matters

Most tech and product culture is saturated with economic logic: ship features, improve metrics, optimize conversion funnels. The tacit assumption is that if you make something objectively better and cheaper, adoption follows. Sutherland's career is a counterargument built from a thousand case studies. Google Glass was technically impressive. Betamax was technically superior to VHS. Electric cars were better on almost every rational dimension before the Prius. In each case, the missing variable was psychological: the wrong signals, the wrong framing, the wrong social context.

Psycho-logic is the antidote to this. It says: ask not only "what is this product?" but "what does this product *mean* to the person who uses it, in public, in front of others, in the story they tell about themselves?" The answer to that question often matters more than the spec sheet.
