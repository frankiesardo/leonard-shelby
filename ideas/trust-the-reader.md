# Trust the Reader

Imagine a reader who is literate, curious, adaptable, intelligent, and open-minded. Write for that person. Nearly everything you were taught about communication assumes the audience is plodding, distracted, and in constant danger of getting lost. That assumption poisons everything it touches.

When you assume a dull audience, you over-explain, over-transition, over-signify. You shackle your sentences together with logical indicators and hand-holding. You remove unfamiliar words so the reader never has the chance to learn them. You dumb down — and in doing so, you guarantee the mediocrity you feared. "Don't preconceive the reader's limitations. They'll become your own."

Klinkenborg observes that the books you love best are probably the ones that trusted you most — "trusted your intelligence, your keenness, your ability to feel an invisible wink, to follow any trail."

## Why it matters

This principle reaches far beyond writing. Teachers who assume students can't handle complexity produce students who can't handle complexity. Product designers who assume users are stupid build products that feel patronizing. Presenters who over-explain lose their audience's respect and attention. In every domain, the assumptions you make about your audience shape what you're able to produce.

Trusting the reader also means trusting yourself. If you can move briskly and freely — going anywhere from anywhere almost instantly — then you can make connections the hand-holding approach would never permit. You can write by implication, leaving space for the reader to complete the thought. That shared act of meaning-making is where the real power of communication lives.

The practical test: when you're tempted to add an explanation, a transition, a qualifier — ask whether a curious, intelligent person actually needs it. Often they don't. Often the explanation crowds out the very clarity it was meant to provide.
