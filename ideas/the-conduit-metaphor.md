# The Conduit Metaphor

Michael Reddy identified the dominant metaphor for how we think about communication, and it is deeply misleading. The Conduit metaphor has three parts:

1. **IDEAS ARE OBJECTS.** Meanings have an existence independent of people.
2. **LINGUISTIC EXPRESSIONS ARE CONTAINERS.** Words and sentences "hold" meanings.
3. **COMMUNICATION IS SENDING.** The speaker puts ideas into words and sends them to the hearer, who extracts the ideas.

Reddy estimated that at least 70% of English expressions about language follow this pattern: "Try to *pack* more thought *into* fewer words." "The meaning is right there *in* the words." "Your reasons *came through* to us." "It's hard to *get* that idea *across* to him."

The metaphor is useful -- it works fine when context doesn't matter and all parties share the same understanding. But it hides the most important aspects of communication. It hides that meaning depends on context: "Please sit in the apple-juice seat" has no meaning in isolation but makes perfect sense at a breakfast table with three glasses of orange juice and one of apple juice. It hides that the same sentence means different things to different people: "We need new alternative sources of energy" means something very different to the president of Mobil Oil and the president of Friends of the Earth. It hides that understanding is an active, constructive process -- the hearer is not a passive recipient unpacking containers but a model-builder constructing meaning from cues.

## Why it matters

The Conduit metaphor is the default assumption of most writing advice, most communication training, and most arguments about what someone "really said." It underwrites the objectivist view that sentences have fixed meanings independent of context, that miscommunication is always someone's fault (they used the wrong words or heard them wrong), and that perfect communication is theoretically possible if you just choose your words precisely enough.

All of this is wrong, or at best deeply incomplete. Understanding is always an act of interpretation shaped by the hearer's conceptual system, background, purposes, and context. Writing well is not about loading the right meanings into the right containers -- it is about creating conditions under which the reader's model-building process will converge on something close to what you intend.
