# Set Your House in Order

Before you criticize the world, get your own life in order. This is not a dodge of political engagement -- it is a prerequisite for it. Peterson draws the argument from Solzhenitsyn, who, sitting in the gulag, asked himself: "Did I, through my actions, contribute to the catastrophe of my life? If so, how?" Instead of blaming the system alone, Solzhenitsyn inventoried his own failures -- cowardice, vanity, willful blindness -- and rebuilt himself from the inside. The result was a moral authority so formidable that it helped dismantle the Soviet Union.

The opposite path is the one taken by the Columbine killers and by Tolstoy at his most despairing: condemning Being itself, appointing yourself judge and executioner. Peterson argues this logic -- "the world is so corrupt it should be destroyed" -- becomes available to anyone who is bitter enough and who has not looked honestly at their own contribution to the mess.

The practical version is disarmingly simple: stop doing what you know to be wrong. You don't need anyone to tell you what that is. You already know. Stop lying. Stop avoiding the conversation you're dreading. Stop pretending the problem doesn't exist. Start there. See what happens.

## Why it matters

This is [work on yourself](work-on-yourself.md) reframed as moral duty rather than self-improvement hack. You are not cleaning your room because Marie Kondo told you to. You are cleaning your room because the alternative is resentment, which metastasizes into ideology, which, scaled up, produces gulags. The personal and the political are connected through the integrity of the individual. A society of people who refuse to confront their own dishonesty is a society primed for totalitarianism.
