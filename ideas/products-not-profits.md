# Products, Not Profits

Steve Jobs's most emphatic legacy statement was a theory of corporate decline. Great companies, he argued, are built by people who love making great products. The company succeeds, becomes dominant, and the product people are gradually displaced by sales and marketing people -- because the salespeople are the ones who "move the needle on revenues." The sales guys then hire more sales guys to run divisions, and the product people stop mattering. "When the sales guys run the company, the product guys don't matter so much, and a lot of them just turn off."

He saw this pattern everywhere: John Akers (salesman) at IBM. The Xerox executives who couldn't capitalize on the inventions coming out of PARC. John Sculley (marketing guy from Pepsi) at Apple. Steve Ballmer at Microsoft. In each case, the company's products became adequate but not great, and the company entered a slow decline even as revenues temporarily held.

Jobs's counter-principle: "My passion has been to build an enduring company where people were motivated to make great products. Everything else was secondary. Sure, it was great to make a profit, because that was what allowed you to make great products. But the products, not the profits, were the motivation."

## Why it matters

This is not anti-capitalism. It is a specific claim about what sustains excellence over time. Profits are the *result* of great products, not the *cause* of them. When you optimize for profit directly -- cutting corners on materials, shipping before the product is ready, adding features to check competitive boxes rather than to delight users -- you produce things that are adequate but uninspiring. When you optimize for the product and trust that excellence will be rewarded, you sometimes produce failures (the NeXT Computer, the PowerMac G4 Cube) but you also produce the Macintosh, the iPod, the iPhone.

Jobs was also making a claim about motivation. A-players are not primarily motivated by money. They want to build something great, to work with other people who want to build something great, and to feel that their work matters. A company that talks mainly about revenue targets and market share will not attract or retain these people. A company that talks about changing the world with its products will.

The hardest version of this principle is the willingness to cannibalize yourself. "If you don't cannibalize yourself, someone else will," Jobs said. The iPhone cannibalized the iPod. The iPad cannibalized the laptop. If your loyalty is to the product, you follow the best product idea wherever it leads, even if it destroys your current cash cow. If your loyalty is to profits, you protect the cash cow until a competitor kills it for you.
