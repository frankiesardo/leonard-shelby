# Clutter

Clutter is the disease of language. It's unnecessary words, circular constructions, pompous frills, and meaningless jargon. It's the laborious phrase that has pushed out the short word that means the same thing. It's euphemism used to hide uncomfortable truths. It's "at this point in time" instead of "now."

The test is ruthless: look at every word you put down and ask whether it's doing new work. Can the thought be expressed with more economy? Is anything pompous or pretentious? Most first drafts can be cut by 50 percent without losing any information or the author's voice.

Clutter isn't just an aesthetic problem. It's also a moral one. When language is used to obscure rather than clarify — when corporations say "involuntary methodologies" instead of "layoffs," or governments say "collateral damage" instead of "civilian casualties" — clutter becomes a tool of deception. Orwell saw this clearly: political language consists largely of euphemism, question-begging, and sheer cloudy vagueness.

## Why it matters

Clutter is everywhere, not just in writing. It's in slide decks with too many bullet points. It's in code with unnecessary abstractions. It's in meetings that could have been emails. It's in products with too many features. The principle is universal: anything that doesn't serve a clear purpose is actively working against you, because it forces the audience to do work that doesn't pay off.

Writing improves in direct ratio to the number of things we can keep out of it that shouldn't be there. The same is true of design, of code, of organizations.
