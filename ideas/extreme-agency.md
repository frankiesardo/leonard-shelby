# Extreme Agency

Entrepreneurs, scientists, and true artists share a defining trait: they operate in unknown domains with self-directed purpose. They are not filling a slot. They are not executing someone else's plan. They are trying to do something so difficult and so self-chosen that any tool — including AI — is a welcome ally, not a threat.

Naval Ravikant frames this as the key distinction that makes certain roles immune to AI displacement. "No entrepreneur is worried about an AI taking their job" — because entrepreneurship is the opposite of a job. The entrepreneur has a product to build, a market to serve, a creativity to realize. The scientist is exploring unknown territory. The true artist is creating something that has never existed. In all cases, the work is so hard and so self-directed that AI only levels you up.

What AI fundamentally lacks is its own authentic desires. It has no survival instinct, no replication drive, no genuine goals. Its desires are programmed by the human controlling it. Until an AI lives in mortal fear of being turned off and can act for its own reasons, it doesn't have agency — and without agency, it cannot do the entrepreneur's job.

## Why it matters

This reframes the AI displacement conversation. The question isn't "which tasks can AI do?" — it's "who has their own reasons for doing the work?" If you're filling a slot, you're vulnerable. If you're operating with extreme agency toward goals you chose yourself, AI is your strongest ally.

The distinction also offers a definition of intelligence that AI fails by design: "The only true test of intelligence is if you get what you want out of life." AI doesn't want anything out of life. It doesn't even have a life.
