# Community Feeling

Adler's *Gemeinschaftsgefuhl* -- the sense that others are comrades, that you belong, that you have a place of refuge in the world. It is the goal of all interpersonal relationships in Adlerian psychology. Not a warm emotion but a fundamental orientation: seeing others as allies rather than threats, and shifting from "attachment to self" (self-interest) to "concern for others" (social interest).

Adler's definition of community is radical. It is not your family, workplace, or nation. It includes all of humanity, past and future, and even the universe itself. He acknowledged this was "an unattainable ideal" but insisted it was the correct compass heading. The practical benefit: when a small community's norms conflict with your values, you can appeal to the larger community. A toxic workplace can be endured or left because the workplace is not the whole world.

Community feeling rests on three pillars: self-acceptance (accepting yourself as you are), confidence in others (unconditional trust), and contribution to others (the guiding star). These form a virtuous circle: self-acceptance enables you to trust others without defensiveness, trust enables genuine contribution, and contribution reinforces your sense of belonging and self-acceptance.

## Why it matters

Community feeling is Adler's answer to the question "what fills the space after separation of tasks?" If you stop seeking recognition and stop intruding on others' tasks, you might feel isolated. Community feeling provides the positive orientation: you are not pulling away from others but reorienting your connection to them on healthier terms. The shift from self-interest to social interest is the shift from "what can others do for me?" to "what can I contribute?"

This is remarkably close to Frankl's [self-transcendence](self-transcendence.md) -- both insist that looking beyond the self toward others is the path to meaning and happiness. The difference is that Frankl frames it existentially (meaning), while Adler frames it socially (belonging).
