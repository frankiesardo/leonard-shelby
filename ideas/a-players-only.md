# A-Players Only

Steve Jobs believed that the difference between a great team and a mediocre one was not 2x -- it was 50x or 100x. A-players are people who are not just skilled but who have exacting standards, genuine passion for their craft, and the ego strength to work with other demanding people. The critical dynamic: A-players want to work with other A-players. If you let a few B-players onto the team, they hire C-players (because B-players feel threatened by A-players), and soon the culture degrades into what Jobs called "the bozo explosion."

The philosophy was behind Jobs's notorious management style. His brutal honesty, his willingness to call someone's work "shit" in front of the whole team, his snap judgments about who was brilliant and who was a bozo -- all served as a filter. People who couldn't handle the intensity left. People who could handle it were often pushed to produce the best work of their lives. "I've learned over the years that, when you have really good people, you don't have to baby them," Jobs said. "By expecting them to do great things, you can get them to do great things."

## Why it matters

The principle has a dark side that Isaacson documents extensively. Jobs's binary view of people (hero or shithead, often on the same day) was not just a management technique -- it was a character flaw that caused real suffering. Engineers broke down in tears. Talented people were fired capriciously. Relationships were poisoned by the casual cruelty.

But the results were undeniable. The original Macintosh team, the Pixar animation group, the iPhone team, Jony Ive's design studio -- each was a collection of extraordinary people who produced extraordinary work under extreme pressure. Jobs's lesson, stripped of its cruelty: mediocrity is contagious, and the single most important job of a leader is to maintain the quality of the team. Everything else -- strategy, execution, culture -- flows from who is in the room.

The hardest part is not hiring A-players. It is being willing to confront B-players -- including friends, loyal employees, and people who are "good enough" -- when they are not meeting the standard. Most leaders lack the stomach for this, and their organizations slowly fill with talent that is adequate but not exceptional.
