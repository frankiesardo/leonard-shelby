# The Discriminator Is Disposable

In a Generative Adversarial Network, two neural networks are locked in a loop. The generator makes. The discriminator judges. The generator improves because the discriminator is honest about what's wrong. But once the generator is good enough, the discriminator is removed. Its entire purpose was to train the generator into competence. Once that's achieved, the discriminator has no independent reason to exist.

The "taste thesis" — that the human role in an AI age is to select, curate, and judge — is asking you to be the discriminator. Machines will learn your taste. They will internalize your preferences. They will anticipate your selections faster than you can make them. The more refined your taste, the faster they learn it, and the sooner you are redundant. This is not a bug. This is how the architecture works.

## Why it matters

The analogy reframes the entire discourse about human value in an AI age. If your contribution is judgment — choosing between options, rating outputs, curating results — you are training your own replacement. The discriminator's purpose is self-elimination. The more clearly you articulate what "good" looks like, the less you are needed to say it.

The alternative is to be the patron, not the discriminator. To be in the room before the first brushstroke. To specify, provoke, fight with the machine in service of something that exceeds you both. That role — co-creation oriented toward the transcendent — is not learnable from your preferences because it is not preference at all. It is will.
