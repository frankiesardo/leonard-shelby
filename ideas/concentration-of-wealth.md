# Concentration of Wealth

The concentration of wealth is natural and inevitable, and is periodically alleviated by violent or peaceable partial redistribution. All economic history is the slow heartbeat of the social organism — a vast systole and diastole of concentrating wealth and compulsive recirculation.

The mechanism: since practical ability differs from person to person, the majority of abilities in nearly all societies is gathered in a minority of men. Wealth concentrates as a natural result of this concentration of ability. The rate accelerates with economic freedom — despotism may slow it; democracy, allowing the most liberty, speeds it up.

When concentration reaches a critical point — where the strength of numbers in the many poor rivals the strength of ability in the few rich — the unstable equilibrium breaks. History has met this moment two ways: legislation redistributing wealth (Solon's Athens, the New Deal) or revolution distributing poverty (Rome's civil wars, the French Revolution, Russia in 1917).

The Durants' key observation: Solon's peaceful reforms saved Athens from revolution. The Roman Senate's refusal to compromise produced a century of civil war. Redistribution by legislation is not merely more humane — it is more effective.

## Why it matters

This is not a left-wing or right-wing insight. It is a pattern that has repeated for five thousand years. Recognizing it doesn't tell you what to do about it — but ignoring it courts the same disasters that Athens, Rome, and France experienced. The question is whether a society can redistribute wisely enough, and early enough, to avoid the violent alternative.
