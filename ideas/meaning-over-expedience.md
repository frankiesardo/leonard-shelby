# Meaning Over Expedience

Expedience is doing what gets you through the moment: the easy lie, the avoided confrontation, the cookie instead of the workout. It is short-term, impulsive, and blind to consequences. Meaning is what emerges when those impulses are organized and subordinated to a higher aim -- when you sacrifice something now for something better later. Peterson argues that the discovery of sacrifice is the foundational human insight, encoded in the oldest stories we have.

The biblical narrative of Cain and Abel is, in Peterson's reading, a story about this distinction. Abel sacrifices the best of his flock and his offering is accepted -- things go well for him. Cain sacrifices reluctantly and poorly; his offering is rejected. Instead of examining his own sacrifice and improving it, Cain turns resentful and murders Abel. This is what happens when expedience meets failure: instead of self-correction, you get bitterness, blame, and destruction. The archetypal alternative is Christ in the desert, who refuses bread (immediate gratification), safety (false security), and political power (expedient domination) in favor of truth and voluntary suffering.

The practical implication: when you have to choose between what is easy and what you know would be right, choosing right is choosing meaning. This is not always heroic. It is often mundane -- telling the truth in a meeting, doing your taxes, having the hard conversation with your partner. But the cumulative effect of choosing meaning over expedience is a life that coheres. The cumulative effect of choosing expedience is a life that falls apart.

## Why it matters

This connects directly to Frankl's [will to meaning](will-to-meaning.md) but adds a sacrificial dimension. Frankl says meaning is the primary human motivation. Peterson says meaning is discovered *through* sacrifice -- through voluntarily giving up lesser goods for greater ones. The person who won't sacrifice anything will never find meaning. And the person who sacrifices resentfully, like Cain, is worse off than if they hadn't sacrificed at all.
