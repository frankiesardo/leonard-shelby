# The Dramatic Question

*Who is this person?* This is the single question that drives all story. It emerges at the ignition point -- the moment when unexpected change strikes the right character in the right way and they react in an unexpected, revealing manner. It is reasked every time the plot forces the protagonist to make a choice. Where the question is present, the reader is engaged. Where it is absent, they drift.

The question is so powerful because the protagonist themselves does not know the answer. The confabulating narrator in their head tells a self-serving story, but the real causes of their behavior lie in subconscious structures to which they have no access. Characters discover who they are moment by moment as the pressure of the drama is applied -- and they are often surprised by who they turn out to be. Every time you read "she heard herself say" or "he found himself doing," the dramatic question is at work.

The question operates on two levels. On the surface, the plot poses it through action and choice. Beneath the surface, the character's theory of control is being tested and rebuilt. The two levels interact: causes and effects ricochet between conscious drama and subconscious change. Shakespeare's genius was to obscure the answer by removing neat causal explanations (the "radical excision" technique), which deepened the mystery and left space for the audience to insert themselves.

## Why it matters

For writers, the diagnostic is simple: in any scene where the dramatic question is being asked and answered in a new way, the reader will be engaged. In any scene where it is not, they will be bored. Plot without character is light and sound. It is the plight of specific, flawed individuals that makes us cheer, weep, and ram our heads into the sofa cushion.
