# Seed Crystals

Seed crystals are people so good and so well-loved that they can almost single-handedly build large parts of your organization. Typically they're experienced leaders -- either managers of large teams or super individual contributors who everyone listens to. Once they join, a tidal wave of other awesome people follows. They create their own gravity.

The metaphor comes from chemistry. Drop a seed crystal into a supersaturated solution and it triggers rapid crystallization -- the whole solution organizes around that tiny nucleus. The same thing happens with hiring. One outstanding person attracts five more. Each of those attracts three more. Within months, an entire arm of the organization has materialized around that first hire.

At Nest, Fadell sought out seed crystals deliberately. They were the first people he and Matt Rogers recruited -- proven performers who were so respected in their fields that their decision to join Nest sent a signal: this company is worth your time. Bill Campbell, Fadell's mentor, looked around the early Nest office at the talent they'd assembled and just grinned.

## Why it matters

In the earliest days of a company, you don't have HR or recruiters. For the first twenty-five or so employees, it all comes down to you and your cofounder -- your vision, your network, your ability to convince people. You can't hire everyone yourself. But you can hire the people who will hire everyone else.

This makes your first hires disproportionately important. They set the culture, the standards, and the talent bar. A great seed crystal doesn't just fill a role -- they architect what that part of the organization becomes. A mediocre early hire does the opposite: they repel strong candidates and attract weaker ones, creating a downward spiral that's extremely hard to reverse.

The implication for founders: before you start a company, you should be able to write down a list of five names -- five people you would call first. If you don't have that list, you probably shouldn't be starting yet.
