# The Backwards Law

The more you pursue feeling better all the time, the less satisfied you become. Wanting a positive experience is itself a negative experience; accepting a negative experience is itself a positive experience. The term comes from the philosopher Alan Watts, and it captures a paradox that shows up across Stoicism, Buddhism, and modern psychology.

The mechanism is simple: pursuing something reinforces the fact that you lack it. The more desperately you want to be rich, the more poor and unworthy you feel. The more you chase happiness, the more you notice its absence. The more you try to be confident, the more your insecurity screams at you. It's like walking toward a house that keeps getting farther away.

This doesn't mean you should want nothing. It means the wanting itself is the trap. The people who end up achieving things are usually the ones who stopped fixating on the outcome and got absorbed in the process — who accepted the pain of the work rather than chasing the pleasure of the result.

## Why it matters

The Backwards Law is the engine behind most self-help failure. Affirmations, visualization exercises, "positive thinking" — all of these start from the premise that you are not enough as you are, and they remind you of that premise every time you use them. The person who truly feels confident has no need to stand in front of a mirror repeating that they're confident. They just are.

The practical move is counterintuitive: instead of asking "how do I feel good?" ask "what struggle am I willing to accept?" The pain you pursue willingly becomes manageable. The pain you flee from becomes your master.
