# Patronage as Co-Creation

For most of human history, the relationship between capital and artistic labor was so intimate that the two were functionally a single body. The patron did not select from finished works. He animated the work before it existed — specifying subject, materials, dimensions — and the maker pushed back with knowledge the patron did not have. The negotiation between them was the generative act. What emerged was a product of their shared labor, not of anyone's judgment.

The Florentine bottega, the medieval cathedral, Julius II and Michelangelo, Diaghilev and the Ballets Russes — the pattern is identical. Capital and labor locked together, oriented toward something beyond either of them (God, the city, the transcendent), making something neither could have produced alone.

This relationship broke when the modern art market separated the maker from the buyer. The collector replaced the patron. The gallery replaced the bottega. What was lost was friction — the argument, the being in the room, the shared orientation toward the transcendent. What remained was taste: the patron's function hollowed out into pure consumption.

## Why it matters

The distinction between patronage and taste maps directly onto the question of what humans are for in an age of AI. If the answer is taste — selecting, curating, judging — then humans are discriminators: the disposable half of the system. If the answer is patronage — specifying, provoking, fighting with the machine in service of something beyond yourself — then humans are co-creators. The patron was always in the room before the first brushstroke. The question is whether you'll be in the room with the model.
