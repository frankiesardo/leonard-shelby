# Good and Bad Values

Good values are: (1) reality-based / evidence-based, (2) socially constructive, and (3) immediate and controllable. Bad values are: (1) superstitious or socially destructive, (2) not immediate, and (3) not controllable. The distinction is the diagnostic tool for most of Manson's framework.

**Examples of good values:** honesty, innovation, curiosity, humility, creativity, charity, standing up for oneself and others. These are process-oriented — you never "complete" honesty. There's always another conversation that demands it.

**Examples of bad values:** dominance through manipulation, feeling good all the time, always being the center of attention, being liked by everybody, being right all the time. These are outcome-oriented, dependent on external validation, and produce a fragile sense of self that crumbles when reality doesn't cooperate.

Manson proposes five counterintuitive values built on this framework:
1. **Responsibility** — taking ownership of everything in your life regardless of fault
2. **Uncertainty** — acknowledging you're wrong about most things and staying curious
3. **Failure** — willingness to fail and use failure as data
4. **Rejection** — saying no and hearing no as necessary acts of commitment
5. **Mortality** — contemplating death to clarify what actually matters

## Why it matters

Your values are the lens through which every experience gets evaluated. The same event — losing a job, getting rejected, having a fight — can be a catastrophe or a growth opportunity depending on the metric you're using to measure it. Shitty values produce shitty problems. A man who values "being tall" as his metric for dating success has a problem he can never solve. A man who values "dating people who like me for who I am" has a problem he can work on every day.

This is why Manson says the real work is at the values level, not the behavior level. Trying to fix your behavior while running on bad values is like trying to navigate with a wrong map — no amount of effort fixes the direction.
