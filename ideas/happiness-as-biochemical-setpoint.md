# Happiness as Biochemical Setpoint

Biologists argue that our subjective well-being is determined mainly by internal biochemistry -- serotonin, dopamine, oxytocin -- not by external conditions like wealth, status, or historical era. Each person has a genetically determined happiness "setpoint," and while external events can temporarily push happiness above or below this baseline, the system always returns to equilibrium. Like an air-conditioning unit set to 25 degrees, it adjusts back regardless of the weather outside.

Harari's striking illustration: a medieval peasant who finishes building his mud hut and a modern banker who pays off his penthouse both experience roughly the same serotonin surge. The penthouse is objectively more comfortable, but the brain doesn't care about objective conditions -- it only measures the delta from baseline. Consequently, the banker is not one iota happier than his medieval ancestor.

If this is true, then the entire project of historical "progress" looks different. Millennia of technological advancement, political revolution, and economic growth may have rearranged the external furniture of civilization without changing how it feels to live in the house. The French Revolution, the Industrial Revolution, the Information Revolution -- none of them changed French, British, or American biochemistry. Those who won a cheerful brain in the genetic lottery were happy before, during, and after.

Harari presents this as one of three competing views of happiness -- alongside the progressive view (more capability = more happiness) and the Buddhist view (happiness is freedom from the chase for pleasant feelings). He does not endorse the biological view exclusively, but he takes it seriously enough to argue that historians must start asking whether their grand narratives of progress actually made anyone happier.

## Why it matters

This challenges the deepest assumption of modern civilization: that improving external conditions improves human experience. If the setpoint hypothesis is even partially true, then the pursuit of happiness through economic growth, technological progress, or political reform may be structurally misconceived -- a category error at civilizational scale. The question then becomes: if material progress does not reliably produce happiness, what does? The biological answer (pharmacology) is chilling. The Buddhist answer (stopping the chase) is demanding. Both deserve more attention than the progressive answer (more stuff) that currently dominates.
