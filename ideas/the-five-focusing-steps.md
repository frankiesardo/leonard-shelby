# The Five Focusing Steps

The systematic process for ongoing improvement within the Theory of Constraints. Deceptively simple. The difficulty is in the discipline, especially step 5.

1. **IDENTIFY** the system's constraint(s). Find the bottleneck — the resource limiting the system's throughput.

2. **EXPLOIT** the constraint. Squeeze every drop of capacity from it without spending money. No lunch breaks on the bottleneck. Quality inspection before the bottleneck, not after. Process only parts that contribute to immediate throughput. Don't waste bottleneck time on parts that will sit in inventory.

3. **SUBORDINATE** everything else to the above decision. The non-bottlenecks march to the bottleneck's drum. Release material only at the rate the bottleneck can consume it. Accept that non-bottleneck efficiency will drop. Red tags, green tags, priority systems — whatever it takes to keep the constraint fed and everything else in line.

4. **ELEVATE** the constraint. Now spend money if needed. Bring back old machines (the Zmegma). Outsource bottleneck work to vendors. Add shifts. Buy new equipment. Invest in the constraint's capacity.

5. **WARNING: If in the previous steps a constraint has been broken, go back to step 1. Do not allow INERTIA to cause a system's constraint.** This is the most important step and the one most often violated. When the constraint shifts — from the oven to material release to the market — the policies you created for the old constraint become the new constraint if you don't update them. The red and green tags that solved one problem created another. The finished-goods orders that kept the old bottleneck busy were unnecessary once the constraint moved to market demand. Inertia is the silent killer.

## Why it matters

The five steps are what make the Theory of Constraints a *process* rather than a one-time fix. Most improvement efforts stall because they solve yesterday's problem. The factory installs a new machine to eliminate a bottleneck, and the constraint moves to the market — but the factory keeps producing at full speed, building inventory nobody ordered.

Step 5 is the innovation. Every other improvement methodology tells you to "keep improving." The five focusing steps tell you *what* to improve and, crucially, *what to stop doing* when conditions change. The explicit warning against inertia is what separates this from generic continuous improvement.

In Rogo's plant, the team cycled through the five steps multiple times. First the bottleneck was the NCX-10 and the heat-treat oven. Then the constraint shifted to the material release system. Then to the market. Each time, the policies that solved the previous constraint threatened to become the new problem.
