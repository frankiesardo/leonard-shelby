# Materialise Labor, Capital, and Customers

Alex Rampell's internal framework for evaluating founders at a16z. The best founders can conjure resources from nothing: convince five people to follow them tomorrow for a 50% pay cut (labor), tell a story compelling enough to raise the next round (capital), and land their first five customers when they have zero track record (customers). Finding all three in one person is rare.

The Toast test: you walk into a restaurant with zero customers, a week of cash, and ask them to adopt your POS system. You have nothing. No social proof, no runway safety net, no track record. The founder who can make that sale — who can materialise a customer out of thin air — has a trait that's almost impossible to teach.

Rampell adds two qualifiers beyond the three materializations. First: study the history of the space. The best founders — Patrick Collison meeting the founder of Visa, Vlad at Robinhood studying brokerage history, Brian Chesky studying bed-and-breakfast history from the 1800s — have encyclopedic knowledge of prior attempts. Second: the Count of Monte Cristo motivation. The drive has to exceed money. Revenge, redemption, a chip on the shoulder — something that keeps you going when someone offers you $100 million.

## Why it matters

This is a people-first investment thesis. Rampell has become "100% convinced this is entirely about people. 100%." The framework gives three observable, testable signals — you can watch whether a founder actually materialises these resources, not just claims they could. It's predictive at every stage: seed, A, D, doesn't matter.
