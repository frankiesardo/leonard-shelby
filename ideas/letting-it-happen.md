# Letting It Happen

Letting it happen is not making it happen. It is not trying hard. It is not controlling your shots. It is giving Self 2 a clear image of what you want and then getting out of the way.

Gallwey demonstrates with Joan, a player who hits 8 out of 10 balls on the racket frame. He asks her to try hard to hit the center -- she improves slightly. He asks her to try to hit the frame -- she improves more. Then he asks her to focus on the seams of the ball, not try to hit it at all, and "let your racket contact the ball where it wants to." She hits 9 out of 10 dead center. Self 1 was occupied watching the seams; Self 2, finally unimpaired, proved to be excellent.

The method has three channels of communication with Self 2:

1. **Ask for results**: Give Self 2 a vivid image of *where* you want the ball to go, not *how* to hit it. Sally imagined the arc of the ball landing deep in the court. Without changing a single technical element consciously, her backswing lowered, her follow-through flattened, and she began flowing into the ball.

2. **Ask for form**: Show Self 2 the desired movement through visual and kinesthetic images. Hold the follow-through position and look at it. Swing in slow motion and feel it. Then let Self 2 imitate -- don't try to make it conform.

3. **Ask for qualities**: Role-play. Act as if you're a professional on a TV show. When you forget yourself and commit to the role, qualities you didn't know you had emerge. This isn't positive thinking ("I'm great") -- it's conscious exploration of your range.

The key word is *let*. You trust the competence of your body and its brain, and you let it swing the racket. Self 1 stays out of it.

## Why it matters

The instinct under pressure is to bear down, grip tighter, try harder. This is exactly backward -- it's Self 1 seizing the wheel, tightening muscles that should be loose, and interfering with the fluid coordination that Self 2 already possesses. The more important the moment, the more Self 1 wants control, and the worse the result.

The hardest part is that letting it happen doesn't feel like *you* did it. When the serve serves itself, Self 1 can't take credit. This threatens the ego. Many players revert to Self 1 control the next day, even though the results are worse -- because trying hard feels like they're in charge. The ego satisfaction of controlling outweighs the better results of trusting.

This insight applies everywhere: the writer who overwrites because he can't trust the reader. The manager who micromanages because she can't trust the team. The parent who over-instructs because they can't trust the child. In each case, the interference produces the very failure it's trying to prevent.
