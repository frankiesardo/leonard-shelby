# The Jump to Universality

Some systems undergo a sudden transition from being able to represent a limited range of things to being able to represent *anything* in their domain. This jump is always abrupt, always transformative, and always marks the beginning of open-ended progress.

Writing systems began as pictograms -- each symbol representing one specific thing. Eventually, alphabets emerged: small sets of symbols that could represent *any* word in a language. Number systems began as tally marks and graduated to positional notation (like our decimal system), which can represent *any* number. The genetic code, at some point in early evolution, became a universal constructor: capable of encoding the instructions for *any* viable organism, not just one specific type. And the human brain, at some point in prehistory, became a universal explainer: capable of understanding *anything* that is understandable.

The pattern is always the same. A system starts out parochial -- good at representing a few things. Variants accumulate. At some point, almost by accident, a variant crosses the threshold into universality. Once that happens, the system's reach becomes unbounded within its domain. Before the jump, progress is slow and incremental. After, it is explosive.

## Why it matters

The jump to universality is what makes the beginning of infinity possible. Parochial systems -- rules of thumb, pictograms, tally marks, instincts -- are bounded. They can be improved, but only within their narrow domain. Universal systems are unbounded. An alphabet can write any future thought. A universal computer can run any program. A universal explainer can understand any phenomenon.

This means that once the jump has been made, *all future progress is possible*. The capacity is there; it only needs to be exercised. The critical question for any system is whether it has made the jump or not. If it has, its potential is infinite. If it has not, its potential is capped by its parochial limitations.
