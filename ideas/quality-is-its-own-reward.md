# Quality Is Its Own Reward

Joe DiMaggio was asked how he managed to play so well so consistently. He said: "I always thought that there was at least one person in the stands who had never seen me play, and I didn't want to let him down."

That's the craftsman's ethic in one sentence. The standard isn't set by the market, the audience, or the competition -- it's set by you, internally, and it doesn't waver depending on who's watching. You do the work as well as you can because that's the only way you know how to do it. Not because someone's keeping score.

Zinsser inherited this from his father, a businessman who charged more for his product because he made it with the best ingredients. The old man had no interest in bargains. That attitude transferred directly to writing: the obsessive care over the smallest detail, the refusal to let anyone else lower the standard, the willingness to fight over a semicolon.

## Why it matters

In any competitive field, the final advantage isn't talent or luck -- it's caring more than the next person about doing the work right. Ninety percent of the edge comes from mastering the fundamentals. The remaining ten percent is the obsessive pride in small details that others dismiss as unimportant. That's where the gap opens.

The principle also protects you from the corrosive effects of working for external validation. Markets shift, editors change, trends cycle. If your standard is internal -- a bone-deep refusal to do less than your best -- you have something that doesn't depend on circumstances. You also produce work that people eventually recognize as reliable, even if it takes time.

The opposite is common and visible everywhere: people who calibrate their effort to the perceived audience. They cut corners when they think no one important is watching. But craft doesn't work that way. You can't turn it on and off. You either care about the details or you don't, and the work always tells the truth.
