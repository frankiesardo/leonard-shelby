# Truthseeking Groups

Individual debiasing through willpower doesn't work. You can't unsee the Muller-Lyer illusion by knowing about it. The solution is architectural: build a small group explicitly chartered to prioritize accuracy over comfort.

Annie Duke distills the charter to three elements: **accuracy focus** (we're here to get closer to truth, not to feel good), **accountability** (we'll call each other out, and accept being called out), and **diversity of viewpoints** (homogeneous groups amplify bias rather than correcting it).

Robert Merton's CUDOS norms, originally designed for scientific communities, provide the operational framework: **Communism** (share all relevant data — more is more), **Universalism** (evaluate ideas independent of who offered them), **Disinterestedness** (be vigilant against conflicts of interest, including letting outcome knowledge bias your evaluation of a decision), **Organized Skepticism** (build dissent into the process — devil's advocates, red teams, premortems).

## Why it matters

The group gets into your head. When you know you'll have to defend your reasoning to people committed to accuracy, you start vetting your own thinking more carefully even when you're alone. The social reward for accuracy reshapes the individual habit loop: instead of getting approval for confirming what the group already believes, you get approval for challenging it.

Erik Seidel's three-sentence charter for poker discussion is the gold standard of economy: "I don't want to hear bad luck stories. If you have a question about strategy, ask me all day. I don't see the purpose of a story about something outside your control." Three sentences that eliminate resulting, enforce process focus, and make the norms explicit.

The empirical evidence is strong. Federal judges — life-tenured, answerable to no one — vote more ideologically on homogeneous panels and more moderately on diverse ones. Scientists betting on whether studies would replicate beat traditional peer review accuracy (71% vs. 58%). Diversity and explicit stakes produce better epistemics.
