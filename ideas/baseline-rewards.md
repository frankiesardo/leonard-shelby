# Baseline Rewards

The starting point for any discussion of motivation is a simple fact: people have to earn a living. Salary, benefits, and basic perks are what Pink calls baseline rewards. If they're not adequate or equitable, nothing else matters. You won't get the predictability of extrinsic motivation or the power of intrinsic motivation. You'll get very little motivation at all.

But once baseline is met, the relationship between money and motivation changes fundamentally. Additional financial incentives don't boost creative performance -- they often impair it. The best use of money in Motivation 3.0 is to take the issue of money off the table, then get it out of sight.

Pink's "Zen of compensation" has three principles:

1. **Ensure internal and external fairness.** Pay people commensurately with colleagues (internal) and with others doing similar work elsewhere (external). Violations of either trigger largely irreversible motivation dips.

2. **Pay more than average.** Akerlof and Yellen (a future Nobel laureate and a future Fed chair) found that companies paying slightly above market attracted better talent, reduced turnover, and boosted morale. Higher wages could actually reduce a company's costs.

3. **If you use performance metrics, make them wide-ranging, relevant, and hard to game.** A single quarterly sales target invites shortcuts. Multiple metrics spanning different time horizons and dimensions of performance are harder to finagle and less likely to narrow focus.

## Why it matters

Most organizations get this backward. They try to motivate creative workers with elaborate bonus structures, stock options, and performance-based pay -- all of which are if-then rewards that trigger the [Sawyer Effect](the-sawyer-effect.md). The science says the opposite: for non-routine work, pay people well enough that they stop thinking about money, then let the work itself be the motivator.

This doesn't mean money doesn't matter. It means money is a threshold, not a lever. Jeff Gunther, who ran a ROWE at his software company, found that employees in a high-autonomy environment were far less likely to leave for a $10,000 or even $20,000 raise. The freedom to do great work was more valuable, and harder to match, than a pay increase.

The broader point: every dollar spent on an incentive plan for creative workers might be better spent raising base pay, improving working conditions, or giving people more autonomy. The incentive plan tries to motivate through money. The alternative motivates by removing money as a distraction.
