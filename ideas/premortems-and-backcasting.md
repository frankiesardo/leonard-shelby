# Premortems and Backcasting

Two complementary techniques for scenario planning. Both work by *starting at the end* and reasoning backward, but they look in opposite directions.

**Backcasting**: imagine you've achieved the goal. Now work backward — what had to happen for this to be true? What were the necessary conditions, milestones, lucky breaks, and prerequisite decisions? This generates a roadmap that standard forward-looking planning often misses because it starts from constraints rather than possibilities.

**Premortems**: imagine you've failed. The project is dead, the launch flopped, the relationship ended. Now ask: why? What went wrong? Gary Klein and others found that "prospective hindsight" — imagining an event has already occurred — increases correct identification of future outcomes by roughly 30%. The mechanism is that it gives permission to name risks that would feel like naysaying if raised in a forward-looking planning meeting.

## Why it matters

Used together, they map the probability space more completely than either alone. Backcasting generates the conditions for success; premortems surface the threats to it. The result is a more honest picture of the future that includes both the paths to the goal and the landmines along the way.

Premortems are especially valuable because they solve a social problem, not just a cognitive one. In a group that has committed to a plan, raising concerns feels like dissent. A premortem reframes dissent as a game: "We've already failed — your job is to explain why." Suddenly the person who names the risk isn't a naysayer but a team player doing the exercise correctly.

Gabriele Oettingen's research adds a counterintuitive finding: positive visualization *alone* actually impedes goal achievement. People who only imagine success are less likely to achieve it than those who also imagine obstacles (a practice she calls "mental contrasting"). The premortem is mental contrasting formalized.
