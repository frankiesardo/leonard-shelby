# Good Explanations over Predictions

Science progresses not by accumulating confirming observations but by seeking better explanations. A good explanation is one that is hard to vary without losing its explanatory power. The problem of induction -- how past observations can justify predictions about the future -- is solved: they cannot. What justifies reliance on a theory is not that it has been confirmed many times, but that it provides the best available explanation and its rivals have been refuted.

Deutsch, following Popper, argues that "only argument ever justifies anything -- tentatively, of course." The key distinction is between theories that contain unexplained qualifications (which are always bad explanations) and theories that are unified and explanatory. You can always construct an unrefuted theory that makes opposite predictions -- "gravity works as usual, except David floats" -- but such theories are bad because they introduce anomalies without explaining them. They spoil existing explanations without solving any problem.

This is why Deutsch insists that the multiverse is real: the many-worlds interpretation is the only explanation of quantum interference that does not contain an unexplained anomaly (like the Copenhagen interpretation's mysterious "collapse of the wave function"). Taking explanation seriously, rather than settling for prediction, is what forces you to accept the deep structure of reality.

## Why it matters

Most scientists and philosophers accept Popper's description of how science works (conjectures and refutations) but do not accept that this solves the "problem of induction." They remain crypto-inductivists -- people who accept that induction is invalid but believe that its invalidity is a problem. Deutsch argues this is because they underrate the role of explanation. Once you see that explanation, not prediction, is the engine of science, the "problem" dissolves. We are justified in relying on our best theories because they are the best explanations available, and any modification that does not improve the explanation is to be rejected.

This has immediate practical consequences: it means we should take our best theories seriously as descriptions of what is actually real, not merely as "useful fictions" or "instruments for prediction."
