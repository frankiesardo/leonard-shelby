# All Problems Are Interpersonal

Adler's structural claim: every psychological problem is, at root, an interpersonal relationship problem. This is not metaphor. Remove all other people from the universe, and loneliness vanishes, inferiority vanishes, rejection vanishes. Even "internal" struggles like low self-esteem require a social context -- you feel inadequate *compared to* someone, *in the eyes of* someone, *measured by* someone's standard.

Feelings of inferiority are universal and not inherently unhealthy -- they drive growth (the "pursuit of superiority"). But they go wrong in two ways. An *inferiority complex* turns the feeling into an excuse: "I can't succeed because I didn't go to university." A *superiority complex* turns it into a performance: status symbols, name-dropping, bragging -- all attempts to mask the underlying inferiority by impressing others. Both are interpersonal maneuvers.

Adler identifies three "life tasks" where these interpersonal problems play out: work (collaboration with others), friendship (trust without conditions), and love (the deepest form of interpersonal commitment). Avoiding these tasks through excuses is what Adler calls a "life-lie."

## Why it matters

If all problems are interpersonal, then all solutions are interpersonal too. You don't fix your psychology in isolation -- you fix it by changing how you relate to others. This is why [separation of tasks](separation-of-tasks.md) is so powerful: it gives you a tool for restructuring interpersonal dynamics rather than just analyzing your inner state.

The claim also explains why hermitism and avoidance don't solve anything. The recluse who retreats from the world still has interpersonal problems -- they just can't see them because they've eliminated the context that makes them visible.
