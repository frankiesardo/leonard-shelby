# Single-Threaded Ownership

"If you want to kill a plant, have two people water it." The plant either gets overwatered and dies, or not watered at all and dies. There is no third outcome. Every important initiative — especially cross-functional ones — needs a single Directly Responsible Individual (DRI) with enough authority to tell people in other divisions what to do, even if they don't formally own those people.

Halligan reports that every CEO at the "adults table" (companies over 100 employees) is a zealot about DRI discipline. At the "kids table" (under 100), everyone talks about it but it hasn't bitten them yet because coordination is still cheap. The inflection point is when departments form and cross-functional work becomes the norm rather than the exception. Everything important happens cross-functionally at scale.

## Why it matters

Committees diffuse accountability. When two people share responsibility, each assumes the other is handling it. The diffusion of responsibility is not a character flaw — it's a structural inevitability. The only fix is structural: assign one person's ass to the line.

The deeper insight is that shared ownership doesn't just halve the accountability — it can eliminate it. The plant metaphor is precise: the failure modes are overdoing it (because both people act) or underdoing it (because both people defer). Neither person has the full picture, and neither person bears the full consequence.

DRI discipline also accelerates decision-making. When one person owns the outcome, decisions get made faster because there's no committee to convene, no consensus to build, no "let me check with my co-owner." The DRI decides, acts, and is accountable for the result.
