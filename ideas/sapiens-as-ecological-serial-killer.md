# Sapiens as Ecological Serial Killer

The romantic image of pre-agricultural humans living in harmony with nature is a myth. Wherever *Homo sapiens* arrived, the megafauna promptly went extinct.

When Sapiens reached Australia around 45,000 years ago, 23 of 24 Australian animal species weighing over 50 kg went extinct within a few thousand years. When they crossed into the Americas around 14,000 years ago, North America lost 34 of 47 genera of large mammals; South America lost 50 of 60. The same pattern repeated on every large island Sapiens colonized: Madagascar (around 1,500 years ago), New Zealand (around 800 years ago), Hawaii, and thousands of smaller islands.

Three factors made Sapiens so devastating: (1) large animals breed slowly, so even modest hunting pressure can tip the balance from growth to decline; (2) unlike African and Asian megafauna, which co-evolved with hominids and learned to fear them, Australian and American animals had never encountered humans and had no instinctive wariness; (3) Sapiens used fire to reshape entire ecosystems, transforming forests into grasslands.

Harari identifies three waves of extinction: the First Wave accompanied the spread of foragers across the globe; the Second Wave accompanied the Agricultural Revolution (as habitats were converted to farmland); the Third Wave is the industrial extinction happening now. We are not new to ecological destruction. We have been the deadliest species on the planet since long before civilization.

## Why it matters

This punctures both the noble-savage narrative and the comforting belief that ecological destruction is a modern aberration we can reverse. The ecological crisis is not a deviation from human nature -- it is an expression of it, going back 45,000 years. The relevant question is not "how do we return to our natural harmony with the environment?" (we never had one) but "how do we manage an inherently destructive species?"
