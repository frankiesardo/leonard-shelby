# Make Your Own Pond

Ben Thompson's distillation of internet media strategy, but it generalizes to any business in a networked economy: don't compete in someone else's pond. Make your own pond and be the only fish in it.

The internet enables the creation of infinite small ponds. Before the internet, being a writer meant competing in one of a handful of large ponds — the Condé Nast pond, the newspaper pond. You survived by being good enough in someone else's hierarchy. The internet changed this: anyone can define a niche, own that niche, and serve an audience that spans the entire world. Thompson did this with tech media (the Stratechery pond), and his advice to aspiring content creators is: you don't want to be in a pond with me. Not because I'm going to beat you — but because you should define a different pond entirely, one where you're the authority.

The math: it's better to be the only expert in a small field than to be the 10th-best expert in a large one. A niche of 10,000 highly engaged people who trust you as *the* source is more valuable than 100,000 casual readers who see you as one voice among many.

## Why this works

Aggregation theory predicts that aggregators capture most of the value in large markets. The countermove is to operate at a scale the aggregator doesn't care about. When your pond is small enough, you're not worth aggregating — and yet if your pond is densely enough defined, you have all the leverage: your audience has no substitute for you. This is [differentiation even if worse](differentiate-even-if-worse.md) in geographic terms — you're not trying to be better than Thompson, you're occupying different territory.

The internet also removed the minimum viable scale problem. Previously, a niche audience of 5,000 people spread across 15 countries couldn't sustain a publication because distribution was local. Now, a niche of 5,000 people paying $100/year is a $500K business regardless of geography.

## AI extends this

Thompson's prediction: AI will multiply the number of people who can run very small, specialized businesses. SaaS assumed large teams who needed seat-based software. A one-person business or a three-person team doing something highly specialized doesn't need Salesforce — they roll their own, or they use AI to do it. The economic case for niche specialization gets stronger as AI raises the productivity ceiling for small operators.
