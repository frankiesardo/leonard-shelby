# Religion as Social Necessity

Even the skeptical historian develops a humble respect for religion, since he sees it functioning, and seemingly indispensable, in every land and age. Religion has many lives and a habit of resurrection.

The Durants catalog every major attempt to kill religion — Ikhnaton's Egypt, Buddha's India, the Greek Enlightenment, the French Revolution, the Communist experiment — and note that each time it came back. When Hébert and Chaumette established the atheistic worship of the Goddess of Reason in 1793, Robespierre replaced it with the Supreme Being within a year, and Napoleon signed a concordat with Pius VII eight years later. The rationalism of America's Founding Fathers gave way to a nineteenth-century religious revival.

The functional argument is blunt: "Heaven and utopia are buckets in a well: when one goes down the other goes up; when religion declines Communism grows." For since the natural inequality of men dooms many to poverty or defeat, some supernatural hope may be the sole alternative to despair. Destroy that hope, and class war is intensified.

There is no significant example in history, before our time, of a society successfully maintaining moral life without the aid of religion. The Durants leave the question open whether the modern secular experiment will be the first.

## Why it matters

This is not an argument for believing. It is an argument for understanding what religion *does* — independent of whether its claims are true. Even if you find religious claims untenable, the functions religion serves (consolation, moral discipline, social cohesion, meaning-making) don't disappear when the religion does. They simply get served by other things, or they don't get served at all. The Durants suspect the latter is more dangerous than most intellectuals realize.
