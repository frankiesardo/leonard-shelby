# Ulysses Contracts

A Ulysses contract is a decision made by a more rational present self to bind a less rational future self. Named for Odysseus, who ordered his crew to tie him to the mast and plug their ears with wax before sailing past the Sirens. He knew his future self would be unable to resist. So his present self made the decision in advance.

The concept is close to a [forcing function](forcing-functions.md) but has a crucial difference: it's *self-imposed* and *temporally structured*. A forcing function is designed into a system by an engineer. A Ulysses contract is designed into your own life by a version of you that has the clarity your future self will lack.

## Why it matters

The practical examples are everywhere once you see the pattern. Automatic retirement contributions (your present self opts in; your future self never has to resist spending the money). Loss limits in poker (set before the session starts, when you're calm). Setting a maximum bid before a house auction. Telling your friend "don't let me text my ex tonight." The decision swear jar — cataloguing phrases that signal you're off the truthseeking path ("I knew it," "I'm certain," "it always happens this way") and imposing a cost when you catch yourself using them.

The key property is the *decision interrupt*. Unlike a true physical constraint (you literally cannot open the childproof cap), a Ulysses contract can usually be broken. You can override the loss limit, raise your bid, send the text. But breaking the contract requires a conscious act — you have to stop and think "I made this rule when I was thinking clearly, and now I want to break it." That moment of friction is the intervention. Sometimes the friction is enough.
