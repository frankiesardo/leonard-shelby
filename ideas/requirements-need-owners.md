# Requirements Need Owners

Every requirement or constraint must be attached to a name, not a department. You can't interrogate a department — departments don't take responsibility, don't update their positions, and don't get embarrassed when their constraint turns out to be nonsense. A person does.

Musk's corollary: the person who put forward a requirement must agree that they own it. If they can't be named, the requirement should be treated as suspect. The glass mat story is the proof case — nobody owned the constraint, so nobody caught that the safety team and the NVH team had each invented a justification for the other team's requirement, and the actual answer was that neither justification was real.

The mechanism of organizational dysfunction this addresses: requirements accumulate, their originators leave, and the organization continues executing on constraints that were never valid and have no living champion. "An intern came up with this two years ago and doesn't work here anymore" is not an edge case — it's the default trajectory of undocumented requirements.

## Why it matters

Accountability without a name is theater. Requirements that belong to "the safety team" will survive long after anyone at the safety team remembers writing them. Requirements that belong to Sarah Chen in safety will get challenged the next time Sarah is in the room, or audited when Sarah leaves.

This is a direct application of [Candor as Survival Mechanism](candor-as-survival-mechanism.md) at the requirements level: the organization needs to be able to challenge constraints, and challenge requires a human to address.
