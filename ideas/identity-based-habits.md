# Identity-Based Habits

There are three layers at which behavior change can occur: outcomes (what you get), processes (what you do), and identity (what you believe). Most people start with outcomes -- "I want to lose twenty pounds" -- and work inward. The more effective direction runs from the inside out: decide who you want to be, then prove it to yourself with small wins.

The distinction is subtle but powerful. An outcome-based habit says "I want to quit smoking." An identity-based habit says "I'm not a smoker." The first is about willpower applied to a specific goal. The second is about becoming a different kind of person, for whom the temptation simply doesn't arise in the same way. Research confirms this: when people were asked "Are you a voter?" rather than "Will you vote?", turnout increased.

Your identity emerges from your habits. You are not born with preset beliefs about yourself. Every action is a vote for the type of person you wish to become. No single instance will transform your beliefs, but as the votes build up, the evidence accumulates and you start to believe the story. Write one page and you're casting a vote for being a writer. Show up at the gym and you're casting a vote for being an athlete. You don't need a unanimous verdict -- just a majority.

The word "identity" literally means "repeated beingness" (from the Latin *identitas* and *identidem*). You are what you repeatedly do. This is not a limitation -- it is the mechanism of change. The practical formula is: (1) decide the type of person you want to be, (2) prove it to yourself with small wins.

The flip side: identity can also trap you. "I'm not a math person." "I'm bad with technology." "I've always been terrible with directions." These beliefs become self-fulfilling. Changing them requires the same process in reverse -- accumulate enough counter-evidence through small actions, and the old belief loses its grip.
