# Writing is Rewriting

Rewriting is the essence of writing well — it's where the game is won or lost. The odds are close to 100 percent that your first draft wasn't born perfect. The newly hatched sentence almost always has something wrong with it: it's not clear, not logical, verbose, pretentious, or full of clutter. Clear writing is the result of a lot of tinkering.

Most people assume professionals don't need to rewrite; the words just fall into place. The opposite is true. Careful writers can't stop fiddling. Zinsser describes rewriting not as punishment but as a gift — "the most satisfying act in writing." He says: "I don't like to write; I like to have written. But I love to rewrite."

## Why it matters

The insight applies to any craft where the first attempt is raw material, not finished product. Software, design, music, argument-building — the initial version is a sketch. The real work is in revision: reshaping, tightening, refining, cutting what doesn't earn its place. People who resist revision often have an emotional equity in their first draft. They can't believe it wasn't born perfect. Letting go of that belief is the threshold of getting good.

Nobody told all the new computer-age writers that the essence of writing is rewriting. Just because something is written fluently doesn't mean it's written well. The word processor made good writers better (endless fussing became easy) and bad writers worse (their verbose sentences looked so pretty on the screen).
