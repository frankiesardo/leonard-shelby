# Abundance Mentality

The deep belief that there is enough success, recognition, profit, and love to go around. Its opposite — the Scarcity Mentality — sees life as a finite pie: if you get a bigger piece, there's less for me.

People with a Scarcity Mentality have a hard time sharing credit. They struggle to be genuinely happy for others' success. They hoard information, compete where cooperation would serve better, and treat every interaction as a zero-sum game. Covey observes that most people are deeply scripted in scarcity from childhood: conditional love ("if you perform, you're valued"), competitive grading ("your A means someone else got a C"), and win-lose athletics all reinforce the paradigm.

The Abundance Mentality flows from a deep sense of personal worth and security. When your identity doesn't depend on being better than someone else, you can celebrate their wins, share your best ideas, and genuinely pursue outcomes where everyone benefits. This is the paradigm shift that makes Win-Win possible — not as a technique, but as a natural expression of how you see the world.

## Why it matters

Win-Win fails without Abundance. A manager who says "let's collaborate" but internally tracks who gets credit is operating from Scarcity. A partner who says "I want you to succeed" but feels threatened by their spouse's promotion is operating from Scarcity. The rhetoric of cooperation layered over the paradigm of competition produces cynicism.

The Race to Bermuda story illustrates this perfectly: a company president talks cooperation while running an internal contest where one manager wins a trip. The system rewards Win-Lose while the words say Win-Win. You can't get the fruits of cooperation from the roots of competition.

Abundance is not naivety. It's the recognition that in interdependent reality — which is most of life — expanding the pie is almost always possible, and trying to do so produces better outcomes than fighting over a fixed one.
