# Style Is Organic

Style is not something you can buy at the style store and drape onto your work. It's organic to the person doing the work, as much a part of them as their hair. Trying to add style is like adding a toupee — at second glance, it doesn't look quite right. The problem isn't that it looks bad; it's that it doesn't look like *you*.

First you must strip your work down before you can build it back up. Master the fundamentals. Learn to hammer the nails. If what you build is sturdy and serviceable, take satisfaction in its plain strength. Style will emerge naturally from under the accumulated debris, growing more distinctive every day, as you relax into who you are.

The writers who try hardest to impress are the ones who lose what makes them unique. Readers — audiences of any kind — want genuineness. They can tell when someone is putting on airs. Therefore the fundamental rule is: be yourself.

## Why it matters

Authenticity is the one thing that can't be faked — in writing, in leadership, in any public-facing work. People who try to sound like someone they're not end up sounding like no one. The paradox is that the more you try to be distinctive, the more generic you become. Distinctiveness comes from the opposite direction: from relaxing into your own voice and trusting that it's enough.

This connects to a deep truth about craft. You can't skip the fundamentals and jump to the ornamental. Fancy prose built on weak grammar collapses. A flashy product built on poor architecture crumbles. First learn to build something solid. Then your personal style — the thing that makes your work yours — will emerge on its own.
