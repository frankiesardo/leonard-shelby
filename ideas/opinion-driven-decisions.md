# Opinion-Driven Decisions

Every decision has elements of data and opinion, but it is ultimately driven by one or the other. Data-driven decisions are those where you can acquire, study, and debate facts and numbers that lead to a fairly confident choice. Opinion-driven decisions require you to follow your gut and your vision, without sufficient data to guide you.

The critical skill is knowing which kind of decision you're facing. When creating something genuinely new -- a first-generation product, an untested market, a disruptive technology -- there is no data because nobody has done it before. Trying to turn an opinion-driven decision into a data-driven one leads to analysis paralysis: testing and retesting, running panel after panel, waiting for certainty that will never come. At Philips, Fadell's team spent millions on consumer panels that gave contradictory results. On Mondays the panel picked option X. On Fridays, option Y. The data wasn't a guide; it was cement shoes.

The answer is not to abandon data. It's to use data where it exists and to rely on insight, experience, and storytelling where it doesn't. As Ivy Ross put it: "It's not data or intuition; it's data and intuition." You use both. And sometimes the data can only take you so far.

## Why it matters

The failure mode is not making bad decisions. It's making no decisions. People who refuse to acknowledge that opinion-driven decisions exist will chase imaginary data into the abyss. They'll call in expensive consultants to offload responsibility. They'll demand proof that a baby can run a marathon before it has learned to walk.

When you do make an opinion-driven decision, explain your reasoning. Walk the team through what data you looked at, what insights you gathered, and why you ultimately made this choice. Take their input. Listen, don't react. Then give the speech: "I understand your position. Here are the points that make sense for our customers, here are the ones that don't. We have to keep moving and in this instance I have to follow my gut. Let's go." People will respect it even if they disagree, because they can see your reasoning and know they were heard.

For V1 of any product, the order is: vision first, customer insights second, data third. For V2, the order inverts: data first, customer insights second, vision third. Knowing when you've crossed from V1 territory to V2 territory is one of the most important judgment calls a leader makes.
