# Aggregation Theory

Ben Thompson's framework for understanding where power sits in internet-era markets. Before the internet, suppliers had power — a hotel chain, a newspaper, a TV network. The internet changed the marginal cost of distribution to near zero, which shifted power toward whoever controls *demand*. Aggregators win not by owning supply but by owning the relationship with end users. Booking.com is worth more than any hotel chain. Google is worth more than any newspaper. The pattern: whoever aggregates demand can extract rent from whoever supplies it.

The framework has a recursive structure. Booking.com aggregates hotels, but Google aggregates Booking.com. Power concentrates at the top of the aggregation stack — whoever controls the final user relationship. This is why Google's default search deal with Apple was the most important revenue line in tech for 20 years.

## How it updates for AI

The key question Thompson wrestles with: who are the AI-era aggregators? The naive answer is that ChatGPT and Claude are the new demand aggregators — everyone will route their information needs through AI, so AI companies have the user relationship. Thompson's skepticism: OpenAI's play looks like AOL (walled garden aggregator) while Google's play looks like early Google (make everyone capable, benefit from being at the front door of demand). The AOL approach failed because the internet got bigger than any walled garden. The question is whether AI becomes a new surface on which the old dynamics replay.

The advertising implication: the best AI ad product is not search ads (context-matched to query) but Meta-style ads (profile-matched to user). The aggregator doesn't need to put ads inside the conversation — they need to understand you deeply enough to manifest that understanding across all their properties.

## Why it matters

Aggregation theory is a lens that explains most of the last 30 years of tech power accumulation. Every platform war — app stores, social networks, search — is a fight over who gets to be the demand aggregator. Founders building in adjacent verticals need to ask: am I building on top of an aggregator's platform (fragile) or becoming an aggregator myself (durable)?
