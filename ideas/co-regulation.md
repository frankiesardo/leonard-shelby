# Co-regulation

Kids are born with all the feelings and none of the skills to manage them. The path from dysregulation (emotions overpower skills → tantrum) to self-regulation (I can handle this) runs through co-regulation — the child borrows the parent's calm, over and over, until enough calm lives in their own body to access independently.

The mechanism is almost physical. A four-year-old melting down on the floor sees their parent staying relatively calm. That calm transfers — not through words or instructions, but through the parent's regulated nervous system. Each episode deposits a small amount of regulation capacity in the child. After hundreds of repetitions, the child has enough stored calm to start regulating on their own.

The turbulence-and-pilot metaphor makes it vivid: you feel safe in storms eventually because someone stayed calm through your storms so many times that their calm became yours. If the pilot panics when you panic, you never learn that turbulence is survivable.

## Why it matters

Co-regulation never fully stops being needed. Adults still need it — that's why venting to a friend who says "I've been there too" makes you feel better even though nothing about your day actually changed. You absorbed some of their calm and perspective. The difference between a well-regulated adult and a poorly regulated one isn't the absence of dysregulation — it's the ratio of self-regulation to co-regulation, and whether they have people to co-regulate with when they need it.

This reframes the parent's job during a tantrum: it's not to stop the tantrum, it's to stay calm *during* it. The calm is the intervention. Every time you stay regulated while your kid falls apart, you're making a deposit.
