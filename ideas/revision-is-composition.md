# Revision Is Composition

There is no separate "drafting" phase. Making something and refining it are the same act. When you compose a sentence in your head, turn it over, swap a word, adjust the rhythm, and then write it down — that's simultaneously composition and revision. The distinction between the two is artificial and harmful.

The standard model says: first you create, then you fix. Creativity happens where the blank page begins; revision is secondary cleanup. This creates a false hierarchy that devalues the most generative part of the work. Klinkenborg inverts it: "Revise at the point of composition. Compose at the point of revision. Accept no provisional sentences."

## Why it matters

The draft-then-revise model encourages you to produce something mediocre quickly and then wrestle with it forever. Worse, it creates a tyranny of what already exists — once words are on the page, they acquire an inertia that makes them hard to discard, even when they should be discarded entirely. You end up trying to fix sentences using only the words they already contain, "as if it were a Rubik's Cube."

This idea is portable well beyond writing. Programmers who separate "getting it working" from "making it good" accumulate technical debt. Designers who sketch sloppily and refine later often find the sloppy sketch constrains them. In any making discipline, treating creation and refinement as a single continuous act means higher quality at every stage and fewer costly reworks.

The practical shift: don't write (or build, or design) anything you consider provisional. Bring each unit as close to its final state as you can before moving to the next. This sounds slower. With practice, it's faster — and the discoveries you make along the way are richer, because you're paying full attention to each piece rather than deferring judgment.
