# The Biological Lessons of History

The laws of biology are the fundamental lessons of history. The Durants distill them into three:

**Life is competition.** Competition is not only the life of trade — it is the trade of life: peaceful when food abounds, violent when the mouths outrun the food. Cooperation is real, and increases with social development, but mostly because it is a tool and form of competition. We cooperate in our group in order to strengthen our group against other groups. States, being ourselves multiplied, write our natures in bolder type.

**Life is selection.** In the struggle for existence some individuals are better equipped than others. Nature has not read the Declaration of Independence. We are all born unfree and unequal. Inequality is not only natural and inborn — it grows with the complexity of civilization. Every invention or discovery is made or seized by the exceptional individual, and makes the strong stronger, the weak relatively weaker.

**Life must breed.** Nature has a passion for quantity as prerequisite to the selection of quality. She is more interested in the species than in the individual, and makes little difference between civilization and barbarism. A nation with a low birth rate shall be periodically chastened by some more fertile group. If the human brood grows too numerous for the food supply, Nature has three agents for restoring the balance: famine, pestilence, and war.

## Why it matters

This is the Durants' foundation — everything else in the book rests on these three laws. The tension between freedom and equality grows from the second lesson. The concentration of wealth grows from the unequal distribution of ability. War persists because competition operates at every level, from individual to species. The unsentimental recognition of these biological facts is what separates the Durants' wisdom from wishful thinking.

But recognition is not endorsement. The Durants argue that acknowledging biology clears the ground for the *realistic* best: approximate equality of legal justice and educational opportunity, not the utopian kind that nature will always undermine.
