# Will to Meaning

The primary motivational force in human beings is not pleasure (Freud's will to pleasure) or power (Adler's will to power) but meaning. People need a reason to live -- a concrete purpose that makes sense of their specific existence at this specific moment. When that purpose is present, they can endure almost anything. When it is absent, even material comfort leaves them hollow.

Frankl called this "the will to meaning" and built an entire school of psychotherapy -- logotherapy -- around it. The claim is not that meaning is one motivation among many. It is the *primary* one. Pleasure and power are substitutes that rush in when meaning is frustrated, not the drivers themselves. A survey of Johns Hopkins students found that 78 percent rated "finding a purpose and meaning to my life" as their first goal; only 16 percent chose "making a lot of money."

Meaning is also not something you invent or impose. It is something you discover -- in the specific demands of a specific situation. "Ultimately, man should not ask what the meaning of his life is, but rather must recognize that it is *he* who is asked." Life poses the questions; you answer by how you live.

## Why it matters

The will to meaning has three concrete channels:
1. **Creating a work or doing a deed** -- accomplishment, contribution, building something
2. **Experiencing something or encountering someone** -- beauty, truth, nature, love
3. **The attitude taken toward unavoidable suffering** -- the most distinctly Franklian path

When none of these channels are open -- or when a person fails to recognize they are open -- the result is the [existential vacuum](the-existential-vacuum.md): boredom, emptiness, and eventually pathology.
