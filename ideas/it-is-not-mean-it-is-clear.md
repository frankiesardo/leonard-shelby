# It's Not Mean, It's Clear

A stranger at a dog park watched Kim Scott murmuring praise at her golden retriever while it ran into traffic. The stranger grabbed the dog by the collar and barked, "SIT!" The dog sat. Then she turned to Scott and said: "I can see you really love your dog. But that dog will die if you don't teach it to sit. It's not mean. It's clear."

The phrase became a management mantra. Most people confuse directness with cruelty because they've never experienced the combination of caring and challenge. In their experience, direct people are assholes and kind people are indirect. So when forced to choose, they choose "nice" -- and in doing so, they deprive the people around them of information they desperately need.

The distinction is precise: *mean* attacks the person; *clear* addresses the behavior or the work. "You're stupid" is mean. "Saying 'um' every third word makes you sound stupid -- and it's a shame because you're clearly smart" (Sheryl Sandberg's actual feedback to Scott) is clear. The second is harder to say, requires more care, and helps more.

## Why it matters

The conflation of clarity with cruelty is the engine that drives [Ruinous Empathy](ruinous-empathy.md). People don't stay silent because they're callous; they stay silent because they believe speaking up would be unkind. The dog park story breaks the frame: obviously, letting the dog run into traffic is not kind. Obviously, the stranger grabbing the dog and saying "sit" is not cruel. The metaphor makes the principle visceral in a way that abstract management advice never could.

Scott applies this to every level: performance reviews, real-time feedback, career conversations, and especially to the moment when you have to fire someone. In every case, the "nice" option -- vagueness, half-truths, delayed action -- causes more suffering than the direct one. The challenge is that the direct path is immediately uncomfortable, while the indirect path is immediately comfortable. Ruinous Empathy optimizes for the next five minutes. Radical Candor optimizes for the next five years.
