# Deeply Feeling Kids

A framework Dr. Becky Kennedy developed for a subset of kids who are more *porous* to the world — bigger pores on your skin means more comes in, more comes out, and everything is more intense. Not a clinical diagnosis. A temperament variation that explains why standard parenting strategies backfire for some kids.

Signs: zero-to-200 in seconds, animalistic meltdowns, calming down takes much longer than expected, they blame you for things that aren't your fault ("Why did you trip me?" — they tripped on the sidewalk), and — the signature move — they push you away in the exact moments they need you most. "GET OUT OF MY ROOM" during a meltdown doesn't mean they want you gone. It means the shame is too high for direct connection.

The critical insight: **for deeply feeling kids, vulnerability sits right next to shame.** When a DFK feels vulnerable (they trip, they mispronounce a word, they miss someone), they simultaneously feel shame about feeling so intensely. They're acutely aware that their reactions are bigger than other kids', and they worry that their intensity will overwhelm the adults they depend on. So they push the adults away first — a preemptive strike against the disconnection they fear is coming.

This is why traditional approaches make things worse. "I can see you're upset" is a front-door approach — it names the vulnerability directly, which activates the shame, which makes everything explode. Harsher discipline confirms the DFK's deepest fear: "I really am too much."

## Side-door strategies

DFKs need you to go *around* the shame, not through it:

- **Thumbs up/down game.** "I'm going to say something — probably wrong — and you give me thumbs up, sideways, or down." No eye contact required. DFKs love the power of rejecting.
- **Lead with something absurd first.** "I think you're upset because I promised to take you to the moon and the space shuttle wasn't ready." They reject it (push you away), which paradoxically lets them accept the next, real thing (let you in). DFKs need to titrate closeness.
- **Be present during meltdowns without intervening.** You can't say anything when the shame is that high. Your physical presence communicates: "I can handle you at your worst, and I'm not leaving."
- **"Did I ever tell you about the time..."** Share your own struggles from *outside* the heated moment. Side-door stories reduce shame because they're not about the kid.

## Why it matters

When you get around the bad behavior and the shame, DFKs are intensely joyful, funny, self-assured leaders. The same porosity that makes them volatile makes them deeply empathic and creatively alive. The goal isn't to dampen the intensity — it's to help them stop being terrorized by it.
