# The Sampling Period

Eventual elites typically devote less time early on to deliberate practice in the activity where they will eventually become experts. Instead, they undergo what researchers call a "sampling period" -- playing a variety of sports, trying different instruments, exploring unstructured environments. They gain a range of physical and cognitive proficiencies, learn about their own abilities and interests, and only later focus and ramp up technical practice.

Roger Federer played squash, basketball, handball, tennis, table tennis, badminton, soccer, and more. His parents were "pully" rather than pushy. He didn't focus on tennis until his teens. Tiger Woods is the exception, not the rule -- and the domains where Tiger-like early specialization works (golf, chess) are kind learning environments with repeating patterns and immediate feedback.

British Olympic programs began specifically recruiting adults to try new sports, creating pipelines for "slow bakers." Germany's 2014 World Cup winners were typically late specializers who played more unorganized soccer and other sports than their peers. A study of athletes in individual sports proclaimed "Late Specialization: the Key to Success."

## Why it matters

The pressure to specialize early is enormous and comes from every direction -- parents, coaches, schools, employers. But it is built on a false premise: that the most competitive, complicated world requires the earliest possible narrowing. The evidence says the opposite. Early sampling builds a broader base of physical and cognitive skills, enables better self-knowledge, and produces people who are more adaptable when conditions change.

This applies far beyond sports. Career researchers found that early career specializers jumped to an earnings lead, but later specializers made up the gap by finding work that better fit their skills and personalities. Technological inventors increased their creative impact by accumulating experience in different domains. The same pattern appears in artists, scientists, and entrepreneurs.

The implication is uncomfortable: the messy, meandering path that makes people feel "behind" is actually the more reliable route to excellence in complex domains.
