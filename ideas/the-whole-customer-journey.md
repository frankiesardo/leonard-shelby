# The Whole Customer Journey

Your product is not your product. Your product is the entire experience a customer has with your company -- from the moment they first hear about you to the moment they throw your thing away, return it, or delete it. Advertising, packaging, installation, usage, support, loyalty -- it is all one continuous chain, and the customer does not distinguish between the links. They experience it as a single thing: your brand.

Most makers fixate on the shiny object -- the device, the app, the physical thing they are building. They pour all their attention into making the product beautiful and functional, then treat everything else as an afterthought. But the product itself may be only a small fraction of what the customer actually experiences. At Nest, the thermostat occupied perhaps 10% of the customer journey. The app on your phone was 70%. The rest was installation, packaging, website, support articles, and energy reports.

The discipline is to prototype the entire journey, not just the artifact. Turn intangible touchpoints into tangible things you can hold, critique, and revise. Nest started with the packaging box -- before the thermostat was finished -- because the box forced them to articulate the product's value props, priority order, and messaging. They created detailed customer personas with names and faces. They tested installation and discovered people spent thirty minutes hunting for tools, so they included a screwdriver in every box. That screwdriver became a marketing asset that lived in kitchen drawers for years.

## Why it matters

The most common failure mode for product teams is building something technically excellent that fails at a touchpoint nobody thought about. The product works, but the onboarding is confusing. The app is brilliant, but customer support is a nightmare. The design is gorgeous, but nobody can find it in the store. Each gap between phases of the journey is a moment where the customer asks "why?" -- why should I care, why should I buy it, why should I keep using it. If you haven't prototyped and smoothed those transitions, customers stumble and fall.

This applies whether you make hardware, software, or services. It applies to B2B and B2C. The principle is universal: think about the human being taking the journey, step by step, and design every step with the same care you give to the product itself.
