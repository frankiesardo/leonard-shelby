# Newton's Laws of Emotion

A framework for how the Feeling Brain generates values, identity, and social structures, presented through the conceit of a "Parallel Universe Newton" who turned his genius toward the internal world rather than the external.

**First Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite emotional reaction.** Pain creates moral gaps -- a sense that something wrong has occurred and someone deserves to be made whole. These moral gaps generate emotions that push toward equalization (revenge, apology, reciprocity). This is the operating system of the Feeling Brain. All feelings are, at root, the desire to equalize across moral gaps: sadness is powerlessness to make up for a loss, anger is the desire to equalize through force, guilt is the feeling of deserving pain that never arrived.

**Second Law: Our self-worth equals the sum of our emotions over time.** When moral gaps persist and cannot be equalized, they normalize. If someone harms us and we cannot retaliate, the Feeling Brain eventually concludes we *deserved* it. This is the mechanism behind shame, low self-worth, and -- in the reverse direction -- narcissistic entitlement. High and low self-worth are two sides of the same coin: both involve imagining yourself as special, separate from the world.

**Third Law: Your identity will stay your identity until a new experience acts against it.** Values snowball over time, with early experiences forming the core. Childhood values are the hardest to dislodge because they have compounded for the longest. Change requires experiences that contradict existing values -- which is why growth always involves pain. There is no change without discomfort, no growth without grieving who you used to be.

**Emotional Gravity:** People who share similar values are pulled into each other's orbit, forming tribes and religions. Those with conflicting values repel each other. The stronger a value, the tighter its orbit and the more difficult it is for outside forces to disrupt it. This emotional gravity is the fundamental organizing principle of all human conflict and community.
