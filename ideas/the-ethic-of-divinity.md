# The Ethic of Divinity

The social world has two obvious dimensions: closeness (how near or far someone is) and hierarchy (who is above or below). But humans perceive a third dimension -- a vertical axis of sacredness, purity, or divinity. Whether or not God exists, people experience this dimension. They feel "lifted up" by acts of moral beauty (the emotion Haidt calls "elevation") and "brought down" by acts of degradation (disgust). The dimension runs from animals and bodily pollution at the bottom to gods, saints, and transcendence at the top.

Richard Shweder identified three ethical frameworks that cultures use: the **ethic of autonomy** (protecting individuals from harm, maximizing freedom), the **ethic of community** (protecting groups, valuing loyalty, obedience, and wise leadership), and the **ethic of divinity** (protecting the soul from degradation, valuing purity, sanctity, and holiness). Educated Western liberals rely almost exclusively on the ethic of autonomy. Other cultures, and religious conservatives in the West, draw heavily on all three.

Disgust is the guardian emotion of this third dimension. Originally evolved to protect the mouth from contaminated food, disgust expanded to guard the body in general (from impure sexual acts, skin violations, contact with death) and eventually to guard the "temple of the body" from moral contamination. This is why so many religious codes focus on food, sex, menstruation, and corpse-handling -- not for health reasons, but because these activities sit on the boundary between human and animal, between sacred and profane.

## Why it matters

Failing to perceive this third dimension creates a blind spot. If you think morality is only about harm and fairness (the ethic of autonomy), then you cannot understand why religious people care so much about purity, why they object to certain sexual practices between consenting adults, or why they feel that certain acts are simply *wrong* even when no one is harmed. You also cannot understand why secular people sometimes feel a thrill of elevation when witnessing extraordinary generosity, or why they feel disgust at acts that technically violate no one's rights. The dimension exists in all of us; some cultures and individuals simply suppress it.

This insight has direct political implications. The liberal-conservative divide is, in large part, a divide over which moral dimensions to prioritize. Liberals focus on autonomy and fairness. Conservatives add loyalty, authority, and sanctity. Neither side is crazy; each sees dimensions the other misses. Understanding this does not mean agreeing with both sides on every issue, but it does mean that the first step toward bridging the divide is seeing that your opponents are responding to real moral intuitions, not delusions.
