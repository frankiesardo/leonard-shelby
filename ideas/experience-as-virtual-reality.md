# Experience as Virtual Reality

We never experience reality directly. Our brains are locked in dark, silent skulls and construct a virtual-reality rendering from sensory data plus complex inborn and acquired theories about how to interpret them. What we experience as "the world out there" is a generated environment -- manufactured for us by unconscious neural processes. Mirages, optical illusions, the apparent stillness of the Earth, our experience of living in a single universe when there are many -- all are artifacts of this rendering.

Deutsch does not treat this as a limitation or a reason for skepticism. It is a feature, not a bug. All reasoning, all thinking, and all external experience are forms of virtual reality. The ecological niche of the human species is virtual reality -- it is the characteristic means by which we survive, as fundamentally as eucalyptus leaves define the koala's niche. Imagination is a straightforward form of VR. Science is VR tested against reality. Mathematics is VR rendering of abstract environments whose "physics" embody mathematical properties.

The key move is to unify these: a flight simulator, a scientific theory, a dream, a mathematical proof, and ordinary perception are all instances of the same underlying process -- the physical embodying of a theory about an environment.

## Why it matters

This reframes epistemology. If all experience is virtual reality, then the question "How do we know anything about the real world?" becomes "How do our virtual-reality renderings manage to correspond to the external reality they are meant to depict?" The answer is: through conjecture and criticism, the same process Popper described for science. Our inborn perceptual theories (evolved by natural selection) are tested against reality every moment, and updated when predictions fail. Science extends this process deliberately.

It also means there is no fundamental boundary between "science" and "imagination." Both are VR renderings. The difference is that science demands its renderings be testable against the external environment, while art and fiction do not. But the underlying cognitive machinery is the same.
