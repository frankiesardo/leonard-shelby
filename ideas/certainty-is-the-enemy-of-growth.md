# Certainty Is the Enemy of Growth

We never go from "wrong" to "right." We go from wrong to slightly less wrong, then to slightly less wrong than that. Growth is an endlessly iterative process of approaching truth without ever reaching it. The person who insists on being "right" before acting will never act. The person who clings to a belief system because it gives them certainty will never update it.

Our brains are meaning machines. Give people a room with random buttons and random lights, and within fifteen minutes each person will have invented a specific sequence they're convinced produces the results. They're all wrong. But they're all certain.

**Manson's Law of Avoidance:** the more something threatens your identity, the more you will avoid it. The woman who won't go on dates because it would challenge her belief that she's undesirable. The man who won't ask for a promotion because it would challenge his belief about what his skills are worth. They prefer the painful certainty of not knowing to the terrifying possibility of finding out.

The practical move: instead of seeking certainty, seek doubt. Doubt your beliefs, your feelings, your assumptions about the future. Being wrong is not a threat to your identity — it's a prerequisite for growth.

## Why it matters

This connects to identity in a deep way. When our values and beliefs become fused with our identity ("I am a victim," "I am a genius," "I am unlovable"), any evidence against those beliefs feels like an existential threat. We don't just resist new information — we experience it as an attack on our self. The solution isn't to have no identity, but to hold identity loosely. Be willing to be wrong about who you are.

Viewed as a scientific process: your values are hypotheses, your actions are experiments, your emotions and thought patterns are data. If the data contradicts the hypothesis, you don't get to ignore the data.
