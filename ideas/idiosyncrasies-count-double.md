# Idiosyncrasies Count Double

When Ogilvy won the Jaguar account in the 1990s, the creative director told the engineers: preserve the slightly odd things. In a Jaguar, you turned on the ceiling light with a switch on the central console — not by reaching up like every other car. Jaguar was planning to eliminate this inconsistency. Ogilvy's response: that's your brand. Keep it.

Rolls-Royce had a floor pedal to dip headlamps. Veuve Clicquot's yellow label was a printing error — they sent the "wrong" bottles to England, and the English loved them so much the yellow became the entire brand identity. MCI's Friends & Family plan generated far more engagement than a simple price cut because nominating your 10 most-called numbers was slightly weird, slightly frictional, slightly personal. People talked about it. Comedians did routines about it.

The test: if a stand-up comedian could do a routine about your product, you're onto something. The things that create "a splinter on the attention" — something slightly raised from the normal shape — are the things people notice, remember, and tell stories about.

## Why it matters

The engineering instinct is to smooth out inconsistencies, optimize away friction, and converge toward the norm. This instinct is often right for functionality and often wrong for memorability. Distinctiveness lives in the imperfections. A perfectly optimized product that behaves exactly like every competitor is invisible. A product with one slightly odd feature that makes no engineering sense but creates a story — that's the one people remember.

This doesn't mean adding weirdness for its own sake. It means recognizing when your product already has something distinctive and resisting the urge to eliminate it. The Jaguar console switch, the Veuve Clicquot yellow, the MCI nomination ritual — none of these were planned. They emerged. The skill is not in creating idiosyncrasy but in recognizing and protecting it.

There's a parallel in writing: Klinkenborg and Zinsser both argue that a writer's voice emerges from the sentences they don't smooth out, the constructions that are slightly odd but unmistakably theirs. Products have voice too.
