# Get on the Plane

If a subject interests you, go after it. It's not going to come looking for you.

Zinsser uses "get on the plane" as a personal exhortation for the moment when curiosity has struck but action feels uncertain. He went to Shanghai with two jazz musicians who had no assurance they'd be allowed to play. He went to Timbuktu to find a camel caravan that was an even bet not to materialize. He went to spring training not knowing if he'd be welcomed or rebuffed. In every case, the outcome was uncertain and the investment was real -- time, money, comfort, pride. In every case, what he found was some of his best work.

The principle isn't recklessness. It's the recognition that the interesting work lives on the other side of uncertainty, and the cost of not going is always higher than the cost of a wasted trip. A little nervousness gives the work an edge.

## Why it matters

Most good work begins with an act of pursuit that could easily be talked out of. The reasons not to go are always rational: it might not work out, you're not qualified, someone else could do it better, the timing isn't right. These reasons are also, almost always, wrong. They're the voice of comfort masquerading as prudence.

The principle applies well beyond travel. Getting on the plane means making the phone call, starting the conversation, writing the first draft, signing up for the class, sending the cold email, attending the event where you know no one. It's the bias toward action when curiosity has been activated.

Zinsser sums it up without qualification: "Decide what you want to do. Then decide to do it. Then do it."
