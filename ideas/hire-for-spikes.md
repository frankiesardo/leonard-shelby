# Hire for Spikes

When eight interviewers score a candidate, the instinct is to hire the person everyone rates a 3 out of 4 — consistently good, no red flags. HubSpot learned to resist this instinct and instead hire the person who gets four 4s and four 2s — the spiky candidate with clear weaknesses but unmistakable strengths.

The principle extends beyond individual hires to team composition. The 2004 Red Sox won after 86 years by mixing homegrown talent with a few expensive free agents — not by building a roster of uniformly good players. The Grateful Dead created an entirely new genre of music by combining a bluegrass banjo player, a country singer, an avant-garde jazz trombonist, a blues harmonica player, and a marching band drummer. Spikiest of spiky teams.

## Why it matters

Uniformly competent people are easy to manage and hard to differentiate with. They produce adequate work. They keep things running. But they rarely create something genuinely new, because creating something new requires extreme strength in one direction — which almost always comes with weakness in another.

The deeper hiring insight: people from big companies almost never work at startups. HubSpot had a "100% attrition rate" on hires from Salesforce, Google, and Microsoft. The impedance mismatch is too large — big-company people expect systems and processes that don't exist at 50 or 500 employees. The corollary: don't underestimate your homegrown talent. People who've been in the trenches with you often outperform the shiny external hire.
