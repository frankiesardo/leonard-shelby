# Taste as Competitive Advantage

Steve Jobs's most devastating critique of Microsoft was not about their technology -- it was about their taste. "The only problem with Microsoft is they just have no taste. They have absolutely no taste." This was not aesthetic snobbery. It was a strategic observation: taste is the ability to know what to leave out, how things should feel, and what makes the difference between something people tolerate and something they love. In Jobs's view, most technology companies failed not because they lacked engineering talent but because they lacked people who could tell the difference between good and great.

Jobs's father, Paul, a machinist and car restorer, taught him the foundational lesson: "When you're a carpenter making a beautiful chest of drawers, you're not going to use a piece of plywood on the back, even though it faces the wall and nobody will ever see it. You'll know it's there, so you're going to use a beautiful piece of wood on the back." This ethic -- caring about the unseen parts -- became Apple's defining characteristic. The original Macintosh team signed their names inside the case, where no user would ever see them. The iPod's circuit board was beautiful even though it was hidden inside a sealed enclosure.

## Why it matters

Taste is not something you add at the end of the engineering process. It is a lens through which every decision is made from the beginning. Jobs understood that great design is not decoration -- it is the elimination of everything that distracts from the essential function and feeling of a product. The iPod Shuffle had no screen. The iPhone had one button. The iPad had no USB ports, no file system, no visible complexity. Each of these decisions was controversial inside Apple and ridiculed outside it. Each turned out to be right.

The harder lesson is that taste cannot be hired. You can hire talented designers and engineers, but unless the people making decisions -- especially the final decisions -- have taste, the product will be a committee compromise. This is why Jobs insisted on being involved in every detail. "Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works."
