# The Life-Lie

A concept Peterson inherits from Alfred Adler and extends through Solzhenitsyn: the life-lie is any habitual falsification of reality through which a person avoids confronting what they know to be true. It is not always a lie of commission. More often it is a lie of omission -- the conflict you never address, the ambition you never pursue, the truth you never speak because speaking it would be uncomfortable. You let things slide, and each time the slide gets easier.

The corrosion is gradual and compounding. "If you betray yourself, if you say untrue things, if you act out a lie, you weaken your character." A person living a life-lie becomes unable to distinguish what they genuinely believe from what they merely pretend. Their internal map of reality drifts further from reality itself, until a crisis arrives and they have no reliable map at all. Peterson uses Milton's Satan as the archetype: a being who decides that it is "better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven," then convinces himself that everything is fine. Each subsequent decision builds on the corrupted foundation of the first lie.

The political dimension is Solzhenitsyn's contribution: individual lies aggregate into systemic lies. A society in which individuals routinely falsify their speech and actions to avoid discomfort is a society that will accept and sustain totalitarian lies. The gulag was built one small cowardice at a time. "The lie is the root of all tyranny, both personal and political."

## Why it matters

The antidote is not heroic truth-telling but something simpler and harder: stop saying things you know to be false. Not "speak the whole truth at all times," which is impossible. Just: stop lying. Notice when you are about to shade the truth for convenience, and don't. This is Peterson's minimum viable ethics. It won't make you a saint, but it will keep you from becoming the kind of person whose character dissolves under pressure.
