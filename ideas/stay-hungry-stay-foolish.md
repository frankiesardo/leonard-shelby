# Stay Hungry, Stay Foolish

The farewell message on the back cover of the final *Whole Earth Catalog*, Stewart Brand's counterculture bible. Beneath a photograph of an early morning country road — "the kind you might find yourself hitchhiking on if you were so adventurous" — were the words: *Stay Hungry. Stay Foolish.* Steve Jobs encountered it in the mid-1970s and adopted it as a personal mantra. He closed his 2005 Stanford commencement address by wishing it for the graduating class.

"Hungry" means never arriving. Not the hunger of deprivation but the hunger of appetite — the refusal to be satisfied with what you already know, what you've already built, who you've already become. It's the antidote to the complacency that success breeds.

"Foolish" means willingness to look stupid. To take the calligraphy class that has no practical application. To start a company after getting fired from the one you founded. To follow your intuition when everyone around you is following the curriculum. Foolishness is the behavioral expression of beginner's mind — the deliberate choice to act before you have permission, credentials, or certainty.

Together they form a compact philosophy: stay unsatisfied enough to keep moving, and stay naive enough to try things that experts would dismiss.

## Why it matters

The opposite of "Stay Hungry, Stay Foolish" is "Stay Comfortable, Stay Credible" — which is the default mode of anyone who has achieved enough to have something to protect. Jobs's career is the case study: after being fired from Apple at 30, the lightness of having nothing to lose freed him to start NeXT, buy Pixar, and eventually return to build the most valuable company on earth. The hunger and foolishness were not incidental to the comeback — they were the mechanism.
