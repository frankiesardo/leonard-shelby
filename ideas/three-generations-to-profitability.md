# Three Generations to Profitability

It takes at least three generations of a new, disruptive product before you get it right and turn a profit. The joke is that it takes twenty years to make an overnight success. In business, it's more like six to ten.

**V1: Make the product.** You're essentially shipping your prototype to early adopters who will buy anything new and cool, fully aware it will be buggy. Customer acquisition costs are sky-high. Features are missing. Marketing, sales, and support are all a little wonky. You're still discovering everything you got wrong. The goal is product/market fit -- proving that at least early adopters will buy it. If they won't, go back to the drawing board.

**V2: Fix the product.** You know what the problems are and how to fix them. V2 usually comes swiftly after V1 because you've learned so much so fast. You widen the market to the early majority. You start making a little money per product -- gross margins -- but probably not enough to cover business costs. You bring more functions in-house, start building real expertise.

**V3: Build the business.** Your focus shifts from the product to the business. You negotiate better deals with partners, optimize customer support and sales channels, reduce prices, and aim for net margins. The product is already great; now you make the company sustainable.

The pattern is invariant: make it, fix it, build it. The iPod, iPhone, and Nest Learning Thermostat all followed it exactly.

## Why it matters

Far too many people -- founders, investors, executives at big companies -- expect profitability from day one. They demand proof that the unit and business economics of a brand-new product are sound before it ships. But that's asking a baby to run a marathon. V1 will never be profitable. Attempting to force it will either kill the product or warp it into something safe and evolutionary instead of disruptive.

The pattern also applies to what you outsource vs. build in-house. V1 requires outsourcing everything (marketing, PR, HR, legal) because the team is tiny. V2 starts bringing functions in-house. V3 locks in internal expertise and selectively outsources smaller projects. Trying to build everything in-house for V1 is a recipe for paralysis.

Customers need time too. The vast majority aren't early adopters. They need time to read reviews, ask friends, and wait for the next version. Customer adoption and product maturity move in lockstep, and neither can be rushed.
