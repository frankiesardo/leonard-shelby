# Desirable Difficulties

Obstacles that make learning more challenging, slower, and more frustrating in the short term, but better in the long term. The term comes from psychologist Robert Bjork, and the research behind it is among the most rigorously supported in all of learning science.

Three key desirable difficulties:

**The generation effect:** Struggling to produce an answer, even a wrong one, enhances subsequent learning. Students who tried to guess vocabulary definitions before seeing them performed far better on later tests than students who were given definitions upfront. The "hypercorrection effect" makes this even stranger: the more confident you are in a wrong answer, the better you learn the right one. Tolerating big mistakes creates the best learning opportunities.

**Spacing:** Leaving time between practice sessions for the same material. Spanish vocabulary learners tested a month after learning retained 250% more eight years later than those tested the same day. The harder it is to retrieve, the more you learn. Short-term rehearsal gives purely short-term benefits.

**Interleaving:** Mixing problem types during practice rather than blocking them. Students who practice one type of math problem at a time perform better during practice but worse on tests. Mixed practice forces the learner to identify the type of problem before selecting a strategy -- the most important step in real-world problem solving.

In a devastating demonstration, monkeys trained with automatic hints got a lot of lists right during practice and almost none right on test day. Monkeys trained with no hints stumbled through practice but retained far more. "Training with hints did not produce any lasting learning."

## Why it matters

Almost everything about how modern education is structured subverts desirable difficulties. Topics are confined to single weeks. Teachers give hints to keep students moving. Parents show kids the "faster, easier way." These practices maximize the appearance of learning while minimizing the reality of it. "If you are doing too well when you test yourself, the simple antidote is to wait longer before practicing the same material again."

This connects to a deep cultural bias: we equate ease with learning and struggle with failure. But for knowledge that is both durable (it sticks) and flexible (it can be applied broadly), "fast and easy is precisely the problem." The most effective learning looks inefficient. It looks like falling behind.

The U.S. Department of Education identified spacing, testing, and making-connections questions as learning strategies with genuine scientific backing. All three impair short-term performance.
