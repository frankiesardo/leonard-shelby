# Theory of Control

The brain's ultimate mission is control. It perceives the physical environment and the people around it in order to control them -- to get what it wants. A person's "theory of control" is the vast, intricate web of beliefs about how the world works that enables them to navigate it. These beliefs are the building blocks of our hallucinated model of reality. Taken together, they tell us: *this is how you open a stuck jar; this is how you lie to a police officer; this is how you behave if you want your boss to think you're sane.*

In storytelling, a character's theory of control is the defining architecture of who they are. It dictates their automatic response to unexpected change, their default strategy for manipulating other people, and the life they've built. The butler Stevens controls through emotional restraint. Kane controls through the appearance of selfless generosity. Lawrence controls through vain rebellion. When a story begins, the protagonist's theory of control is established. Then the plot systematically tests it, breaks it, and forces the character to rebuild.

The theory of control has a dual function that is critical for plot structure: it tells the character both how to *get* what they want and how to *avoid* what they dread. When a protagonist abandons their old theory (typically at the story's midpoint), the protection it offered vanishes and their deepest fears are unleashed. This is the engineering genius of the five-act structure -- the ratcheting of drama at exactly the moment when the audience risks getting bored.

## Why it matters

This concept explains why changing who we are is so difficult. Our beliefs are not ideas we hold; they are the building blocks of the world we perceive. Correcting a flaw means first managing to see it (we literally cannot, because it is part of our perception), then breaking down the structure of our reality and rebuilding it. The brain treats threats to the theory of control with the same fight-or-flight response it deploys against physical attack.
