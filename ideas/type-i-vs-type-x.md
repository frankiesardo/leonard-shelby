# Type I vs Type X

Pink's central framework. Type X behavior is fueled by extrinsic desires -- it cares less about the inherent satisfaction of an activity and more about the external rewards it leads to. Type I behavior is fueled by intrinsic desires -- it cares less about external rewards and more about the inherent satisfaction of the activity itself.

The naming pays homage to McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y, and to Friedman and Rosenman's Type A and Type B. The key properties of Type I behavior:

- **Made, not born.** These are proclivities shaped by circumstance, not fixed traits. Any Type X can become a Type I.
- **Outperforms Type X in the long run.** Type X can produce fast short-term results through intense focus on extrinsic rewards. But the approach is unsustainable and doesn't build mastery.
- **Doesn't disdain money.** Type I's care about fair compensation -- but once pay is adequate, money fades as a motivator. For Type X's, money is the table.
- **A renewable resource.** Type X is like coal: effective but finite, with nasty externalities. Type I is like the sun: inexpensive, clean, endlessly renewable.
- **Promotes well-being.** People oriented toward autonomy and intrinsic motivation have higher self-esteem, better relationships, and greater general well-being. People with Type X aspirations (money, fame, beauty) show poorer psychological health.

Type I depends on three nutrients: autonomy (self-direction), mastery (getting better at what matters), and purpose (connecting to something larger).

## Why it matters

The Type I / Type X distinction is a lens for evaluating almost any system. Is this incentive plan fostering Type I or Type X? Is this school cultivating intrinsic curiosity or extrinsic compliance? Is this management approach treating people as players or pawns?

Deci and Ryan's self-determination theory provides the scientific foundation. Human beings have three innate psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. When satisfied, we're motivated, productive, and happy. When thwarted, we wilt. The main mechanisms of Motivation 2.0 -- surveillance, if-then rewards, imposed goals -- are more stifling than supportive.

The University of Rochester study is the capstone. Graduates with "profit goals" (wealth, fame) who achieved those goals were no happier than in college and showed increases in anxiety and depression. Graduates with "purpose goals" (helping others, learning) who achieved them reported higher satisfaction and well-being. Even when you get what you want, it's not always what you need. Attaining the wrong goals makes you worse off, not better.
