# Fame as Moat

Rory Sutherland's framing: "Having a great brand means you get to play the game of capitalism on easy mode." Fame — genuine public recognition and trust in a brand — is a structural competitive advantage that compounds over time. It's not vanity. It's not marketing spend for its own sake. It's the difference between being automatically included in the consideration set versus having to earn your place through argument.

A famous brand doesn't need to win every comparison. People don't comparison-shop it the same way they comparison-shop an unknown. They buy it to reduce uncertainty. The famous brand is the default choice when choosing feels risky — when the downside of a bad choice (social embarrassment, product failure, regret) matters more than the upside of a slightly better deal.

This maps to signaling theory: advertising is often not informative (the ad doesn't tell you anything new about the product) but it is a *signal* — expensive, visible investment is itself evidence of brand seriousness. A brand that can afford to run consistent advertising for years is implicitly proving it hasn't failed yet.

## Why it matters

The compounding nature of fame is why Sutherland advises founders to start building brand signals from day one, not after product-market fit. Fame is slow to build and slow to destroy — the delay in both directions means you're always working ahead of or behind where you think you are. Companies that defer brand investment until "later" often find there's no later; they've optimized their way into commodity.

The counterintuitive implication: sometimes it's worth doing something slightly less rational economically if it builds fame faster. Durex handed out condoms at the Olympics. Red Bull put a man in space. These investments make no sense if you model advertising as information transmission. They make perfect sense if you model advertising as fame-building: a massive, one-time, memorable event that buys years of brand recognition.
