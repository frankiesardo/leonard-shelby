# Unity of Form

Before you start writing, choose your unities and stick to them. Unity of pronoun (first person or third?), unity of tense (past or present?), unity of mood (casual or formal?), unity of scope (what one corner of the subject will you cover?). These decisions are your anchor. Without them, your material controls you instead of the other way around.

Every successful piece should leave the reader with one provocative thought — not two, not five, just one. Decide what single point you want to make. It will affect your route, your destination, your tone.

Think small. Decide what corner of your subject you're going to bite off, and be content to cover it well and stop. Nobody can write a book "about" something. Tolstoy couldn't write a book about war and peace. He made certain reductive decisions about time and place and individual characters — one man pursuing one whale. Every project must be reduced before you start.

## Why it matters

This is a principle of constraint as a creative force. It applies to any act of making. A product with a clear scope ships; one that tries to be everything stalls. A talk with one thesis persuades; one with five confuses. An architect who knows the constraints of the site designs a better building than one who has infinite freedom.

The key insight is that these decisions must be made *before* you begin, not discovered along the way. You can always adjust them — if the material leads you somewhere better, follow it — but you need a starting frame. Constraint is not the enemy of creativity; it's the condition for it.
