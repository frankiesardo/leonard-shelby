# Progress as Increasing Heritage

Progress is real, but not for the reason optimists usually claim. Human nature hasn't improved. Science is neutral — it kills as readily as it heals. Our comforts may have weakened our stamina. We are the same trousered apes at two thousand miles an hour as when we had legs.

The Durants redefine progress as "the increasing control of the environment by life." Not happiness — our capacity for fretting is endless and it would be silly to call a child more advanced than a sage just because the child is happier. Not technology per se, because technology is merely new means of achieving old ends.

What *has* progressed is the heritage we transmit. The heritage we can pass on today is richer than that of Pericles (it includes all the Greek flowering that followed him), richer than Leonardo's (it includes him and the Renaissance), richer than Voltaire's (it embraces all the Enlightenment). If education is the transmission of civilization, we are unquestionably progressing.

But civilization is not inherited — it must be learned and earned by each generation anew. If the transmission were interrupted for one century, civilization would die and we would be savages again. The great experiment of universal education has just begun.

## Why it matters

This is the most hopeful idea in the book, and it earns its hope honestly. It doesn't require you to believe that people are getting better — only that the pedestal of accumulated knowledge, art, and wisdom is getting taller. Each generation stands on the shoulders of all previous ones. The obligation this creates is clear: transmit the heritage. That's what education is for. That's what gardens of ideas are for.

"If a man is fortunate he will, before he dies, gather up as much as he can of his civilized heritage and transmit it to his children. And to his final breath he will be grateful for this inexhaustible legacy, knowing that it is our nourishing mother and our lasting life."
