# Live Data Advantage

A commodity model with live proprietary data beats a frontier model with only public training data. This is the real version of the "data network effect" argument — which was historically dismissed as a handwavy moat claim, but now has teeth.

The word "live" is doing most of the work. Static proprietary datasets — historical records, scraped content, one-time research outputs — are valuable but commoditizing. *Live* data is continuously updated, context-specific, and impossible to replicate without being in the system: health telemetry, financial transaction streams, operational logs of running infrastructure, real-time customer behavior. This kind of data is contextually richer than any amount of general training, and routing even a modest model through it produces dramatically better outputs for the specific domain.

Anish Acharya's example: live health data. The question of "how proprietary can that actually be?" is real, but once you have it, it's extraordinarily difficult to replicate — not because of IP protection but because it requires continuous presence in the workflow.

## Why it matters

The prior "data moat" argument was weak because data could be licensed, scraped, or generated. The live version is different: it requires ongoing instrumentation, customer trust, and workflow integration. You can't buy your way to it after the fact. This makes it one of the few defensible advantages that compounds with time — more time in the workflow means richer live data means better model outputs means stronger retention.

The corollary: systems of record with high human workflow density (core banking, clinical EMR, logistics operations) are more defensible than they appear. The live data they accumulate isn't just a feature — it's the asset.
