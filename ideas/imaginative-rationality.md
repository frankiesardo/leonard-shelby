# Imaginative Rationality

Western culture offers two positions on truth and meaning: objectivism (there are absolute truths independent of human understanding) and subjectivism (truth is personal, imaginative, and unconstrained by external reality). Lakoff and Johnson reject both and propose a third: experientialism, which they call "imaginative rationality."

The core claim: metaphor unites reason and imagination. Reason involves categorization, entailment, and inference. Imagination involves seeing one kind of thing in terms of another. Metaphorical thought is therefore *imaginative rationality* -- it is rational because it involves systematic entailments and inferences, and it is imaginative because it involves understanding one domain in terms of another.

This means ordinary rationality is already imaginative (our everyday categories are largely metaphorical), and the products of the imagination are already partly rational (poetic metaphors work through the same entailment mechanisms as conventional ones). The objectivist dream of a thought-system purified of metaphor is impossible. The subjectivist dream of imagination free from rational constraint is equally impossible. What we have is a conceptual system grounded in bodily and cultural experience, structured by metaphor, and tested by ongoing successful functioning in the world.

Truth on this account is real but always relative to a conceptual system. "The fog is in front of the mountain" is true -- but only relative to projections of entity structure onto fog and front-back orientation onto mountains. "Inflation has gone up" is true -- but only relative to the metaphors INFLATION IS A SUBSTANCE and MORE IS UP. This is not relativism in the "anything goes" sense, because our conceptual systems are constrained by the physical world and by the practical requirements of functioning within it. But it is the end of the idea that we can access reality-as-it-is, independent of any human conceptual system.

## Why it matters

The practical consequence is intellectual humility without paralysis. You can make truth claims, but you must recognize that they are made from within a conceptual system that highlights certain aspects of reality and hides others. You can change metaphors -- and should, when the current ones are hiding something important -- but you cannot escape metaphor entirely.

This matters for how you argue (recognizing that your opponent may be operating from a different but equally grounded conceptual system), how you lead (the metaphors you use to frame problems constrain the solutions your team can imagine), and how you think about your own life (the narrative metaphors you live by shape the reality you experience).
