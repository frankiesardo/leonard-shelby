# Frustration Tolerance

Frustration tolerance is the ability to persist in the gap between wanting something and having it. Dr. Becky Kennedy argues it's the most important skill of this generation — for kids *and* adults — because technology has systematically collapsed that gap.

The Blockbuster-to-Netflix transition captures it perfectly. Wanting to watch a movie used to mean: talk to parents, wait a day or two, drive to Blockbuster, browse the aisles, maybe discover they're out of stock. The space between wanting and having was 48 hours of tolerated frustration. Today, wanting a movie and having it are separated by seconds. That's convenient for movies. It's catastrophic for the development of persistence.

When a five-year-old sits down to learn to read — the first thing in their life that requires sustained effort with delayed reward — they hit a wall. The circuitry they need (work hard → struggle → keep going → eventual reward) is the exact opposite of the circuitry they've built (do nothing → instant reward). No wonder it looks like a meltdown.

"Entitlement is essentially the fear of frustration." It's not that entitled people believe they *deserve* things — it's that they can't tolerate the feeling of not having them yet.

## Why it matters

The vicious cycle: kids are less tolerant of frustration (because technology collapsed the wanting-having gap), so they have more bad behavior. Meanwhile, *parents'* frustration tolerance has also eroded (we don't wait for anything either), so they're less able to sit with their kids' meltdowns. Result: more capitulation ("fine, have the iPad"), which teaches even less tolerance. Both sides are losing the skill simultaneously.

The practical implications are counterintuitive: deliberately build friction back in. Wait for the bus instead of taking a cab. Let your kid be bored. Don't switch shows after five minutes. These aren't punishments — they're reps. Frustration tolerance, like any skill, requires practice at tolerable doses.
