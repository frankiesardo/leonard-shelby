# The Two-Minute Rule

When you start a new habit, it should take less than two minutes to do. "Read before bed each night" becomes "read one page." "Do thirty minutes of yoga" becomes "take out my yoga mat." "Run three miles" becomes "tie my running shoes."

This isn't a motivational trick -- it's a recognition that a habit must be established before it can be improved. You have to standardize before you can optimize. You can't improve a habit that doesn't exist. The Two-Minute Rule is about mastering the art of showing up.

The logic is straightforward. Habits are the entry point, not the end point. They are the cab, not the gym. Twyla Tharp's morning ritual wasn't two hours of dancing -- it was hailing a taxi to the studio. Once she was in the cab, everything else followed. The decisive moment is the first two minutes: changing into workout clothes, opening the notebook, putting on the running shoes.

If the Two-Minute Rule feels forced, do it for two minutes and then stop. Go for a run but you *must* stop after two minutes. Study Arabic but you *must* stop after two minutes. One reader used this approach to lose over a hundred pounds: he went to the gym every day but only stayed for five minutes. After weeks of showing up, he thought, "Well, I'm always here anyway -- might as well stay longer."

The principle also works because it reinforces identity. Show up at the gym five days in a row -- even for two minutes -- and you are casting votes for being someone who doesn't miss workouts. The smallest action that confirms the type of person you want to be is more valuable than any single heroic effort that you can't sustain.

Once the habit is established, use "habit shaping" to scale it up: master the first two minutes, advance to an intermediate step, master that, then advance again. The focus always remains on the first two minutes of the behavior.
