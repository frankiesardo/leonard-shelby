# Growth Trajectories

Replace the word "potential" with "growth trajectory." Kim Scott and Scott Forstall (then SVP of iOS at Apple) made this single word change on Apple's performance management matrix, and it transformed how managers thought about their people.

The traditional "performance-potential" matrix labels people as "high potential" or "low potential," which is both presumptuous and static. It implies that a person's capacity for growth is a fixed attribute -- something they either have or don't. "Growth trajectory" reframes the same information as a *current state* that changes over time. A person might be on a steep growth trajectory now (wanting new challenges, ready for more responsibility) and shift to a gradual trajectory later (wanting to go deep, valuing stability). Neither is inherently better. Both are necessary on a healthy team.

The shift matters most when it intersects with life circumstances. The new parent who temporarily wants stability isn't "low potential." The person recovering from burnout who needs a season of deep, focused work isn't "coasting." They're on a different growth trajectory right now, and pushing them toward a steep trajectory when they want a gradual one is as destructive as holding someone back when they're ready to accelerate.

## Why it matters

The word "potential" carries an implicit judgment: high is good, low is bad. This creates a management culture where everyone is pressured to demonstrate steep-growth ambition, whether they feel it or not. People performing exceptional work on a gradual trajectory -- Scott's "rock stars" -- get undervalued, passed over, or pushed into roles that don't suit them.

The reframe also changes the manager's posture. Instead of evaluating potential (a judgment about who someone is), you're observing trajectory (a description of what someone wants right now). The first is arrogant and often wrong. The second is humble and invites conversation: "What trajectory are you on? What would help?"

This maps directly to Russ Laraway's career conversations: instead of asking "Where do you want to be in five years?" (which assumes a steep trajectory), ask about dreams, values, and the skills someone wants to develop next. The trajectory reveals itself through the conversation, rather than being imposed from above.
