# Dominance Hierarchies Are Older Than Trees

The neural circuitry governing social status is not a cultural invention. It is approximately 350 million years old -- predating trees, dinosaurs, and even most modern land animals. Peterson's opening chapter demonstrates this through the lobster: a creature separated from humans by hundreds of millions of years of evolution, yet one whose nervous system responds to serotonin in ways startlingly similar to our own. A lobster who wins a fight stands tall, extends its claws, and patrols its territory. A defeated lobster curls in, retreats, becomes tentative. Prozac -- a serotonin reuptake inhibitor designed for human depression -- literally works on lobsters.

The distribution of resources in lobster populations follows the same Pareto curve that governs human wealth, scientific citation counts, and city sizes: a small number of victors claim a disproportionate share. This is not because lobsters read *Atlas Shrugged*. It is because hierarchies are a permanent feature of the competitive environment to which all complex social life has adapted.

## Why it matters

The point is not that inequality is morally right or that hierarchies should never be challenged. The point is that hierarchies are not a recent human invention that can be abolished by better social engineering. They are a biological inheritance embedded in the oldest parts of the nervous system. Any serious attempt to address human inequality must reckon with this -- not as an excuse but as a constraint. Treating hierarchy as purely a product of culture or malice guarantees you will misunderstand the problem and misdesign the solution. Competence, not just power, is what determines position in well-functioning hierarchies.
