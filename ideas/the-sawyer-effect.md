# The Sawyer Effect

Extrinsic rewards can transform an interesting task into a drudge. They can turn play into work. Pink names this after Tom Sawyer's fence-painting trick, but the direction is reversed: where Tom made work look like play, contingent rewards make play feel like work.

The mechanism is simple. When you promise someone a reward for doing something they already enjoy, the reward becomes the reason. Remove the reward, and the reason evaporates -- along with the interest that preceded it. Deci's 1969 Soma puzzle experiment proved this: people who were paid to solve puzzles, then had the pay removed, spent less time on the puzzles than people who were never paid at all. The pay didn't just fail to help. It actively damaged the pre-existing motivation.

The effect is specific to contingent, expected, if-then rewards ("If you do X, you'll get Y"). Unexpected "now that" rewards ("Now that you've done such great work, here's a bonus") don't trigger it, because the person wasn't working toward the reward. The distinction matters enormously for how organizations should think about compensation and recognition.

## Why it matters

Pink documents seven deadly flaws of carrots and sticks, all flowing from the Sawyer Effect:

1. They extinguish intrinsic motivation
2. They diminish performance (on creative tasks)
3. They crush creativity (narrowed focus kills peripheral vision)
4. They crowd out good behavior (Swedish blood donors gave less when offered money)
5. They encourage cheating and shortcuts
6. They become addictive (the same brain pathway as cocaine)
7. They foster short-term thinking

The most alarming finding: in a study funded by the Federal Reserve, participants offered the highest financial incentives performed worst on creative tasks. Not slightly worse. Worst. Higher incentives led to worse performance in eight of nine tasks.

The practical implication is that most corporate incentive structures -- bonuses, commissions, pay-for-performance -- are not just ineffective for creative work but actively counterproductive. The Sawyer Effect is not an edge case. It is the default outcome for any work that requires thought.
