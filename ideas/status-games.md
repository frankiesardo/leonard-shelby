# Status Games

Humans are driven by two incompatible social urges: to *get along* with people (so they like us and consider us cooperative members of the tribe) and to *get ahead* of them (so we are on top). This conflict is the heart of the human condition and the stories we tell about it. Wanting to get along and get ahead simultaneously is a recipe for dishonesty, hypocrisy, betrayal, and Machiavellian maneuvering.

Status is a human universal. Our subjective well-being, self-esteem, mental and physical health depend on the level of status others accord us. We pursue it with ferocity, mostly unconsciously, and we monitor the status of others with obsessional intensity. Even ravens pay close attention to gossip about status reversals in neighboring flocks.

Human hierarchies are fluid, not static (we share this with chimpanzees). Because everyone's status is always in flux, it generates running narratives of loyalty and betrayal, ambition and despair, loves won and lost, schemes and intrigues. Status reversal -- especially humiliation, the removal of any ability to claim status -- is one of the most psychologically toxic experiences a human can endure. It is implicated in serial murder, honor killings, and genocide. In story, humiliation of the villain is often rapturous because it satisfies our deep tribal craving for justice.

No matter who we actually are, the hero-making brain makes us feel like we deserve more status. We are all Oliver Twist: "Please, sir, I want some more." This is why we identify so easily with underdog heroes.

## Why it matters

Status play permeates every effective story. Hard to conceive of one that does not rely on some form of status movement to squeeze our primal emotions. In management and leadership, understanding that people are playing simultaneous games of connection and dominance explains most office politics, most team dysfunction, and most resistance to feedback.
