# Simplicity in Writing

The secret of good writing is to strip every sentence to its cleanest components. Every word that serves no function, every long word that could be a short word, every adverb carrying meaning already in the verb, every passive construction that leaves the reader unsure of who is doing what — these are adulterants that weaken a sentence. They usually increase in proportion to education and rank.

Simplicity requires courage. A plain sentence feels exposed, and writers instinctively reach for padding the way a speaker reaches for "um." But the reader — assailed by competing demands on their attention — will not fight through fog to find your meaning. If they're lost, it's not because they're dumb. It's because the writer wasn't careful enough.

## Why it matters

Simplicity is not the absence of sophistication. It's the result of hard thinking. Zinsser points out that you can strip an eight-page article to four pages, always with improvement — and that the real work begins when you cut it to three. The discipline applies far beyond prose: to presentations, interfaces, code, arguments. Whatever your medium, the person receiving your work shouldn't have to decode it.

Roosevelt translated a blackout order from "Such preparations shall be made as will completely obscure all Federal buildings..." into "Tell them that in buildings where they have to keep the work going to put something across the windows." The information is identical. The effort shifted from the reader to the writer, where it belongs.
