# The Personal Transaction

The product any writer has to sell is not the subject being written about, but who they are. Readers stay with a piece not because the topic grips them — they may have no prior interest in it at all — but because the writer's enthusiasm, warmth, and humanity are pulling them forward. The personal transaction between writer and reader is what turns information into writing worth reading.

This means being yourself on the page. Style is not decoration you buy at the style store and drape over your words. It's organic to the person doing the writing. Trying to add style is like adding a toupee — at second glance, it doesn't look quite right. The reader wants to hear a genuine person, not a performance.

## Why it matters

The principle extends far beyond prose. In any act of communication — a presentation, a lecture, a conversation — what holds people is the sense that a real person is present: someone with convictions, idiosyncrasies, and skin in the game. Institutions that strip the humanity from their communications (passive voice, jargon, committee-speak) end up sounding like no one at all, and no one listens.

Zinsser puts it bluntly: "Sell yourself, and your subject will exert its own appeal. Believe in your own identity and your own opinions. Writing is an act of ego, and you might as well admit it."
