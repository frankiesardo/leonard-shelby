# Careers as Dots Not Tunnels

Conventional wisdom frames a career as a 40-year tunnel: you pick one entrance, walk through it for decades, and emerge into retirement. This makes every career decision feel existential and every pivot feel like failure. But careers have never really worked this way — they just seemed to. The median young person stays in a job for only 3 years.

The more accurate model: a career is a series of dots — short experiments that connect in ways you can only see in retrospect. Steve Jobs pointed out that you can't connect the dots looking forward, only looking backward. If you study the biographies of successful people, their paths look like a chain of connected dots, not a straight tunnel.

This reframing changes everything about how you approach career decisions. Instead of "which tunnel do I pick for the rest of my life?", the question becomes "which experiment should I run next?" Dot #1 is just a first date — a hypothesis test with low stakes.

## Why it matters

The tunnel metaphor causes three specific kinds of damage:

1. **Tyranny of choice.** If each option is a 40-year commitment, the number of options is paralyzing. If each option is a 3-year experiment, the number of options is exciting.
2. **Fear of switching.** Tunnels make career changes feel like failure. Dots make them feel like data collection.
3. **Premature identity crisis.** Tunnels fuse your career with your identity ("I *am* a lawyer"). Dots keep career and identity separate.

You're literally not qualified to plan dot #4 from where you stand now. By the time you get there, you'll be a different person with different knowledge, different values, and a different world to navigate. Pretending otherwise is delusional, not responsible.
