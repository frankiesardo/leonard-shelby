# Boldness over Hedging

Every little qualifier whittles away some fraction of the audience's trust. "A bit," "sort of," "kind of," "rather," "quite," "pretty much" -- these tiny timidities dilute both your style and your persuasiveness. Don't say you were a bit confused and sort of tired and a little depressed. Be confused. Be tired. Be depressed. Good communication of any kind is lean and confident.

Zinsser's all-time champion of hedging is Elliot Richardson's "And yet, on balance, affirmative action has, I think, been a qualified success." A 13-word sentence with five hedging words. Leaders who bob and weave like aging boxers don't inspire confidence -- or deserve it.

Sell yourself, and your subject will exert its own appeal. Believe in your own identity and your own opinions. If you're going to make a point, make it.

## Why it matters

Hedging is the verbal equivalent of never making eye contact. It signals uncertainty, which invites the audience to discount what follows. In a presentation, "I think this might possibly be worth considering" registers completely differently from "This matters. Here's why." The content can be identical; the hedging changes how it's received.

The impulse to hedge comes from fear -- fear of being wrong, of offending, of being held to a position. But the cost of hedging is higher than the cost of occasionally being wrong. When you commit to a point, people engage with it. They may disagree -- that's fine, that's the intellectual fun. When you hedge, they tune out. There's nothing to engage with.

This doesn't mean being reckless or dishonest. If you genuinely don't know, say "I don't know." That's bold in its own right. What you shouldn't do is pretend to say something while actually saying nothing.
