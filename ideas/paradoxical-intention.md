# Paradoxical Intention

Fear brings about precisely what one is afraid of. A man afraid of blushing blushes more. A woman afraid of insomnia stays awake trying to sleep. Frankl calls this "anticipatory anxiety" -- the fear that feeds the symptom that feeds the fear. The vicious circle tightens with every revolution.

The cure is paradoxical: *intend* the feared outcome. The sweating phobic resolves to "pour at least ten quarts." The insomniac resolves to stay awake as long as possible. The stutterer tries to stutter as badly as he can. In each case, the wind is taken out of the sails of anxiety because you cannot fear what you are actively wishing for. The technique was developed by Frankl as early as 1939 and remains one of logotherapy's most practical contributions.

Paradoxical intention works because it leverages two uniquely human capacities: self-detachment and humor. By deliberately exaggerating the feared outcome, the patient puts distance between themselves and their neurosis. They laugh at the absurdity of pouring ten quarts of sweat. That laughter breaks the feedback loop.

A related concept is "hyper-intention": the more you force yourself toward a goal (orgasm, sleep, success), the more it eludes you. Pleasure, sleep, and success must ensue as side-effects -- they cannot be directly pursued. The cure for hyper-intention is "dereflection": redirecting attention away from oneself and toward the proper object -- the partner, the task, the cause.

## Why it matters

Paradoxical intention is effective regardless of the cause of the neurosis. It works in phobic conditions, obsessive-compulsive patterns, insomnia, sexual dysfunction, and writer's cramp. It is short-term (sometimes a single session), and its effects can be permanent. A bookkeeper with severe writer's cramp was told to try to write as badly as possible; he found he could not scrawl, and the cramp disappeared within 48 hours.

The deeper insight is philosophical: much of human suffering is amplified by the very effort to avoid it. Stop fleeing from the symptom, turn around and embrace it with irony, and it often dissolves.
