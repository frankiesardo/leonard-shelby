# Forgiveness as Freedom

Forgiveness doesn't condone what happened. It doesn't mean forgetting. It doesn't mean rolling over. You still see the wrong clearly, feel the suffering it caused, and resolve to do whatever you can to stop it from continuing. Then — and this is the hard part — you refuse to carry the hatred in your own heart.

Kornfield tells the story of two former prisoners of war who meet 25 years after being tortured. One asks: "Have you forgiven our captors yet?" The second says: "No. Never." The first replies: "Well then, they still have you in prison, don't they?"

The woman in a bitter divorce who vowed "I will not bequeath a legacy of bitterness to my children about their father" demonstrated the same principle. She was getting a kick-ass lawyer. She was protecting her kids. But she would not play the game of mutual destruction — not for her ex-husband's benefit, but for her own and her children's.

## Why it matters

Without forgiveness, the cycle never ends. The Hutus and the Tutsis, the Bosnians and the Serbs, the Northern Irish — "your people marched through our neighborhood 200 years ago and we're not going to let this stop." At some point, someone has to say: it stops here.

Forgiveness is not quick. You can't paper it over. It can be a long process. But in the end, it is actually for the benefit of your own heart. The question is existential: do you want to live with a closed heart full of bitterness, or can you carry the suffering without letting it define you? Nelson Mandela walked out of 27 years in prison with such magnanimity that he changed not just South Africa but the world's imagination of what's possible.

The actions and the thoughts work together. Not speaking the bitterness aloud (the woman's vow) doesn't immediately stop thinking it — but it stops feeding it. The thoughts, deprived of reinforcement, gradually lose their grip.
