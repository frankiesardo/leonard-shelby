# Process over Product

We are a culture that worships the winning result: the league championship, the high test score, the byline. Writers fixate on the finished article before they've made any of the earlier decisions that determine its shape. This fixation on the product deflects them from the process — and the process is where the real work happens.

If the process is sound, the product will take care of itself. Less glamorous gains made along the way — learning, wisdom, growth, confidence, dealing with failure — aren't given the same respect because they can't be given a grade. But they're what actually produce the good work.

Zinsser taught a writing course where no writing was required. Students came each week and talked through their ideas — what they wanted to write about, why, and how. Freed from the deadline and the demand for a finished product, they relaxed. They considered different approaches. Some would work and some wouldn't. The right to fail was liberating. And when they eventually did write, their work was stronger for it.

## Why it matters

This idea applies anywhere the pressure of the outcome short-circuits the quality of the work. A team rushing to ship a feature without thinking through the design. A student writing a paper to get the grade instead of to learn. A company optimizing for quarterly results instead of building something durable.

The tyranny of the final product is the tyranny of skipping the messy, uncertain, unglamorous middle where the real thinking happens. When you give yourself permission to focus on process — to explore, to fail, to iterate — the product that eventually emerges is almost always better than what you'd have produced by fixating on it from the start.
