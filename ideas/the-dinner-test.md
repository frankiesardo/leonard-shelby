# The Dinner Test

A story told on stage or in a pitch should sound like a slightly elevated version of what you'd tell a friend over dinner. No performance art. No dramatic opening gambits ("BANG!"). No unattributed dialogue openers. If you wouldn't say it at a dinner table without your friend refusing to eat with you again, don't say it on stage.

The elevation is minor: less verbal detritus, more shape, no interruptions. But the register stays conversational. The test keeps you honest — it prevents the "public speaking voice" that makes audiences disengage.

Dicks pairs the dinner test with two prerequisites: the story must contain change (a five-second moment), and it must be *your* story. Third-person stories require no vulnerability, and vulnerability is what creates connection.
