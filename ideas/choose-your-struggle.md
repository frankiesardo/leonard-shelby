# Choose Your Struggle

"What do you want?" is a useless question. Everyone wants the same things — happiness, love, money, success. The question that actually determines how your life turns out is: "What pain are you willing to sustain? What are you willing to struggle for?"

Manson illustrates this with his own rock star fantasy. He spent years imagining himself on stage, crowds screaming, pouring his heart into the music. But he didn't want the *process* — the daily drudgery of practice, the logistics of finding a group, the pain of getting gigs, hauling forty pounds of gear with no car. He loved the summit but hated the climb. So it never happened. That wasn't failure or lack of willpower. He just didn't want it badly enough — he wanted the result, not the struggle.

The people who end up with chiseled abs enjoy the struggle of the gym. The people who fly to the top of the corporate ladder enjoy the politics and the long hours. The people who make it as artists enjoy the uncertainty and the rejection. They don't merely tolerate the process — they're defined by it.

## Why it matters

This reframes the entire question of life purpose. Instead of "what would make me happy?" (which invokes the Backwards Law), ask "what problems do I enjoy solving?" or "what kind of pain feels meaningful to me?" This is not "no pain, no gain" — it's a more precise claim: your struggles determine your successes. The joy is in the climb itself, not in the imagined summit.

It also explains why so many people feel stuck. They want the reward of a career change but not the struggle of starting over. They want the intimacy of a relationship but not the struggle of tough conversations. They fantasize about the destination and so never begin the journey.
