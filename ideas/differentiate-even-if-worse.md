# Differentiate Even If Worse

James Dyson's principle: "Make it different, even if it's worse." This is one of the most counterintuitive rules in product building, and one of the most powerful.

You can make a seven-out-of-ten solution to anything by copying the seven-out-of-ten already in the market. But if you build from scratch -- from "tabula rasa axioms," as Tobi puts it -- you get a six-out-of-ten that you have complete mastery over. The crucial difference: you can iterate the six past the seven. The copy has a ceiling; the original has a trajectory.

This is why the SpaceX Raptor engine evolution photo is "today's Picasso." Each iteration removes complexity, removes weight, removes parts. The team moves forward by subtraction -- something that almost never happens in industry, because subtraction requires understanding every decision deeply enough to know which ones to undo. You can only subtract from something you built yourself.

Edwin Land (Steve Jobs' hero, founder of Polaroid) had a personal motto: "Don't do anything that somebody else can do." Not out of ego -- out of the recognition that "me-too" work has a ceiling.

## Why it matters

Copying feels safe because the outcome is predictable. But predictable means bounded. If you copy, your best case is matching the original. If you build from first principles, your best case is unlimited -- because you understand every tradeoff and can revisit any of them.

This applies to everything: products, company structures, compensation systems, office design. Shopify rebuilt its compensation system from scratch (employees choose their own cash/stock mix), designed its own office pods, built its own organizational model in code. Each was initially "worse" by conventional metrics. Each turned out to be a profound competitive advantage because they owned every decision.

The world belongs to the fast -- the people who iterate, adjust, and prune. You can only prune what you planted.
