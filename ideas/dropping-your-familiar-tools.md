# Dropping Your Familiar Tools

Psychologist Karl Weick noticed that smokejumpers and hotshot firefighters were dying because they refused to drop their tools when running from advancing fires. In the 1949 Mann Gulch fire, thirteen firefighters died. The crew foreman yelled at the men to drop their tools. Two did immediately and sprinted to safety. Others ran with their tools and were caught by the flames. One man sat down, exhausted, never having removed his heavy pack.

In four separate fires in the 1990s, twenty-three elite wildland firefighters refused orders to drop their tools and perished beside them. Even one survivor who eventually dropped his chainsaw felt like he was doing something unnatural: "I irrationally started looking for a place to put it down where it wouldn't get burned."

Weick saw the same pattern everywhere. Navy seamen who ignored orders to remove steel-toed shoes when abandoning a ship, and drowned or punched holes in life rafts. Fighter pilots refusing orders to eject from disabled planes. Karl Wallenda, the world-famous high-wire performer, who fell 120 feet and grabbed his balance pole rather than the wire beneath him.

"Dropping one's tools is a proxy for unlearning, for adaptation, for flexibility," Weick wrote. "It is the very unwillingness of people to drop their tools that turns some of these dramas into tragedies."

The Challenger disaster is the canonical example in organizational life. NASA engineers could not accept a qualitative, photographic argument about O-ring danger because their culture demanded quantitative data. "In God We Trust, All Others Bring Data." Their most powerful tool -- rigorous quantitative analysis -- became the thing that killed seven astronauts. Roger Boisjoly knew the cold temperature was "away from goodness," but when asked to quantify his concern, he could not, and his colleagues fell silent rather than present arguments outside the accepted rubric.

## Why it matters

Experienced groups regress to what they know best under pressure. They behave like a collective hedgehog, bending an unfamiliar situation into a familiar comfort zone. Tools define identity: "When a firefighter is told to drop his firefighting tools, he is told to forget he is a firefighter."

Hotshot Paul Gleason practiced an alternative. He viewed his leadership not as decision making but as "sensemaking." "If I make a decision, it is a possession, I take pride in it, I tend to defend it and not listen to those who question it. If I make sense, then this is more dynamic and I listen and I can change it." He employed "hunches held lightly" -- decisive directions with transparent rationale and an open invitation to revise.

The lesson is not that tools are bad. The lesson is that the moment your tools define who you are, you cannot let go of them even when they're killing you.
