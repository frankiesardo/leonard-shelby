# The Corporate Raider Exercise

Tobi Lütke's favorite annual exercise: imagine Shopify went bankrupt, you bought it on a fire sale as a corporate raider, and you're marching in on day one. Previous management was crazy. What do you change?

This is a psychological hack for overcoming sunk cost fallacy -- one of the most destructive cognitive biases in company building. The sunk cost fallacy makes you continue investing in something because of what you've already invested, not because of its future value. The corporate raider exercise bypasses this by psychologically severing you from the past decisions. "That was somebody else's problem. This is a new company now."

Tobi goes further: he actively writes "hit pieces on the past" -- brutal assessments of everything wrong with systems he himself built. He trash-talks his own prior work in front of the company to create permission for others to do the same. "I want people to feel no deference to it."

The complementary insight: nostalgia is more dangerous than it seems. It was listed on death certificates in the 1800s. We used to know it was a bad indulgence. Now it's "barely a vice." But in company building, nostalgia for past decisions -- reluctance to undo what was hard-won -- is lethal.

## Why it matters

Every growing company accumulates decisions that were right at the time but wrong now. The question is whether you can see them clearly. The corporate raider exercise creates psychological distance: instead of defending past choices (because they're yours), you evaluate them as an outsider who bought the company on the cheap and needs to turn it around.

This is David Ogilvy's "divine discontent" made operational. It's not about being miserable -- Tobi gets *excited* when he finds something bad, because it means there's an obvious blueprint for improvement. The energy comes from the discovery of opportunity, not from self-flagellation.
