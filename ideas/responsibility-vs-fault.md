# Responsibility vs. Fault

Fault is past tense: who caused the problem. Responsibility is present tense: who must deal with it now. They overlap frequently, but they are not the same thing. If someone hits you with their car, it's their fault. But it's your responsibility to deal with the injury, the recovery, the insurance, the aftermath. Conflating fault and responsibility is one of the most common ways people stay stuck.

The conflation works like this: "I didn't cause this problem, therefore it's not my responsibility." This feels righteous and often is factually correct. But it's strategically catastrophic. The person who waits for the at-fault party to fix things is the person who never moves forward. You can be right and still be miserable.

Manson frames it as a rewrite of the Spider-Man quote: the original says "With great power comes great responsibility." The better version: "With great responsibility comes great power." The more responsibility you accept for your situation — even for things that aren't your fault — the more power you exercise over your life.

## Why it matters

William James's one-year experiment is the proof of concept. At rock bottom, he decided to spend a full year acting as if he were 100% responsible for everything in his life. He went on to become the father of American psychology. His "rebirth" wasn't a change in circumstances — it was a change in ownership.

This also explains the trap of victimhood. Manson doesn't deny that bad things happen to people unfairly. His point is that regardless of who caused the situation, you are always responsible for how you interpret it and how you respond. Even if someone wrongs you, choosing to stew in bitterness is still *your* choice.
