# The Five-Second Moment

Every story worth telling is about one singular moment — a flip from one state to another that takes five seconds or less. Either transformation ("I used to be one kind of person, now I'm a new kind of person") or realization ("I used to think X, something happened, now I think Y"). The moment is the point of the story; everything else — setup, stakes, humor, characters — exists only to bring that moment into focus.

The practical consequence is radical: you build your story from the end. You identify the five-second moment first, then construct the beginning as its opposite. If the ending is "she found her confidence," the beginning is "she had no confidence and needed help." Romantic comedies do this transparently — they hate each other at the start, love each other at the end — and knowing the structure doesn't ruin it because the journey is the entertainment.

This is Matthew Dicks's central insight from 59 Moth Story Slam wins: the storyteller who knows exactly what their five-second moment is will always outperform the one with better language, more humor, or a more dramatic life. Story is not about what happened to you. It's about the moment you changed.
