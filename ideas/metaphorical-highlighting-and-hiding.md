# Metaphorical Highlighting and Hiding

Every metaphor is a spotlight and a shadow at the same time. When you understand ARGUMENT IS WAR, the combative aspects of argument come into focus -- attacking, defending, winning, losing. But the cooperative aspects disappear: the other person giving you their time, the mutual search for understanding, the possibility that you might both learn something. No metaphor captures the totality of what it maps onto. Each one is partial by design.

This is not a bug. It is how understanding works. To comprehend anything, you have to foreground some features and background others. The danger is not that metaphors hide things -- they must -- but that we forget they are hiding anything at all. TIME IS MONEY is so deeply embedded in industrial culture that it feels like objective truth, not a choice. But it hides the ways time is unlike money: you cannot save it in a bank, you cannot get it back, and no one can give you theirs.

The political implications are stark. Carter's WAR metaphor for the energy crisis highlighted the need for sacrifice, an external enemy, and centralized command. It hid the possibility that the "enemy" was our own consumption patterns, that decentralized approaches might work better, and that the metaphor itself foreclosed certain solutions. "People in power get to impose their metaphors" -- and the hidden aspects of the dominant metaphor become the things no one can think about.

## Why it matters

Any time you choose a frame -- for a product, an argument, a policy, a personal narrative -- you are choosing what to illuminate and what to obscure. The question is never "is this metaphor right?" but "what does this metaphor make visible, and what does it make invisible?" The discipline of noticing what a frame hides is one of the most powerful critical thinking skills there is.

This also explains why switching metaphors can feel like revelation. When you go from LIFE IS A RACE to LIFE IS A GARDEN, an entirely different set of experiences becomes salient. The experiences were there all along -- the metaphor didn't invent them -- but they were hidden by the previous frame.
