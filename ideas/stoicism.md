# Stoicism

Not the colloquial "stiff upper lip." The actual practice: a system for living well by focusing on what you can control, accepting what you cannot, and developing the wisdom to tell the difference.

## Core principles

- **Dichotomy of control.** Your opinions, intentions, desires, and aversions are up to you. Everything else — health, wealth, reputation, other people — is not. This is the foundation.
- **Virtue as the sole good.** External things are "preferred" or "dispreferred," never good or evil in themselves. Only your character — your judgments and actions — can be good or evil.
- **Live according to nature.** Meaning: live according to your rational nature as a human being. Use reason. Participate in community. Accept your mortality.
- **Voluntary discomfort.** Deliberately practice hardship — cold, hunger, fatigue — so that involuntary hardship doesn't break you. This is [antifragility](antifragility.md) applied to the self.

## Why this is foundational

Stoicism is the operating system underneath most of what I care about. Covey's [circle of influence](circle-of-influence.md) is the dichotomy of control repackaged for business. Adlerian psychology (as in [The Courage of Being Disliked](../sources/books/the-courage-of-being-disliked.md)) arrives at similar conclusions from a different direction. Frankl's logotherapy is stoicism stress-tested in a concentration camp.

Even the engineering management books circle back here: Radical Candor requires the stoic ability to separate your ego from your work. Building antifragile teams requires accepting that failure is not something to prevent but something to metabolize.

## The Eastern connection

The parallels with Taoism are not superficial. The stoic *logos* (rational order of the universe) maps onto the Tao. The stoic emphasis on flowing with nature rather than fighting it maps onto [wu wei](wu-wei.md). The martial arts emphasis on yielding rather than resisting is the physical embodiment of both.

I don't think this is coincidence. I think there's a convergent truth here: when you get quiet enough and honest enough, across cultures and millennia, you arrive at similar insights about how to live.
