# The Hero-Maker Narrative

The brain is a hero-maker. If we are psychologically healthy, it makes us feel like the plucky, brave protagonist in the story of our own lives. It does this through a suite of interlocking mechanisms: it rewrites our memories to make us look better, it hunts for evidence that confirms our pre-existing beliefs (and stops looking the moment it finds any), it generates self-justifying narratives that make our most partisan instincts feel morally correct, and it gives us a pleasurable neurochemical spike when we successfully deceive ourselves.

Moral superiority is thought to be a "uniquely strong and prevalent form of positive illusion." Even prisoners rate themselves above average on kindness and morality (they concede only that, on law-abidingness, they score about average). Four causes of human violence: greed, sadism, high self-esteem, and moral idealism. The first two are vanishingly rare. It is convictions of personal and moral superiority that drive most acts of evil.

The hero-maker also generates a signature framing: we are the brave, scrappy David; our opponents are the almighty Goliath. This David-and-Goliath division seems to be a universal maneuver of human cognition, observable in every political tribe, every culture war, every office feud.

The confabulating narrator -- the inner voice that explains our behavior to us -- has no wired-in access to the true causes of our emotions and actions. It observes what the brain is doing and invents a "makes-sense" story after the fact. Split-brain experiments proved this: a patient told to WALK by one hemisphere stood up and, when asked why, said "I'm going to get a Coke." The narrator is not lying, exactly. It is confabulating -- telling a story that is fictional while believing it is true.

## Why it matters

Understanding the hero-maker is essential for both storytelling and self-knowledge. In fiction, great drama forms around a clash of competing hero-maker narratives -- two characters whose moral perceptions of reality feel genuine but are catastrophically opposed. In life, recognizing the hero-maker at work is the first step toward intellectual honesty. When we become irrationally emotional and defensive, we are betraying the parts of us that require the most aggressive protection -- the place where our perception is most warped and tender.
