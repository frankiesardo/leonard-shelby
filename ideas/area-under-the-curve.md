# Area Under the Curve

Not all great companies are hypercurves. Some look slow or flat at any given cross-section but accumulate enormous value through depth, defensibility, and compounding adoption. The area under the curve — total value created over time — can be much larger than the growth rate at any moment suggests.

Figma is the canonical example: years in build mode, seemingly stagnant, then emerged with N=1 network effects and deep workflow integration that no fast-slope competitor could replicate. Anish Acharya's framing: "There are 20-year overnight successes that are systematically underestimated in venture lore." Investors who lionize the hockey-stick companies and dismiss the area-under-the-curve companies are pattern-matching on slope when they should be integrating.

The distinction matters most when evaluating businesses that are:
- Building in markets where enterprise adoption cycles are inherently slow (payroll, core banking, clinical software)
- Accumulating network effects or workflow integration that take years to fully express
- Developing technical depth (hardware, biotech, deep software infrastructure) that requires the long runway

## Why it matters

Venture capital has a structural bias toward high-slope companies because they show up clearly in portfolio reviews and fundraising narratives. Area-under-the-curve companies require trusting a trajectory that's hard to defend in a quarterly update. This means they're systematically underpriced relative to their eventual outcome — and systematically underfunded.

For founders: the "length to the start line" matters. Figma spent years building before the market was ready. The founders who understood this and didn't panic at flat early metrics were able to hold long enough to see the full shape of the curve. Pressing for slope too early often destroys the conditions for accumulation.
