# Money as Universal Fiction

Money is the most universal and efficient system of mutual trust ever devised. Unlike every other imagined order -- Christianity, nationalism, Marxism -- money does not require its users to share any beliefs, values, or identity. It asks only that you trust that *others* trust it. This minimal requirement is what makes money the supreme unifier of humankind.

A Christian and a Muslim who disagree about everything else can still trade using the same coins. A Spanish conquistador and an Aztec merchant who share no language, religion, or cultural reference can still negotiate through gold. Money converts anything into anything else: land into loyalty, violence into knowledge, sex into salvation. No other human invention has this universal convertibility.

Money evolved from barley (Sumer, ~3000 BC) through silver and gold coins (Lydia, ~640 BC) to paper promises (China, Song dynasty) to pure information (modern banking). At each stage, money became more abstract and more powerful. Today, more than 90% of all money exists only as electronic data in bank computers -- no coins, no notes, just numbers on screens. The entire global economy runs on shared belief in these numbers.

The dark side: money's universal convertibility erodes everything it touches. Things that were once governed by non-monetary values -- honor, loyalty, love, art -- gradually acquire price tags. The market colonizes every domain of life. Money creates trust between strangers but can corrode trust within communities.

## Why it matters

Understanding money as fiction (not deception, but inter-subjective reality) demystifies economics. Financial crises become legible as crises of shared belief, not mechanical failures. The power of central banks becomes explicable: they are the priests of the money religion, maintaining faith in the fiction. And the persistent feeling that "money isn't everything" turns out to be a real tension between the universal fiction of money and the local fictions of community, meaning, and identity.
