# Experiential Realism

Experiential realism is the philosophical position that meaning, truth, and reason are grounded in embodied human experience — not in a correspondence between symbols and a mind-independent world (objectivism), and not in unconstrained subjective imagination (relativism). It's the third path.

Lakoff and Johnson propose it as the alternative to what they see as a false dichotomy. Experiential realism shares objectivism's commitments: a real world exists, stable knowledge is possible, truth is more than mere coherence, and not all conceptual systems are equally good. But it grounds all of this in how particular bodies interact with particular environments, rather than in a God's-eye view of reality.

## Why it matters

The key move is rejecting the correspondence theory of truth without falling into "anything goes." Truth is real, but it's always relative to a system of understanding. And systems of understanding are constrained by embodiment — we can't just make up any concepts we want, because our bodies and environments place limits on what concepts are possible and useful.

This resolves several philosophical puzzles at once:
- **Why does science work?** Not because our symbols correspond to mind-independent reality, but because our embodied understanding tracks real regularities in the environments we function in
- **Why do metaphors feel true?** Because they're grounded in bodily experience that's shared across humans
- **Why is math effective?** Because mathematical structures arise from basic human activities (counting, measuring, shaping) and the image schemas that structure our understanding of those activities
- **How can truth be relative without being arbitrary?** Because embodiment constrains relativity — shared bodies produce shared understanding
