# Separation of Tasks

For any interpersonal problem, ask one question: "Who ultimately bears the consequences of this choice?" That person owns the task. Everyone else should stay out of it.

A child's studying is the child's task -- the child receives the grades, the child faces the consequences of ignorance. A parent can inform, can offer assistance, can express concern -- but cannot intrude. Forcing a child to study is an act of trespassing on the child's task. It doesn't produce learning; it produces rebellion or dependence. The same applies in every relationship: whether your friend likes you is their task. Whether your boss approves of your work style is their task. Whether your partner changes a habit is their task.

The diagnostic question ("who bears the consequences?") is simple. The practice is hard, because most of us have spent our lives intruding on others' tasks or allowing others to intrude on ours. A parent who can't stop micromanaging their child's homework. An employee who shapes every decision around what the boss might think. A friend who takes responsibility for another friend's happiness. All task-boundary violations.

The separation of tasks is not isolation or indifference. Adler draws a sharp line between *non-interference* (not caring) and *non-intervention* (caring but not intruding). You tell the child that studying matters. You make yourself available to help. But you do not force. "You can lead a horse to water, but you can't make him drink."

## Why it matters

Most interpersonal suffering comes from two sources: intruding on other people's tasks, or allowing them to intrude on yours. The desire for recognition is the most common form: you make "being approved of" your task, when in fact others' opinions are their task, not yours. Freedom, in Adler's framework, is the willingness to be disliked -- to accept that whether someone likes you is their business, and to act according to your own principles regardless.

"The courage to be happy also includes the courage to be disliked."
