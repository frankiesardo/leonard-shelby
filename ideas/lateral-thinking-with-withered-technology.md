# Lateral Thinking with Withered Technology

Gunpei Yokoi's creative philosophy at Nintendo: use technology that is old enough to be extremely well understood and easily available, and put it to use in ways no one else has considered. If you cannot think more deeply about new technologies, think more broadly about old ones.

"Lateral thinking" is a term coined in the 1960s for reimagining information in new contexts. "Withered technology" means tech that is so mature it doesn't require specialist knowledge. The combination is devastatingly effective.

The Game Boy (1989) had a processor from the 1970s, four grayscale shades on a greenish-tinted screen, and graphics that smeared during motion. It competed against the Sega Game Gear and Atari Lynx, both technologically superior in every way. It destroyed them. The Game Boy was cheap, pocketable, nearly indestructible, and ran for days on AA batteries. Old hardware was familiar to developers, who pumped out games at incredible speed. It sold 118.7 million units, the bestselling console of the twentieth century.

Yokoi's earlier hits followed the same pattern. The Love Tester: a transistor connected to a galvanometer. Lefty RX: a single-channel RC car that could only turn left. Game & Watch: LCD screens from cheap calculators repurposed as gaming displays. In each case, the technology was commodity; the innovation was in the application.

## Why it matters

The default competitive strategy is the arms race: better specs, newer tech, more features. Yokoi's philosophy inverts this. It is a strategy of creative constraint, finding the novel application rather than the novel component. Clayton Christensen called the Wii -- a direct descendant of Yokoi's doctrine -- the most important kind of innovation: "empowering innovation" that creates new customers rather than better-serving existing ones.

This generalizes beyond tech products. Freeman Dyson distinguished "frogs" (deep specialists) from "birds" (broad visionaries) and argued both are needed. Yokoi was a bird. He admitted he wasn't a great engineer: "I don't have any particular specialist skills. I have a sort of vague knowledge of everything." His advice to young employees: "Do not be an engineer. Be a producer." The producer doesn't need to know a semiconductor's inner workings -- just that it exists and what it might do.

Yokoi's greatest failure -- the Virtual Boy -- came when he abandoned his own principles and tried to use cutting-edge experimental technology.
