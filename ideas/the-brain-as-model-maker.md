# The Brain as Model-Maker

We do not perceive reality directly. The brain locks us inside a "vault of silence and darkness" and constructs a controlled hallucination based on predictions, updating it only when sensory fact-checkers detect something unexpected. What we experience as "out there" is actually generated inside our heads. Color, sound, pain -- all are effects manufactured by neural processes, not properties of the external world.

This has immediate consequences for storytelling. When a reader's eyes scan words on a page, the brain converts those symbols into electrical pulses and builds a model of whatever the words describe. The reader literally hallucinates the story. This is why specific detail matters (the brain models "a dark blue carpet" more vividly than "a carpet"), why active voice is more immersive (it mirrors the sequence of real perception), and why show-don't-tell works (the brain needs raw material to build its model, not pre-digested conclusions).

The same model-making architecture explains theory of mind (we build models of other people's minds), animism (we project minds into objects), dreams (hallucinations without fact-checking), and metaphor (cross-activation of neural models). It also explains our profound vulnerability to illusion: we see what we expect to see, miss what we don't expect, and defend our model as fiercely as our body.

## Why it matters

Every character in fiction and every person in life inhabits a private hallucination. Their model is shaped by their past, their culture, their personality, their damage. Two people standing side by side literally experience different worlds. This is the foundation of dramatic conflict: not disagreements about facts, but incompatible realities.

For writers, the practical implication is that description is never neutral. Everything a character perceives is filtered through their particular model. All description, properly written, is description of character.
