# Building Your Trigger

You do not find your trigger for peak performance -- you build it backward. Start with an activity that already puts you in a state of serene focus. Create a routine that precedes it. Then transplant the routine to precede the high-stakes situation. Gradually condense the routine until a deep breath can do what an hour once did.

Waitzkin worked with a financial advisor named Dennis whose peak state was playing catch with his twelve-year-old son -- pure presence, nothing else existed. So they built a routine before the catch: light snack, fifteen minutes of breathing meditation, ten minutes of stretching, ten minutes of Bob Dylan. For a month, Dennis did this routine every day before playing catch. The physiological link between the routine and the state of presence was forged.

Then they moved the routine to precede important meetings instead. Same steps, same state of mind, completely different context. It worked immediately. Dennis was fully present in situations that normally stressed him.

Then the condensing began. Meditation shortened from fifteen minutes to twelve, then less. Stretching trimmed from ten to eight. Steps were reordered, combined, made flexible. Over time the routine compressed to about twelve minutes -- more potent than ever. The next step would have been to gradually reduce the music until Dennis only had to think of the song to click into the zone.

## Why it matters

The biggest roadblock to recovering between bursts of intensity is the fear of not being able to get it back. If entering the zone is hit-or-miss, letting go of focus feels dangerous. A reliable trigger eliminates that fear. You know you can come back, so you can afford to release.

Most people search for the perfect motivational tool -- the right song, the right mantra -- as if it is hovering in the cosmos waiting to be discovered. That is backward. Any serene activity can be the anchor. The routine creates the bridge between that anchor and performance. The incremental condensing makes the bridge portable.

The same logic that shrinks a full Tai Chi form into a single breath applies here. Waitzkin condensed his pre-competition form from the full set of sixty movements to three-quarters, then half, a quarter, then a single deep inhalation and release. He also learned to do the form entirely in his mind, without moving. The visualization proved almost as powerful as the physical practice -- the internal essence was what mattered, not the external shape.

The practical implication: virtually everyone has an activity that puts them in flow, but they dismiss it as "just taking a break." That break is the seed of a trigger. Protect it. Build a routine around it. Then condense.
