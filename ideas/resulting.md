# Resulting

Resulting is the tendency to evaluate the quality of a decision by the quality of its outcome. A poker term that Annie Duke generalized: when the result is bad, we call the decision bad; when the result is good, we call the decision good. But outcomes are a blend of decision quality and luck, and conflating the two prevents learning from either.

Pete Carroll's infamous pass call in Super Bowl XLIX is the cleanest example. The play was statistically defensible — low interception probability, clock management advantages, an extra down if incomplete. It was intercepted. Overnight, a reasonable strategic decision became "the worst call in Super Bowl history." Four days later Carroll said it plainly: "It was the worst *result* of a call ever. The call would have been just fine, and nobody would have thought twice about it."

## Why it matters

Resulting is not just a post-hoc bias — it actively warps future decisions. A CEO who fires a president for defensible reasons gets a bad outcome, and changes his entire decision-making style in response. He didn't get new information about the *process* — he got information about the *result*, which is contaminated by factors outside his control. The course correction is responding to noise as if it were signal.

The correction is to evaluate decisions at the point they were made, with the information available at that time. This requires separating two questions that feel like one: "Was the outcome good?" and "Was the decision good?" A good decision with a bad outcome is still a good decision — it just landed on the wrong side of the probability distribution.
