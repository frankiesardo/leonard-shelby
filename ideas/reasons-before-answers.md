"If you had enough reasons, you could do the most incredible things." Jim Rohn's mentor Shoaff told him he had plenty of intelligence but not enough reasons. His bank balance wasn't a measure of his IQ — it was a measure of his motivation.

The insight: *reasons come first, answers come second*. Life has a mysterious way of hanging on to all the answers and only giving them up to people inspired by reasons. You don't get the how-to until you have the why-to. This is why goal-setting works — not because goals give you a map, but because goals give you *reasons*, and reasons generate the energy that finds the map.

Reasons come in layers. Personal reasons (recognition, respect, the feeling of winning). Family reasons (doing it for someone else — sometimes we'll do for others what we won't do for ourselves). And "nitty-gritty" reasons — small, specific humiliations or desires that carry surprising power. Rohn's Girl Scout cookie story, where he couldn't afford two dollars and had to lie to a child, was one of his most powerful reasons. Carnegie writing his life goal on a slip of paper in his twenties was another.

This connects to [two creations](two-creations.md): the mental creation (the reason, the vision) must precede the physical creation (the plan, the execution). And to [Quadrant II](quadrant-ii.md): the most important work is often building your reasons — clarifying what matters — before jumping to action.
