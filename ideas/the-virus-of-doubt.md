# The Virus of Doubt

Before you tell people what your product does, make them feel the problem it solves. Get into their heads, remind them about a daily frustration, get them annoyed about it all over again. If you can infect them with the virus of doubt -- "Maybe my experience isn't as good as I thought, maybe it could be better" -- you prime them for your solution.

Steve Jobs mastered this. In his 2007 iPhone keynote, he didn't start with the phone's features. He started by describing the problems with existing smartphones: "The most advanced phones are called smartphones, so they say. And the problem is that they're not so smart and they're not so easy to use." He spent minutes making the audience uncomfortable with the status quo before revealing Apple's answer. By the time he showed the iPhone, the audience was already convinced they needed something different.

The technique works because humans are loss-averse. We react more strongly to the threat of losing something (or discovering we've been tolerating something bad) than to the promise of gaining something new. The virus of doubt exploits this: once people realize their current experience is worse than they thought, they're primed to seek a cure.

## Why it matters

This is not manipulation. It is empathy weaponized for communication. Every product that solves a real problem has a "why" -- a pain that its customers endure daily. The virus of doubt simply makes that pain visible. Without it, even brilliant solutions can fail to register, because people have normalized their suffering. They've adapted to terrible thermostats, confusing phones, and awful smoke alarms. They don't know they need something better until someone reminds them of how bad things are.

The technique applies far beyond product launches. It works in pitches to investors, in proposals to leadership, in internal advocacy for change. Any time you need someone to take action, start with the pain they've learned to ignore.
