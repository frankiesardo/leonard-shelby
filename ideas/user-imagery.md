# User Imagery

People don't just evaluate a product. They evaluate the kind of person who uses the product — and they decide whether they want to be that kind of person. This "user imagery" can kill adoption of objectively superior products.

Ford and Edison collaborated on an early electric car in the 1900s. It was quiet, fast, had reasonable range. It failed because the attributes that made it appealing to women (no hand-cranking, no fumes, no noise) got it stereotyped as a "woman's car." Meanwhile the farm boys racing gasoline engines defined what a "real" car looked like. User imagery killed the electric car for a century.

The pattern repeats. Google Glass launched to developers — technically proficient but not cool. Electric car owners today are resented as "smug environmental tossers" by non-owners, even though most EV buyers are car enthusiasts and tech nerds, not environmentalists. Air fryer evangelists annoy people rather than converting them. Early adopters of any technology are by definition slightly weird, and weird people do not confer the social-norm reassurance needed for mass adoption.

## Why it matters

Marketing people are unusually high in openness to experience. They want to stand out. The majority of the population is much lower on openness — they want to fit in. This asymmetry is lethal: the people designing and marketing products systematically misunderstand the social anxiety that prevents normal people from trying something new.

The practical implication: you need conventional people to adopt your product before it can cross the chasm, but conventional people only adopt when they see other conventional people using it. The way to break the deadlock is not to celebrate your weird early adopters — that just confirms the product is for weirdos. Instead, find the bridge that lets normal people see themselves using it. Tesla succeeded partly because it looked like a luxury car, not a science experiment. The Prius failed on user imagery for years precisely because it looked different and signaled "I'm saving the planet" — which is social one-upmanship, not fitting in.
