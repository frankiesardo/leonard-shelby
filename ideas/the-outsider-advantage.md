# The Outsider Advantage

On InnoCentive, a platform where organizations post unsolved problems for anyone to attempt, the further a challenge was from a solver's own expertise, the more likely they were to solve it. This is not a fluke -- it is a documented, repeatable pattern. Outsiders bring knowledge from unexpected domains that insiders cannot see.

Alph Bingham, who founded InnoCentive after noticing that the cleverest solutions in his organic chemistry classes always came from knowledge outside the curriculum, called it "outside-in thinking." A lawyer solved a molecular synthesis challenge by thinking about tear gas. A chemist solved an oil spill problem by thinking about concrete vibrators from a single day of construction work years earlier. NASA's thirty-year solar particle storm problem was solved by a retired Sprint engineer using radio wave techniques.

Nicolas Appert, a "jack of all trades" who had been a candy maker, vintner, chef, brewer, and pickle maker, solved the food preservation problem that had stumped the finest scientific minds for over a century. His wide-ranging culinary experience gave him an advantage over scientists who focused on the science of preservation.

Organizations naturally default to "local search" -- using specialists and methods that have worked before. This works for incremental improvements in kind environments. For truly stuck problems, local search is the problem itself.

## Why it matters

The implication for how we organize knowledge work is profound. If you want to solve the hardest problems, you need to find ways to expose them to people who have no business looking at them. Kaggle's top machine learning competition solver said plainly: "It's very hard to win a competition just by using well-known methods. We need more creative solutions." Pedro Domingos, a machine learning professor, put it bluntly: "Knowledge is a double-edged sword. It allows you to do some things, but it also makes you blind to other things."

Harvard's Karim Lakhani found that "big innovation most often happens when an outsider who may be far away from the surface of the problem reframes the problem in a way that unlocks the solution." The more specialized organizations become, the more opportunities there are for outsiders -- a counterintuitive consequence of hyperspecialization.
