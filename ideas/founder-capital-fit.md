# Founder-Capital Fit

Nobody talks about founder-capital fit, but it's one of the most common failure modes in well-funded startups. The question: "If I give you a billion dollars, what will you do with it?" 99 times out of 100, the answer is bad news — not because of waste, but because of *decision quality*.

Infinite capital removes the forcing function that makes founders choose. When you face option A (bad) and option B (bad), the right move is to pick one and commit. But there's a hidden third option: make no choice at all. With unlimited runway, you can afford to not decide — and that's fatal. You do 10 things instead of 2, none of them work, the team is disincentivized, culture degrades.

Alex Rampell calls this moral hazard on both the primary and secondary side. Too much primary capital: the company never has to make hard decisions. Too much secondary for the founder: they're rich, comfortable, and no longer in the same boat as employees and investors. The alignment breaks. "Addition by subtraction" — sometimes a smaller team with less money, forced into creative constraint, produces dramatically better outcomes.

## Why it matters

The rare exception: founders with the Count of Monte Cristo motivation, whose drive exceeds money and who will make sharp decisions regardless of bank balance. These are the founders where raising at any price is fine — the capital won't change their behavior. For everyone else, too much money is a slow poison.
