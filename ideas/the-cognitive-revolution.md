# The Cognitive Revolution

Around 70,000 years ago, *Homo sapiens* underwent a cognitive mutation that changed the rules of the game. The key innovation was not better tools, larger brains, or superior physical strength -- it was *fictive language*: the ability to talk about things that do not exist.

Other animals communicate about real things ("Lion! Run!"). Sapiens uniquely can discuss entities that exist only in imagination: gods, nations, corporations, human rights. This seems trivial but it is world-changing, because shared fictions enable cooperation among strangers. A chimpanzee troupe maxes out at about 50 individuals; a human group held together by personal acquaintance tops out around 150 (Dunbar's number). But a group united by a shared myth -- "We are all children of God," "We are all citizens of Rome" -- can scale to millions.

The Cognitive Revolution also decoupled cultural evolution from genetic evolution. Chimpanzees cannot change their social behavior without a genetic mutation that takes millennia. Sapiens can reorganize their entire society within a generation by telling different stories. A woman born in Berlin in 1900 could live under the German Empire, the Weimar Republic, Nazism, Communism, and democratic capitalism -- five radically different imagined orders -- without any change in her DNA.

## Why it matters

This reframes what it means to be human. We are not the "rational animal" or the "tool-using animal" -- we are the *storytelling animal*. Our superpower is not intelligence in the abstract but the specific ability to create and inhabit shared fictions. Every institution, every law, every brand, every nation is a story we tell together. Understanding this makes the world more legible: when you see a conflict, ask "which stories are clashing?" When you see cooperation at scale, ask "which story is binding these people?"
