In the John Woo version of a Mexican standoff, three people stand in a triangle, each aiming at the other two. That's what's happening right now between product managers, engineers, and designers. Every coder believes AI lets them also be a PM and a designer. Every PM thinks they can now code and design. Every designer knows they can PM and code. The twist: they're all correct.

The result isn't that two roles die and one survives. It's that the boundaries between stovepipe roles dissolve and the most valuable people become triple threats — capable across all three domains, with real depth in at least one. The [talent stack](the-talent-stack.md) becomes the career strategy: be the person who can design, build, *and* decide what to build.

The same standoff is playing out in Hollywood (director/writer/actor) and will likely appear anywhere three complementary creative roles exist. In every case, AI doesn't eliminate the roles — it makes each one capable of absorbing the others. The people who lean into this convergence become what Andreessen calls "superpowered individuals." The people who cling to their stovepipe become fungible.

This is the M-shaped professional: not just T-shaped (one deep skill, broad awareness) but deep in two or three verticals, with AI filling the gaps. The [intersection of humanities and technology](the-intersection-of-humanities-and-technology.md) that Jobs talked about is no longer a rare gift — it's the default mode for anyone willing to use the tools.
