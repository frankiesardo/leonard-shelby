# Imposter Yearnings

Many of the desires and fears driving your life decisions weren't put there by you. They're imposters — someone else wearing a mask of your face, sitting on your Yearning Octopus where an authentic yearning should be.

Tim Urban's diagnostic tool is the "Why Game." You take a yearning and keep asking why until you reach the root. Three outcomes:

1. **Real skin.** You trace the why back through a chain of authentic reasoning built from your own experience and reflection. This yearning is genuinely yours.
2. **A mask.** You trace the why back to a moment when someone else installed it — a parent's expectation, a professor's offhand remark, society's definition of success, a childhood dream that no longer fits. You never independently evaluated whether you actually agree. Pull off the mask and you'll find your mom, your culture, a celebrity, or your 7-year-old self.
3. **A haze.** You can't trace it clearly either way. Might be real, might be a well-hidden mask. You'll have a hunch.

The imposters aren't just in the wants — they're in the fears too. If your father convinced you that you need a prestigious career, he probably also convinced you that your authentic desire to be a carpenter isn't "really you." He threw that authentic yearning into what Urban calls "Denial Prison" — the basement of the basement of your subconscious.

## Why it matters

You're not your parents, your friends, your generation, your past decisions, or your 7-year-old self. You're Current-Age You — the only person qualified to want what you want. But recognizing this creates a painful gap: once you evict the imposters, your octopus may look barren. You may not know who you are anymore. This feels like crisis but is actually progress — a necessary fall from naive overconfidence into wise, realistic humility.

The key distinction: do you treat external influences as *information* that your authentic self has considered and chosen to embrace? Or are the influences themselves *inside your brain*, masquerading as you?
