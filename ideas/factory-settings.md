# Factory Settings

The nature of our earliest attachment relationships becomes a set of defaults — factory settings — that our body runs on for the rest of our lives. These settings answer foundational questions: Is it safe to feel upset around people who love me? Do I trust my own emotions? Can I recover from disconnection, or is a rupture permanent? What parts of me are lovable, and what parts exist in aloneness?

The metaphor comes from a friend of Dr. Becky Kennedy's: just like a device's factory settings can be changed, our childhood defaults aren't destiny. But they're powerful, because the body generalizes early lessons aggressively. You learned to look both ways crossing the street as a child; your body won't let you skip that in Tokyo even though you've never been there. The same circuits that learned "my feelings overwhelm the people I love" in childhood fire when a 25-year-old considers whether to tell her partner about a bad day at work.

Internal Family Systems adds depth: when an emotion (say, anger) wasn't safe to express in childhood, the body develops a *protector part* — an internal voice equally as powerful as the emotion, designed to shut it down. "Don't be angry. Something's wrong with you." That protector was adaptive at five. At forty, it still thinks it's 1985, still protecting against a danger that no longer exists.

The IFS intervention: don't fight the protector. Thank it. "Thank you for keeping me safe all those years. You were right — expressing anger wasn't safe then. I'm not five anymore. I'm going to ask you to step back so I can practice something new." Compassion toward the part is what loosens its grip.

## Why it matters

Factory settings explain the uncanny repetition of patterns across completely different life situations. The girl who was told "you're overreacting" becomes the woman who overrides her gut at a bar because "I do tend to make a big deal out of nothing." The circuits are different, the contexts are different, but the factory setting is the same: other people know how I should feel better than I do.

Awareness is the first step — "huh, that's interesting, I wonder what else I could know about that" — but it's not sufficient on its own. The next steps: learn about the pattern (books, therapy, Good Inside's "reparenting" framework), then narrate it to someone you trust. "When I'm upset, I tend to pull away. I learned that early on. It's not what I want to keep doing." This is where attachment theory meets IFS meets practical change.
