# The Gulf of Execution and Evaluation

Every interaction with a designed object involves two gaps. The Gulf of Execution is the gap between what you want to do and what the object allows you to figure out. The Gulf of Evaluation is the gap between what the object did and your ability to figure out what happened. When both gulfs are wide, you stare at the thing, baffled. When both are narrow, the interaction feels invisible -- you think about the goal, not the tool.

Norman's seven stages of action map the complete cycle: (1) form a goal, (2) plan the action, (3) specify the action sequence, (4) perform it, (5) perceive the new state, (6) interpret it, (7) compare the outcome with the goal. The first four stages cross the Gulf of Execution; the last three cross the Gulf of Evaluation. Each stage is a place where design can fail.

The bridging mechanisms have names. Feedforward (signifiers, constraints, mappings) bridges the execution side -- it tells people what they can do before they do it. Feedback bridges the evaluation side -- it tells people what happened after they did it. When feedforward is absent, people guess. When feedback is absent, people repeat the action harder, which is why people push broken doors with increasing force.

## Why it matters

The two gulfs are the universal diagnostic for any product problem. When a user struggles, you can always locate the failure at a specific stage: Did they not know what action was possible (Gulf of Execution, stages 2-3)? Could they not perform it (stage 4)? Could they not perceive what happened (Gulf of Evaluation, stage 5)? Could they not interpret it (stage 6)? Could they not tell whether it matched their goal (stage 7)?

This framework applies equally to a door handle, a software interface, a management process, or a conversation. Wherever someone is trying to accomplish something through an intermediary system, both gulfs exist. The designer's job is to narrow them until the system disappears and only the goal remains.
