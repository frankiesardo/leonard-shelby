# The Socratic Method in Management

Supply question marks instead of exclamation marks. Jonah, despite knowing the answers to Alex Rogo's manufacturing problems, refuses to give them directly. Instead, he asks questions that force Alex to derive the principles himself. "What is the goal?" "What is productivity?" "Did your inventories go down?"

This is not pedagogical stubbornness. It's a deliberate strategy. As Jonah explains: "Alex, if I simply told you what to do, ultimately you would fail. You have to gain the understanding for yourself in order to make the rules work."

The reasoning: rules and answers are brittle. When conditions change — and they always change — someone who memorized the answer can't adapt. Someone who derived the answer from first principles can re-derive it for new conditions. This is the difference between training and education. Training gives you the formula. Education gives you the ability to create formulas.

## Why it matters

Telling people what to do is faster. Asking questions is slower, more frustrating, and often makes the asker look like they're being deliberately difficult. Alex repeatedly begs Jonah for direct answers. Jonah repeatedly leaves for the airport.

But look at what happened. By the end of the book, Alex's team could independently identify when the constraint had shifted, recognize that their old policies had become the new constraint, and articulate the five focusing steps — a framework Jonah never explicitly taught them. They had internalized the thinking process, not just the conclusions.

The limitation Goldratt acknowledges through Julie: pure Socratic questioning, without preparation, feels patronizing or negative. "Improvising with this method is hazardous — it's like throwing a sharpened boomerang." The method requires anticipating the other person's responses and having the right questions prepared, not just asking any question. This is what separates Socratic dialogue from interrogation.
