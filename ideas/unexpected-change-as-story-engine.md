# Unexpected Change as Story Engine

"Almost all perception is based on the detection of change," says neuroscientist Sophie Scott. "Our perceptual systems basically don't work unless there are changes to detect." In a stable environment, the brain is calm. When it detects change, neural activity surges. This is the biological basis of all storytelling: every story you will ever hear amounts to "something changed."

Unexpected change is a portal through which danger arrives to swipe at our throats. Paradoxically, it is also opportunity -- the crack through which the future arrives. This duality makes change simultaneously threatening and hopeful, which is why it triggers curiosity. The threat of change can be as powerful as change itself: "There's no terror in the bang, only in the anticipation of it" (Hitchcock).

Curiosity itself is shaped like a lowercase "n." It is weakest when people know nothing about a question and also when they are certain of the answer. Maximum curiosity -- the zone in which storytellers play -- is when people think they have some idea but are not quite sure. Brain scans show that curiosity begins as a kick in the reward system: we crave the answer the way we crave drugs or chocolate.

George Loewenstein identifies four involuntary curiosity triggers: (1) posing a question or puzzle, (2) exposure to a sequence with an anticipated but unknown resolution, (3) violation of expectations, (4) knowledge that someone else possesses information we lack. Without realizing it, Loewenstein has written a perfect description of police-procedural drama.

## Why it matters

The practical implication for any storyteller: begin with change or the threat of change. Pack it into your first sentences. Then manage the curiosity gap -- revealing just enough to keep the reader in the zone of maximum curiosity, never so much that the question is answered, never so little that they cannot form a theory.
