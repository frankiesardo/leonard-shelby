# Good Explanations

A good explanation is one that is hard to vary while still accounting for the phenomena it purports to explain. This is David Deutsch's central criterion for distinguishing genuine knowledge from myth, dogma, and ad hoc rationalization.

The ancient Greeks explained the seasons by saying that Persephone spent winter in the underworld, making her mother Demeter grieve, and returned in spring. This "explains" the seasons -- but you could swap in any gods with any emotions and the story would work equally well. It is easy to vary, therefore it is a bad explanation. The real explanation -- Earth's axial tilt causes different hemispheres to receive more or less direct sunlight throughout the year -- is hard to vary. Change the tilt and you change the seasons. Remove the tilt and you lose seasons entirely. Every detail is functional.

This criterion is not about evidence or method. Observations help us test explanations, but they do not generate them. The hard-to-vary criterion is about the *structure* of the explanation itself. A theory that can accommodate any observation explains nothing. A theory that would be ruined by changing any detail explains everything it addresses.

## Why it matters

The quest for good explanations is, according to Deutsch, the sole engine of all human progress. Science, moral philosophy, political institutions, art -- all improve when people seek explanations that are hard to vary. The Enlightenment was the moment this quest became systematic rather than sporadic. Before that, most cultures treated certain authorities -- gods, tradition, sacred texts -- as the source of explanations. The Enlightenment replaced authority with criticism: any explanation, no matter its source, must survive attempts to vary it.

This has a devastating implication for many popular ideas. "The universe is fine-tuned for life" is easy to vary (fine-tuned by whom? for what kind of life?). "We should be sustainable" is easy to vary (sustain what? at what cost?). Most political slogans, most management platitudes, most self-help advice is easy to vary. The hard-to-vary test is a universal solvent for bad thinking.
