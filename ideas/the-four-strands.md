# The Four Strands

Deutsch argues that the deepest understanding of reality comes from the unification of four fundamental theories: quantum physics (Everett's many-worlds interpretation), epistemology (Popper's conjectures and refutations), the theory of evolution (Dawkins' selfish gene), and the theory of computation (Turing's universality). Each theory, taken individually, has been simultaneously accepted in practice and ignored as an explanation of reality. Each seems cold or incomplete on its own -- physics reduces us to insignificance, evolution makes us gene vehicles, computation threatens to mechanize consciousness, epistemology seems to undermine the very idea of justified belief.

But taken together, they form something new: a non-hierarchical explanatory structure where each strand fills the gaps left by the others. The Turing principle explains why knowledge can grow. Evolution explains how. Epistemology explains what distinguishes knowledge from error. Quantum physics provides the arena in which all of it happens -- and explains why that arena is comprehensible at all.

## Why it matters

The key insight is that the structure is not a hierarchy. It is not "physics at the bottom, chemistry above, biology above that, mind at the top." Each strand contains principles that are emergent from the perspective of the other three, yet help to explain them. This is what makes the fabric of reality different from reductionism. It means that the theory of evolution is not reducible to physics, even in principle, as an *explanation* -- even though its *predictions* may be. And it means that moral and aesthetic values, which seem disconnected from science, can in principle be understood as playing a real role in the emergent explanatory structure.
