# Humor as a Serious Tool

Humor is the secret weapon of the communicator. It's secret because so few people realize that humor is often their best tool -- and sometimes their only tool -- for making an important point.

The mechanism is specific: humor heightens some crazy truth to a level where it will finally be seen as crazy. One strong editorial cartoon is worth a hundred solemn editorials. One Catch-22 is more powerful than all the books and movies that try to show war "as it is." Heller and Kubrick heightened the truth about war just enough to catch its lunacy, and we recognize it as lunacy. The joke is no joke.

Sermons are the death of the approach. The humorist's job is not to say "this is outrageous" but to find a comic device -- satire, parody, irony, lampoon, nonsense -- that makes the outrageous visible without ever saying the word. The audience does the moral reasoning themselves, which is why the point lands harder.

## Why it matters

Humor is how the unsayable gets said. In organizations, in politics, in culture -- wherever direct confrontation is risky or ineffective -- humor is a side door. A well-aimed joke can puncture pretension, expose hypocrisy, or reframe a debate in ways that a thousand earnest arguments cannot. Lyndon Johnson was brought down partly by Jules Feiffer and Art Buchwald. Senator Joseph McCarthy was partly toppled by Walt Kelly's Pogo.

The principle is not that everything should be funny. It's that when seriousness has failed to move people -- when the outlandish has become routine -- humor is often the only force that can make them see again. The humorist's creed: "I'm here and I'm involved."

Two practical constraints: humor must be about something concrete, and the humorist must maintain control. Don't exaggerate beyond recognition. Don't make the same joke twice. Trust the sophistication of the audience that gets it, and don't worry about the rest.
