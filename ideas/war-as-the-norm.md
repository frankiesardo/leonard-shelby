# War as the Norm

War is one of the constants of history, and has not diminished with civilization or democracy. In the last 3,421 years of recorded history, only 268 have seen no war.

The causes of war are the same as the causes of competition among individuals: acquisitiveness, pugnacity, and pride. The critical asymmetry is that the individual submits to restraints because the state guarantees him protection. The state itself acknowledges no substantial restraints, either because it is strong enough to defy interference, or because there is no superstate to offer it protection and no international moral code wielding effective force.

Peace, then, is not the natural state — it is an unstable equilibrium preserved only by acknowledged supremacy or equal power. The Durants stage a debate between "the general" and "the philosopher." The general argues that war is natural selection on the international plane and that the Ten Commandments must be silent when self-preservation is at stake. The philosopher argues that the unprecedented destructiveness of modern weapons introduces an element unfamiliar to history — that we must "challenge a thousand evil precedents, and dare to apply the Golden Rule to nations."

Neither voice wins. The general smiles at the philosopher's idealism; the philosopher refuses to accept the general's fatalism. The Durants leave the tension unresolved.

## Why it matters

The insight "states have our instincts without our restraints" is one of the most useful mental models in the book. It explains why nations behave in ways that would be criminal for individuals. It also clarifies what would be required to change the pattern: not moral exhortation, but the creation of a superstate strong enough to offer nations the same protection that nations offer individuals. The question is whether such a thing can exist without becoming tyranny.
