# Making Sandals

To walk a thorny road, you can pave every inch with leather — or you can make sandals. One approach tries to control the world. The other adapts yourself. The internal solution is always more resilient.

The parable comes from ancient India and Waitzkin returns to it throughout *The Art of Learning* as a unifying principle. It applies to distraction, injury, dirty opponents, anger, fear — any adversity that can't be eliminated from the environment. Instead of fighting the obstacle, you transform your relationship to it. Instead of demanding that the world cooperate, you develop the capacity to thrive regardless.

The progression has three stages. First, learn to survive adversity — flow with it like a blade of grass in a hurricane. Second, learn to use adversity as fuel — the earthquake that sharpens focus, the broken hand that makes time slow down. Third, learn to create the conditions internally — become your own earthquake, summon the intensity without needing an external catalyst.

The emotional dimension is where this gets hardest. When a dirty opponent head-butted Waitzkin in a Push Hands final, his vision went red. Ten seconds of blind rage nearly cost him the championship. The problem wasn't anger itself — anger is natural, even useful. The problem was being governed by it. Over the following year, he deliberately sought out dirty training partners. He practiced staying cool while they targeted his neck, eyes, groin. He discovered that his anger came from fear — he didn't know how to handle illegal attacks and his ego used righteous indignation as a shield. Once he learned the technical defenses, the anger dissolved. His weakness became a strength.

## Why it matters

Every high-stakes domain has its thorns. The unfair referee, the toxic colleague, the injury before the big event, the distraction you can't eliminate. The person who needs conditions to be right is permanently fragile. The person who has made sandals is permanently adaptable.

The elite performers who convert adversity into fuel do so with remarkable consistency. Reggie Miller used hostile crowds as motivation — he goaded opposing fans into hating him, then destroyed their teams. Keith Hernandez hit better after being thrown at by pitchers. Michael Jordan punished anyone who talked trash by scoring fifty. Frank Robinson was so dangerous after being hit by a pitch that the Cardinals' manager fined any pitcher who threw at him. These aren't anomalies. They're the natural result of training yourself to channel disruption into intensity.

The injured athlete is the clearest case. Most people see injury as purgatory — hovering between competing and sitting on the bench. But forced limitation can be a gift. Waitzkin broke his right hand seven weeks before the National Championships. Instead of stopping, he trained one-handed, discovering that his left arm could control two of his opponent's limbs. He used visualization to prevent atrophy. He won the Nationals slightly favoring his newly empowered left arm. The injury became one of the most creative periods of his martial arts career.

The key insight: if getting hit with adversity can produce a breakthrough, you don't need the adversity itself. You need the *effect*. A basketball player should play lefty for months. A soccer player should ban right-footed shots. Manufacture the constraint. Make the sandals before you need them.
