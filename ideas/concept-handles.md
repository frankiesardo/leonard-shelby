# Concept Handles

A concept handle is a catchy phrase that sums up a complex topic — a portable name that lets people carry an idea around in their head and use it to think with. "Concept handle" is itself a concept handle.

Scott Alexander argues this might be the most valuable thing a writer can produce. There are many things people have vague, nebulous ideas about that they can't quite put into words. Changing those into crystal-clear ideas they can manipulate and discuss with others is a big deal. Eliezer Yudkowsky is the master: "belief in belief," "semantic stopsigns," "applause lights," "Pascal's mugging" — each one an interesting idea, but more importantly an interesting idea with a short catchy name that everyone knows, so they can talk about it easily.

The mechanism: when you figure out something interesting and briefly cram it into someone's head, giving it a memorable name means it *sticks*. Without a handle, the insight evaporates. With one, it becomes a tool the reader can use to solve other problems long after they've forgotten your essay.

## Why it matters

This is the difference between writing that informs and writing that changes how people think. An essay with good arguments but no concept handles leaves readers nodding and then forgetting. An essay that coins even one sticky phrase gives the reader permanent new cognitive equipment.

The principle extends beyond writing. Product naming, framework design, team vocabulary — anywhere an abstraction needs to survive outside one person's head, it needs a handle. The best handles are short, vivid, slightly surprising, and immediately evocative of their referent.
