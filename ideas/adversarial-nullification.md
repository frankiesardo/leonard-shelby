# Adversarial Nullification

In zero-sum domains, freely available AI cancels itself out. The residual edge is entirely human.

Naval Ravikant's thought experiment: imagine every man on a date wearing an earpiece with an AI whispering what to say — a Cyrano de Bergerac bot. Then every woman gets an earpiece too, telling her which parts of what he's saying are AI-generated and which are genuine. The AI advantage evaporates. If you have a trading bot, it's nullified by every other trading bot until the remaining gain goes to the person with the human edge. If you have an AI writing your tweets, it's competing against everyone else's AI-written tweets.

The mechanism is straightforward: when a tool is freely and widely available, it stops being a differentiator. This is true of all technology eventually, but AI accelerates the cycle because adoption is nearly frictionless — there's no hardware to buy, no skill to learn. You just talk to it.

## Why it matters

This frame cuts through both AI hype and AI anxiety. In non-zero-sum domains — learning, creating, exploring, building for yourself — AI is pure upside. But in competitive, zero-sum domains — markets, dating, status, fame — AI is an arms race that converges to parity. The alpha that remains after convergence is the part that was always human: creativity, judgment, agency, genuine desire.

Early adopters do get a temporary edge, the same way early Google users looked like geniuses in social conversations. But that edge is transient. The durable advantage belongs to whoever brings something the AI can't supply.
