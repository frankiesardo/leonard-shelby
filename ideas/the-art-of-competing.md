# The Art of Competing

The surfer waits for the big wave. Not because he wants a smooth ride to the beach -- he could have that on a medium wave. He waits because the big wave draws from him his greatest effort. The obstacles are the point. Without them, he would never discover the true limits of his capacities.

Gallwey's insight: your opponent in tennis is not your enemy. Your opponent is your friend -- to the extent that he makes things difficult for you. Only by competing against you does he cooperate with you. Only by creating the greatest possible obstacles does he give you the opportunity to find out to what heights you can rise. True competition is identical with true cooperation.

This reframes everything. Instead of hoping your opponent double-faults, you actually want him to get his first serve in -- because that sharpens your return. Instead of avoiding his backhand weakness (being "nice"), you attack it relentlessly -- because that's the only way it gets better. The real nice guy is the competitor.

The deeper issue: competition goes wrong when self-image is at stake. When winning means "I'm a worthy person" and losing means "I'm not," every match becomes a life-and-death struggle. The fear of losing produces the very tension that causes losing. And even winning provides only temporary relief, because you have to keep winning to maintain your sense of worth.

The solution is to separate self-worth from performance entirely. "The value of a human being cannot be measured by performance -- or by any other arbitrary measurement." The score measures what happened on the court. It does not define you.

With self-worth decoupled from outcome, a different kind of motivation emerges: not the desperate need to prove something, but the genuine desire to discover what you're capable of. Maximum effort comes not from trying to beat the other person, but from playing every point to win -- because that's where the growth happens. The process of overcoming obstacles is more rewarding than the victory itself.

## Why it matters

Most people exist somewhere on a spectrum between two traps. One: competing desperately because their identity depends on winning. Two: refusing to compete at all because competition feels hostile. Both positions stem from the same error -- equating performance with self-worth.

The alternative: compete fully and fiercely, but for the right reasons. Not to prove you're better than someone, but to discover what you're capable of when pushed. The opponent becomes a training partner, the obstacle becomes a gift, and losing becomes data rather than devastation.

Gallwey discovered this during a tournament where he lost 6-4, 6-4 but walked off feeling he'd won. He had overcome his own nervousness. He had played with all his energy. The inner game -- the one he actually cared about -- was won. The external score was incidental.
