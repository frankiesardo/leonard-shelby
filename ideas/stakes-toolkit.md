# The Stakes Toolkit

Matthew Dicks assumes, 100% of the time, that no one wants to hear what he has to say. From that assumption, he deploys five named tools to sustain audience attention:

1. **Elephant** — Plant something big at the very beginning that makes the audience wonder what's going to happen. A big spaceship shooting at a small spaceship. It doesn't even have to be what the story is about — it just needs to make people lean in.
2. **Backpack** — Tell the audience your plan before you execute it, so they carry your hopes and dreams with them. The Ocean's 11 technique: show the heist plan, then watch it unfold.
3. **Breadcrumbs** — Drop hints about what's coming without revealing the full picture. Chekhov's gun: if it's on the wall in act one, it fires in act three.
4. **Hourglass** — When the audience is on the edge of their seat, slow time down. Load the moment with sensory details. Make them wait. This is where storytelling becomes time manipulation.
5. **Crystal Ball** — Predict a terrible future, even a false one, to make the audience worry about what's coming.

The key mistake most people make: front-loading all the stakes. Instead, build continuously through the first half, then let the second half be the roller coaster to the end.
