# Moral Codes Follow Economic Systems

Moral codes are not eternal truths descending from above — they are adaptive responses to economic conditions. When the economy changes, morality eventually follows.

The Durants trace three moral eras. In the hunting stage, pugnacity, greed, and sexual readiness were survival advantages — probably every vice was once a virtue. In the agricultural stage, new virtues displaced the old: industriousness over bravery, regularity and thrift over violence, chastity and monogamy as economic necessities. For fifteen hundred years this agricultural code — continence, early marriage, divorceless monogamy, multiple maternity — maintained itself in Christian Europe and produced some of the strongest characters in history.

Then the Industrial Revolution undermined the agricultural code. Men, women, and children left home and family to work as individuals in factories. Economic maturity came later, marriage was delayed, cities provided every stimulus for sex and every discouragement for marriage. The authority of father and mother lost its economic base. Science raised the test tube over the crosier. The old code began to die.

We are now caught in the interregnum — the relaxing interval between one moral code and the next. The agricultural code has lost its economic basis, but the industrial civilization hasn't yet forged its replacement. The Durants take comfort in noting that civilizations decay quite leisurely: Greece produced masterpieces for 250 years after moral weakening began with the Sophists. May we take as long to fall as Imperial Rome.

## Why it matters

This reframes morality from a question of individual willpower to a question of structural fit. When people moralize about cultural decline, they're usually observing the gap between a dying moral code and one that hasn't yet been born. The Durants don't celebrate this gap, but they don't panic about it either. The question is what the new code will look like — not whether there will be one.
