# Failure as Investment

Failure is not a necessary evil. It is not evil at all. It is a necessary consequence of doing something new. Catmull argues that the cost of preventing errors is almost always greater than the cost of fixing them. Organizations that make "zero defects" their goal end up with something worse than defects: they end up with people who won't take risks.

The insight is about the accounting of failure. Most organizations treat failure as pure cost -- something to be minimized. Catmull reframes it as investment. When Pixar spent two million dollars on a short film that had to be scrapped, the producer Joe Ranft said, "Better to have train wrecks with miniature trains than with real ones." The information gained from that failure was worth far more than the money spent.

Trust in this context has a specific meaning: it doesn't mean trusting that someone won't screw up. It means trusting them *even when* they screw up. The distinction matters. The first version of trust is conditional and breeds fear. The second is unconditional and breeds courage.

## Why it matters

The desire for everything to run smoothly is a false goal. It leads to measuring people by the mistakes they make rather than by their ability to solve problems. When people are evaluated by their error rate, they learn to avoid situations where errors are possible -- which means they avoid exactly the novel, uncertain, high-value work that organizations most need.

Catmull's framing inverts the usual risk calculus: the real risk isn't that people will fail. The real risk is that the fear of failure will make them stop trying. An organization that penalizes failure doesn't get fewer failures -- it gets failures that are hidden, discovered late, and far more expensive to fix.
