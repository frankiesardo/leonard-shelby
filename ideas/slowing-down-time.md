# Slowing Down Time

When you deeply internalize the fundamentals of your discipline, your unconscious handles the complexity, freeing your conscious mind to focus on less. Because you're processing less consciously, each detail gets more attention — as if the font size on a page got bigger. Time appears to slow down. You see frames your opponent doesn't even know exist.

The mechanism is *chunking*: the mind's ability to compress large amounts of information into clusters bound by patterns. Studies on chess players showed that stronger players didn't have better raw memory — they had better pattern recognition. They saw five or six pieces as a single unit defined by their interrelationships (attack, defense, tension). A Grandmaster consciously looks at *less* than an expert, but sees *much more*, because chunks of information flow through the unconscious while the conscious mind zooms in on what matters.

Think of it like a camera's shutter speed. Years of training effectively shifts the heavy processing to the unconscious — a high-speed processor. Now the conscious mind, handling less data, revs its shutter speed from 4 frames per second to 300. The trained mind isn't necessarily working faster. It's working more efficiently, which means the conscious mind has less to deal with. Each moment contains hundreds of frames where an untrained mind sees only a few.

Waitzkin experienced this when his hand broke during a martial arts competition. Pain jolted his perception into an extreme narrow focus. His opponent's attacks, objectively lightning-fast, felt like clouds gently rolling toward him, easily dodged. The trained version of this state shares the narrow conscious focus, but instead of ignoring surrounding information, it converts that information into unconsciously integrated data.

## Why it matters

This explains why masters in any field can appear mystical. A profoundly refined martial artist operates within segments of time too small for untrained minds to perceive. A veteran quarterback reads the defense in a blink that takes a rookie three seconds. A senior developer spots the bug by glancing at code that a junior would debug for hours.

It also explains why you can't skip the fundamentals. Time slows down *because* the fundamentals have been internalized. There's no shortcut — the chunking, the carved neural pathways, the thousands of hours of pattern recognition are the prerequisite. The perception is the reward, not the method.

The practical path: internalize techniques through [Making Smaller Circles](making-smaller-circles.md) until they require zero conscious attention. Then train under pressure so the unconscious handles increasingly complex scenarios. Over time, your conscious mind becomes free to notice the blink of an eye, the shift of weight, the exhalation — the micro-moments where competitions are decided.
