# Homework for Life

Every night, look back on the day and write down one moment that would be worth telling as a story — even if it's not really worth telling. Use a spreadsheet: date in column A, the moment in column B (one line, the width of a screen). That's it. No journaling, no long-form writing, no pressure. Just one moment.

Matthew Dicks started finding 1.8 moments per day. After 12 years, he finds 7.6 — not because his life got more interesting, but because his lens improved. Only about 10% of recorded moments become actual stories, but the other 90% are equally valuable because they hold onto your days. The practice also cracks open old memories: noticing a student find confidence connects to other forgotten moments about confidence, and those days return to you.

The deeper insight: time doesn't fly. It goes by unaccounted. If you can only remember 89 of 365 days in a year, of course it feels fast. Homework for Life is the refusal to let days disappear.
