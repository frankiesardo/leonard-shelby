# The Doorman Fallacy

A five-star hotel has a doorman. A management consulting firm defines his function as "opens the door," replaces him with an automatic door mechanism and an infrared sensor, and claims $40,000 in annual cost savings. Two years later the hotel is a catastrophe. The rack rate has fallen off a cliff because the doorman was doing dozens of things that were never captured in the "opens the door" definition: security (no vagrants sleeping in the doorway), hailing taxis, handling luggage, recognizing regular guests by name, providing a visible signal of status and hospitality to arriving visitors.

The costs were on a spreadsheet. The value creation was not. The consultants took credit for the savings and were long gone by the time the consequences materialized.

This is a general pattern: when you define a role by its most visible, measurable function, you systematically miss the tacit, relational, and contextual value that a human provides. The narrower your definition, the more confident you are that automation is a good trade — and the more you destroy.

## Why it matters

The doorman fallacy is the meta-error behind most "digital transformation" projects, most call center automation, and most AI-replaces-humans arguments. The tech pitch is always the same: "Person X does this function. We will replace it with algorithm Y." The pitch works because cost savings are concrete and immediate while value destruction is diffuse and delayed. By the time the damage shows up, the people who made the decision have moved on.

The antidote is not to reject automation but to resist the temptation to define roles narrowly. Before replacing anything, ask: what else is this person doing that we haven't measured? What would we lose if the human component disappeared entirely? Roger Martin's formulation: "Any idiot can cut costs. What takes real skill is cutting costs in a way that doesn't destroy value."
