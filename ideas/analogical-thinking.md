# Analogical Thinking

The most creative problem solvers reason by analogy across distant domains, not by digging deeper into their own. Kepler reinvented astronomy by analogizing planetary motion to light, heat, odor, magnetism, and boats propelled by oars. He did not arrive at his laws by more careful observation of the sky -- he arrived by thinking about things that had nothing to do with the sky.

Psychologist Dedre Gentner distinguishes surface analogies (this looks like that) from deep structural analogies (this works like that). Surface analogies come to mind easily and are fine for familiar problems. But for novel problems in wicked environments, deep structural analogies from distant domains are what unlock solutions.

Duncker's radiation problem demonstrates this vividly. Only 10% of people solve it cold. Given a single distant analogy (a military fortress story), 30% solve it. Given two distant analogies, 50%. Given two analogies plus a nudge to use them, 80%. The analogies are doing immense cognitive work -- as much as being told the underlying principle directly. But most people don't spontaneously reach for them.

Kevin Dunbar studied molecular biology labs and found that the most productive ones made analogies constantly -- one every four minutes in some meetings. The labs with the most diverse professional backgrounds produced the most and most varied analogies, and were the most likely to turn unexpected findings into breakthroughs. A lab staffed entirely with E. coli specialists got stuck on a problem that a diverse lab solved in a single meeting.

## Why it matters

Our default mode is "local search" -- we look for solutions within the domain of the problem, using methods that have worked before. This is fine for kind environments. In wicked environments, it is a trap. The more deeply you're embedded in a domain, the harder it is to see solutions that reside in adjacent or distant fields.

The private equity investors in Lovallo's study estimated their own project's return at 50% higher than conceptually similar projects -- until they were forced to make analogies to outside cases, at which point they slashed their estimates. The inside view is always overconfident. The outside view -- systematically generating analogies from diverse reference classes -- is the corrective.

Breadth of training predicts breadth of transfer. The more contexts in which something is learned, the more the learner creates abstract models rather than relying on particular examples. This is the cognitive foundation of creativity.
