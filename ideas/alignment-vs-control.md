# Alignment vs Control

The most important distinction in AI safety discourse, articulated clearly by Max Tegmark: **control** and **alignment** are not the same thing, and most people who think they want safety actually only want alignment.

**Control** means you have the power to shut it down. The machine does what you say, and if it doesn't, you can stop it.

**Alignment** means the AI pursues goals compatible with human flourishing — but you may not be able to stop it. An aligned superintelligence is the boss of Earth but has decided to be nice to us.

The distinction matters because most AI labs are building for alignment, not control. OpenAI's published goal was explicitly to "replace all valuable human work." The assumption baked in is: if we can make it want the right things, it doesn't matter that we can't control it. Tegmark argues this is a catastrophic error. A superintelligence you can't shut down but which has benign intentions is one clever prompt injection, one bad training signal, or one misaligned successor away from catastrophe.

## The Greek Tragedy

The geopolitical version of this trap: the US and China both want to win the AI race to gain control. But "winning" the race means building something smarter than any human institution, and no nation-state can actually *control* something smarter than itself. The path is: race → company becomes more powerful than government → government loses control of the machine. Politicians think they're in the dominance race; they're actually in the superintelligence race, and these are not the same thing.

## Why the distinction matters for governance

Harari's policy implication: ban AI legal personhood. Not because AI research is bad but because legal personhood without control is exactly alignment-without-control at a societal level. An AI entity that can own bank accounts, sue in court, and donate to politicians without any human behind the decisions is a controllability problem dressed in a corporate law problem.

The pharma model offers a template: companies prove safety before they can sell. Every other industry operates this way. The reason we can trust restaurants and medicines is that the burden of proof runs from company to regulator, not the other way around. AI should be no different.
