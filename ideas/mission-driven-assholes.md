# Mission-Driven Assholes

There is a category of difficult person that gets confused with genuine assholes but whose motivations are fundamentally different. Mission-driven "assholes" are crazy passionate, speak frankly, trample office politics, and steamroll the delicate social order. They are neither easygoing nor easy to work with. But unlike real assholes, they care. They give a damn. They listen. They push their team to be better, often against the team's will.

The test is motivation. A mission-driven "asshole" is driven by the work -- making the product better, serving the customer, fulfilling the mission. A real asshole (political, controlling, or garden-variety) is driven by ego -- taking credit, maintaining power, punishing dissent. The mission-driven variety can be reasoned with: ask why they're so fired up, present a better approach, and if it serves the customer, they'll change their mind. Eventually. Real assholes won't.

Fadell places himself in this category and makes no apologies. He is loud, passionate, and refuses to let things slide. Steve Jobs was the same -- a passionate hurricane. "The best thing for the product would always win out eventually because the product was all that mattered."

A reliable signal: look at the mythos around them. There are always a few choice stories, and the people who worked with them closely are always telling everyone they're not that bad, really. Most tellingly, the team ultimately trusts them and looks back on the experience fondly, because they pushed the team to do the best work of their lives.

## Why it matters

Organizations are full of people who are difficult in various ways. Conflating them all as "toxic" or "assholes" loses crucial information. Political assholes build coalitions to protect themselves. Controlling assholes strangle creativity to feed their ego. Asshole-assholes contribute nothing. Mission-driven "assholes" create the best products.

The practical advice for dealing with each type is completely different. With a mission-driven "asshole": push back, bring data, present a better idea. They'll respect you more for standing up. With a political asshole: band together with others, build a counternarrative, protect the work. With a controlling asshole: try kindness, then ignore them, then go around them, then quit.

The distinction also matters for self-awareness. If you find yourself pushing people hard, you need to be honest about your motivation. Is it the work? Or is it your ego? The answer determines whether you're a leader or a bully.
