# Build

Tony Fadell's career memoir doubles as a densely packed advice encyclopedia. The man who led the teams behind the iPod, iPhone, and Nest Learning Thermostat distills thirty years of building products and companies into a book organized roughly along the arc of a career: from getting your first job, to managing people, to building products, to starting companies, to running them, to leaving them. What makes Build valuable is not any single thesis but its breadth -- Fadell has been an individual contributor, a first-time manager, a CTO, a VP at Apple under Steve Jobs, and a founder/CEO at Nest. Each failure (General Magic, Philips, RealNetworks, Fuse) deposited a layer of scar tissue that the next success (iPod, iPhone, Nest) was built upon. The book's voice is blunt, energetic, and allergic to corporate euphemism. It reads like advice from a loud, experienced uncle who genuinely wants you to avoid his mistakes.

## Key ideas

**The whole customer journey matters more than the product.** Your product is only a fraction of the customer experience. From the moment someone hears about your brand to the moment they throw your product away, every touchpoint is your company. At Nest, the thermostat itself was maybe 10% of the experience -- the app, the website, the installation, the packaging, the support articles accounted for the rest. The screwdriver they included in every box was never just a tool; it was a marketing asset that lived in kitchen drawers for years, reminding people of Nest every time they opened it. Prototype the entire journey, not just the thing. [See: [The Whole Customer Journey](../../ideas/the-whole-customer-journey.md)]

**Start with "why" and infect people with the virus of doubt.** Before telling anyone what your product does, remind them of the pain. Steve Jobs's iPhone keynote spent time making the audience annoyed at their existing phones before revealing the solution. This technique -- the [virus of doubt](../../ideas/the-virus-of-doubt.md) -- primes people to want what you're offering. "Why" drives the "what" -- in product development, in storytelling, in pitching investors, in hiring.

**Most great decisions are opinion-driven, not data-driven.** Data is essential but it can become a crutch. When creating something genuinely new, there is no data -- no one has made this before. Analysis paralysis kills more products than bad ideas do. At some point you have to follow your gut, explain your reasoning to the team, and take responsibility for what comes next. The key is knowing which kind of decision you're facing and not trying to turn an opinion into a data problem. [See: [Opinion-Driven Decisions](../../ideas/opinion-driven-decisions.md)]

**The best ideas are painkillers, not vitamins.** Vitamins are nice-to-have. Painkillers solve a problem you feel every day. The Nest thermostat was a painkiller -- Fadell was literally freezing in his Tahoe cabin, spending vacations elbow-deep in wiring trying to hack a remote thermostat. The best startups begin with founders solving their own daily frustrations. Let the idea chase you: if after months of research you can't stop thinking about it, it's probably worth doing. [See: [Painkillers Not Vitamins](../../ideas/painkillers-not-vitamins.md)]

**It takes three generations to get a product and business right.** V1 is shipping your prototype to early adopters and finding product/market fit. V2 is fixing what you screwed up and reaching gross margins. V3 is refining the business to reach net margins. The iPod, iPhone, and Nest all followed this pattern. Too many founders expect profitability from day one, and too many investors demand it. But you make the product, you fix the product, you build the business. Every time. [See: [Three Generations to Profitability](../../ideas/three-generations-to-profitability.md)]

**Deadlines are the best creative constraint.** You need heartbeats -- regular, predictable rhythms that drive teams toward milestones. External deadlines (Christmas, conferences) are the most powerful because they can't be moved. Internal heartbeats (team sprints, project check-ins) keep everyone in sync. Without them, you get General Magic: four years of perfectionism that produced a product nobody wanted. With them, you get the iPod: ten months from green light to launch. [See: [Heartbeats and Handcuffs](../../ideas/heartbeats-and-handcuffs.md)]

**Distinguish mission-driven "assholes" from real ones.** Not everyone who's difficult to work with is toxic. Mission-driven "assholes" are passionate, loud, unrelenting -- but they care about the work, not their ego. They'll listen to reason if you push back with data or a better idea. Real assholes -- political, controlling, or just plain mean -- protect themselves, not the product. The test: can they be reasoned with? Do they give credit? Do their teams look back on the experience fondly? Steve Jobs was a mission-driven "asshole." The difference matters enormously. [See: [Mission-Driven Assholes](../../ideas/mission-driven-assholes.md)]

**Seed crystals attract talent.** Your first hires should be people so good and so well-respected that they create their own gravity, pulling in more great people behind them. At Nest, each seed crystal built out an entire arm of the organization. This is how you build a world-class team without a recruiting department -- through the reputation and relationships of your earliest people. [See: [Seed Crystals](../../ideas/seed-crystals.md)]

## Key quotes

> "The only failure in your twenties is inaction. The rest is trial and error."

> "Your product isn't only your product. It's the whole user experience -- a chain that begins when someone learns about your brand for the first time and ends when your product disappears from their life."

> "If you're not solving a real problem, you can't start a revolution."

> "Nothing in the world is ever 100 percent sure. ... You just have to take that first step into the unknown. Combine everything you've learned and take your best guess at what's going to happen next. That's what life is. Most decisions we make are data-informed, but they're not data-made."

> "You make the product. You fix the product. You build the business. Every product. Every company. Every time."
