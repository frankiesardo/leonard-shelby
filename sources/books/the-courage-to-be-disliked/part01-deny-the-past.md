# Part 1: Deny the Past (The First Night)

The opening night establishes Adler's most provocative claim: teleology over etiology. Where Freud says "you are who you are because of what happened to you," Adler says "you are who you are because of the goals you are currently pursuing." The past does not determine the present. Trauma, in the Adlerian view, does not exist as a causal force -- we choose what meaning to give our experiences, and that meaning serves present purposes.

The philosopher introduces this through a series of escalating challenges. A reclusive friend who won't leave his room: Freud would say childhood abuse caused it. Adler says the man has the *goal* of not going out, and manufactures anxiety to achieve it. A coffee shop outburst: the young man thinks his anger was involuntary. The philosopher shows that anger is a tool fabricated to make the waiter submit -- proven by the mother who can switch her angry voice to a polite one the instant the phone rings.

The first night also introduces the distinction between "lifestyle" (Adler's term for what we'd call personality or character) and fate. Your lifestyle is something you chose, around age ten, and something you can choose again. The reason people don't change is not that they can't, but that they have chosen not to -- because the known unhappiness feels safer than the unknown alternative.

## Key ideas

- **Teleology vs. etiology.** People are not determined by past causes but moved by present goals. The recluse doesn't stay inside because of trauma; he stays inside because staying inside serves a purpose (attention, safety, avoidance of judgment). This is the Adlerian inversion of Freudian thinking.
- **Emotions as tools.** Anger, sadness, and anxiety are not involuntary eruptions but instruments fabricated to achieve interpersonal goals. You get angry *in order to* shout, not because you were provoked beyond control.
- **Lifestyle as choice.** What we call "personality" or "disposition" is actually a set of choices made early in life. Because they were chosen, they can be re-chosen. The reason most people don't change is not inability but unwillingness -- the courage to be unhappy in a new way is harder to summon than the comfort of familiar suffering.
- **The world is subjective.** "None of us live in an objective world, but instead in a subjective world that we ourselves have given meaning to." The issue is not how the world is, but how you are.

## Key quotes

> "No experience is in itself a cause of our success or failure. We do not suffer from the shock of our experiences -- the so-called trauma -- but instead we make out of them whatever suits our purposes." (Adler, quoted by the philosopher)

> "It's not that you can't do it. You are just choosing not to do it."

> "If we focus only on past causes and try to explain things solely through cause and effect, we end up with determinism. Because what this says is that our present and our future have already been decided by past occurrences, and are unalterable."

## Q&A

**How does Adler's teleology relate to Frankl's "last human freedom"?**
Both deny that external circumstances determine internal response. Frankl says the freedom to choose your attitude cannot be taken away. Adler says the same thing from the other direction: you are always already choosing your attitude, whether you acknowledge it or not. The difference is emphasis -- Frankl focuses on the freedom itself, Adler on the *purpose* behind the choice.

**Isn't denying trauma irresponsible?**
Adler isn't saying bad things don't happen or that their influence isn't strong. He's saying the influence is not deterministic. Two people can experience the same abuse and construct opposite lifestyles from it. The meaning you assign to the experience is the decisive factor, and that meaning is under your control.
