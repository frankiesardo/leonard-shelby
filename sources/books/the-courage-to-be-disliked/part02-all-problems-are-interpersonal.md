# Part 2: All Problems Are Interpersonal Relationship Problems (The Second Night)

The second night makes Adler's boldest structural claim: every problem you have is, at root, an interpersonal relationship problem. Remove all other people from the universe, and your problems vanish. Loneliness requires other people. Inferiority requires comparison. Even the feeling that "I'm not good enough" is meaningless without a social context to be not-good-enough *in*.

The philosopher walks the youth through Adler's theory of inferiority. Feelings of inferiority are universal and subjective -- they are assessments, not facts. Height, income, education -- none of these are objectively inferior; they only feel that way when measured against someone else. This is healthy: the "pursuit of superiority" is the engine of growth. But it goes wrong in two ways. An *inferiority complex* turns the feeling into an excuse ("I can't succeed because I didn't go to university"). A *superiority complex* turns it into a performance ("I'm successful, so I'm worth more than you"). Both are evasions.

The night also introduces Adler's three "life tasks" -- work, friendship, and love -- as the arenas in which interpersonal problems play out. And it introduces the concept of "life-lies": the stories we tell ourselves to avoid these tasks. "I can't get close to people because I was hurt before" is a life-lie. The hurt is real; the causal claim is the lie.

## Key ideas

- **All problems are interpersonal relationship problems.** This is not metaphor. Adler means it literally. If you were the last person on Earth, you would have no reason to feel inferior, no competition, no rejection. Every psychological problem requires a social context.
- **Feelings of inferiority are subjective assumptions.** Being short is a fact. Feeling inferior about it is an interpretation. The same height that feels like a disadvantage in basketball feels like an advantage in a low-ceilinged submarine. The feeling lives in the comparison, not the trait.
- **Inferiority complex vs. superiority complex.** An inferiority complex uses feelings of inferiority as an *excuse* not to act. A superiority complex uses external markers (status, wealth, name-dropping) to cover up feelings of inferiority. Both are failures of courage.
- **Life is not a competition.** When you see others as competitors, you see the world as hostile. When someone else succeeds, it feels like your loss. The shift is to see others as comrades walking the same flat plane, not rivals on a ladder.
- **Life-lies.** The excuses we construct to avoid life tasks. "I would have been a great writer if not for my day job." "I can't love anyone because I was betrayed." The function of the lie is not to explain the past but to protect the present self from the risk of change.

## Key quotes

> "Let me repeat: all problems are interpersonal relationship problems."

> "Feelings of inferiority are subjective assumptions. They are not objective facts."

> "An inferiority complex is an excuse. One is merely using one's feeling of inferiority as an excuse."

> "If there is no competition, there is no need for winning or losing, and one does not have to fear losing."

## Q&A

**How does "all problems are interpersonal" connect to Manson's "responsibility vs. fault"?**
Manson separates fault (who caused it) from responsibility (who deals with it). Adler goes further: the interpersonal framing means you always have agency in the relationship dynamics that produce your suffering. You can't change the other person, but you can change the relationship -- by changing yourself within it.

**Isn't saying "life is not a competition" naive?**
Adler isn't denying that competitive structures exist. He's saying that *framing your existence* as a competition poisons your relationships. You can do excellent work without needing to beat someone. The pursuit of superiority (growth) is healthy; the pursuit of superiority *over others* (competition) is not.
