# Part 3: Separation of Tasks (The Third Night)

The third night delivers Adler's most practical tool: the separation of tasks. For every problem, ask: "Whose task is this?" The answer is determined by who bears the consequences. A child's studying is the child's task -- the child receives the results. A parent's worry about the child's studying is the parent's task. When you intrude on someone else's task, or allow them to intrude on yours, you get interpersonal conflict.

This principle demolishes the desire for recognition. Why do you want to be liked? Because you've made "being liked" your task, when in fact whether someone likes you is *their* task. You cannot control it, and trying to control it is an intrusion. The philosopher extends this to freedom itself: real freedom is not freedom from organizations or obligations. Real freedom is the willingness to be disliked. The person who cannot tolerate being disliked is a slave to other people's judgments -- they are "rolling downhill" following natural inclinations. The person who separates tasks and accepts that dislike is the other person's business is "climbing uphill." That climb is freedom.

The phrase that gives the book its title appears here: "The courage to be happy also includes the courage to be disliked."

## Key ideas

- **[Separation of tasks](../../../ideas/separation-of-tasks.md).** Ask "who ultimately bears the consequences?" to determine whose task it is. Do not intrude on other people's tasks. Do not allow others to intrude on yours. This single principle resolves the majority of interpersonal conflicts.
- **Do not live to satisfy the expectations of others.** You are not living to satisfy your parents' expectations, your boss's expectations, or society's expectations. Those expectations are other people's tasks. Meeting them is not your obligation.
- **Desire for recognition makes you unfree.** The need for approval is a chain. If you must be liked to feel okay, you will never act freely. You will shape every word and action to manage other people's responses -- which are, by definition, outside your control.
- **Freedom is being disliked by other people.** Not *wanting* to be disliked, but not *fearing* it. The cost of freedom in interpersonal relationships is that some people will disapprove. The person who can pay this cost can live authentically. The person who cannot pays a higher cost: a life of performance and suppression.
- **Intervention vs. assistance.** Intervention is intruding on someone else's task and trying to control the outcome. Assistance is offering help while respecting the separation -- "you can lead a horse to water, but you can't make him drink."

## Key quotes

> "In general, all interpersonal relationship troubles are caused by intruding on other people's tasks, or having one's own tasks intruded on."

> "Freedom is being disliked by other people."

> "It is not 'for your own good', but for the parents'. And it is because the child senses this deception that he rebels."

> "The courage to be happy also includes the courage to be disliked. When you have gained that courage, your interpersonal relationships will all at once change into things of lightness."

## Q&A

**How does separation of tasks relate to Covey's Circle of Influence?**
They are nearly the same idea in different vocabularies. Covey says: focus on what you can control (Circle of Influence), not what you can't (Circle of Concern). Adler says: focus on your tasks, not other people's tasks. Both recognize that most suffering comes from trying to control what is not yours to control. The key difference is that Adler makes it explicitly interpersonal -- it's not just about controlling "circumstances" but about not intruding on other *people's* domains.

**Isn't this just selfishness?**
No. Separation of tasks is not indifference. You still care. You still offer assistance. You still tell the child that studying matters. But you do not force, manipulate, or attach your sense of self-worth to their response. The line is: I will help, but I will not control.
