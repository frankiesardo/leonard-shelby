# Drive

Daniel Pink's thesis is disarmingly simple: the carrot-and-stick model of motivation -- what he calls Motivation 2.0 -- worked for the routine, algorithmic tasks of the twentieth century but is increasingly incompatible with the creative, heuristic work that defines the twenty-first. Drawing on decades of research by Edward Deci, Richard Ryan, Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, Carol Dweck, Teresa Amabile, and others, Pink argues that human beings have a third drive beyond biological needs and external rewards: an innate desire to direct their own lives, to get better at things that matter, and to be part of something larger than themselves. The book's power is less in any single finding than in its relentless accumulation of evidence -- from Harlow's monkeys to Deci's Soma puzzles to the Madurai performance experiments -- that what science knows about motivation and what business does are wildly out of sync. Pink distills it all into a clean operating-system metaphor: upgrade from Motivation 2.0 (extrinsic rewards and punishments) to Motivation 3.0 (autonomy, mastery, and purpose), and both performance and satisfaction improve. It reads like a well-organized brief for the prosecution of every incentive plan, performance bonus, and pay-for-performance scheme in corporate history.

## Key ideas

**Extrinsic rewards can destroy the very behavior they're designed to encourage.** Pink calls this the [Sawyer Effect](../../ideas/the-sawyer-effect.md) -- after Tom Sawyer's trick of making fence-painting look desirable. Contingent, expected, if-then rewards ("If you do this, you'll get that") narrow focus, extinguish intrinsic motivation, crush creativity, crowd out altruism, encourage shortcuts, become addictive, and foster myopic thinking. Deci's Soma puzzle experiment proved it in 1969: pay people to play, then stop paying them, and they play less than people who were never paid at all. A meta-analysis of 128 studies confirmed the pattern. The Swedish blood donation study showed it for prosocial behavior. The Madurai experiment showed higher financial incentives led to worse performance on creative tasks. The seven deadly flaws of carrots and sticks are not edge cases -- they're the default outcome for any work that requires thought.

**Autonomy is the sun around which motivation orbits.** Deci and Ryan's self-determination theory identifies three innate psychological needs -- autonomy, competence, and relatedness -- but autonomy is first among equals. Pink breaks it into [four dimensions](../../ideas/autonomy-over-the-four-ts.md): task (what you do), time (when you do it), technique (how you do it), and team (whom you do it with). Atlassian's FedEx Days -- 24-hour bursts where engineers work on anything they want -- produced some of the company's best features. Google's 20 percent time birthed Gmail and Google News. Best Buy's ROWE (results-only work environment) increased productivity 35% and slashed turnover. These aren't perks. They're structural recognitions that human beings are designed to be players, not pawns.

**Mastery is an asymptote -- you approach it but never reach it.** The pursuit of mastery is the second element of Type I behavior, and it obeys [three laws](../../ideas/mastery-as-asymptote.md). First, mastery is a mindset: Dweck's [incremental theory of intelligence](../../ideas/incremental-theory-of-intelligence.md) -- the belief that ability grows through effort -- is its prerequisite. Entity theorists avoid challenges; incremental theorists seek them. Second, mastery is a pain: Ericsson's deliberate practice and Duckworth's grit research show that the path involves years of grueling, focused effort. Third, mastery is an asymptote: like Cezanne stalking the perfect synthesis, you get closer and closer but never arrive. The joy is in the pursuit. Flow -- Csikszentmihalyi's state of optimal challenge -- is the engine that pulls you through the rough parts.

**[Goldilocks tasks](../../ideas/goldilocks-tasks.md) are the gateway to flow and mastery.** When what you must do exceeds your capabilities, you get anxiety. When it falls short, you get boredom. But when the match is just right -- a notch or two beyond your current abilities -- you enter flow. Smart organizations engineer these conditions deliberately. Stefan Falk at Ericsson required managers to meet with employees monthly to calibrate challenge levels. Green Cargo became profitable for the first time in 125 years after adopting this approach. Csikszentmihalyi's deprivation experiment showed that removing flow-producing activities from people's lives for just 48 hours produced symptoms resembling generalized anxiety disorder. Flow isn't a luxury -- it's oxygen for the soul.

**Before motivation can work, [baseline rewards](../../ideas/baseline-rewards.md) must be adequate and fair.** Pay people enough -- internally equitable and externally competitive -- to take the issue of money off the table. Then get it out of sight. Once baseline is met, additional financial incentives don't boost creative performance; they often impair it. The best use of money in Motivation 3.0 is to remove money as a factor. Pay more than average (Akerlof and Yellen found this actually reduces costs through better retention and morale). Use wide-ranging metrics that are hard to game. And for creative work, shift from if-then rewards to unexpected "now that" rewards -- recognition after the fact rather than bribes before it.

**Purpose is the third leg of the tripod, and without it the other two wobble.** Autonomous people working toward mastery perform at very high levels. But those who do so in service of something larger achieve even more. A University of Rochester study tracked graduates with "profit goals" (wealth, fame) versus "purpose goals" (helping others, learning). Those who achieved their profit goals were no happier than in college -- and showed increases in anxiety and depression. Purpose goals led to higher satisfaction and well-being. Pink calls the emerging organizational forms -- B Corporations, L3Cs, social businesses -- "purpose maximizers" that are incompatible with the old operating system. The profit motive is a powerful fuel but an insufficient one. Purpose provides the activation energy for living.

## Key quotes

> "When money is used as an external reward for some activity, the subjects lose intrinsic interest for the activity."

> "The secret to high performance isn't our biological drive or our reward-and-punishment drive, but our third drive -- our deep-seated desire to direct our own lives, to extend and expand our abilities, and to live a life of purpose."

> "Control leads to compliance; autonomy leads to engagement."

> "The desire to do something because you find it deeply satisfying and personally challenging inspires the highest levels of creativity, whether it's in the arts, sciences, or business."

> "Effort is one of the things that gives meaning to life. Effort means you care about something, that something is important to you and you are willing to work for it."

> "One cannot lead a life that is truly excellent without feeling that one belongs to something greater and more permanent than oneself."

> "Human beings have an innate inner drive to be autonomous, self-determined, and connected to one another. And when that drive is liberated, people achieve more and live richer lives."
