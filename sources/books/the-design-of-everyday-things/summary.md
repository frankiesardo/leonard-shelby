# The Design of Everyday Things

Don Norman's foundational design text starts with a question that sounds trivial and turns out to be profound: why can't you figure out which way a door opens? The answer implicates nearly everything about how humans interact with the built world. The core argument is that when people struggle with products, the fault almost always lies in the design, not the person. Norman builds an entire framework around this claim -- affordances, signifiers, constraints, mappings, feedback, conceptual models -- giving designers (and anyone who builds things for other people) a vocabulary for diagnosing why something doesn't work and a toolkit for making it better. What makes the book last is that Norman grounds everything in cognitive science: the seven stages of action, the distinction between conscious and subconscious processing, the three levels of emotional response (visceral, behavioral, reflective), the taxonomy of human error (slips vs. mistakes). The revised edition updates the examples but the psychology hasn't changed, because people haven't changed. The book is, at bottom, a plea: stop blaming users, start designing for how humans actually think and act.

## Key ideas

**Good design is invisible; bad design screams.** [Affordances and signifiers](../../ideas/affordances-and-signifiers.md) are the core vocabulary. An affordance is what an object allows you to do (a chair affords sitting); a signifier is what tells you what you can do (a flat plate on a door says "push"). Norman's crucial distinction: affordances exist whether or not you perceive them, but only perceived affordances matter for design. Signifiers are what make affordances visible. When a door needs a sign saying "push," the signifier has failed -- the hardware itself should communicate the action. This applies far beyond doors. Every interface, every product, every tool either communicates its operation or forces people to guess.

**The gap between intention and action is a design problem.** Norman frames all human-technology interaction through two [gulfs](../../ideas/the-gulf-of-execution-and-evaluation.md): the Gulf of Execution (how do I figure out how to operate this?) and the Gulf of Evaluation (how do I figure out what happened?). Both gulfs are bridged by the seven stages of action -- goal, plan, specify, perform, perceive, interpret, compare -- and seven corresponding design principles: discoverability, feedback, conceptual models, affordances, signifiers, mappings, and constraints. Feedforward bridges execution; feedback bridges evaluation. When either gulf is wide, people blame themselves. When both are narrow, the product feels natural.

**People run on [conceptual models](../../ideas/conceptual-models.md), and most of them are wrong.** A conceptual model is the story a person tells themselves about how something works. The thermostat example is Norman's favorite: people who think a thermostat is a valve (more heat comes out when you turn it higher) will crank it to maximum to heat a room faster, wasting energy and overshooting the target. The thermostat is actually a switch -- on or off, full blast or nothing. The design gave no clue to the correct model. Good design projects the right conceptual model; bad design either projects the wrong one or projects nothing at all. This connects deeply to [The Brain as Model-Maker](../../ideas/the-brain-as-model-maker.md) -- we cannot not build models. The question is whether the design guides us toward a model that matches reality.

**Put [knowledge in the world](../../ideas/knowledge-in-the-world.md), not just in the head.** Humans have limited working memory and are terrible at precise recall. Good design compensates by embedding the information people need into the environment itself -- through visible affordances, natural mappings, physical constraints, and clear signifiers. The less people have to remember, the fewer errors they make. Norman's light switch solution -- mounting switches on a floor plan so each switch sits where its light is located -- is a perfect example of natural mapping replacing memorization. Constraints (physical, cultural, semantic, logical) further reduce the need for knowledge in the head by eliminating wrong actions before they happen.

**[Human error is almost always design error.](../../ideas/human-error-is-design-error.md)** When 75-95% of industrial accidents are attributed to "human error," Norman argues the real error is in the system. His taxonomy is precise: slips (correct intention, wrong execution -- capture errors, description-similarity errors, mode errors) versus mistakes (wrong intention -- rule-based, knowledge-based, memory-lapse). Each type has different design implications. The root cause analysis principle -- keep asking "why" until you reach the system, not the person -- is the core discipline. The Swiss cheese model shows that accidents require multiple failures to align; no single human error should ever be able to cause catastrophe. Blame and punish is satisfying but useless. Redesign the system.

**[Forcing functions](../../ideas/forcing-functions.md) are the strongest form of error prevention.** When a situation is constrained so that failure at one stage prevents the next step, errors become physically impossible. Interlocks (microwave won't run with the door open), lock-ins (the "save before closing?" dialog), and lockouts (fire stairway gates that prevent descent past the ground floor) are all forcing functions. Norman also champions the Japanese poka-yoke philosophy -- adding simple physical constraints, jigs, or signifiers that make errors impossible. The ATM that returns your card before dispensing cash. The gas cap that clicks when it's tight. The power plug shaped so it fits only one way. These aren't clever tricks; they are the mature response to the fact that humans will always err.

**Design thinking means solving the right problem.** Norman's double-diamond model (diverge to find the right problem, converge on it, then diverge to find the right solution, converge on that) is a discipline against the universal temptation to jump to solutions. Human-centered design -- observe, ideate, prototype, test, iterate -- sounds obvious but is routinely undermined by schedule pressure, competitive forces, and the gap between what people say they want and what they actually need. The key insight: never solve the problem you're given. Always ask what the real problem is. The quarter-inch drill example captures it perfectly -- people don't want the drill, they don't even want the hole, they want the shelf on the wall.

## Key quotes

> "The same technology that simplifies life by providing more functions in each device also complicates life by making the device harder to learn, harder to use. This is the paradox of technology."

> "When people fail to use your products properly, take that as a sign of where the product can be improved."

> "Two of the most important characteristics of good design are discoverability and understanding."

> "The idea that a person is at fault when something goes wrong is deeply entrenched in society. That's why we blame others and even ourselves. Unfortunately, the idea that a person is at fault is imbedded in the legal system."

> "It is relatively easy to design for the situation where everything goes well, where people use the device in the way that was intended, and no unforeseen events occur. The tricky part is to design for when things go wrong."

> "We should deal with error by embracing it, by seeking to understand the causes and ensuring they do not happen again. We need to assist rather than punish or scold."

> "Complexity is good; it is confusion that is bad."
