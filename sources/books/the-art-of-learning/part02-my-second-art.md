# Part II: My Second Art

Waitzkin walks away from chess fame and into a Tai Chi studio as a complete beginner. The narrative is about starting over, but the real content is a set of principles about depth, ego, and the transfer of mastery between domains.

## Summary

After years of suffocating chess celebrity, Waitzkin discovers Tai Chi Chuan through William Chen's studio. The transition forces him to confront ego — he's a world-class competitor in one field becoming a clumsy novice in another. This is where "investment in loss" crystallizes: getting thrown around nightly by Evan, a 200-pound training partner, for months. Evan's aggression becomes a priceless learning opportunity precisely because it's uncomfortable. The ego screams to avoid it, but avoiding it is the path to stagnation.

The philosophical core of Part II is "Making Smaller Circles" — depth over breadth. Rather than learning many techniques superficially, Waitzkin refines the simplest movements until they become invisible. A properly trained punch starts as a full wind-up, then condenses incrementally until the motion is barely perceptible but the power remains. This is how Mike Tyson's knockouts looked impossible. The same principle applied to chess: Waitzkin studied endgames (3 pieces) before openings (32 pieces) to internalize deep principles. This connects to his method of "numbers to leave numbers" — study the technical until it dissolves into intuition.

The "Using Adversity" chapter shows Waitzkin converting a broken hand during a tournament into a training breakthrough — forced to fight one-handed, he develops sensitivity and strategy he'd never have found otherwise. And "Slowing Down Time" explains the science of chunking: experts don't process faster, they recognize patterns that let them process less, which makes their conscious perception seem to operate in slow motion.

## Key ideas

- **Investment in loss** — You have to be willing to look bad for extended periods. Waitzkin got tossed around by a stronger training partner nightly for months. Michael Jordan missed more last-minute shots than anyone in NBA history. That's not a footnote to greatness — it's the mechanism.
- **Making Smaller Circles** — Instead of learning more techniques, refine what you know until it becomes invisible. Condense a full-body punch into a twitch that carries the same power. Depth beats breadth because it opens a channel for the intangible, unconscious, creative components of hidden potential.
- **Numbers to leave numbers** — Immerse yourself in technical fundamentals until they dissolve into intuition. A chess master doesn't count piece values; a Tai Chi practitioner doesn't think about hip rotation. The technique disappears and what remains is feel. Each layer of dissolution becomes the foundation for the next.
- **Beginner's mind** — Starting over in a new discipline is psychologically liberating. Waitzkin found that being a novice at Tai Chi reconnected him with the pure joy of learning that chess fame had suffocated.
- **Using adversity** — Every constraint is an opportunity. A broken hand forces you to develop the other hand and deepen the mental game. The response to adversity reveals character and creates capability that comfort never would.
- **Slowing down time** — Experts look at less and see more. Through chunking (pattern recognition that collapses many details into single units), skilled performers effectively have more time to make decisions. This is why a chess master can glance at a position and see the whole game, or why a martial artist can read a punch before it's thrown.

## Q&A

**Q: What does "investment in loss" mean, and why is it essential for growth?**
A: It means deliberately putting yourself in situations where you will fail and look bad, because that's where the deepest learning happens. Waitzkin spent months getting thrown around by a much larger training partner. The ego resists this — it wants to practice only against weaker opponents where you look good. But the path to mastery requires extended periods of visible incompetence.

**Q: What is the "Making Smaller Circles" method?**
A: Start with a technique in its full, large form until the body mechanics are completely internalized. Then incrementally condense the movement while maintaining its essence. A punch that starts as a full wind-up eventually becomes nearly invisible but retains its power. The principle: once you know what the correct motion *feels* like, you can express it in smaller and smaller external form.

**Q: How does "numbers to leave numbers" work as a learning strategy?**
A: Study fundamentals so deeply that they become unconscious intuition. The name comes from chess — at first you calculate piece values numerically, but eventually you just *see* the position without counting. The same applies to Tai Chi: first you think about hip rotation and weight distribution, but eventually the technique dissolves and what remains is feel. Each layer of internalization becomes the foundation for the next creative leap.
