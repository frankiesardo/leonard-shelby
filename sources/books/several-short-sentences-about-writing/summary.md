# Several Short Sentences About Writing — Verlyn Klinkenborg

A book that dismantles everything you were taught about writing and rebuilds it from the only unit that matters: the sentence. What makes it genuinely different from every other writing book is that the form *is* the argument — the entire text is delivered in short, aphoristic, self-contained declarations, each one practicing what it preaches. The core thesis: most of what you think you know about writing (outlines, drafts, transitions, flow, inspiration) is not only wrong but actively harmful, and the real work is learning to pay attention — to your sentences, your perceptions, and the silence between them.

## Structure

This book has no traditional chapters. It reads as a continuous stream of short, declarative observations. The full text is in [source.md](source.md) — Prologue, then the main body, then "Some Prose and Some Questions" (close-reading examples from great writers and student sentence diagnosis).

## Key ideas

- **The sentence is the only unit that matters.** Your job as a writer is making sentences. Not paragraphs, not outlines, not arguments. Shape, form, structure, genre — these clarify themselves when your sentences become clear. Everything else is downstream.
- **Unlearn what school taught you.** Outlines, transitions, topic sentences, the "nut graf," the appearance of logic — all of it trained you to distrust yourself and condescend to the reader. You were taught to connect sentences with scaffolding instead of learning to write clearly enough that no scaffolding is needed.
- **Noticing is the writer's real discipline.** Writing begins with attention, not words. Practice noticing as part of your ordinary life — a pinpoint of awareness, the detail that stands out. Don't rush to convert perception into metaphor or "material." Let it be. What you notice is important because you noticed it.
- **Self-authorization is non-negotiable.** No one gives you permission to write. Your education taught you that your perceptions are worthless and that what you discover by thinking is unauthorized. Being a writer is an act of perpetual self-authorization. Only you can do it.
- **There is no flow.** "Flow" is a myth that harms writers. Writing is hard, conscious, deliberate work — making sentences word by word. The difficulty is not a sign of failure; it's the nature of the work. Writer's block is largely a byproduct of believing writing should be easy.
- **Revision is composition.** There is no separate "drafting" phase. Compose at the point of revision. Accept no provisional sentences. Bring each sentence as close to final as you can before moving to the next. The distinction between composition and revision is useless and creates a false sense of priority.
- **Trust the reader.** Imagine a reader who is literate, curious, intelligent, and open-minded — then write for that person. Nearly everything you were taught assumes the reader is plodding and needs constant herding. The books you love most are the ones that trusted you.
- **Pursue clarity, not style.** Don't chase "voice" or "style" — chase clarity. Style reveals itself in the pursuit of clarity. Your clarity will differ from anyone else's without your intending it. Where ambiguity rules, there is no style or anything else worth having.

## Key quotes

> "Know what each sentence says, / What it doesn't say, / And what it implies. / Of these, the hardest is knowing what each sentence actually says."

> "Being a writer is an act of perpetual self-authorization. No matter who you are. Only you can authorize yourself."

> "If you notice something, it's because it's important."

> "Flow is something the reader experiences, not the writer."

> "You can get anywhere from anywhere, always and almost instantly."
