# Meditations

Private journal of a Roman Emperor, never intended for publication. That's what makes it extraordinary: this isn't philosophy performed for an audience. It's a man reminding himself, night after night, how to be decent under impossible pressure.

## Key ideas

- **Dichotomy of control.** The only things truly in your power are your judgments, intentions, desires, and aversions. Everything external — health, reputation, other people's actions — is not up to you. Focus accordingly.
- **The obstacle is the way.** "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way." Difficulty is not a detour; it's the curriculum.
- **Memento mori.** You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do, say, and think. Not morbid — clarifying.
- **Amor fati.** Love your fate. Not merely accept what happens, but embrace it as exactly what was needed.
- **The inner citadel.** Your mind is a fortress. External events can only disturb you if you let them past the gate. The work is maintaining the gate.
- **We are all working with the same logos.** Other people are not enemies; they are fellow humans governed by the same rational nature. Even when they wrong you, understand that they act from ignorance, not malice.

## Why this is perennial

This is not a book I read once. It's a book I return to. Every re-reading surfaces different passages depending on what I'm going through. The Meditations is not a system of philosophy — it's a practice. It's the tai chi form for the mind: you run through it not to learn new moves but to refine the ones you already know.
