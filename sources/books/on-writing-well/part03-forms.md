# Part III: Forms

## Summary

This is the longest and most practical section of the book — nine chapters covering nine forms of nonfiction — but it's not really a catalog. Zinsser's argument is that the same principles govern all of them: find the human element, choose specific details over generalities, resist the exhausted clichés of your field, and never relinquish control of the writing. Every form has its own trap (travelese, sportswriting lingo, bureaucratic jargon, academic pedantry), and every trap is a version of the same failure: the writer disappears behind convention instead of doing the harder work of being clear and personal.

The section opens by making the case that nonfiction *is* literature — not a lesser species waiting for permission — and then walks through each form to show what good work looks like in practice. Interviews, travel writing, memoir, science, business, sports, arts criticism, humor: in every case, the best examples succeed because the writer found a way to be human on the page. Lewis Thomas writing about cells, Red Smith writing about baseball, Garrison Keillor writing satire — they all come across first as people, not as specialists performing a role.

What makes this part useful isn't the genre-specific advice (though there's plenty). It's the repeated demonstration that "writing well" is the same job regardless of subject. The principles from Parts I and II don't bend for different forms — they sharpen. If anything, the harder the material, the more you need clarity, brevity, and humanity.

## Chapters

- **11. Nonfiction as Literature** — Stop apologizing for nonfiction; it's the dominant and legitimate form of American writing.
- **12. Writing About People: The Interview** — Get people talking in their own words, then distill ruthlessly while playing fair.
- **13. Writing About Places: The Travel Article** — Kill the adjectives, avoid travelese, and find the central idea of the place.
- **14. Writing About Yourself: The Memoir** — Give yourself permission to write personally, but memoir is selective composition, not diary.
- **15. Science and Technology** — Lead the reader step by step from one narrow fact outward; anyone who thinks clearly can write clearly.
- **16. Business Writing** — Institutions aren't cold; their writing is. Find the missing "I."
- **17. Sports** — Avoid the exhausted synonyms of sportswriting; the best sports pieces are about people, not scores.
- **18. Writing About the Arts** — Criticism is serious intellectual work: love the medium, use specific detail, take a clear stand.
- **19. Humor** — Humor is the serious writer's secret weapon; it works by heightening a crazy truth just enough to expose it.

## Key ideas

- **The human element is the universal solvent.** Every form of nonfiction — even the driest institutional report — comes alive when you find the people behind the subject and let them talk.
- **Every field has its clichés; your job is to refuse them.** Travelese ("nestled," "quaint," "wondrous"), sports lingo ("circuit clout," "twin killing"), business jargon ("enhanced positive learning environments"), academic pedantry — they're all the same disease. The writer defaults to convention instead of thinking.
- **Sequential writing is the master skill for hard material.** Science, technology, business: lead the reader from one known fact to the next. The upside-down pyramid — start narrow, broaden gradually — works for any complex subject.
- **Memoir is not autobiography.** It's a window, not a panorama. Think narrow: a corner of your past, framed by a specific time and place. The art is in what you leave out.
- **Distill the essence; don't dump your notebook.** Whether it's interview quotes, travel details, or research, your reader deserves the tightest package. You'll always have more material than you need. That's the point.
- **Good criticism requires love of the medium, specific detail, and a firm opinion.** Don't hide behind ecstatic adjectives ("enthralling," "luminous") or hedge with last-minute evasions. Take your stand.
- **Humor is serious work.** The humorist's job is to say "something crazy is going on here" when everyone else treats it as normal. Control, specificity, and a real target are what separate humor from mere silliness.
- **Be yourself, regardless of form.** The writer who hides behind jargon, convention, or institutional voice is squandering their richest resource: themselves. "I" is the most interesting element in any story.

## Key quotes

> Good writing is good writing, whatever form it takes and whatever we call it.

> His own words will always be better than your words, even if you are the most elegant stylist in the land. They carry the inflection of his speaking voice and the idiosyncrasies of how he puts a sentence together.

> As a writer you must keep a tight rein on your subjective self — the traveler touched by new sights and sounds and smells — and keep an objective eye on the reader.

> Memoir isn't the summary of a life; it's a window into a life, very much like a photograph in its selective composition. It may look like a casual and even random calling up of bygone events. It's not; it's a deliberate construction.

> Writing is not a special language owned by the English teacher. Writing is thinking on paper. Anyone who thinks clearly can write clearly, about anything at all.

> A simple style is the result of hard work and hard thinking; a muddled style reflects a muddled thinker or a person too arrogant, or too dumb, or too lazy to organize his thoughts.

## Q&A

**Q:** Why does Zinsser insist that a speaker's own words are always better than the writer's paraphrase?
**A:** Because direct quotes carry the speaker's voice — inflection, idiom, regional flavor, enthusiasm. The writer's paraphrase filters everything into secondhand experience. Quotes let the reader hear the person directly.

**Q:** What is the "upside-down pyramid" principle for science writing?
**A:** Start at the bottom with the one narrow fact the reader must know first. Each subsequent sentence broadens the previous one, gradually widening from fact into significance and speculation. The reader can follow broad implications only if they started from one specific point.

**Q:** How does Zinsser distinguish memoir from autobiography?
**A:** Autobiography spans an entire life. Memoir assumes the life and ignores most of it — it takes the reader to one unusually intense corner of the past, framed by a specific time and place. It's selective composition, not comprehensive record.

**Q:** What's the main trap in travel writing, and how do you avoid it?
**A:** The trap is enthusiasm — telling the reader everything you saw and felt, drowning them in generic adjectives and known attributes. The fix: be intensely selective, choose only significant details, and find the central idea of the place rather than cataloging its surface.

**Q:** Why does Zinsser argue that humor is "serious work"?
**A:** Because the humorist's job is to say important things that regular writers can't get said in regular ways. Humor heightens a crazy truth just enough to expose it as crazy — one editorial cartoon is worth a hundred solemn editorials. The joke is no joke.

**Q:** What does Zinsser mean by "find the missing I" in business and institutional writing?
**A:** Most institutional writing is cold and impenetrable because the writer hides behind jargon and passive constructions — no person is visible doing anything. The fix is to write as a human being: use first person, active verbs, and plain language. The warmest resource any institution has is the people who work there.

**Q:** What is the common failure across all nonfiction forms that Zinsser identifies?
**A:** The writer disappears behind the conventions of their field — travelese, sports lingo, academic jargon, bureaucratic fuzz — instead of doing the harder work of writing clearly, personally, and with specific detail. Every form has its own version of this trap.

**Q:** What does Zinsser say distinguishes a critic from a reviewer?
**A:** A reviewer reports on an industry product for an average reader — "Is this movie worth seeing?" A critic performs a serious intellectual act: evaluating work within the context of what has been done before in that medium, placing the new alongside the old. Both need specific detail and clear opinions, but criticism requires deep knowledge of the field.

**Q:** What principle does Zinsser draw from Joseph Mitchell's interview technique?
**A:** Mitchell spliced and rearranged quotes extensively — compressing months of conversation into what reads like a single afternoon — but he never fabricated words or inferred what someone might have said. The standard is: you can rearrange for dramatic effect, but you must play fair with the person's actual words and intent.
