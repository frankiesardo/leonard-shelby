# Antifragile

Taleb's central claim: there's a whole category of things beyond "resilient" that actually *benefit* from shocks, volatility, and stressors. He calls this antifragile. Fragile things break under stress. Resilient things survive. Antifragile things get stronger.

## Key ideas

- **Antifragility is not resilience.** Resilience is staying the same. Antifragility is improving. Your immune system is antifragile. A china cup is fragile. A rock is resilient.
- **Via negativa.** Often the best intervention is to *remove* something harmful rather than *add* something helpful. Applies to health, systems design, management. What you don't do matters more than what you do.
- **Skin in the game.** People who bear the consequences of their decisions make better decisions. Asymmetric risk (upside without downside) creates fragility in systems.
- **The barbell strategy.** Instead of medium-risk everything, combine extreme safety with small bets on extreme upside. Applies to career, investing, learning.
- **The Lindy effect.** The longer something has survived, the longer it's likely to continue surviving. Old ideas and old books are more likely to remain relevant than new ones. This is why the [perennial tier](../../STRUCTURE.md) matters.
- **Optionality.** Having options is itself antifragile. You don't need to predict the future; you need to be positioned to benefit from whatever happens.

## Connections

- Deeply connected to [stoicism](../../ideas/stoicism.md) — the stoics practiced voluntary discomfort to build antifragility of character.
- The Cognitect blog series [The New Normal](the-new-normal-cognitect.md) applies antifragility to software architecture: systems that break a lot learn to fix themselves. Chaos engineering is antifragility for infrastructure.
- Connects to the [inner game](../../ideas/inner-game.md) — the martial arts idea that you don't avoid difficulty, you metabolize it.
- The [Lindy effect](../../ideas/lindy-effect.md) is the heuristic I use for the tier system itself: perennial sources are perennial *because* they've survived.

## Personal resonance

This book changed how I think about management. I used to optimize for stability. Now I optimize for *learning rate under stress*. A team that deploys often and handles incidents well is antifragile. A team that never deploys is fragile — it just doesn't know it yet.

Also deeply resonant with tai chi: you don't resist force, you redirect it. The yielding posture is the antifragile one.
