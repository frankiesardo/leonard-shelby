# Metaphors We Live By

Lakoff and Johnson's 1980 book makes a single claim and then spends thirty chapters proving it from every conceivable angle: metaphor is not a decorative feature of language but the fundamental structure of human thought. We don't just *talk* about arguments in terms of war -- we *experience* arguments as war, *conduct* them as war, *win* and *lose* them as war. The book catalogs hundreds of everyday expressions to show that our entire conceptual system is built on metaphorical mappings from concrete, bodily experience (up/down, container, path, object) onto abstract domains (time, emotion, morality, causation, truth itself). The philosophical payoff is radical: if thought is inherently metaphorical, there is no "literal" God's-eye view of reality. Truth is always relative to a conceptual system, and conceptual systems are always grounded in the particular bodies and cultures that produce them. Lakoff and Johnson call this "experientialism" -- a third path between the myth of objectivism (there are absolute truths independent of human understanding) and the myth of subjectivism (truth is purely personal and imaginative). The book is dense with examples but light on jargon, and its central insight -- that the metaphors you live by shape the reality you inhabit -- has implications for everything from product design to political rhetoric to personal identity.

## Key ideas

**Metaphor is not language -- it is thought.** The book's foundational claim. When we say "I *demolished* his argument," this is not a figure of speech decorating a literal meaning. It *is* the meaning. There is no non-metaphorical way to talk about argument that is somehow more "real." We conceptualize arguments in terms of war because our conceptual system maps the structure of physical combat onto the structure of verbal disagreement. The linguistic expressions follow from the conceptual mapping, not the other way around. This means that studying everyday metaphorical expressions gives us direct evidence about the structure of thought. [See: [Conceptual Metaphor](../../ideas/conceptual-metaphor.md)]

**Every metaphor highlights and hides.** TIME IS MONEY highlights the aspects of time that are like money -- it can be spent, wasted, budgeted, invested. But it hides the aspects that are unlike money -- you can't get time back, you can't give someone else your time, time does not accumulate in banks. ARGUMENT IS WAR highlights the combative aspects and hides the cooperative ones. The [Conduit metaphor](../../ideas/the-conduit-metaphor.md) -- IDEAS ARE OBJECTS, WORDS ARE CONTAINERS, COMMUNICATION IS SENDING -- hides the fact that meaning depends on context, that the same sentence means different things to different people, and that understanding is an active, constructive process. You can never see everything through a single metaphor. The choice of metaphor is therefore always a political and existential act. [See: [Metaphorical Highlighting and Hiding](../../ideas/metaphorical-highlighting-and-hiding.md)]

**Abstract thought is grounded in bodily experience.** We understand HAPPY IS UP not by arbitrary convention but because drooping posture goes with sadness and erect posture goes with positive emotional states. MORE IS UP because adding substance to a pile raises its level. KNOWING IS GRASPING because we understand objects by picking them up and examining them. The entire edifice of abstract thought -- morality, causation, time, truth -- is built on these bodily foundations through chains of metaphor. Orientational metaphors (up/down, in/out) give rise to ontological metaphors (treating abstractions as entities and substances), which give rise to structural metaphors (mapping the full structure of one domain onto another). This is not a weakness of human thought -- it is the *only* way thought works. [See: [Experiential Grounding](../../ideas/experiential-grounding.md)]

**Metaphors create realities, not just descriptions.** When Carter declared the energy crisis "the moral equivalent of war," the WAR metaphor was not merely a description -- it licensed an entire set of actions: identifying an enemy, setting targets, marshaling forces, demanding sacrifices. Amory Lovins offered a competing SOFT vs. HARD PATHS metaphor that highlighted completely different features of the energy system and would have led to completely different policies. "People in power get to impose their metaphors." New metaphors don't just relabel existing reality -- they bring new aspects of experience into focus and create new coherences that become real when people start acting in terms of them. The Westernization of cultures worldwide is partly a matter of introducing TIME IS MONEY into societies that previously had no such metaphor. [See: [Metaphors Create Realities](../../ideas/metaphors-create-realities.md)]

**There is no literal, objective standpoint.** The objectivist tradition in Western philosophy assumes that truth is a matter of fitting words to a world of pre-existing objects with inherent properties. But Lakoff and Johnson show that even our simplest perceptions involve metaphorical projection: we project entity structure onto fog and mountains, we project fronts and backs onto objects that have none, we categorize by prototypes rather than necessary and sufficient conditions. If the conceptual system through which we understand reality is itself metaphorical, then there can be no "God's-eye view" that escapes metaphor. Truth is real but always relative to a conceptual system grounded in experience. This is not relativism -- our conceptual systems are constrained by the physical and cultural environments we function in -- but it is the end of the dream of absolute, unconditional truth. [See: [Imaginative Rationality](../../ideas/imaginative-rationality.md)]

**New metaphors create new similarities.** The comparison theory of metaphor says metaphors can only describe pre-existing similarities. Lakoff and Johnson argue the opposite: the LOVE IS A COLLABORATIVE WORK OF ART metaphor *creates* similarities between love and art that did not exist before the metaphor. It picks out a range of love experiences (the ones involving cooperation, discipline, shared aesthetics, frustration, dedication) and organizes them into a coherent whole by mapping them onto the structure of collaborative artistic creation. The resulting structural similarity -- not just isolated shared features, but the *way the features fit together* -- is itself a product of the metaphor. Without the metaphor, those love experiences would not cohere as an identifiable set.

## Key quotes

> "The essence of metaphor is understanding and experiencing one kind of thing in terms of another."

> "The most fundamental values in a culture will be coherent with the metaphorical structure of the most fundamental concepts in the culture."

> "New metaphors have the power to create a new reality. This can begin to happen when we start to comprehend our experience in terms of a metaphor, and it becomes a deeper reality when we begin to act in terms of it."

> "People in power get to impose their metaphors."

> "Metaphor is one of our most important tools for trying to comprehend partially what cannot be comprehended totally: our feelings, aesthetic experiences, moral practices, and spiritual awareness."

> "The idea that metaphor is just a matter of language and can at best only describe reality stems from the view that what is real is wholly external to, and independent of, how human beings conceptualize the world."

> "We reject the objectivist view that there is absolute and unconditional truth without adopting the subjectivist alternative of truth as obtainable only through the imagination, unconstrained by external circumstances. The reason we have focused so much on metaphor is that it unites reason and imagination."
