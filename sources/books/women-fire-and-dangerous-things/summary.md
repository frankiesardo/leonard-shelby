# Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things — George Lakoff

Lakoff's 1987 magnum opus makes one enormous claim and spends 600 pages proving it from every conceivable angle: the way we categorize reveals the nature of mind, and what it reveals is that reason is embodied and imaginative, not disembodied and transcendental. The title comes from Dyirbal, an Australian Aboriginal language where *balan* — a grammatical classifier — groups women, fire, and dangerous things in the same category. This isn't madness; it's a [radial category](../../ideas/radial-categories.md) linked by chains of association (women → sun goddess → fire → danger). The classical theory of categories (shared properties = membership) cannot explain this; [idealized cognitive models](../../ideas/idealized-cognitive-models.md) can.

The book is really three books. Part I assembles the empirical evidence against classical categories, drawing on Rosch's prototypes, Berlin and Kay's colors, Dyirbal classifiers, and basic-level categorization. Part II draws out the philosophical consequences: if categories aren't classical, then objectivist metaphysics, formal semantics, the computational theory of mind, and even the uniqueness of mathematical truth all collapse. Part III — the longest — provides three grueling case studies (anger, the word *over*, English *there*-constructions) that demonstrate cognitive models working at maximum resolution.

This is the deeper, more technical sequel to [Metaphors We Live By](../metaphors-we-live-by/summary.md). Where that book showed metaphor is the structure of thought, this one shows categorization is the structure of reason — and both grow from the body. The philosophical payoff is [experiential realism](../../ideas/experiential-realism.md): a third way between objectivism (there is one truth independent of all minds) and relativism (anything goes). Truth is real, knowledge is stable, but both are grounded in how particular bodies interact with particular environments.

## Structure

- [Part I: Categorization and Cognitive Models](part01-categorization-and-cognitive-models.md) — The empirical case: prototype effects, basic-level categories, idealized cognitive models, radial categories, metonymic reasoning

## Key ideas

- **Idealized cognitive models (ICMs).** The central theoretical apparatus. An ICM is a structured, idealized representation of some domain of experience — propositional, image-schematic, metaphoric, or metonymic. Categories are defined relative to ICMs, and prototype effects are by-products. The "mother" concept, structured by a cluster of five ICMs (birth, nurturance, marriage, genetics, genealogy), shows how conflicts arise when the cluster doesn't converge. This is the successor to the frame/script tradition, generalized to handle metaphor, metonymy, and imagery.

- **Embodied reason.** Reason is not a transcendental capacity independent of the body. It grows from bodily experience: perception, motor activity, spatial orientation, physical manipulation. Basic-level categories — the most cognitively natural level of categorization — are defined by gestalt perception, motor programs, and mental imagery. Abstract thought is possible only through chains of metaphor grounded in embodied experience. This is the book's deepest claim and the foundation for experiential realism.

- **Radial categories.** The most radical challenge to classical theory. A radial category has a central case and conventionalized extensions linked by chains of association — motivated but not predictable. Dyirbal *balan* (women, fire, dangerous things), Japanese *hon* (sticks to TV programs), the senses of *over*, and *there*-constructions all exhibit this structure. Extensions must be learned individually; no rule generates them. Yet they're not arbitrary — each link makes sense given the center and the linking model.

- **Prototype effects ≠ category structure.** Lakoff's crucial methodological distinction. Goodness-of-example ratings reflect how well something fits an ICM, not degrees of category membership. Penguins are fully birds but poor examples because they don't fit the bird ICM (flies, sings, perches). Armstrong et al.'s demonstration of prototype effects for "odd number" — a classically defined category — actually confirms prototype theory under the ICM interpretation.

- **Experiential realism.** The alternative to objectivism. Shares objectivism's commitment to a real world, stable knowledge, and truth beyond coherence — but grounds meaning in embodied understanding rather than correspondence. Truth is relative to a conceptual system, and conceptual systems are grounded in bodies and environments. Not relativism: embodiment constrains what concepts are possible. The third way between objectivist absolutes and postmodern "anything goes."

- **Cognitive semantics.** Meaning is not a mapping from symbols to the world but is constituted by cognitive models. Truth is defined relative to understanding. Syntax is not autonomous — it is motivated by semantics. Compositional semantics fails: the meaning of "pet fish" is not computable from "pet" + "fish" but requires an ICM of fish-keeping. This framework handles metaphor, metonymy, polysemy, and radial categories — all invisible to formal semantics.

## Key quotes

> "Categorization is not a matter to be taken lightly. There is nothing more basic than categorization to our thought, perception, action, and speech."

> "The main thesis of this book is that we organize our knowledge by means of structures called *idealized cognitive models*, or ICMs, and that category structures and prototype effects are by-products of that organization."

> "To question the classical view of categories in a fundamental way is thus to question the view of reason as disembodied symbol-manipulation."

> "There is a large body of evidence from many fields that supports a view of mind that is centered in the bodily and imaginative capacities of human beings."

> "Grand theories don't count for much, unless they are substantiated down to the minutest details."

## Connections

- **[Metaphors We Live By](../metaphors-we-live-by/summary.md)** — The prequel. MWLB showed metaphor is the structure of thought; WFDT shows categorization is the structure of reason and both grow from the body. The experientialist position is stated in MWLB and defended in depth here. ICMs are the generalization of the conceptual metaphor framework.
- **[Sapiens](../sapiens/summary.md)** — Harari's imagined orders are shared cognitive models at civilizational scale. The Cognitive Revolution — when Sapiens gained the ability to create fictions — is precisely the emergence of ICMs that go beyond individual embodied experience.
- **[The Fabric of Reality](../the-fabric-of-reality/summary.md)** — Deutsch and Lakoff disagree sharply on whether reason is transcendental. Deutsch argues mathematical truth exists in a Platonic realm; Lakoff argues (via Putnam and Mac Lane) that math grows from human cognitive activities. But both agree that reality is comprehensible and that good explanations matter more than mere prediction.
- **[The Design of Everyday Things](../the-design-of-everyday-things/summary.md)** — Norman's affordances and conceptual models are practical applications of ICMs. The gulf of execution and evaluation is what happens when the designer's ICM doesn't match the user's.
- **[Range](../range/summary.md)** — Epstein's analogical thinking is a practical form of what Lakoff calls metaphoric reasoning: understanding one domain in terms of another. The outsider advantage comes from having richer cross-domain mappings.
