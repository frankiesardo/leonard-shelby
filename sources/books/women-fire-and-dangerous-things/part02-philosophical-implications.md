# Part II: Philosophical Implications

## Summary

Part II pivots from empirical evidence to philosophical argument. Lakoff lays out the objectivist paradigm — the interlocking set of metaphysical and epistemological doctrines that have dominated Western philosophy for two millennia — and systematically dismantles it. If categories are not defined by shared properties, then the objectivist account of meaning (symbol-to-world correspondence), truth (objectively determined), cognition (disembodied symbol manipulation), and even mathematics (transcendental and unique) all collapse. In their place, Lakoff proposes experiential realism: meaning is embodied and imaginative, truth is relative to understanding, semantics must be cognitive rather than formal, and reason is a human capacity rooted in bodily experience. He uses Putnam's model-theoretic argument to show that objectivist semantics cannot fix reference, Whorf's work to argue for a moderate relativism constrained by embodiment, and Mac Lane's philosophy of mathematics to suggest that even mathematical truth grows out of human cognitive structures.

## Chapters

- **Ch 11: The Objectivist Paradigm** — Defines objectivist metaphysics (the world has a determinate structure independent of understanding) and epistemology (thought mirrors that structure via symbols). Distinguishes objectivism from basic realism.
- **Ch 12: What's Wrong with Objectivist Metaphysics** — Classical categories fail for biological species (no sharp boundaries, no essences). Natural kinds are cognitive constructs, not mind-independent givens.
- **Ch 13: What's Wrong with Objectivist Cognition** — Objectivist semantics cannot handle metaphor, metonymy, polysemy, frames, or prototype effects. Meaning is not compositional in the classical sense.
- **Ch 14: The Formalist Enterprise** — Formal semantics (Montague grammar) inherits all objectivist problems. It works for a narrow range of phenomena but cannot be the foundation for natural language semantics.
- **Ch 15: Putnam's Theorem** — Putnam's model-theoretic argument: if meaning is just symbol-to-world correspondence, then reference cannot be fixed — there are always multiple equally valid interpretations. Objectivist semantics is incoherent on its own terms.
- **Ch 16: A New Realism** — Experiential realism as an alternative: committed to the real world and stable knowledge, but grounding meaning in embodied understanding rather than correspondence. Not relativism — constrained by the body and the environment.
- **Ch 17: Cognitive Semantics** — What a non-objectivist semantics looks like: meaning as cognitive model structure, truth as relative to understanding, syntax motivated by semantics.
- **Ch 18: Whorf and Relativism** — Moderate Whorfian position: languages can differ in conceptual structure, but all are constrained by shared embodiment. Universal embodiment does not entail universal concepts.
- **Ch 19: The Mind-As-Machine Paradigm** — Critique of strong AI and the computational theory of mind. If meaning is embodied and imaginative, then machines that only manipulate formal symbols cannot genuinely understand.
- **Ch 20: Mathematics as a Cognitive Activity** — Mathematics is not transcendentally true and unique. Independence results (Gödel, Cohen) show multiple consistent extensions of ZFC. Putnam's theorem shows we can't fix a unique referent for "mathematics." Mac Lane's suggestion: mathematics grows from basic human activities (counting, measuring, shaping).
- **Ch 21: Overview** — Recapitulates the argument: classical categories fail → objectivism fails → experiential realism is the viable alternative.

## Key ideas

- **Experiential realism (experientialism).** Lakoff's proposed alternative to objectivism. It shares objectivism's commitment to the real world, stable knowledge, and truth beyond mere coherence — but locates meaning in embodied understanding rather than in symbol-to-world correspondence. Concepts are not mirrors of nature but structures that grow from how bodies interact with environments. This is not relativism: embodiment constrains what concepts are possible.

- **Putnam's model-theoretic argument.** If meaning is just a mapping from symbols to models of the world, and if "the world" is itself described by a theory, then there are always multiple reference schemes that make the theory come out true. No amount of theory can determine a unique correspondence between words and things. This devastates objectivist semantics from within — using its own tools.

- **Cognitive semantics.** Meaning is not a relation between symbols and the world but is constituted by cognitive models — structured, embodied, imaginative. Truth is defined relative to understanding (not correspondence). Syntax is motivated by semantics, not independent of it. This framework can handle metaphor, metonymy, polysemy, and prototype effects — all of which are invisible to formal semantics.

- **Mathematics as embodied cognition.** The independence of the continuum hypothesis from ZFC, combined with Putnam's argument, shows that "mathematics" cannot be both transcendentally true and unique. Mac Lane's alternative: the branches of mathematics arise from basic human activities (counting, measuring, shaping, moving) and the image schemas that structure our understanding of those activities. CONTAINERS map to sets, LINKS map to functions, PATHS map to continuity.

- **Moderate Whorfian relativism.** Languages can and do structure experience differently — but not arbitrarily. Universal embodiment (shared bodies, shared physics) constrains conceptual variation. Lakoff rejects both strong relativism (anything goes) and strong universalism (all languages encode the same concepts).

## Q&A

**Q:** How does Putnam's model-theoretic argument undermine objectivist semantics?
**A:** Objectivism says meaning is a correspondence between symbols and things in the world, fixed by the structure of the world itself. Putnam showed that for any consistent theory, there are multiple reference schemes (multiple ways to map symbols to objects) that make the theory equally true. Since the theory can't determine which mapping is the "right" one, objectivist correspondence cannot fix meaning. You need something outside the formal system — like embodied understanding — to determine what words refer to.

**Q:** What distinguishes experiential realism from relativism?
**A:** Experiential realism holds that concepts are grounded in embodied experience — our bodies, our environments, our neural structures. This grounds truth in something real and constrains what concepts are possible. It's not "anything goes." But it does reject the God's-eye view: there is no standpoint outside all conceptual systems from which to determine absolute truth. Truth is real, but always relative to a system of understanding that is itself shaped by the body.

**Q:** A colleague says "math proves that pure reason exists independent of the body." How would Lakoff respond?
**A:** He'd point to the independence results: the continuum hypothesis is neither provable nor disprovable from the standard axioms of set theory (ZFC). This means there are multiple consistent, non-identical mathematics. Combined with Putnam's argument (you can't fix a unique referent for "mathematics"), this shows math can't be both transcendental and unique. The alternative: mathematics arises from basic human activities — counting, measuring, shaping — and the image schemas (containers, paths, links) that structure our understanding of those activities. Math is real, but it's *our* math, not God's.
