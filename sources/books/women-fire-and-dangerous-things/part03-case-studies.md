# Part III: Case Studies

## Summary

Part III is where the theory meets the data at maximum resolution. Three case studies demonstrate that radial categories, cognitive models, and the experientialist apparatus are not just theoretical possibilities but empirical necessities — they account for phenomena that classical approaches cannot. Case Study 1 (anger) shows that an "amorphous" emotion has elaborate conceptual structure organized by metaphor and metonymy. Case Study 2 (*over*) shows that a single word's 100+ senses form a radial category structured by image-schema transformations. Case Study 3 (*there*-constructions) — the longest and most technical — shows that grammatical constructions themselves form radial categories, pairing form with meaning in ways that neither generative grammar nor formal semantics can capture. The case studies are a tour de force of detailed analysis, intended to demonstrate that cognitive linguistics can handle the full complexity of natural language.

## Chapters

- **Introduction** — Frames the case studies as substantiation of the general theory. Radial structure is the phenomenon that departs most from classical categories and therefore requires the most detailed evidence.
- **Case Study 1: Anger** — Anger is structured by the central metaphor ANGER IS THE HEAT OF A FLUID IN A CONTAINER, with extensions (ANGER IS FIRE, ANGER IS INSANITY, ANGER IS AN OPPONENT, ANGER IS A DANGEROUS ANIMAL). The prototypical anger scenario has five stages: offending event → anger → attempt at control → loss of control → retribution.
- **Case Study 2: Over** — The preposition *over* has 100+ senses organized in a radial category around the central ABOVE-ACROSS schema. Senses are linked by image-schema transformations (contact/no-contact, extended/point, covering, reflexive, etc.), metaphorical extensions, and instance/similarity links. This is polysemy as prototype-based categorization.
- **Case Study 3: There-Constructions** — English *there*-constructions (existential, deictic, presentational, activity-start, narrative-focus, paragon, exasperation) form a radial category with the central spatial deictic (*There's Harry*) at the prototype. Each construction is a form-meaning pairing. The analysis demonstrates that syntax is not autonomous from meaning and that motivation — not prediction — is the right relation between constructions.
- **Chapter 32** — Continues the *there*-construction analysis: complex constructions as intersections of simpler ones (paragon-intonation + deictic, exasperation + activity-start). The ACTIVITY IS MOTION metaphor underlies several extensions.
- **Afterword** — The objectivist view is an opinion, not a fact. The converging evidence from categorization studies, linguistics, anthropology, and psychology supports the experientialist alternative.

## Key ideas

- **The conceptual structure of emotions.** Anger is not a blob of feeling but a richly structured concept organized by metaphor. ANGER IS THE HEAT OF A FLUID IN A CONTAINER generates inferences: pressure builds, the person tries to keep it in, eventually they blow their top. The metaphor is grounded in actual physiological responses (increased body heat, blood pressure). Other metaphors (ANGER IS FIRE, ANGER IS INSANITY) capture different aspects. The prototypical anger scenario is a five-stage folk model that structures how we understand, experience, and talk about anger.

- **Polysemy as radial categorization.** The 100+ senses of *over* are not homonyms (unrelated meanings sharing a form) nor a single abstract meaning. They form a natural category with a prototypical spatial sense (ABOVE-ACROSS) and extensions linked by specific, identifiable transformations: adding contact, removing the trajector, extending the landmark, reflexivizing, and metaphorical mapping to abstract domains. Each sense is motivated but not predictable. This reframes the problem of word meaning: it's not "what does *over* mean?" but "what is the structure of the *over* category?"

- **Grammar as radial category structure.** The most radical claim: grammatical constructions are not generated by abstract rules but are stored pairings of form and meaning organized in radial categories. The central *there*-construction is the spatial deictic; the existential, presentational, and other *there*-constructions are conventionalized extensions linked to the center by shared features and metaphorical mappings. Syntax is not autonomous — it's motivated by semantics and pragmatics.

- **Motivation vs. prediction.** A concept central to all three case studies. In a radial category, noncentral members are *motivated* by the center — they make sense given the center — but are not *predictable* from it. You can see why *over* extended to mean "finished" (the trajectory has an endpoint, the endpoint is focused, and the activity metaphorically traverses a path), but you couldn't have predicted it in advance. This is a fundamental property of human categorization that classical theory cannot accommodate.

## Q&A

**Q:** Someone says anger is "just a feeling" with no conceptual structure. What evidence does Lakoff provide?
**A:** Lakoff shows that anger has a prototypical five-stage scenario (offending event → anger → attempt at control → loss of control → retribution) and is organized by a system of metaphors (ANGER IS HEAT, ANGER IS FIRE, ANGER IS INSANITY, ANGER IS A DANGEROUS ANIMAL). These metaphors generate coherent inferences — you can "simmer," "boil over," "blow your top," "see red" — and they're grounded in actual physiological responses. The conceptual structure is as elaborate as anything in formal logic; it just happens to be structured by metaphor rather than by definitions.

**Q:** How does the *over* analysis challenge the idea that words have single meanings?
**A:** *Over* has 100+ identifiable senses (spatial, covering, completion, excess, repetition, etc.) that cannot be reduced to a single abstract meaning — the differences matter for inference and truth conditions. But they're not arbitrary homonyms either — the senses are systematically related through image-schema transformations and metaphorical extensions. The word itself is a radial category, with a central spatial sense and motivated extensions. This is the norm for natural language, not the exception.

**Q:** Why does Lakoff choose *there*-constructions as a case study for grammar?
**A:** Because they're extremely well-studied, have a huge amount of recalcitrant data that resists analysis in generative frameworks, and they clearly demonstrate that syntax is not autonomous from meaning. The deictic *there* ("There's Harry") and the existential *there* ("There is a God") have different syntax, semantics, and pragmatics — but they're systematically related. Only a theory that treats constructions as form-meaning pairings organized in radial categories can capture both the regularities and the idiosyncrasies.
