# Aggregation Theory — Ben Thompson

The Internet shifted power from distributors to aggregators. Pre-Internet, profits went to whoever controlled scarce distribution: newspapers, cable networks, record labels. The Internet made distribution free, supply abundant, and the scarce resource became *demand* — the user relationship. Aggregators like Google, Amazon, and YouTube own that relationship and commoditize their suppliers.

## Key ideas

- **Winner-take-all is structural, not accidental.** Zero marginal costs + network effects + zero distribution costs create a virtuous cycle that concentrates entire categories around one or two aggregators. This isn't monopoly through control of supply — it's monopoly through control of demand.

- **The middle gets destroyed.** Aggregators own the head. The long tail fills every niche. The medium-sized firms that once filled specific distribution slots — regional newspapers, mid-list publishers, 10-person software shops — get crushed from both directions.

- **Suppliers are commoditized.** The aggregator's power comes from making suppliers interchangeable. Uber doesn't care which driver shows up. YouTube doesn't care which creator uploads. The aggregator's value is in matching supply to demand, not in any particular supplier.
