# Against Taste — Will Manidis

The tech consensus has settled on "taste" as the answer to what humans are for once AI gets good enough. Manidis argues this is not empowerment but the most elegant demotion in the history of human self-regard. What we call taste is the residue of patronage — a relationship that used to be generative, refocused entirely towards consumption. The patron animated the work before it existed; the collector evaluates it after. Taste places man at the end of the chain of creation rather than at the beginning, and in doing so strips creative activity of its orientation toward the transcendent.

## Key ideas

- **Patronage was co-creation, not selection.** The Florentine bottega system, medieval cathedrals, Michelangelo and Julius II — in every case the patron was in the room before the first brushstroke, locked in a generative argument with the maker. The negotiation itself was the creative act. What emerged was a product of shared labor, not anyone's judgment.

- **Taste arrived when the transcendent left and the patron left the room.** The modern art market separated capital from labor. The collector replaced the patron, the critic replaced the guildmaster, the gallery replaced the bottega. What was lost was friction — the argument, the being in the room, the orientation toward something that exceeded both parties.

- **The discriminator is disposable.** In a GAN, the discriminator trains the generator into competence — then is removed. The taste discourse asks you to be the discriminator: the part of the system whose entire purpose is to make itself unnecessary. The more refined your taste, the faster machines learn it, and the sooner you are redundant.

- **Taste cannot produce the genuinely new.** It can only operate on what already exists, recognize what has already been validated. The premiere of the Rite of Spring was a riot because the audience's cultivated taste stood between them and the most important art of the century. What produced the Rite was Diaghilev in the rehearsal room — provoking, not selecting.

- **"A fine disregard for the rules."** Webb Ellis broke football by running with the ball — not from ignorance but from such total command that his violation produced something new. The function of humans after AGI is this same fine disregard: to handle the transcendent from inside, not to curate from outside.

- **Will over taste.** The patron who built the cathedral was not exercising taste but will — and worship. That will was devoted to something beyond himself. Strip the transcendent out and what remains is acquisition, however exquisite. "The Kingdom is built by hands, not by judges with haute taste."

## Key quotes

> "Taste is what you call the patron's function after you have removed the patron from the process of making. It is the disgusting residue of a relationship that used to be generative, refocused entirely towards consumption."

> "The collector replaced the patron. The critic replaced the guildmaster. The gallery replaced the bottega. Taste replaced patronage. What was lost was friction."

> "The taste discourse is asking you to be the discriminator."

> "A fine disregard. The disregard of someone who has internalized the rules so much that he can feel where they bend, where they want to break, where the new thing is hiding inside the old one."

> "Christ was a carpenter before he was a teacher. Moses was a shepherd before he was a prophet. The Kingdom is built by hands, not by judges with haute taste."

## Connections

- Directly challenges [Taste as Competitive Advantage](../../ideas/taste-as-competitive-advantage.md). Jobs's taste — knowing what to leave out — is real and valuable, but Manidis would say even Jobs was a patron, not a selector. He was in the room, fighting with engineers, specifying the materials. The taste that mattered was embedded in the making, not separated from it.

- The patron's orientation toward the transcendent is [Self-Transcendence](../../ideas/self-transcendence.md) applied to creative labor. Frankl: "Being human always points to something other than oneself." The patron pointed toward God, the city, the future. The collector points at a living room wall.

- The apartment "pointed at nothing" is [The Existential Vacuum](../../ideas/the-existential-vacuum.md) expressed through aesthetics. Activity with no telos. Acquisition with no orientation beyond itself. The Sunday neurosis of the collector class.

- The [Will to Meaning](../../ideas/will-to-meaning.md) runs underneath the whole argument. The patron's labor had meaning through its first channel — "creating a work or doing a deed" — because it was oriented toward something beyond the self. Taste reduces that channel to consumption.

- Peterson's [Meaning Over Expedience](../../ideas/meaning-over-expedience.md) maps precisely: the patron sacrifices for the transcendent; taste is the expedient substitute. And the Genesis 2:15 framing — man's calling is to tend, to make — is Peterson's reading of the same passage.

- Hauntology — the slow cancellation of the future — is [Static vs. Dynamic Societies](../../ideas/static-vs-dynamic-societies.md) applied to culture. Taste can only rearrange the archive. Dynamic societies create new knowledge. A culture of taste is a static culture.

- The Rite of Spring anecdote is [Protect the New](../../ideas/protect-the-new.md) from the audience side: taste functions as the organizational immune system that kills the genuinely new. Diaghilev protected the new by provoking it into existence.

- The audience's inability to evaluate the genuinely new is [Cognitive Entrenchment](../../ideas/cognitive-entrenchment.md) applied to aesthetics. The more refined your taste, the more entrenched your pattern-matching, the less you can perceive what falls outside your existing categories.

- Webb Ellis's "fine disregard for the rules" is [From Form to Formlessness](../../ideas/numbers-to-leave-numbers.md) — internalize the rules so deeply they become transparent, then transcend them. Not ignorance of form but command so total that violation becomes generative.

- The [Ethic of Divinity](../../ideas/the-ethic-of-divinity.md) is the moral dimension Manidis is arguing was lost. The patron operated in the third dimension — sacredness, orientation toward the divine. Taste operates in the ethic of autonomy — individual preference, selection, curation.

- Patronage was [Process Over Product](../../ideas/process-over-product.md): the negotiation, the being in the room, the decades-long collaboration. Taste is pure product orientation — evaluating the finished thing.

- The patron participates in Covey's [Two Creations](../../ideas/two-creations.md) — the first creation, the vision, the animation before the work exists. The collector arrives only for the second creation, the finished artifact.
