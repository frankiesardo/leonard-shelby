# Aggregation Theory — Ben Thompson

The Internet inverted the value chain. Pre-Internet, profits went to whoever controlled scarce distribution — newspapers, publishers, taxi companies, hotels. They integrated backwards into supply because owning supplier relationships gave leverage over the many consumers. The Internet made distribution free and transaction costs zero, which meant distributors could integrate *forward* with consumers at scale instead. The result: suppliers get commoditized, the user experience becomes the competitive battleground, and whoever owns the consumer relationship wins everything.

## Key ideas

- **The value chain flipped.** Three parts: suppliers, distributors, consumers. Pre-Internet, distributors integrated backwards into supply (newspapers owned content, taxi companies owned fleets). Post-Internet, distribution is free, so the winning move is integrating forward into the consumer relationship. Google commoditized publishers. Uber commoditized drivers. Airbnb commoditized hotels. In each case, the aggregator owns the demand side and lets supply compete for access.

- **Winner-take-all is structural.** Every aggregator Thompson lists can serve every consumer on earth, and each becomes a *better* service the more consumers it serves. This isn't a bug or a temporary market condition — it's the fundamental dynamic. The virtuous cycle (more users attract more suppliers attract more users) makes the aggregator's position nearly unassailable.

- **The middle gets destroyed.** The old incumbents who integrated backwards into supply — newspapers, book publishers, broadcast networks, taxi companies — lose their moat because their distribution advantage is gone. They're too large to be niche, too small to be the aggregator. The value shifts to the aggregator at the head and the commoditized long tail of suppliers.

- **Every industry has a critical function that can be digitized.** Airbnb digitized trust. Uber digitized dispatch. Thompson's prediction: nearly every industry will discover its critical differentiator can be commoditized, and when it is, competition shifts to user experience — where new entrants built around the right incentives have the advantage.

## Key quotes

> "The best distributors/aggregators/market-makers win by providing the best experience, which earns them the most consumers/users, which attracts the most suppliers, which enhances the user experience in a virtuous cycle."

> "The profound changes caused by the Internet are only just beginning; aggregation theory is the means."
