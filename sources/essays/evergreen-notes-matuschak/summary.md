# Evergreen Notes — Andy Matuschak

The foundational essay on what makes notes worth keeping forever. Matuschak reframes note-taking as a *thinking* practice, not a *recording* practice. Most notes are transient — jotted once and never revisited. Evergreen notes are the opposite: written to evolve, accumulate, and compound over time. The key insight is that "better note-taking" misses the point entirely; what matters is "better thinking." Notes are the byproduct of that thinking, not the goal.

## Key ideas

- **Atomic.** Each note captures exactly one idea. Small enough to be fully understood on its own, large enough to be meaningful.
- **Concept-oriented.** Notes are organized around *concepts*, not around the source they came from. You don't file notes under "Book X" — you file them under the idea they express.
- **Densely linked.** Notes gain value through connections. A note with no links is a dead end. A note with many links is a hub of thought.
- **Associative over hierarchical.** Rigid folder hierarchies force premature decisions. Associative linking lets structure emerge organically from the ideas themselves.
- **Write for yourself.** Disregard audience by default. The notes are thinking tools, not publications.
- **Note-taking as knowledge work.** Evergreen note-writing is not preparation for the real work — it *is* the real work. The act of distilling and connecting is where insight happens.

## Why this is evergreen

This is the methodological backbone of this garden. Every source summary that gets written, every idea that gets extracted, every connection that gets drawn — all of it follows Matuschak's principles. Atomic, concept-oriented, densely linked. The garden *is* an evergreen note practice.
