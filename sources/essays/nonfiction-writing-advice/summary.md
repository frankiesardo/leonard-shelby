# Nonfiction Writing Advice — Scott Alexander

Ten rules for writing nonfiction that people will actually read, from one of the most effective online writers of the last decade. Not a style guide — a manual for getting ideas from your head into someone else's. The two most important insights: **concept handles** (give your ideas catchy, portable names so they survive outside your essay) and **microhumor** (the background-level wit that separates enjoyable writers from technically proficient ones). The meta-lesson: good writing is not about following rules, it's about developing instincts — and then knowing when to break them.

## Key ideas

- **Chunk your writing.** Small paragraphs, section breaks, multiple levels of structure. Finishing a section gives the reader a micro-burst of accomplishment. A long piece disguised as many short pieces is far more readable.
- **Vary everything.** Tone, sentence length, visual texture. The Gary Provost passage on sentence rhythm is the single best illustration of this principle.
- **Maintain flow of ideas.** Use structural words ("But", "So", "Nevertheless") to telegraph the architecture of your argument. Never break the flow of *ideas*, even as you break the flow of sentences and paragraphs.
- **Microhumor.** Not jokes — just the tiniest ghost of a smile. The number one thing separating enjoyable writers from competent-but-tedious ones. Works at a background level people don't even notice.
- **Concrete examples first.** Build up to the abstract principle from specific cases. People rarely hold consistent meta-level positions, so you need the object-level examples to anchor them.
- **Tribal signaling.** To persuade someone, you must first signal that you are not their enemy. Understand the signals. Have genuine sympathy. This is by far the most important persuasive technique.
- **Anticipate counterarguments.** You sound more credible when you raise the objections yourself. Preempting an opponent robs them of their low-hanging fruit.
- **Concept handles.** A catchy phrase that sums up a complex topic. This might be the most valuable thing a writer can produce: taking vague nebulous ideas and crystallizing them into something people can manipulate and discuss.
- **Learn, then break the rules.** "Break any of these rules rather than say something outright barbarous." — Orwell.

## Key quotes

> "If you figure out something interesting and very briefly cram it into somebody else's head, don't waste that! Give it a nice concept-handle so that they'll remember it and be able to use it to solve other problems!"

> "I think this microhumor stuff is really important, maybe the number one thing that separates really enjoyable writers from people who are technically proficient but still a chore to read."

> "Crossing tribal signaling boundaries is by far the most important persuasive technique I know, besides which none of the others even deserve to be called persuasive techniques at all."
