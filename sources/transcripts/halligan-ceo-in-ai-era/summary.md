# How to Be a CEO When AI Breaks All the Old Playbooks — Brian Halligan

Brian Halligan, HubSpot co-founder and Sequoia's in-house CEO coach, distills twenty years of CEO experience into a set of operating principles for scaling companies. The most important insight: the job of a CEO changes completely between startup and scale-up, and the skills that got you there (perspiration, hands-on building) are almost the opposite of the skills that keep you there (inspiration, hiring, decisive judgment). The meta-lesson across all his "Halliganisms" is that leaders chronically underestimate how hard it is to be honest, decisive, and clear — and the cost of hedging is always higher than the cost of being wrong.

## Key ideas

- **[Constructive Dissatisfaction](../../../ideas/constructive-dissatisfaction.md).** The best CEOs live in a perpetual state of positive dissatisfaction — never taking stock of accomplishments, always focused on the end state. Combined with humility, this is the engine that keeps companies from coasting. [4:12]

- **The LOCKS algorithm.** Halligan evaluates founders on five dimensions: Lovable (can you inspire followership?), Obsessed (deep founder-market fit, not six-month-old ideas), Chip on shoulder (a boulder, not a chip), Knowledgeable (deep domain expertise), and Student (constantly learning, going way back in history). The best founders are "five-tool CEOs" who can code, sell, recruit, and set vision. [16:51]

- **[Hire for Spikes](../../../ideas/hire-for-spikes.md).** HubSpot's hiring improved dramatically when they stopped picking the uniformly-good 3-out-of-4 candidate and started picking the spikier 4-and-2 candidate. Weaknesses are acceptable; mediocrity is lethal. The same principle shows up in team composition — the Grateful Dead and the 2004 Red Sox were both "spikiest of spiky teams" that created something new by combining radically different strengths. [10:43]

- **Hire slow, fire fast — and do blind references.** CEOs dramatically overrate their ability to interview. The real signal is in blind references with hard questions like "would you enthusiastically rehire this person?" and "were they top 1% of your employees?" Avoid the big-company hire: 100% attrition rate on people from Salesforce, Google, Microsoft at HubSpot. [7:59]

- **[Solve for Enterprise Value](../../../ideas/solve-for-enterprise-value.md).** EV > TV > MV: always optimize for the whole company first, then your team, then yourself. The failure mode at scale is managers who suboptimize for their department (TV) at the expense of the company (EV). HubSpot fought this with employee NPS by department, public feedback, and explicitly scoring it in performance reviews. [50:43]

- **[Single-Threaded Ownership](../../../ideas/single-threaded-ownership.md).** "If you want to kill a plant, have two people water it." Every important initiative needs a DRI (Directly Responsible Individual) with the power to tell people in other divisions what to do. Committees never work. This doesn't bite until scale — when everyone's in one room, coordination is free; when you have departments, it's not. [43:52]

- **Don't nibble the shit sandwich.** From Ruth Porat: when you have bad news, deliver it all at once. Companies that do serial small layoffs instead of one decisive cut destroy trust and drag out the pain. Overcorrect, swing the pendulum hard, make it obvious to everyone what's going on. [38:28]

- **Never waste a good crisis.** Most of the best things that happened at HubSpot came out of crises — the 2019 outage led to a complete rethinking of how they deployed software. The pattern: crisis, overcorrect, lock in the improvement. [42:02]

- **Startup to scale-up: perspiration to inspiration.** In startup mode, it's 90% perspiration and 10% inspiration. At scale, it inverts. The CEO's job becomes hiring, inspiring, making one-way door decisions quickly, and repeating the same message until it sinks in. Planning cycles compress from a year to three months. Optionality becomes a tax. [1:01:27]

## Connections

- Halligan's emphasis on feedback and "the number one thing CEOs have to learn" maps directly to [Radical Candor](../../../ideas/radical-candor.md) and the [Ruinous Empathy](../../../ideas/ruinous-empathy.md) trap — most young founders avoid hard conversations because it's unnatural
- The "don't nibble" and "never waste a good crisis" principles echo the [Boldness over Hedging](../../../ideas/boldness-over-hedging.md) principle applied to organizational decisions rather than writing
- The LOCKS algorithm's "obsession" dimension connects to [Extreme Agency](../../../ideas/extreme-agency.md) — Halligan is describing the same self-directed, all-consuming drive that makes founders immune to AI displacement
- [Exothermic Leadership](../../../ideas/exothermic-leadership.md) — constructive dissatisfaction is the emotional fuel that Tobi Lütke calls "heat injection"
- The "five-tool CEO" concept connects to Tony Fadell's [Opinion-Driven Decisions](../../../ideas/opinion-driven-decisions.md) — the new breed of CEO who can code, sell, and make gut calls
- [Focus Is Saying No](../../../ideas/focus-is-saying-no.md) — Halligan's warning about hopping to the second act too quickly when AI makes everything possible
- [A-Players Only](../../../ideas/a-players-only.md) — hiring for spikes rather than averages is the HubSpot-specific version of Jobs's talent philosophy
