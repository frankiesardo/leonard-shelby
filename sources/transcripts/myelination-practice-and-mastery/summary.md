# How to Practice Effectively — Annie Bosler & Don Greene

The biological mechanism behind "from form to formlessness." When you repeat a physical motion, layers of myelin sheath build up around the axons — the nerve fibers connecting brain to muscles. Myelin is insulation: more layers means faster, more efficient signal transmission. What athletes call "muscle memory" isn't in the muscles at all — it's myelinated neural superhighways. This is the physical substrate of what Waitzkin calls [Numbers to Leave Numbers](../../ideas/numbers-to-leave-numbers.md): conscious repetition literally builds insulation around the pathway until the signal travels so fast and cleanly that the movement feels effortless.

## Key ideas

- **Myelination is the mechanism of mastery.** Repetition adds layers of myelin around axons, creating faster and more efficient neural pathways. This is not metaphorical — it is literal insulation, like wrapping electrical cable. The more you practice a movement correctly, the thicker the sheath, the faster the signal. This is why practice makes a movement feel "automatic" — the signal no longer leaks or slows.

- **Quality over quantity.** Effective practice is consistent, intensely focused, and targets weaknesses at the edge of current ability. Coordination is built with repetitions "whether correct or incorrect" — which means sloppy practice myelinates the wrong pathways. Start slow, get it right, then increase speed. This is [Making Smaller Circles](../../ideas/making-smaller-circles.md) stated as neuroscience.

- **Mental rehearsal builds myelin too.** Basketball players who only mentally practiced free throws improved at nearly the same rate as those who physically practiced. Once a motion has been established, vivid mental imagery reinforces the same neural pathways. This validates Gallwey's image-based learning: showing [Self 2](../../ideas/self-1-vs-self-2.md) a clear visual image isn't just psychological — it's building real neural insulation.

## Key quotes

> "It may be the myelination of neural pathways that gives these athletes and performers their edge — with faster and more efficient neural pathways."

> "Coordination is built with repetitions, whether correct or incorrect."

> "Once a physical motion has been established, it can be reinforced just by imagining it."
