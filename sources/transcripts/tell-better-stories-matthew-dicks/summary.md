# How to Tell Better Stories — Matthew Dicks

Matthew Dicks is a 59-time Moth Story Slam winner, elementary school teacher, and the author of *Storyworthy* — a book that is less about "storytelling" in the corporate buzzword sense and more about the atomic structure of what makes any human utterance worth listening to. The single most important idea: every story worth telling is about one five-second moment of transformation or realization, and 98% of the story is just the context to make that moment land. Everything else — stakes, humor, surprise, structure — is in service of getting an audience to experience that flip alongside the storyteller. This is not advice about being a better speaker. It is advice about being a better noticer of your own life.

## Key ideas

- **The five-second moment.** Every story is about a singular moment — a flip from one state to another. Either transformation (I used to be one kind of person, now I'm a new kind of person) or realization (I used to think X, something happened, now I think Y). The moment might be one second long. It is the point of the story; everything else is context to bring it into focus. Dicks demonstrates this with everything from a classroom anecdote about a student named Eileen finding her confidence, to Star Wars as a story about a boy who had no religion and then found one. The practical consequence is radical: you start building your story from the end — from the moment — and work backward. The beginning is the opposite of the end. If the moment is "Eileen has confidence," the beginning is "Eileen does not have confidence and I need to help her." Romantic comedies do this transparently — they hate each other at the start, they love each other at the end — and knowing the ending doesn't ruin it because the journey is the entertainment.

- **The dinner test.** A story told on a stage or in a pitch should be a slightly elevated version of the story you'd tell someone over dinner. No performance art. No unattributed dialogue openers ("Jim, it's time to come in for dinner!"). No opening with a sound effect ("BANG, the door opened!"). If you wouldn't say it at a dinner table without your friend refusing to eat with you again, don't say it on stage. The elevation is minor: less verbal detritus, more shape, no interruptions. But the register stays conversational. Dicks pairs this with two other prerequisites: the story must contain change, and it must be *your* story — not someone else's. Third-person stories require no vulnerability, and vulnerability is what creates connection.

- **Stakes: the toolkit for keeping attention.** Dicks assumes, 100% of the time, that no one wants to hear what he has to say. From that assumption, he deploys five named tools to sustain audience attention: (1) **Elephant** — plant something big at the very beginning that makes the audience wonder what's going to happen (a big spaceship shooting at a small spaceship); it doesn't even have to be what the story is about. (2) **Backpack** — tell the audience your plan before you execute it, so they carry your hopes and dreams with them (the Ocean's 11 technique). (3) **Breadcrumbs** — drop hints about what's coming without revealing the full picture (Chekhov's gun). (4) **Hourglass** — when the audience is on the edge of their seat, slow time down, load the moment with details, and make them wait. (5) **Crystal ball** — predict a terrible future, even a false one, to make the audience worry. The key mistake most people make: front-loading all the stakes. Instead, spread them throughout, building continuously through the first half, then let the second half be the roller coaster to the end.

- **Humor as competitive advantage.** Of the four things that hold attention — stakes, surprise, suspense, and humor — humor is the one everyone wants and almost no one dares to deploy. Corporate speakers pull out their jokes the moment they see that prior speakers were unfunny, which is exactly when humor would make them rise like a phoenix from the ashes. Dicks offers two humor strategies safe for business: *nostalgia* (the past is inherently funny — a 22-pound VCR with a corded remote, job hunting via newspaper classifieds delivered by a 16-year-old on a Schwinn) and *one of these things is not like the other* (two expected items, then one unexpected one — the Sesame Street game that every comedian uses). The beautiful thing about humor embedded in story: if the joke doesn't land, you're still telling a story. No one even realizes you were trying to be funny.

- **Homework for Life.** The most important practice Dicks teaches, regardless of whether you ever plan to speak publicly. Every night, look back on the day and write down one moment that would be worth telling as a story — even if it's not really worth telling. Use an Excel spreadsheet: date in column A, the moment in column B (one line, the width of a screen). Dicks started finding 1.8 moments per day; after 12 years, he finds 7.6 — not because his life got more interesting but because his lens improved. Only about 10% of recorded moments become stories, but the other 90% are equally valuable because they hold onto your days. The practice also cracks open old memories: seeing a student find confidence connects to other forgotten moments about confidence, and those days return to you. Time doesn't fly — it goes by unaccounted. If you remember 89 of 365 days, of course it feels fast. Homework for Life is the refusal to let days disappear.

- **Speaking with adjacency.** When you need a story to explain a product or idea, don't match content to content — match *theme, meaning, or message*. A scientist selling specialized lab tubes doesn't tell a story about tubes. He tells a story about buying different apples for every member of his picky family at the grocery store. Then he snaps it into place: "That's what my company does — we believe you deserve to get exactly what you need." That snap — when the audience realizes the story about apples was really about tubes — is extraordinarily powerful. It works because you're not asking people to care about your product; you're making them feel something universal and then connecting that feeling to your product. Dicks uses the same technique with misbehaving students: he tells them a story about his dog when he was 12, and they sit there confused about why he's not yelling at them, until the story snaps into place and the lesson lands harder than any lecture would.

- **Start with location and action.** The single most tactical advice: begin every story you ever tell with where you are and what you're doing. "I'm standing in the kitchen" activates imagination — the listener applies a thousand adjectives instantly, probably placing you in their own kitchen. Add action — something happening right away — and you signal that the movie is on. Nobody talks during the movie. This opening technique is especially powerful for people who struggle to command attention — women, people of color, anyone from a marginalized group have reported back to Dicks that starting with location and action silences the room and creates space to speak in a way that nothing else does.

## Connections

- **[Unexpected Change as Story Engine](../../ideas/unexpected-change-as-story-engine.md).** Dicks's five-second moment *is* unexpected change distilled to its most practical form. Where Will Storr explains the neuroscience of why change grips attention, Dicks gives you the craft formula: find the moment of change, make that the end of your story, then make the beginning its opposite. Same engine, different manual.

- **[The Dramatic Question](../../ideas/the-dramatic-question.md).** Dicks's stakes toolkit — elephant, backpack, breadcrumbs, hourglass, crystal ball — are all techniques for sustaining the dramatic question. Each one is a way to keep the audience asking "who is this person and what will happen to them?" without answering prematurely.

- **[Noticing as Discipline](../../ideas/noticing-as-discipline.md).** Homework for Life is noticing-as-discipline applied to your own life. Klinkenborg says "if you notice something, it's because it's important." Dicks says the same thing — you're not looking for stories, you're looking for moments that touch your heart or mind. The spreadsheet is just the mechanism that forces you to practice the attention.

- **[Life as a Series of Moments](../../ideas/life-as-a-series-of-moments.md).** Adler says life is not a line but a series of dots. Dicks would agree — and add that each dot deserves to be written down. The homework-for-life practice is what turns the Adlerian philosophy into a daily habit. It's the difference between believing your days matter and actually accounting for them.

- **[Humor as a Serious Tool](../../ideas/humor-as-a-serious-tool.md) / [Microhumor](../../ideas/microhumor.md).** Dicks's humor strategies — nostalgia, "one of these things is not like the other" — are the oral storytelling equivalents of Scott Alexander's microhumor. They don't announce themselves as jokes. They're embedded in the story so that if they miss, nobody noticed you were trying. Same principle, different medium.

- **[Trust Your Material](../../ideas/trust-your-material.md).** The apple-tubes scientist told no data at his conference and got more leads than the other four scientists combined. Dicks's version of "trust your material" is trust that a story about buying apples will sell lab equipment better than a data presentation. The audience does the connecting — and they remember it because they did the work themselves.

- **[Concept Handles](../../ideas/concept-handles.md).** Dicks is a concept handle machine: five-second moment, homework for life, elephant, backpack, breadcrumbs, hourglass, crystal ball, dinner test, speaking with adjacency. Each one is a portable name for a technique you can carry around and deploy. This is why Storyworthy is so actionable — every idea has a handle.

- **[Slowing Down Time](../../ideas/slowing-down-time.md).** The hourglass technique is slowing down time applied to oral narrative — when the audience is maximally engaged, you stretch the moment with detail and make them wait. Waitzkin's perceptual time dilation and Dicks's narrative time dilation share the same structure: compress when nothing is at stake, expand when everything is.
