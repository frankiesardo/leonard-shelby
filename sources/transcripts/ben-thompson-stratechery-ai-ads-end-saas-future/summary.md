# Ben Thompson on AI Ads, the End of SaaS, and the Future of Media — Stratechery

A wide-ranging conversation between Ben Thompson (Stratechery) and Patrick Collison (Stripe) covering advertising models for AI, the future of SaaS as AI changes headcount economics, aggregation theory applied to AI-era commerce, and the media business of one. Thompson is at his sharpest defending advertising as a genuine societal good — a contrarian position in tech — and sketching what the ideal AI ad product would look like. The most interesting thread is his distinction between OpenAI's AOL-style aggregator play vs Google's infrastructure-layer play.

## Key ideas

- **Advertising is a societal good, not an evil.** Thompson argues forcefully that ads are what fund universal access to information — the same search engine serves a president and someone on the street. The tech elite's knee-jerk anti-ad stance is a stated vs revealed preference: given the option to pay for Facebook, users choose ads. The failure of companies like Meta is not running ads but *not championing* ads.

- **AI ads should be Meta-style, not Google-style.** OpenAI's current approach — showing ads contextually related to the conversation — is the worst possible model. It raises suspicion (why is this ad about what I'm asking?), it limits inventory to conversations that match available ads, and it conflates the answer with the pitch. The right model: build a deep user profile from conversations and serve ads that are relevant to *the person*, not *the query* — exactly what Meta does. Google's ideal play is never putting ads in Gemini at all, but using Gemini to improve ad targeting across YouTube and Search.

- **OpenAI is the new AOL; Google is the new Google.** OpenAI wants to be the interface for everything — a walled garden aggregator. A bullish reading is Netscape; a bearish one is AOL. Google, by contrast, wants to make everyone capable of building on AI, knowing it benefits most when it controls the front door to demand. The Stripe/OpenAI agent commerce announcement vs Google's agent infrastructure announcement illustrates this tension perfectly.

- **Agent commerce has levels.** Level 1: AI fills out forms (just better UI for checkout). Level 2: AI-powered search with natural language parameters ("jacket for -10°C ski trip"). Level 3: persistent user preference profile that anticipates needs before you search. Level 4: anticipatory ads — the AI surfaces a winter jacket ad in October before you know you need one. Facebook advertising is already the most successful agent in the world: you give it a customer acquisition cost, it delivers customers at that price.

- **SaaS isn't dead, but its growth story is.** Systems of record (Workday, Salesforce) are safe — nobody is Cursor-coding their HR system. But the seat-based pricing model breaks as AI reduces headcount growth. The bigger risk: the entire Silicon Valley playbook (VC funds a SaaS company targeting a specific TAM) was a factory that produced predictable returns, and that factory is now exposed to AI-powered self-serve, smaller teams, and lower seat counts. The business survives; the valuation multiple doesn't.

- **Make your own pond.** Thompson's media advice: you can't compete against Bill Simmons at being Bill Simmons. The internet enables infinite niche ponds — the right move is to become the only fish in your own pond. AI extends this to all businesses: more small teams and solo operators who don't need seat-based software at all.

## Key quotes

> "The reality of advertising is… the president uses the same search engine as the guy on the street or the same AI. That's because of ads."

> "The ideal outcome for Google is they never put ads in Gemini, but they understand so much about you because of what you do in Gemini that they can then manifest that through ads on YouTube, through ads on Google."

> "The largest and most successful agent in the world today is Facebook advertising." — Mark Zuckerberg, paraphrased by Thompson

> "You don't want to be in a pond with me. What you want to do is you want to make your own pond."

> "Burnout's not a function of how much work you're doing. It's doing work you don't enjoy."
