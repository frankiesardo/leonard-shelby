# What Most People Miss About Marketing — Rory Sutherland

Rory Sutherland, Vice Chairman of Ogilvy UK and author of *Alchemy*, joins Lenny Rachitsky to make the case that the most important variable in whether a product succeeds or fails is almost never the product itself. Marketing and psychology do as much — often more — work than technology or execution. The failure of Google Glass, the success of the Toyota Prius, the mystery of why electricity was once considered terrifying: these are all marketing problems, not engineering ones. The core provocation: "all value is perceived value," and businesses that pretend otherwise are playing the game on hard mode.

## Key ideas

- **[Psycho-logic](../../ideas/psycho-logic.md) over logic.** Economic logic says: give people more utility for less cost. Psychological logic says: the way something is framed, signaled, and contextualised matters as much as what it does. Human behavior is "non-linear and non-proportionate" — a small reframe can change willingness to buy more than a 20% price cut. Marketing's job is not to deceive people into buying things they don't need; it's to frame genuine value in the psychological register where people actually make decisions. Businesses that treat marketing as a decorative layer on top of a rational product argument systematically underperform against businesses that understand psycho-logic.

- **[MAYA — Most Advanced Yet Acceptable](../../ideas/maya-most-advanced-yet-acceptable.md).** Raymond Loewy's principle: the sweet spot between novelty and familiarity. Too novel and people refuse: too threatening, too uncertain, too much cognitive load. Too familiar and nobody cares: no reason to switch. The Prius got the signals right (the distinct silhouette, the visible hybrid badge, the "I'm different" aura) — not just the engineering. Google Glass got the signals wrong — too weird, no clear frame, made everyone else uncomfortable. Timing and framing are often more important than technical quality in determining whether a genuinely good innovation gets adopted.

- **[Fame as a moat](../../ideas/fame-as-moat.md).** "Having a great brand means you get to play the game of capitalism on easy mode." Fame is not vanity — it's a structural competitive advantage. A well-known brand doesn't need to win every comparison; it gets considered automatically. It can charge a premium not because it's objectively better but because it's trusted. Building fame requires consistency and distinctiveness over time. For early-stage founders: don't wait until you have a marketing budget to think about fame. Brand signals compound; start building them from the start.

- **Marketing and innovation in parallel, not series.** Most companies sequence: build the product, *then* figure out the marketing. This is almost always wrong. The marketing insight might change what you build. The psychological frame might be as important as the feature. Rory's advice for founders: run psychological/brand thinking in parallel with technological and economic thinking from day one. "Try and use those three things in parallel. What most businesses do is they try and do things in series."

- **The comedian test for distinctiveness.** "If you could imagine a stand-up comedian doing a routine about your product, you're onto something." The urge to appear serious is a marketing disaster. Distinctiveness — the quality of being immediately recognisable, slightly strange, memorable — matters more than polish. This isn't about being quirky for its own sake; it's about escaping the gravity of generic. The generic brand premium is zero. Distinctive brands earn attention without spending for it.

- **Steve Jobs was a marketer.** Not a technologist. "He was a pitchman, he was a brilliant salesman, he was a fantastic marketer." This is not a diminishment — it's the correct diagnosis of why Apple succeeded where better-engineered competitors didn't. The lesson isn't "hire marketers"; it's "treat marketing as a first-class competence at the top of the org, not a downstream function."

- **Metrics-driven workplaces strip psychological meaning.** Rory's critique of over-managed, over-measured organisations: when you reduce everything to a number, you destroy the psychological meaning that makes people care about their work. Humans aren't economic units; they're motivated by narrative, identity, mastery, and contribution. A factory where every second is tracked and every action is measured produces compliance, not creativity.
