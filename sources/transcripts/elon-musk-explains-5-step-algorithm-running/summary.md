# Elon Musk explains his 5-step algorithm for running companies — Elon Musk

A compact, sequenced framework for process improvement that Musk distilled from building SpaceX and Tesla. The insight that makes it useful is not any individual step but the *order*: most engineers and managers habitually skip to step 3, 4, or 5 without doing 1 and 2, thereby optimizing and accelerating things that should have been deleted. Musk freely admits he's made this exact mistake himself, multiple times.

## Key ideas

- **Step 1: Make your requirements less dumb.** Requirements are always wrong. It doesn't matter who gave them to you — the smarter the person, the more dangerous the requirement, because you're less likely to question it. Every requirement must come with a name, not a department. A department can't be held accountable; a person can. And that person must agree to own the requirement. Otherwise you're executing on constraints that an intern invented two years ago and has since left the company. See: [Requirements Need Owners](../../ideas/requirements-need-owners.md).

- **Step 2: Delete the part or process step.** The bias is always toward adding — "let's include this just in case." But "just in case" arguments can be made for almost anything. If you're not occasionally adding things *back* that you deleted, you're not deleting aggressively enough. Musk's rule of thumb: 10% of deleted things should come back. That's the signal calibration — you're cutting to the right depth.

- **Step 3: Simplify or optimize. Only now.** This is the most commonly violated rule. See: [Delete Before Optimizing](../../ideas/delete-before-optimizing.md). Optimizing something that should be deleted is pure waste. The educational system trains people to answer the question — you can't tell the professor the question is dumb, or you fail. That convergent logic creates a mental straitjacket. Smart engineers end up perfecting things that shouldn't exist.

- **Step 4: Accelerate cycle time.** Once you've done 1, 2, and 3, go faster. Not before. Speed applied to the wrong thing is just faster waste.

- **Step 5: Automate.** Only automate what's already been simplified and proven. Automation of a bad process is a $2 million mistake — literally, in the case of the glass mat robots on the Model 3 line.

- **The glass mat story is the whole lesson in miniature.** Five glass mats between the Model 3 floor pan and battery pack were choking the production line. Musk's mistake sequence: first tried to fix the automation, then to accelerate it, then to optimize it — all before asking *why the mats existed at all.* Safety team said fire protection. NVH team said noise and vibration. Neither was correct. The mats were useless. They were deleted. A $2 million automation robot became unnecessary. The lesson is both about process and about accountability: nobody owned the requirement, so nobody caught it.

## Connections

- **[Theory of Constraints](../../ideas/theory-of-constraints.md)** is the systems-level frame behind this algorithm. Goldratt's first focusing step — identify the constraint — is the spirit of step 1 and 2: don't optimize anywhere until you know what actually matters. Musk's glass mat story is a perfect illustration of what happens when you improve a non-bottleneck.

- **[Bottleneck](../../ideas/bottleneck.md)** — the glass mat robot was a bottleneck created by a bad requirement. Exploiting or accelerating a false bottleneck is worse than doing nothing: it consumes resources and creates confidence that the system is improving.

- **[Cognitive Entrenchment](../../ideas/cognitive-entrenchment.md)** — step 3 being the most violated rule is exactly cognitive entrenchment in action. Engineers trained to solve well-specified problems reach for optimization as the default. The straitjacket is domain expertise applied to the wrong question.

- **[Beginner's Mind](../../ideas/beginners-mind.md)** — the corrective to the straitjacket. Asking "what are these mats for?" after years of automation work is a beginner's question. The expert would never ask it; it feels too naive. That's the trap.

- **[Companies as Technology](../../ideas/companies-as-technology.md)** — Lütke's idea that a company is a social technology you can engineer from first principles rhymes perfectly here. Both Musk and Lütke advocate rederiving from axioms rather than inheriting constraints. The difference is Musk gives a specific algorithm for doing so at the process level.

- **[Boldness over Hedging](../../ideas/boldness-over-hedging.md)** — the "just in case" arguments that accumulate into process bloat are the opposite of bold. They're the organizational immune response to uncertainty, and they compound into dysfunction.

- **[The Outsider Advantage](../../ideas/the-outsider-advantage.md)** — the person who finally asks "what are these mats for?" is always the outsider. Specialists assume the constraint is real. The outsider questions whether the constraint should exist at all. Step 1 is deliberately adopting an outsider perspective on requirements you've been handed.
