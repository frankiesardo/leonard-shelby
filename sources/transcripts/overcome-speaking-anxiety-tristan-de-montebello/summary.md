# Why Most Public Speaking Advice Is Wrong — Tristan de Montebello

De Montebello's core insight is that speaking is not a conscious, tactical skill but a subconscious flow-oriented process -- more like freestyle rapping than reciting a script. Most advice fails because it adds conscious rules on top of a system that works best when the conscious mind gets out of the way. The path to better speaking is not more frameworks but fewer bugs in the software: strip away the self-consciousness, the leaking, the hedging, and the monotone safety mode, and what's left is the extraordinary hardware we already have. The approach rhymes deeply with Gallwey's Inner Game: Self 1 is the enemy; Self 2 already knows how to speak.

## Key ideas

- **Speaking is a meta skill, not a specialized skill.** Like fitness, improving your speaking sends ripple effects through everything else -- work, friendships, family, confidence in groups of strangers. The more constrained you feel in your speaking, the more transformative it will be to unlock it. De Montebello frames it as "debugging and upgrading the software" on hardware that evolution already perfected.

- **Enjoyment is a barometer, not a luxury.** If speaking doesn't feel enjoyable, you're doing it wrong. Humans are evolved for communication; it's naturally rewarding when we feel like ourselves. When it stops being fun, that's a signal you're overthinking. This applies equally to practice: if your practice isn't enjoyable, you'll quit in two weeks, just like a bad fitness program.

- **Speaking is a subconscious flow-oriented process.** The breakthrough comes when you stop thinking about speaking and start getting into a flow state. When you speak best, you're not thinking about the speaking part at all -- you're in tune with what you're trying to convey. This reframes all practice: instead of adding conscious tactics, address the root causes that pull you out of flow.

- **Stay in character -- don't leak.** People can't see what you feel. Even when your internal world is screaming that something went wrong, the audience just sees a normal, competent speaker. The moment you break character -- "sorry, I'm not making sense" -- you force a lens of doubt onto your audience that wasn't there before. The pilot analogy: sharing the blinking red light in the cockpit doesn't calm anyone; it just makes every bit of turbulence feel fatal. The cue: "stay in it." The worse it gets, the more you stay in it. The feedback loop is self-reinforcing: you stay in character, the audience responds as if you're confident, and that makes you feel more confident.

- **End strong.** Freestyle rappers' fMRI scans show that the brain exits flow right before the finish. In speaking, this manifests as trailing off, hedging ("I don't know if that makes sense"), or actively voicing doubt at the landing. The fix: anticipate it, use summary prompts ("so to wrap up...", "my point here is..."), and land the plane. Peak-end rule means the ending disproportionately shapes how people remember the whole thing.

- **Games as flight simulators.** You can't learn to speak by not speaking, just as you can't learn to cook by reading cookbooks. The Ultra Speaking games (Conductor, Triple Step, Conviction Prompts) create deliberate turbulence in short reps with immediate feedback. Each game targets a specific root cause: Conductor builds range and emotional access, Triple Step builds resilience against distraction, Conviction Prompts build executive presence. The games are to speaking what a flight simulator is to flying -- you practice the worst-case scenarios so real scenarios feel easy.

- **The accordion method.** Prepare talks by speaking, not writing. Start at three minutes, compress to two, one, thirty seconds -- each round forces you to shed noise and find the essential pillars. Then expand back up: thirty seconds to one minute to two to three. By the time you open back up, you have a football field of space and only the strongest ideas remain. The result is a talk that's internalized (not memorized), plastic (works at any time length), and impossible to derail because you know the structure in your bones.

- **The bow and arrow.** Switch from "what do I want to say" to "what do I want my audience to remember." The answer should be a single sentence -- the arrow. Everything else (stories, data, anecdotes) is the bow that gives the arrow weight and impact. Works at macro (whole talk) and micro (each slide) levels. If a slide doesn't have a one thing, it shouldn't exist or should be split.

- **[Self-Authorization](../../ideas/self-authorization.md)** -- The "bullshitter immune response" passage: many people with good ideas atrophy their communication muscles because they fear resembling someone who has style without substance. De Montebello's counter: if you're worried about being a bullshitter, you won't become one. Give yourself permission to match their communication skill while keeping your substance.
- **[Hitters Don't Talk About the Set](../julio-velasco-hitters-dont-talk-about-the-set/summary.md)** -- Velasco's hitters solve the bad set instead of judging it; de Montebello's speakers stay in character instead of announcing the stumble. Same principle: respond to reality instead of narrating your judgment of it.
