# Big Rocks — Stephen R. Covey

Covey brings a volunteer named Elizabeth on stage — she directs an international firm, wants a rich personal and family life, and admits she gets "bogged down in the thick of thin things." He fills a bowl with small pebbles (the trivial daily stuff that accumulates), then asks her to fit large rocks labeled with her real priorities: Q2 planning/preparation/prevention, relationships and family, employment, major projects, service/community/church. They don't fit. She tries rearranging, squeezing, giving up her vacation block. Still doesn't work. Then Covey offers a fresh bowl — a "totally different paradigm." Elizabeth puts the big rocks in first, then pours the pebbles around them. They all fit. The physics make the argument: **if the big rocks don't go in first, they never go in at all.**

## Key ideas

- **The thick of thin things.** [0:35] Covey's opening question cuts right to it — Elizabeth admits she gets bogged down. The small pebbles are a physical metaphor for email, interruptions, low-priority tasks. They don't feel like much individually, but "little by little they just accumulate" until there's no room for what matters.

- **Big rocks have names.** [1:18] The rocks aren't abstract. Covey labels them: Quadrant II planning and preparation, relationships and family, employment opportunities, major projects, service and community. These are specific, nameable [Quadrant II](../../../ideas/quadrant-ii.md) priorities — not vague goals but real commitments that need real space.

- **You can't retrofit priorities.** [2:05] Elizabeth tries moving pebbles around to squeeze in one more big rock. It almost works — but not quite. You can't add what matters most after the trivial stuff has already filled the container. This is why "working harder" and "being more efficient" don't solve the real problem.

- **A totally different paradigm.** [2:53] The solution isn't better pebble management — it's starting fresh with an empty bowl and a different order of operations. Put the big rocks in first, then let the small stuff fill the gaps. This is the core of Habit 3: schedule your priorities, don't prioritize your schedule.

## Key quotes
