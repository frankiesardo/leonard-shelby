# What Most People Miss About Marketing — Rory Sutherland

Rory Sutherland on Lenny's Podcast, speaking directly to product builders and startup founders. Where his Knowledge Project interview is a broad philosophical argument against rationalism, this one is a practical toolkit: how to think about product design, branding, and adoption when human psychology doesn't follow the engineering playbook. The single most important takeaway: the reason good products fail is almost never the product — it's timing, user imagery, or the failure to market something genuinely new. And we systematically forget this because survivorship bias only shows us the winners.

## Key ideas

- **MAYA — Maximally Advanced Yet Acceptable.** Raymond Loewy's principle: there's a pace of change consumers will absorb. Push too far and they reject it, not because it's bad but because it's too unfamiliar. Loewy made a fan so quiet nobody believed it worked. A razor so silent it felt broken. The iPhone succeeded partly because the iPod preceded it — consumers migrated their behavior rather than reinventing it. Don't be too weird. Be the right amount of weird.

- **Idiosyncrasies count double.** Preserve the slightly odd things. Jaguar's console-mounted ceiling light switch. Rolls-Royce's floor pedal for headlamps. Veuve Clicquot's accidentally yellow label that became the entire brand identity. The comedian test: if a stand-up comic could do a routine about your product, you're onto something. Distinctiveness that creates a "splinter on the attention" — something slightly off from the normal shape that people notice and remember.

- **User imagery kills adoption.** The Ford/Edison electric car of the 1900s was technically excellent — quiet, fast, good range. It failed because it was stereotyped as a "woman's car" while farm boys raced gasoline engines. Today's electric cars face the same problem: non-owners see EV drivers as "smug environmental tossers," even though most EV buyers don't care about the environment — they're car enthusiasts and tech nerds. Google Glass failed partly because the first adopters were developers, not cool people. Early adopters are by definition slightly weird, and weird people don't confer the social-norm reassurance needed for mass adoption.

- **The two default modes.** Humans don't utility-maximize. We have two hardwired defaults: do what I did before (habit) and do what everybody else does (social copying). Both are spectacularly rational in evolutionary terms — an organism that learned everything from first principles would eat a lot of poisonous berries. The implication: the single strongest predictor of heat pump adoption is not cost savings or environmental messaging — it's whether three neighbors already have one.

- **Brand is playing capitalism on easy mode.** Eric Johnson's definition: "Having a great brand means you get to play the game of capitalism on easy mode." Fame compounds nonlinearly and is impossible to quantify with short-term ROI. Brand building is like a pension — painful and seemingly worthless for the first few years, then suddenly you look at the balance and wonder where all the money came from. For startups: be consistent, be distinctive, be famous. When you're not famous, you have to find all your customers. Past a certain escape velocity of fame, customers start finding you.

- **Small autonomous teams.** Octopus Energy's multicellular structure. Shopify's customer service teams of 10, modeled on sports teams by COO Toby Shanahan. AO's "treat the customer like your grandmother" heuristic. When teams are small enough, natural human instincts of reciprocation and obligation kick in — pulling a sick day in a team of 10 feels very different from a team of 100. Design for human psychology, not the org chart.

- **The ancient Persians: deliberate sober, then deliberate drunk.** A two-stage decision-making model: rational approval AND emotional approval before acting. Creative people always have to present to rational people. Rational people never present to creative people. The asymmetry means psychological solutions get killed before they're born.

- **Marketing and innovation are two sides of the same coin.** Either work out what people want and find a clever way to make it, or work out what you can make and find a clever way to make people want it. Do both in parallel, not in series. The Fordist production-line mentality of doing things sequentially is a lie — Wrigley started with soap, pivoted to baking powder, and ended up a chewing gum company. We post-rationalize linearity that was never there.
