# Is SaaS Dead? Do Margins Still Matter? — Anish Acharya (20VC)

Anish Acharya, GP at a16z leading consumer and fintech at Series A, interviewed by Harry Stebbings. The headline topic — is SaaS dying — is the least interesting part. What matters: the moat vocabulary is changing. Lock-in is eroding (coding agents collapse switching costs), but live proprietary data is compounding. The apps layer has real value as a multi-model aggregation point. And the most interesting consumer products will be "weird" — built in emotional, human territory that big-corp committees won't touch. Underneath all of it: ambition, not execution, is now the binding constraint.

## Key ideas

- **The SaaS apocalypse is overblown.** IT spend is only 8–12% of enterprise spend. "You have this innovation bazooka with these models — why would you point it at rebuilding payroll or ERP?" Companies will use AI to attack the other 90% (core competitive advantage, process optimization) before they rebuild software they already have. Evidence: 75% of SaaS public companies have *raised* prices since ChatGPT launched, many by 25%+. Price is a measure of product-market fit — you don't raise prices under competitive pressure. Some SaaS business models will be secular losers, but "the market is oversold software" as a thesis is wrong.

- **Coding agents reduce switching costs, which is net positive.** The most interesting near-term AI story in enterprise software isn't vibe-coding new ERP systems — it's reducing migration complexity. Moving from SAP to Oracle was a multi-year, high-risk project that typically failed. Coding agents cut that complexity dramatically. "[Hostages not customers](../../../ideas/hostages-not-customers.md)" is the old model. Reduced switching costs → more competition → better products. This is healthy for the ecosystem even if it's bad for entrenched incumbents.

- **Incumbents make existing products better; startups own the new categories.** History is consistent: capable incumbents use new tech cycles to make their existing categories stronger (Microsoft makes a better word processor; Google makes a better search engine). Startups win the *categories that didn't exist* before the cycle. AI movie-making, AI native law — no incumbent owns these. The real question is Alex Rampel's: will the incumbent acquire innovation before the startup acquires distribution?

- **The apps layer creates real value through multi-model aggregation.** If models were a single monopolist, they'd extract 100% of gross margin downstream (the "Beatles" problem). Instead, models are roughly 80% substitutes and 20% specialists — which creates value for an orchestration/apps layer that can mix and match. Cursor as an IDE that routes to Gemini for frontend and Codex for backend. Creative tools that mix Midjourney's aesthetic opinionation with Ideogram's neutral-palette design mode. This looks more like AWS/GCP (oligopoly, reasonable margins) than Uber/Lyft (pure substitutes, competed away pricing).

- **[Weird wins](../../../ideas/weird-wins.md).** The models are emotional, non-predictable, and very human — which is different from every technology before. Big corps have committees explicitly designed to prevent persuasion, disagreement, and sexuality from showing up in products. Startups can build in the pocket of human experience that the labs and big tech find uncomfortable: companionship, AI personas, contextual social coaching. Not as gimmicks but because these products serve real unmet needs. The willingness-to-be-embarrassed threshold is a competitive advantage. Related: consumer startups are often dismissed as silly before they're enormous. Once you've sold a company, you care too much about sounding cool at dinner parties to take those shots.

- **Contextual companions beat direct AI companions.** A senior citizen won't accept "an AI that calls every night to check in on them" — that threatens their self-respect. But an AI that calls to confirm they've taken their medicine, then lightly flirts about World War II? That's a context in which companionship is delivered with a level of indirection. The same principle applies to kids: an AI Minecraft companion that models pro-social behavior while playing together is more useful than a direct tutoring bot. The indirection is the product design.

- **[Live proprietary data is the new real moat.](../../../ideas/live-data-advantage.md)** Anish was historically skeptical of "data network effects" — it was the moat people claimed when they couldn't think of a real one. His view has changed. *Live* proprietary data — health data, running operational systems — beats a frontier model without context. Put a commodity model against live data, beat a cutting-edge model with only public data. This matters for thinking about which systems of record are defensible (high-frequency, human-workflow-heavy ones like core banking) vs. which aren't (on-prem databases with no engagement layer).

- **Unbundle the margin to see the business.** AI-native companies have lower blended gross margins, but this conflates two things: (1) CAC disguised as cost-of-goods (free trial compute, subsidized access to acquire users) and (2) the durable margin on paying power users. Separate them. Look at M2 as your real M1 (tourists come in at M1 and you pay nothing to acquire them). Power users now pay $200–300/month, not $25 — the 10x price increase in consumer software is real and changes unit economics permanently. M12 retention of 50%+ is solid; 60–70% is very exciting.

- **Not in a bubble: supply is absorbed as fast as it's created.** OpenAI 3x'd capacity, 3x'd topline — demand absorbed all of it. In actual bubbles, supply outruns demand and prices fall. Here, prices are rising. The subsidization that exists ("intelligent subsidization" from big tech and labs in the form of free trial compute) converts to paying power users — healthy calories, not the empty-calorie subsidies of 2021 where startup money went straight to Google/Facebook ad buys.

- **Agent maximalism is premature.** "Chill out for the day and your AI does everything" is not where we are. You need humans in the loop for exception handling. Instructions to models are often frustratingly vague (think about how you manage your team). The more interesting frame: agents remove *low-NPS tasks* (the work you have to do, not the work you want to do) while dramatically expanding the circumference of what individuals can attempt. Execution and expertise are no longer constraints. Ambition is the only binding constraint left.

- **[Area-under-the-curve companies](../../../ideas/area-under-the-curve.md) are underrated in venture lore.** Not everything that matters grows like a hypercurve. Some companies — Figma, Stripe early on — spend years in the build phase and emerge with defensibility (network effects, workflow integration) that fast-slope companies can't match. These are the "20-year overnight successes." The area under the curve can be much larger than the slope at any given point suggests. Investors systematically undervalue them.

## Connections

- **[Task Loss vs. Job Loss](../../../ideas/task-loss-vs-job-loss.md)** — Anish reinforces the Andreessen frame: AI changes tasks first, job bundles reshuffle. His version: jobs like customer support / sales / collections / operations collapse into a single AI-powered workflow. The agent isn't replacing the job; it's dissolving the artificial boundaries between tasks that were only separated by human archetype constraints.

- **[Extreme Agency](../../../ideas/extreme-agency.md)** — "Execution or expertise is no longer a constraint. Ambition is the only binding constraint left." This is Naval's frame restated: the people who won't be displaced are those with authentic, self-directed purpose. Anish extends it to product strategy — the circumference of what any individual can attempt is growing, so the differentiator is who actually *wants* to attempt something hard enough to do it.

- **[Latent Demand](../../../ideas/latent-demand.md)** — The "weird wins" frame is the supply-side version of latent demand. The demand for companionship, social coaching, and persona-driven products was always there — prior technology (and institutional gatekeeping) couldn't reach it. Now it can.

- **[Beginner's Mind](../../../ideas/beginners-mind.md)** — The consumer founder's "willingness to be embarrassed" maps onto shoshin. Post-exit founders lose it because status anxiety calcifies them. The best consumer founders are embarrassable.

- **[Marc Andreessen: The Real AI Boom Hasn't Even Started Yet](../marc-andreessen-real-ai-boom-hasnt-even-started/summary.md)** — Overlapping a16z worldview, but Anish is more bullish on incumbents and more skeptical of the SaaS apocalypse than the firm's standard line. He also flips the data-moat argument — where Marc focuses on skill stacking, Anish focuses on live data as the new defensibility.

- **[What Happens After Coding Is Solved](../after-coding-is-solved-boris-cherny/summary.md)** — Both Anish and Boris point to the same underlying dynamic: the bottleneck shifts when coding gets easier. Boris focuses on what shifts in software development workflows; Anish focuses on what shifts in enterprise adoption curves and switching costs.
