# A child psychologist's guide to working with difficult adults — Dr. Becky Kennedy

Dr. Becky Kennedy, clinical psychologist and CEO of Good Inside, joins Lenny Rachitsky to argue that the same principles that make great parents make great leaders. The core insight: all humans — whether they're 2 or 45 — need the same things, and bad behavior at any age is just feelings overpowering skills. If you treat the behavior as a skill gap rather than a character flaw, you unlock a completely different set of interventions — ones that actually work. This is the best bridge I've seen between parenting advice and leadership advice, and the overlap is uncomfortable in how precise it is.

## Key ideas

- **Repair is the number one relationship strategy.** Secure attachment isn't defined by getting it right all the time — it's defined by the presence of repair. Going back to someone after a moment you weren't proud of, taking responsibility, and saying what you'd do differently reestablishes trust and connection. At work, this means saying "I cut you off earlier, I used a harsh tone, I'm sorry" — not pretending it didn't happen. Perfect is creepy; repair is human.

- **Separate identity from behavior.** The "good inside" framework requires you to hold two things at once: this is a good person *and* this behavior is not okay. The moment you collapse those — "they're late, so they're lazy" — you trigger defensiveness and the conversation becomes about identity, not behavior. Starting with "we're on the same team, I know you're a good person" takes identity off the table so you can actually talk about what needs to change. Directly parallels [Radical Candor](../books/radical-candor/summary.md)'s challenge directly, care personally.

- **The Most Generous Interpretation (MGI).** A practical tool for separating behavior from identity. When someone's behavior frustrates you, ask: what's the most generous interpretation of why they're doing this? The story you tell yourself about someone at night becomes the leader you are the next morning. MGI doesn't excuse the behavior — it opens a door to a productive conversation instead of a resentful monologue.

- **Sturdiness over softness or rigidity.** The pilot metaphor: in turbulence, you don't want a pilot who yells at you for screaming, or one who opens the cockpit door and asks who wants to fly. You want one who says "I hear you, that makes sense, and I know what I'm doing." Sturdy leadership means seeing someone else's emotional experience as real for them without being overwhelmed by it yourself. Two things at once: I see you, and I'm still in charge.

- **Boundaries are what you will do, not what you ask others to do.** If you're setting a "boundary" that requires the other person to comply, you're making a request, not setting a boundary — and you're giving all your power away. A real boundary: "I'm going to stand between you and the buttons." Not: "Please don't press the buttons." This reframe is directly applicable to management: stop asking people to do your job for you and take the action yourself.

- **Resilience over happiness.** Optimizing for happiness — removing every discomfort, shielding from every difficulty — is the fastest way to build fragility. The resilient formula: "I believe you" (this is hard, your feelings make sense) + "I believe in you" (you can do this, and I won't take the struggle away from you because the pride of getting through it is the thing that builds capability). This is the [Ruinous Empathy](../../../ideas/ruinous-empathy.md) lesson reframed for parenting: not giving feedback to spare feelings is not kindness, it's deprivation.

- **Connect before correcting.** People feel your intention, not just your intervention. Connection isn't a soft skill — it's the bridge between two people that makes cooperation possible. You can't connect as a transaction ("let me ask about your weekend so I can assign you work"). It requires being present without an agenda, even for 30 seconds.

## Connections to Adler and the garden

Dr. Becky never names Adler, but this is Adlerian psychology applied to parenting and management. The overlaps with [The Courage to Be Disliked](../../books/the-courage-to-be-disliked/summary.md) are striking:

- **Boundaries = [Separation of Tasks](../../../ideas/separation-of-tasks.md).** Dr. Becky's definition — "what you tell someone else you will do, requires the other person to do nothing" — is Adlerian task separation made concrete. The boundary is *your* task. Whether the other person gets upset is *their* task. The elevator button example is a perfect illustration: you don't ask the child to comply (intruding on their task), you physically stand between them and the buttons (doing your task).

- **"Good inside" = [Horizontal Relationships](../../../ideas/horizontal-relationships.md).** Adler says neither praise nor rebuke — both are vertical acts that position you above the other person. Dr. Becky says separate identity from behavior. Both reject the vertical move of evaluating someone's character. "We're on the same team" is an explicitly horizontal framing. And her "I believe you + I believe in you" formula is Adler's encouragement approach: not evaluating from above, but helping someone develop the courage to face their own tasks.

- **MGI is teleological thinking.** Adler's foundational move is [teleology over etiology](../../../ideas/teleology-vs-etiology.md) — ask what purpose the behavior serves now, not what caused it in the past. Dr. Becky does exactly this: instead of "my kid is a sociopath" (etiological, identity-collapsing), she asks "what's the most generous interpretation?" — which forces you to look at present needs and missing skills rather than fixed character.

- **"Feelings overpowering skills" rejects etiology.** Dr. Becky's core insight — bad behavior is feelings that overpower skills — is a forward-looking, skill-gap framing rather than a blame-the-past framing. You don't punish someone into learning how to swim. You teach the skill. This is Adler's "from possession to practice": you are not your traits, you are your choices (and your skills).

- **Connect before correcting ≈ [Community Feeling](../../../ideas/community-feeling.md).** Connection as the bridge between two people that enables cooperation is Adler's *Gemeinschaftsgefuhl* in miniature — the sense that others are comrades, not threats. Dr. Becky's insistence on agenda-free presence maps to Adler's shift from "what can this person do for me?" to genuine concern for the other.

- **Resilience over happiness echoes [The Courage to Be Normal](../../../ideas/the-courage-to-be-normal.md).** Optimizing for happiness builds fragility; optimizing for resilience — the ability to handle the widest range of difficult experiences — builds capable humans. This is Adler's rejection of the "desire to be special" in favor of ordinary existence, earnestly lived.

Also connects to [Radical Candor](../../books/radical-candor/summary.md) (Kim Scott is mentioned explicitly in the interview) and the [Ruinous Empathy](../../../ideas/ruinous-empathy.md) pattern — Dr. Becky's story about not giving feedback to spare feelings and then having to fire someone is the exact Bob story from Radical Candor.
