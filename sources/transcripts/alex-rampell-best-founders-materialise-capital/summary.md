# The Best Founders Materialise Capital, Customers & Labour — Alex Rampell (20VC)

Alex Rampell, GP at a16z leading the $1.7B apps fund, interviewed by Harry Stebbings. This is a venture mechanics masterclass wrapped around a founder-evaluation framework. The headline thesis: the best founders can materialise labor, capital, and customers from nothing — and the ones who build lasting companies have a Count of Monte Cristo motivation that money alone can't satisfy. Behind the founder talk, Rampell lays out three investment pillars: green field bingo (selling to new companies, not incumbents' hostages), software that does the job of labor, and walled gardens of proprietary data. The most portable ideas are his coinage of "[hostages not customers](../../ideas/hostages-not-customers.md)" and the "[green field bingo](../../ideas/green-field-bingo.md)" strategy that explains why Stripe worked.

## Key ideas

- **[Hostages not customers](../../ideas/hostages-not-customers.md).** Rampell's original framing. The best enterprise software companies don't just have customers — they have hostages. SAP, Workday, NetSuite: once your data is in their system of record, switching is a multi-year, career-threatening project. The hostage dynamic means incumbents don't need great products. The startup's counter-move is [green field bingo](../../ideas/green-field-bingo.md) — don't try to free the hostages, sell to companies that haven't been captured yet.

- **[Green field bingo](../../ideas/green-field-bingo.md).** The strategy of selling to new companies rather than fighting incumbents for their hostages. Stripe didn't steal a single customer from Chase Paymentech — they sold to companies that didn't exist yet. Mercury never stole a customer from SVB until SVB failed. As long as the rate of new company creation is high enough, you can build a dominant position without ever confronting the incumbent's lock-in. The inverse tells you where this strategy fails: new hospitals aren't created fast enough to make a better EHR work via green field alone.

- **[Materialise labor, capital, and customers](../../ideas/materialise-labor-capital-customers.md).** Rampell's internal framework for evaluating founders. Can this person snap their fingers and have five people follow them tomorrow for a 50% pay cut? Can they tell a story compelling enough to raise the next round? Can they land their first five customers when they have zero? Finding all three in one person is rare — and when you do, "give them money."

- **The Count of Monte Cristo motivation.** The drive beyond money. Rampell's favorite book is about a man who doesn't care about wealth — he wants revenge. The best founders have something similar: a chip on their shoulder, a hostile takeover to avenge, a need to prove the world wrong. Dave Duffield gets ousted from PeopleSoft, starts Workday — "Fuck you, Larry Ellison." Renaud Laplanche gets fired from Lending Club, starts Upgrade, which is now worth 10x more. The motivation matters because at some point someone will offer the founder $100 million, and the Count of Monte Cristo says no.

- **[Founder-capital fit](../../ideas/founder-capital-fit.md).** Nobody talks about this, but it's the most common failure mode of well-funded startups. "If I give you a billion dollars, what will you do with it?" 99 times out of 100 the answer is bad news. Not wasteful-spending bad — *decision-quality bad*. Infinite capital removes the forcing function that makes founders choose. Option A is bad, option B is bad, and the third option — making no choice at all — is the worst. With infinite capital, you can afford to not choose, and that's fatal. Necessity is the mother of invention; remove necessity, remove invention.

- **Software that does the job of labor.** The hyper-growth AI category. These companies sell into markets where the dominant "software" was Microsoft Office — or nothing. Eve sells to plaintiff attorneys: AI handles the small-ticket cases that no human would bother with on contingency. The revenue ramps insanely fast because you're replacing an $80K/year hire with a $20K/year subscription in a market that previously spent $0 on software. But the critical question: can you back into a system of record? If it's just outbound phone calls on top of 11 Labs, you'll attract 9,000 competitors built in Lovable by 17-year-olds.

- **The walled garden of proprietary data.** Rampell's third investment pillar. VLex bought every physical legal record in Spain, digitized them, and sold to law firms — $20M ARR after 25 years. Then added AI and grew 5x. Why? Because even if AGI arrives tomorrow, GPT-5.5 can't draft a response to a Spanish court case without the data. "I'd rather have GPT-3.5 plus infinite domain data than a sentient being with no data." This is the data-moat argument that actually works: not historical datasets you can scrape, but continuously accumulated domain data that requires presence in the workflow.

- **Death of the middle in VC.** Most asset classes converge to large generalists or small specialists. The mid-size generalist dies because they can't out-help the big firms (who are connected to everyone on the planet) and can't out-know the small specialists (who understand your specific domain cold). This maps onto the find-pick-win framework: venture's job is to find, pick, and win investments — and winning is a sales job. You win by being either the best generalist or the best specialist, not by being a mediocre version of both.

- **Startup vs. incumbent: the distribution race.** "The battle of every startup versus incumbent is whether the startup gets the distribution before the incumbent gets the innovation." VisiCalc lost to Lotus 1-2-3 in 5 years. Lotus lost to Excel in 15 years. In 2025, this cycle can take weeks. Every layer of past innovation (cloud, mobile, AI) has compressed the time to build and distribute a competitive product. But systems of record remain sticky — data gravity is real even when software creation is cheap.

## Connections

- **[Is SaaS Dead? Do Margins Still Matter? — Anish Acharya](../a16z-anish-acharya-saas-dead-margins-still-matter/summary.md)** — Same show, same firm, significant conceptual overlap. Anish restates several of Rampell's original framings — hostages not customers, the distribution race, the walled garden data thesis — and extends them with AI-specific analysis (weird wins, contextual companions, multi-model aggregation). Rampell is the source; Anish is the application layer.

- **[Extreme Agency](../../ideas/extreme-agency.md)** — Rampell's "high agency people" framework rhymes with Naval's extreme agency. But Rampell's version is more operational: agency manifests as the ability to materialise resources, not just as self-directedness. The Count of Monte Cristo motivation is what sustains agency through the long grind.

- **[Beginner's Mind](../../ideas/beginners-mind.md)** — Rampell on the danger of knowing too much: he passed on Stripe's seed round because he knew payments *too well* and saw all the reasons it couldn't work. His fix: bring a sparring partner with beginner's mind to every pitch meeting. Always ask "what if it works?"

- **[Task Loss vs. Job Loss](../../ideas/task-loss-vs-job-loss.md)** — Rampell's three SaaS categories (impervious, vulnerable, middle) map onto the task-loss framework. Zendesk is vulnerable because AI does the *task* of answering tickets. But the *job* of customer support might evolve rather than disappear — Tony Hsieh's Zappos model of turning support into a revenue center by investing in relationships.
