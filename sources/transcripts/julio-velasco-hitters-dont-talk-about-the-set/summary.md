# Hitters Don't Talk About the Set — Julio Velasco

Velasco's rule for his volleyball hitters is a masterclass in applied nonjudgmental awareness, delivered in three minutes. The setup: a hitter spikes the ball out of bounds and immediately blames the setter — "the set was too low, too far from the net." The setter turns around and blames the receivers — "I can't set properly when you pass badly." The receivers can't blame anyone because they're receiving the opponent's serve. The chain of blame is a closed loop of Self 1 judgment that produces zero improvement.

Velasco's rule: **hitters don't talk about the set. They solve it.** Don't judge the set as good or bad. See it as it is — "the ball is low" — and open the mental file for "low ball solutions." Your brain is an extraordinary computer; it has files for every scenario. But if instead of opening the "low ball" file, the hitter opens the "this set is bad" file, they waste the fraction of a second they need to act. They're spending time judging reality instead of responding to it.

## Key ideas

- **See reality as it is, not as you want it to be.** "If reality is as it is and not as I want it to be, the ball is low. My brain — which is an extraordinary computer — must open all the files titled 'low set' and in those files there are solutions." The hitter who judges the set ("it's bad") opens the wrong file. The hitter who sees the set ("it's low") opens the right one. This is [Nonjudgmental Awareness](../../ideas/nonjudgmental-awareness.md) in its purest applied form: perception without evaluation creates space for action.

- **Judgment breaks the chain of competence.** Velasco describes a cascade: hitter blames setter, setter blames receivers, receivers can't blame anyone. Every link in the chain is spending energy on judgment instead of solution. His intervention is to cut the judgment at the first link — the hitter — and replace it with problem-solving. The distinction between "the ball is bad" and "the ball is low" is the distinction between Self 1 interfering and Self 2 computing.

- **Positive judgment is still judgment.** Velasco's hitters learn not to say "the set was bad" directly. Instead, they say "perfect!" when the set is good — which implicitly communicates that other sets were not. Velasco sees through this immediately. Any evaluation of the set — positive or negative — means the hitter is still focused on what someone else is doing instead of on their own job. This mirrors Gallwey's insight that even positive self-talk ("great shot!") feeds the ego cycle.

## Key quotes

> "Gli schiacciatori non parlano dell'alzata, la risolvono." — Hitters don't talk about the set. They solve it.

> "Se la realtà è com'è e non come io voglio che sia, la palla è bassa. Il mio cervello, che è un computer straordinario, deve aprire tutti i file con il titolo 'palla alzata bassa.'" — If reality is as it is and not as I want it to be, the ball is low. My brain, which is an extraordinary computer, must open all the files titled "low set."

> "Voglio schiacciatori che schiacciano bene palloni alzati male." — I want hitters who hit well on badly set balls.

## Connections

- **[Self 1 vs Self 2](../../ideas/self-1-vs-self-2.md)** — The core of the clip. Self 1 judges the set ("it's bad"); Self 2 solves it ("it's low — here are my options"). Velasco is training his hitters to bypass Self 1's judgment so Self 2 can compute. But crucially, Self 1 is not absent — it is *seeing* reality clearly ("the ball is low") rather than *evaluating* it ("the ball is bad"). This is Self 1 in service of Self 2.
- **[Nonjudgmental Awareness](../../ideas/nonjudgmental-awareness.md)** — Velasco's rule is Gallwey's chapter 3 applied to volleyball. "The ball is low" is observation. "The set is bad" is judgment. The difference between the two determines whether the hitter's brain opens solution files or complaint files.
- **[The Inner Game of Tennis](../../sources/books/the-inner-game-of-tennis/summary.md)** — Gallwey's "bounce-hit" technique occupies Self 1 with observation so Self 2 can play. Velasco's "open the right file" achieves the same thing — redirect Self 1's attention from evaluation to perception, and Self 2 handles the rest.
- **[Letting It Happen](../../ideas/letting-it-happen.md)** — "I want hitters who hit well on badly set balls" is the volleyball version of letting it happen. Don't wait for perfect conditions. Respond to what's actually there.
- **[Precise Speech](../../ideas/precise-speech.md)** — Peterson's idea that naming something correctly tames the chaos. "The ball is low" is precise speech. "The set is bad" is imprecise — it assigns blame instead of describing reality.
- **[Separation of Tasks](../../ideas/separation-of-tasks.md)** — The hitter's task is hitting. The setter's task is setting. When the hitter judges the set, they've crossed into someone else's task. Adler (via *The Courage to Be Disliked*) would recognize this instantly.
