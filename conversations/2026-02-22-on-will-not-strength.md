## Thread

Challenging the pop version of the Backwards Law ("stop wanting and you'll get it") through the lens of martial arts practice. Arrived at a sharper distinction: use will not strength. Keep the direction, drop the tension.

## Dialogue

**Q:** The Backwards Law is the highest-connectivity idea in the garden with zero conversation coverage. Gallwey, Manson, Adler, Haidt, Frankl, and Watts all arrive at the same paradox from different directions: trying harder makes the thing recede. It directly challenges the agency-first positions from the agency conversation. Is "letting it happen" compatible with ambition?

**A:** Disagree with the framing. It's not that trying harder doesn't work — it's "use will not strength." In martial arts, beginners move their muscles by tensing them. Shoulders rise, lower back closes. You're up in your head. That's normal. But you still want to try hard — you just let go of unnecessary strength. You keep telling your body: let go of that tension. Keep the back open, lower the shoulders. Breathe deep. Same for the mind: unnecessary thoughts get a "thank you, but not now." Another concept from my teacher: "From External to Internal." It's easier to understand these concepts in the body first. You feel tension, you let go. You learn to relax in the face of challenge. Then you apply it to the mind — but the mind is much harder to control. You first have to master your body, your elephant, and only then do you have the tools to work on the rider.

**Q:** "Use will not strength" is sharper than anything Gallwey or Manson offer — they collapse it into "stop trying," which loses the directed intention. But when you say "master the body first," Haidt's argument is that the rider can't master the elephant through direct control — only through indirect means. So when you master your body in martial arts, are you overriding the elephant or building a communication channel the rider never had?

**A:** Neither. Mastering the body means becoming a good rider for the elephant. Look after it, push it more over time. Get comfortable being uncomfortable. Build a routine that puts it in shape. Get excited at your discipline, at the idea of making yourself do something and keeping your commitment. And then you can work on the rider — your thoughts, patterns, demons.

**Q:** So the sequence is: become a good caretaker of the elephant first, then turn to the rider's own problems. That's backwards from Haidt — he says the rider is the problem (rationalizing, overthinking). Your sequence says the rider is the *advanced* problem. The elephant is the beginner problem. Where did you learn this — did your teacher lay it out explicitly or did you reconstruct it?

**A:** If you cannot take care of your body, how can you take care of your mind? Taking care of the body is easy. The inner work, the mental work, the spiritual work — that's the hard part. And yes, I'm reconstructing and integrating these theories as I go.

**Q:** You're saying the body is easier because it's more concrete — you feel tension, you release it, the feedback loop is immediate. But is that actually true? People spend decades with terrible posture, chronic tension, injuries they ignore. The body's feedback is only "easy" if you've trained the skill of noticing. So is the real sequence not body-then-mind, but *noticing-then-everything* — and the body just happens to be where noticing is easiest to practice?

**A:** OK, if you want to get technical: it's mind first. The mind notices "I want to improve" — the will to meaning, the desire, the X-shaped hole. Then you get to work on the body. "Sweeping the floor of the temple." You practice the skills that you can later turn toward the mind. But the main point stands: you need to will, you need to strive, to accomplish something. You don't need to tense up. You don't need to try too hard. I don't believe in "as soon as you stop wanting something, you obtain it." That's too enlightened, and half the people who use it don't know anything about Buddhism. Maybe they obtain it because they relax, they project confidence, and that's why things start to work. But you can be relaxed and work toward a goal — versus being relaxed in general and hoping a roast chicken flies into your mouth.

**Q:** Here's the steelman for the Backwards Law — it's not really about effort, it's about monitoring the gap. Three empirically documented mechanisms: (1) Performance choking — Beilock's explicit monitoring theory, Self 1 interference with research behind it. (2) Paradoxical intention — Frankl used this clinically. Insomniacs told to stay awake fall asleep faster. Replicated, not philosophy. (3) Hedonic adaptation — lottery winners return to baseline happiness within months. The wanting doesn't stop when you get the thing. So the steelman is: stop measuring the gap while performing. The monitoring *is* the unnecessary tension. Which sounds a lot like what you've been saying — the will is the direction, the strength is the constant checking.

**A:** Then how do athletes improve? They need to know the gap between their performance and what's required to win. Aren't we just talking about obsessive behavior? Like "stop obsessively tracking things"?

**Q:** Direct hit on the steelman. Athletes absolutely track the gap — that's what coaching is. But they track it in *training*, not in *performance*. The sprinter reviews footage on Monday. On race day, they run. Waitzkin has the same structure: "making smaller circles" is obsessive gap-tracking in practice, the soft zone is dropping all of that in competition. So maybe it's: don't track the gap and perform at the same time. They use different cognitive modes and interfere with each other. Is that a real distinction in your practice?

**A:** It's partly true. Coach K calls it "Next Play" — don't think about the previous play because you'll mess up the current one. Don't dwell on the past. But you definitely need to track the score. So it's a nonjudgmental commentary in your mind — "we're 8 points behind" as matter of fact, not as narrative. That's the right compromise. And yet: somebody like Michael Jordan would use the previous play as fuel to give 110% on the current play. So unless you can channel it — unless you can turn the emotion of rage into a Nick Cave song — the second best is to avoid passing judgment. The past is the past. This is true for giving a talk or facing your boss: being present in the moment, even after a false start.

**Q:** That's a three-tier hierarchy: (1) Best case: channel the emotion into fuel — Jordan uses the missed shot as rage, Nick Cave turns pain into art. (2) Second best: nonjudgmental awareness — "we're 8 points behind" as fact. Coach K's "Next Play." (3) Failure mode: dwelling — the missed shot becomes a narrative about who you are. The Backwards Law only describes the failure mode and prescribes tier two. It doesn't acknowledge that tier one exists. But is tier one sustainable, or does it burn people out? Jordan was famously miserable. The cost seems high.

**A:** That's for another topic. Are extremely successful people something to imitate? Do you really want to be Michael Jordan, or Picasso, or Steve Jobs, or Musk? These people are possessed by something incredible that drives them to extreme lengths to accomplish the impossible. It is not for everybody to live life like that.

## Positions

- **"Use will not strength" is the correct framing, not the Backwards Law.** The pop version ("stop wanting and you'll get it") is lazy mysticism. The real skill is keeping direction and intention while dropping unnecessary tension — physical and mental.
- **The body-mind sequence has three stages.** (1) The mind initiates — the will to meaning, the X-shaped hole, "I want to improve." (2) The body is where you practice the skills — noticing tension, releasing it, discipline under pressure. "Sweeping the floor of the temple." (3) You bring those skills back to the mind for the harder inner work. From external to internal.
- **Relaxation is earned through competence, not granted through detachment.** You can relax into a stretch because you've stretched before. You can relax in the meeting because you know your value. Relaxation without a foundation is recklessness.
- **The Backwards Law's steelman is half-right.** Don't track the gap during performance — register facts ("we're 8 points behind"), don't build narratives. "Next Play." But the real insight is narrower than the pop version claims.
- **There is a three-tier hierarchy of relating to setbacks during performance.** (1) Channel emotion into fuel (Jordan, Nick Cave) — highest performance, highest cost. (2) Nonjudgmental awareness — sustainable, effective. (3) Dwelling and narrative-building — the failure mode where choking lives.
- **Tier one (channeling emotion) is possession, not prescription.** It's real but not a model to follow. Most people should aim for nonjudgmental awareness.

## New concept handles

- **"Use will not strength"** — keep directed intention, drop unnecessary tension. Physical and mental. The martial arts corrective to the Backwards Law.
- **"From external to internal"** — skills learned in the body (concrete, immediate feedback) can later be applied to the mind (abstract, murky feedback). The body is the training ground for mental skills.
- **"Sweeping the floor of the temple"** — the body work that precedes the mind work. Discipline, routine, physical practice as prerequisite to inner work.

## Open questions

- Is tier one (channeling emotion into fuel) always costly, or can it be sustainable for certain temperaments?
- Are extremely successful people models to learn *from* or cautionary tales? The possession that drives them to greatness also seems to consume them. Where is the line?
- Does "from external to internal" have a limit? Are there mental skills that *can't* be learned through the body first?
- How does "use will not strength" interact with the X-shaped hole? Is the X-shaped hole the source of will, and is unnecessary strength what happens when the hole feels too urgent to fill?
