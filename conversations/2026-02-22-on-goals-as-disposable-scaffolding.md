## Thread

The tension between match-quality / sampling approaches (Range, "connect the dots backwards") and goal-driven approaches (Covey's "begin with the end in mind," Rohn's "give me your list of goals"). Whether goals are destinations or disposable discovery tools.

## Dialogue

**Q:** Your PhD story undermines your own argument. You started a PhD — that's not a greedy algorithm following local interest. That's a goal-driven leap toward "being a professor." The greedy algorithm would have kept you doing whatever was fun and available *before* the PhD. So the real story isn't "sampling beats goal-setting." It's "I set a goal, pursued it, realized the daily reality didn't match, and quit." That's match-quality *discovery* — which required the goal-driven attempt to surface. Without Covey's "begin with the end in mind" pulling you into the PhD, you never would have learned that academia wasn't your match. So: did goal-setting actually serve you there, even though the goal was wrong?

**A:** Yeah fair enough. But then I would say: "treat goals like plans" — "if you fail to plan, you're planning to fail" (Rohn again), but be quick to discard your plans and adapt. It's not the "I must get this promotion this year" — it's more of "I plan to get promoted, if I can't here are my options, and I am not scared of hopping to another job and doing something completely different."

**Q:** That's a cleaner position. Goals as disposable scaffolding, not identity. But you're collapsing a real tension into unity too quickly. There's a cost to disposable goals that you're not pricing in. Covey's "two creations" isn't just "have a plan." It's that the mental creation — the vision of what you're building — shapes what you notice. Jobs didn't stumble into the Mac because he was sampling. The calligraphy class only became useful because he had an obsessive vision of what a computer should feel like. The dots connected backwards, sure — but only because there was a specific person with a specific obsession catching those dots. Your greedy algorithm — go deep on what's available, gain competence, look around — what's the selection function? When you "look around and see what opportunities are unlocked," how do you pick? If you don't have a strong pull (the X-shaped hole), aren't you just optimizing for local maxima?

**A:** That's key isn't it? You don't know anything, you sample, then you set a goal about what you think might be your Ikigai, you pursue it, you reconsider it, you sample something else, you find a new peak to climb. Rinse and repeat. If done badly, you end up constantly second guessing yourself (should I stay or should I go) or pursue that promotion and sacrifice everything else and feel empty once you obtain it. I myself don't know how to navigate it. Sometimes it's a good idea to push to obtain difficult things (the promotion, again) only for the sake of accomplishing hard things. And gain competence. Once you gained competence in one domain it's much easier to gain competence in another domain (even Range says so).

**Q:** When you pushed for that promotion — the one you're calling "for the sake of accomplishing hard things" — what did it actually feel like day to day? Not the narrative you tell about it now. The actual texture of the pursuit. Was there a point where the difficulty itself became the thing that was interesting, or was it always "endure this to get the credential"?

**A:** The difficulty itself was interesting. And the allure of a checkmark "Society recognizes I am able to do X" was interesting to me. I was very aware of the grind and the physical toll (staying up late, skipping gym) and mental toll (less time with family, more stressed out with the kids). You can do difficult pursuits in bursts, right? There's a time to push and a time to rest. But it seems these days with AI everyone is giving 110% (and is expecting 110% from their employees) all the time. There's no end in sight. I guess at one point you either transform in the kind of person that is able to achieve these sort of goals ("pressure makes diamonds") or you burn out. A great definition of burnout: it's not about working hard — it's about being forced to do things you don't want to do. So when you want to go to the school play but you're forced to work — that's burnout. And you monitor yourself and your state and think: "can I survive this? can I push a little harder?" careful not to crash and burn. I guess that's where the wisdom is (the Serenity Prayer).

**Q:** You just said three things that don't sit together. One: "difficulty itself was interesting." Two: "there's a time to push and a time to rest." Three: burnout is being forced to do things you don't want to do. If the difficulty is genuinely interesting, where does the burnout come from? You described skipping gym, less time with family, stress with kids. That's not "being forced to do things you don't want to do" — you wanted the promotion. You chose the pursuit. So either the burnout definition is wrong, or what you actually wanted was more complicated than the checkmark. The promotion pursuit wasn't pure match-quality joy. It was partly "society recognizes I can do X" — your words. That's external validation. And external validation has a specific failure mode: it never stops asking. You get the checkmark, and the next one appears. The 110% culture you're describing isn't new — it's what happens when an entire system runs on external checkmarks. So which part was actually yours? The difficulty being interesting — that sounds like intrinsic pull, the X-shaped hole. The societal checkmark — that sounds like Covey's "begin with the end in mind" but with someone else's end. Can you separate them? When you were staying up late, which one was keeping you going?

**A:** There's a degree beyond which I start to feel resentful. You can only stretch so much. You can only lift so much weight. You can only skip so many family dinners. I am not just doing something because "it's challenging" — oh what a great excuse to skip time with my children — no! Just like the research about the 'flow' state: it needs to be at the right level of challenge, just beyond your comfort zone. You need to relax into this new discomfort. And you do so as you rebalance your whole life: when to see friends, when to exercise, when to go on a family vacation. And yeah, the checkmark itself is meaningless: I was already performing at a higher level. The job title change was backwards-facing. Nothing changed except a bigger paycheck and more expectations. But it's a bit like saying: I want to play basketball and I don't care about the score — well the score tells the truth. You either win or not. You either have the new title or not. It's not a matter of "feeling like a winner" or "feeling like I operate at a higher level." External validation keeps you honest.

**Q:** "External validation keeps you honest." That's a genuinely unfashionable position — most of your garden leans toward intrinsic motivation, Gallwey's Self 2, the backwards law. And here you are saying: no, the scoreboard matters because it's the reality check. But does it actually keep you honest, or does it keep you legible? Those are different things. The basketball score tells truth about who won that game. The promotion tells truth about what that organization values. But you just said yourself — you were already performing at the higher level. The title was backwards-facing. So the scoreboard was behind reality, not ahead of it. If you'd used the scoreboard as your signal, you would have underestimated yourself until the title landed. Your tai chi practice — there's no scoreboard there. No belt system worth respecting. No promotion. How do you stay honest about your progress in that domain? And is the answer different in a way that matters?

**A:** Well in tai chi there are martial applications: you either take down the opponent or not. You either do so effortlessly or not. There are many signals for truth. And yeah it's unfashionable, but I think Jordan Peterson might say it in my garden: kids need to be socialized by others. The group is a quick feedback mechanism on the right way to play, to push boundaries, without ending up in a fight. In a sense bullies perform the social obligation of normalizing the outliers into saying "this is weird" (not saying this is a good thing, but it's one way to see it). So Jordan does see a lot of value in the social function. I can interview to another position and tell them about my responsibilities and they can realize I was performing at a higher level — but then the next question is: why no new job title? That in itself is a signal.

**Q:** You said "sometimes it's just chance — too many bad things at the same time and you snap." That's more honest than any framework. But the frameworks help you before the snap. Duckworth and Epstein actually reconciled: grit without match quality is stubbornness, match quality without grit is dilettantism. The sequence matters — explore first, then commit. Duckworth's real model is a goal hierarchy: top-level goal in ink (your ultimate concern), mid-level and low-level goals in pencil (disposable). Quitting the PhD wasn't a failure of grit — it was correctly quitting a mid-level goal. Annie Duke's *Quit* gives the sharpest operational tools: (1) Kill criteria — set before you start: "If by [date] I haven't [reached state], I quit." (2) The Fresh Start Test — "Knowing everything I know now, would I choose this today?" If no, you're only staying because of sunk costs. (3) The Close-Call Rule — Levitt's coin-flip study with 22,500 people found those who changed were significantly happier at 6 months. When it's genuinely a coin flip, quit — because we systematically over-stay. And the burnout vs. poor fit distinction from clinical research: if time off resets you and you still believe in the mission, it's burnout (fix the conditions). If dread returns immediately and the work itself fails to engage you, it's poor fit (leave). But here's the thing — the felt sense you're describing (flow vs. impostor syndrome) is actually the best real-time diagnostic. Not Duckworth's hierarchy, not Duke's precommitment. Those are planning tools. In the moment, your body is already running the computation: Am I growing or shrinking? Is the discomfort the kind that builds capacity (investment in loss) or the kind that erodes identity? You said "very hard to tell." But you did tell. You left the PhD. You stayed for the promotion. Your body knew before your mind had a framework. Is the real problem that you don't trust that signal?

**A:** Yes — you don't trust the signal, because you don't know if you're falling prey to sunk cost fallacy to keep going, or if you're giving up too early and will regret it.

## Positions

- **Goals are disposable scaffolding, not identity.** Treat them like plans: useful for initiating action and discovering match-quality, but hold them loosely. The PhD goal was wrong, but pursuing it was necessary to discover that.
- **The navigation algorithm is iterative, not greedy.** Sample broadly, notice what pulls you, pursue it hard enough for the scoreboard to give signal, monitor resentment as a canary, and be willing to walk away when the cost-to-signal ratio breaks.
- **External validation keeps you honest.** Unfashionable but defensible. The scoreboard is a lagging indicator — it confirms rather than predicts — but a world without scoreboards lets you delude yourself. The title, the interview question, the martial application: all reality checks.
- **Resentment is the signal, not difficulty.** The difficulty itself can be interesting (flow-state zone). Burnout comes when the costs spill into domains you care about more — family, health, identity — not from hard work itself.
- **Competence transfers across domains.** Once you've gained mastery in one area, the meta-skills (learning how to learn, tolerating being bad, pushing through plateaus) make the next domain easier. This is the strongest argument for the "E-shaped engineer" path.
- **Duckworth and Epstein are not opposed.** Grit without match quality is stubbornness. Match quality without grit is dilettantism. The sequence is: explore first, then commit. Duckworth's real model is a goal hierarchy — top-level in ink, everything else in pencil.
- **The body knows before the mind has a framework.** Flow vs. impostor syndrome, growing vs. shrinking, investment-in-loss discomfort vs. identity-eroding discomfort — the felt sense is the best real-time diagnostic. But trusting it is the hard part.

## Frameworks referenced

- **Duckworth's goal hierarchy:** Top-level goal (ink) → mid-level goals (pencil) → low-level goals (pencil). Quit tactics freely. Protect the ultimate concern.
- **Annie Duke's kill criteria:** "If by [date] I haven't [reached state], I quit." Set before starting.
- **Duke's Fresh Start Test:** "Knowing everything I know now, would I choose this today?"
- **Duke/Levitt Close-Call Rule:** When it's genuinely a coin flip, quit. We systematically over-stay.
- **Burnout vs. poor fit diagnostic:** Recovery test (does time off reset you?), values alignment (do you believe in the mission?), identity resonance ("I'm burned out" vs. "I don't think I'm really this").
- **Godin's three curves (The Dip):** Dip (push through), Cul-de-Sac (quit now), Cliff (quit before you fall).

## Open questions

- **The trust problem.** You don't trust your own signal because you can't distinguish sunk cost fallacy (staying too long) from quitting too early (regret). Duke says: if it's a close call, quit — because the bias is toward over-staying. But that's a statistical argument, not a felt one. How do you make it felt?
- **Does the scoreboard need to be external?** Tai chi has martial applications as a truth test — but that's self-selected, not institutional. Is there a version of "keeping yourself honest" that doesn't depend on organizational recognition?
- **The 110% culture problem.** If the system demands sustained intensity with no rest cycles, does the "seasons" model (time to push, time to rest) become impossible? Or does maintaining seasons become the act of resistance?
- **Is the E-shaped path a choice or a rationalization?** "My scattered interests will connect eventually" is also what someone says when they can't commit. How do you distinguish genuine range from avoidance of depth?
