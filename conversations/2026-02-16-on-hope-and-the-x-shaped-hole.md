## Thread

Manson argues hope is destructive. Frankl argues meaning requires future-orientation. Both are evergreen. Are they contradicting each other?

## Dialogue

**Q:** Manson says hope is the problem — it requires believing the present is broken. Frankl says the opposite — prisoners who lost their "why" died. You value both. So which is it? Is hope something you cultivate or something you're suspicious of?

**A:** Manson doesn't equate "stop hoping" with "there's no why to live." He rejects living hoping the circumstances will improve. This is also Jim Rohn: don't wish things were easier, wish you were better. Hoping things will improve misses the point. You have to focus on what you can do yourself instead. Frankl is not really talking about hope. He's talking about a why. He quotes Nietzsche: "A man with a Why can tolerate any How." For Frankl the why was hoping one day he'd be back and could finish his book. He knew the chances were slim. He wasn't telling himself "I sure hope this ends soon so I can publish my book." It's a different kind of hope. It can be described as "faith" — faith that, if someday he returned home, he had a book to publish.

**Q:** So there are two things called "hope" and they're almost opposites. Hope-as-wishing (passive, circumstance-dependent — what Manson attacks) and hope-as-why (active, internal — Frankl's version). You reached for the word "faith." Is the actual line that useful hope is the kind that doesn't depend on the outcome?

**A:** Yeah. And personally I hope to see my kids grow and become successful adults and I hope to be a good parent. But that doesn't mean I'm passive. This is a hope that spurs you toward action. That's a wish. Kind of reminds me of the movie Wish — "I make this wish to be something more than I have been." It's human desire, not hedonistic, but for growth. The desire for mastery, for closure. Desire for things in and of themselves (Daniel Pink in Drive? somebody else?). It's a different kind of pleasure. You desire a world where a book was written with these ideas, and you bring about that world. You don't desire the fame the book imparts on you.

**Q:** You're describing something you already have in your garden — Manson's Formula of Humanity, which is Kant: treat things as ends in themselves, never merely as means. Manson uses it for people, but you're applying it to desires. There are means-desires and ends-desires. And yes, that's Pink in Drive — intrinsic motivation. You just connected Frankl, Manson, Rohn, Kant, Pink, and a Disney movie into one idea. Does this cluster have a name for you?

**A:** I'll connect one more — in Build, Fadell says something like "builders just want something to exist." And that's motivating enough. I suspect there's something similar in Creativity Inc. It comes from the feeling that, if that thing existed, the world would feel more intelligible. More "everything in its right place." A missing piece of the puzzle. A missing foundation created. It's almost like the void, the lack of it, is calling you forward and you need to respond to it. It's the call of adventure.

**Q:** Is this always what it feels like, or is this the story you tell after the fact? When you're actually in the middle of building something — parenting on a hard Tuesday, grinding through a project — does it feel like "responding to the call of the void"? Or does it sometimes just feel like stubbornness, or obligation, or habit?

**A:** For sure. People need the skills and discipline to carry forward the ambition. To respond to the call of adventure.

**Q:** [Brainstorming concept handles — "the pull of absence," "creative gravity," "the summons," others.]

**A:** What about how it resonates with "there is a book-shaped hole in this world"? Like you can see the shape of the hole and that's attracting you. Like when you feel like you need to stick a fork in a power socket. Is there a term for this?

**Q:** The fork-in-socket feeling is l'appel du vide — "the call of the void." The urge to jump at the edge of a cliff. You're describing the creative version: not the void calling you to destroy yourself, but calling you to fill it. Also: horror vacui (nature abhors a vacuum) and the Zeigarnik effect (unfinished things nag at you).

**A:** "The call of the void" — very interesting. In literature it has a negative connotation but it could also be positive. Let's call it "the X-shaped hole."

*Continued 2026-02-18*

**A:** Frankl's argument for the [will to meaning](../ideas/will-to-meaning.md) has exactly the structure of the X-shaped hole. The [existential vacuum](../ideas/the-existential-vacuum.md) is his proof — *because the void has a shape*. If we were Freudian pleasure machines, affluent comfort would satisfy us. If we were Adlerian power machines, status would do it. But the Sunday neurosis hits when you have pleasure and power and still feel empty. The shape of the hole doesn't match what you're pouring into it. The void is meaning-shaped, and that's how Frankl knows the will to meaning is primary — the absence announces what belongs there.

**Q:** So the existential vacuum *is* an X-shaped hole — but one where the X is "meaning." And the X-shaped hole from the earlier conversation is the concrete, particular instantiation? Frankl says meaning must be discovered in specific situations — "it is he who is asked."

**A:** Right. The general drive (will to meaning) and the particular pull (the X-shaped hole) are the same mechanism at different resolutions. Frankl provides the universal: humans need meaning. The X-shaped hole provides the specific: *this* book, *this* company, *these* children. And the reframing of l'appel du vide fits too — Frankl says the vacuum is "not itself a disease" but "a distinctly human predicament." The emptiness isn't pathological. It's a signal. The anxiety is how the will to meaning announces itself. It only becomes pathological when you misread the signal and reach for the wrong shape — pleasure, power, fame. That's the internal X-shaped hole again, where no external thing fits.

**Q:** There's also a connection to Lakoff here. In [Women, Fire, and Dangerous Things](../sources/books/women-fire-and-dangerous-things/summary.md), emotions have elaborate conceptual structure — anger isn't a formless blob but a five-stage scenario organized by metaphor. The existential vacuum has the same property. It's not formless dread — it's a *shaped absence* that carries information about what should go there. The shape of the void is conceptual content.

**A:** That's interesting. And the connection to [embodied reason](../ideas/embodied-reason.md) — the void isn't abstract. You feel it in your body. The Sunday neurosis, the restlessness, the boredom that isn't really boredom. It's a bodily signal, not just a philosophical position.

## Positions

- **Hope has two forms, and they're almost opposites.** Hope-as-wishing is passive and circumstance-dependent — this is what Manson and Rohn reject. Hope-as-why is active and internal — this is Frankl's version. The line between them: useful hope doesn't depend on the outcome.
- **The Formula of Humanity applies to desires, not just people.** There are means-desires (I want X so I get Y) and ends-desires (I want X to exist because it should exist). Ends-desires are what drive builders, parents, writers. This connects Kant → Manson → Pink → Frankl → Fadell.
- **The X-shaped hole.** The world has gaps shaped like specific things — a book, a company, a theory, a family. That absence exerts a pull, like a creative version of l'appel du vide. You can feel the shape of what's missing and you can't stop thinking about it. The mechanisms underneath: l'appel du vide (the involuntary pull), horror vacui (nature abhors a vacuum), the Zeigarnik effect (the incomplete nags at you).
- **The call of the void can be generative, not just destructive.** Literature treats l'appel du vide as pathological. But the same mechanism — an absence pulling you toward it — drives creation when pointed at what should exist rather than at self-destruction.
- **Skills and discipline are the bridge.** Feeling the pull isn't enough. You still need the craft and stubbornness to do the work on a hard Tuesday. The X-shaped hole provides direction; discipline provides the legs.
- **The X-shaped hole can be in you or in the world.** Sometimes the hole is internal — and you try to fill it with things that soothe the ego (fame, fortune, sex, money). That's the unhealthy version: no external thing fits the shape, so you keep consuming without resolution (the [backwards law](../ideas/the-backwards-law.md)). The healthy version points outward: the hole is in the world, and you build the thing that fills it. This is "make something people want" — not "make something that makes you feel important." When you focus on the X-shaped hole in the world, the ego question dissolves.
- **The [existential vacuum](../ideas/the-existential-vacuum.md) IS an X-shaped hole — where the X is "meaning."** Frankl's proof for the [will to meaning](../ideas/will-to-meaning.md) has the same structure: the void has a specific shape, and wrong-shaped fillers (pleasure, power) don't fit. The emptiness is not pathological but a signal — the mechanism by which the will to meaning announces itself. It becomes pathological only when you misread the signal and keep pouring wrong-shaped things into it.
- **The X-shaped hole is the will to meaning made concrete.** Frankl provides the universal (humans need meaning); the X-shaped hole provides the particular (*this* book, *this* family, *this* company). The two are the same mechanism at different resolutions.
- **The void carries conceptual content.** The existential vacuum is not formless dread. Like Lakoff's analysis of anger (which has a five-stage structure organized by metaphor), the void is a *shaped absence* — you can feel what's missing from the shape of the emptiness. This is also an [embodied](../ideas/embodied-reason.md) phenomenon: you feel it in your body as restlessness, the Sunday neurosis, boredom that isn't really boredom.

## Open questions

- Is there a meaningful difference between the X-shaped hole and "calling" / "vocation" (from the Latin vocare, to call)? Or is it the same idea with religious packaging?
- Where does obligation fit? Parenting sometimes feels like the X-shaped hole (you can see who your kids could become) and sometimes just feels like duty. Are these different motivations or the same one at different energy levels?
- Fadell and Catmull (Creativity Inc.) likely have more to say about this. Worth checking Build and Creativity Inc. for explicit statements about why builders build.
- The discipline/skills point was acknowledged but not explored. What's the relationship between feeling the pull and having the capacity to respond? Can the pull exist without the skill, or does competence sharpen the pull? (Waitzkin's "[making smaller circles](../ideas/making-smaller-circles.md)" might connect here — the deeper your skill, the more precisely you see the shape of the hole.)
- If the void carries conceptual content (it's shaped, not formless), does Lakoff's framework of [idealized cognitive models](../ideas/idealized-cognitive-models.md) apply? Is the X-shaped hole an ICM of what's missing? Could you map the image schemas: the void as CONTAINER, the missing thing as ENTITY, the pull as FORCE?
- Frankl found the vacuum in 25% of European students and 60% of American ones. Is the X-shaped hole culturally variable? Do different cultures produce differently shaped voids?
