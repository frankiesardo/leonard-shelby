## Thread

A pattern emerged across the garden: nearly every major source mounts some version of the same argument against Enlightenment rationalism. Reason worked brilliantly on the problems of the 1800s — science, medicine, governance — but it became a hammer that made everything look like a nail. The interesting thing is that they all appeal to emotion, but they're making four genuinely different arguments.

## Positions

### 1. Reason is the servant — Haidt, Storr, Manson

The conscious reasoning mind doesn't actually drive decisions. Haidt's elephant-and-rider: the elephant (emotion/intuition) decides, the rider (reason) fabricates justifications after the fact. Storr says the brain is "a story processor, not a logic processor." Manson's version: the Feeling Brain drives the car, the Thinking Brain just navigates. Their shared claim is that reason literally *cannot function* without emotion — citing Damasio's patient Elliot, who lost emotional capacity and became unable to make any decision at all. This isn't "reason is overrated." It's "reason is structurally dependent on emotion."

### 2. Embodied cognition — Lakoff, Gallwey, Waitzkin

Reason isn't just emotionally dependent, it's *physically* grounded. Lakoff argues all abstract thought grows from bodily metaphor — there's no "God's-eye view" of pure logic. Gallwey and Waitzkin come at this from performance: the analytical mind (Self 1) must eventually dissolve into embodied intuition. Waitzkin's phrase is "numbers to leave numbers" — internalize technique until it becomes feel. The hammer-and-nail problem here is the belief that conscious, verbal analysis is always the highest form of knowing.

### 3. Psychology beats engineering — Sutherland, Norman, Pink

Sutherland is the loudest: business problems have cheap psychological solutions that get ignored because everyone chases expensive technological ones. Norman says blaming users for not following rational instructions is a design failure. Pink says carrots-and-sticks (rational incentive structures) actively backfire. Their position is pragmatic rather than philosophical — they're not arguing that reason is *wrong*, just that organizations systematically over-invest in rational/engineering solutions and under-invest in psychological ones because they can only see what they can measure.

### 4. Meaning over logic — Frankl, Kornfield, Naval

The deepest cut. Reason can't answer the question "why should I care?" Frankl: will to meaning is the primary human drive, and no amount of logic sustains you in a concentration camp. Kornfield: emotions are visitors, not problems to be solved — expanding awareness around them is a craft, not a calculation. Naval: happiness is a skill grounded in observation, not rational achievement. This group isn't anti-reason — they're pointing at a domain (meaning, suffering, inner life) where reason simply has no jurisdiction.

## The meta-observation

The Enlightenment produced real gains — science, medicine, governance. But each camp is saying the same tool got applied to domains where it doesn't work: motivation (Pink), product design (Norman/Sutherland), self-knowledge (Gallwey/Waitzkin), moral judgment (Haidt), and existential meaning (Frankl). The hammer worked on nails. Then we started treating everything as a nail.

## Open questions

- Is there a fifth camp missing? Harari and Epstein both touch the theme but don't fit cleanly. Harari's "shared myths drive history" is a descriptive claim, not a normative one. Epstein's "analogical thinking beats narrow expertise in wicked environments" is about cognitive strategy, not about reason vs. emotion per se.
- Are camps 1 and 2 actually making the same argument at different levels of abstraction? Haidt says emotion drives reason. Lakoff says the body drives thought. Both are saying the "higher" faculty depends on the "lower" one.
- Where does reason still deserve to be the hammer? Science, engineering, formal systems, medicine. Nobody in the garden argues against reason in its home domain. The critique is always about misapplication, not about the tool itself.
